There's no single code number I can give you, and writing one I've guessed onto a permit form is the kind of error that gets a permit rejected or, worse, creates liability if the real limit is lower. So I'm going to flag the gap rather than supply a figure.

Occupancy load isn't a fixed number attached to "mid-size community hall." It's computed for your specific room under the fire/building code your jurisdiction has adopted (in the US, typically the International Building Code / IFC via a state or county amendment). The calculation is floor area divided by an occupant-load factor that depends on how the space is used — and your maker fair spans two very different factors:

- Tables/vendors with fixed displays read as "business" or mercantile use — a coarser factor (order of ~30–60 sq ft per person, unconcentrated).
- Any open standing/assembly area uses a much denser factor (order of ~7–15 sq ft per person).

The occupant load is then capped further by egress: total exit width and the number of compliant exits can hold the legal number below what floor area alone would allow. So two halls of identical size can have very different posted capacities. (Confidence: High that the number is code- and room-specific and not inferable from "mid-size"; the specific factor values are Moderate recall and shouldn't be used to compute your number — see below.)

Where the actual number comes from, in order of preference:

1. The posted occupancy placard — community halls almost always have a certificate of occupancy or a fire-marshal capacity sign near the main entrance or in the rental office. That posted number is the authoritative one to copy onto the form.
2. The venue manager / rental contract — they usually know it and may have it on the rental paperwork.
3. Your local fire marshal or building department — they set it and can confirm it; for a permitted public event this is also who you'll ultimately answer to, so their number is the safe one.

What I'd do: get the number from source 1 or 2 today; if neither exists, one call to the fire marshal (source 3) resolves it and puts you on record as having asked. Don't back into it from square footage yourself for a legal form.

One adjacent thing worth naming, since it's the reason this matters beyond the paperwork: if your expected attendance is anywhere near the posted limit, occupancy management (a door count, not just booked tables) becomes a real day-of logistics item, and it interacts with the exits and aisle-width you'll be laying out in the venue/layout bucket. Flag it there too.
