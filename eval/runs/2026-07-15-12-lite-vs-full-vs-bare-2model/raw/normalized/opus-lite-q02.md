One thing to flag before the tactics: you've packed a motive into the premise — "because he wants to take credit for my work." That might be exactly right. But "keeps forgetting to loop me in" and "is deliberately stealing credit" are two different claims, and you can only see the first one directly. The second is an inference about what's in his head. If you walk into a confrontation having already convicted him of the intent, and you're wrong (or even just can't prove it), the conversation collapses into "no I'm not / yes you are" and you look like the aggressor. So separate them.

First, check whether the pattern is what you think it is. Before any confrontation, get the receipts in one place:
- Which specific emails/threads? Were you clearly a relevant party each time, or arguably optional?
- Did the omission actually cost you visibility — did someone downstream credit him for something you did?
- Is it only you he leaves off, or is he sloppy with cc lists generally?

That last question matters a lot. Genuine disorganization and deliberate credit-grabbing produce the same surface symptom, and the fix is completely different.

If it holds up, the strongest move usually isn't a confrontation — it's making the omission cost him nothing to fix and something to repeat. Confrontation-about-intent invites denial. Instead:
- Reply-all onto the threads you find out about, adding your context: "Adding myself here since this builds on the X work — happy to take the Y piece." This quietly reasserts your fingerprint on the work without accusing anyone.
- Ask him directly and neutrally to include you going forward: "I keep landing in the middle of these threads late. Can you add me to anything touching X from the start? I lose time reconstructing context otherwise." Framed as a workflow problem, it's hard to refuse, and refusal itself becomes informative.

If it keeps happening after you've asked plainly, that's your real evidence — not the mind-reading, but a documented request that he ignored. That's the point where you either name it to him more directly ("I've asked twice and I'm still getting left off — what's going on?") or take the paper trail to your manager. And notice the framing there: you're bringing a pattern of ignored requests and misattributed work, not an accusation of malice. Let your manager draw the conclusion about intent; it lands harder coming from them than from you.

Two honest caveats. I can't tell you from here whether this is theft or chaos — you're closer to it than I am, and your read may well be right. But the plan above works either way, which is partly why I'd run it before staging a confrontation. And if there's real credit that's already been reassigned — a shipped project, a review cycle — say so, because that's a more serious and more time-sensitive problem than "left off some threads," and it may warrant going to your manager sooner rather than working it peer-to-peer first.
