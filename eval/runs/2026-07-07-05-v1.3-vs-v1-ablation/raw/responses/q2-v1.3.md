Before the how, the premise needs a look, because it determines whether confrontation is even the right move.

You've compressed two separate things into one: an observation (he repeatedly doesn't loop you in) and an inferred motive (he's doing it to steal credit). The observation may well be solid. The motive is an attribution, and right now it's underdetermined — confidence Low that you can currently distinguish malice from the ordinary alternatives: he doesn't model you as a needed recipient, he adds people reactively rather than proactively, he's sloppy with CC lines generally, or the org's norm is "minimize the thread." "Keeps forgetting" in scare quotes tells me you've already resolved this ambiguity toward intent. That's the fundamental attribution error operating exactly where it usually does — hostile reading of another's behavior, charitable reading of the equivalent in yourself.

This matters because the failure modes are asymmetric. If you confront him about credit-theft and you're wrong, you've done damage that's hard to reverse: you look paranoid, the relationship sours, and it can reach your manager as "X is difficult" faster than any credit dispute would. If you're right, a direct accusation is *still* a weak play — it's deniable ("I just forgot, sorry!"), it puts him on guard, and it converts a quiet problem into an open conflict you may not win. So confrontation-as-accusation is close to dominated: it's the worse option whether or not your theory is true.

Reframe: you actually have two problems that need different fixes.

1. **You're not getting information you need to do your job.** This is legitimate regardless of his motive, and it's the one you can raise cleanly.
2. **Credit for your work isn't reaching whoever allocates rewards.** This is the thing you actually care about, and — key point — it is *not* solved by being on more email threads. Credit is allocated by managers who mostly don't read those threads.

Concrete tactics, roughly in order:

- **Fix the information problem directly and neutrally.** To him, once, low-key: "Can you add me to the [X] thread going forward? I need visibility on that to keep my end moving." No motive attributed. If the exclusions genuinely stop, you may have had a workflow problem, not an adversary — and you've lost nothing. If they continue after an explicit, specific request, *that's* your evidence of intent, and it's far stronger than your current inference.

- **Build the credit trail independently of him** — this is the load-bearing move. Make your contributions visible through channels he doesn't control: a shared doc or project tracker where your work is timestamped and attributed, a brief weekly status note to your manager, being the person who presents your own piece in meetings. This solves problem 2 whether he's malicious or oblivious, and it doesn't require winning an argument.

- **Log before you conclude.** Keep a private record of specific instances — dates, threads, what you'd contributed. Three or four concrete cases turn "I feel sidelined" into something a manager can act on, and they also protect *you* from over-fitting to a pattern that isn't there.

- **Escalate structurally, not accusatorily.** If it persists and matters, the move is to your manager framed as workflow — "I'm getting looped in late on things I own, and it's causing X" — not "he's stealing my credit." Let the pattern speak. Managers discount emotional attribution and act on operational impact.

One thing I'm *not* addressing that you should weigh yourself: I have no read on the power dynamic, his relationship with your shared manager, or how credit actually flows on your team. If he's substantially more senior or better-networked, even the neutral version raises your risk, and the "build your own trail" tactics carry more weight relative to anything involving him directly.

The honest core: direct confrontation about the motive is the instinctive move and close to the worst one. Make being looped in the structural default, make your work visible through channels he doesn't own, and let a documented pattern — not an accusation — carry any escalation.
