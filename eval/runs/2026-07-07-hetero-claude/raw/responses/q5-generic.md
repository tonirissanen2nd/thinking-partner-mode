The honest answer is: it depends, and anyone who gives you a confident universal answer ("retention always wins!") is selling a heuristic, not analyzing your situation. But I can give you a real framework and a default lean.

## The default lean: usually retention, but for a specific reason

The common advice "retention beats acquisition" is *often* right, but the reasoning people give is usually sloppy. Here's the actual logic:

**The real question is marginal return on the marginal dollar, not average cost.** The famous stat that "acquiring a donor costs 5x more than keeping one" is an *average*, and averages are misleading at the margin. What you care about is: if I spend the next $1 on acquisition vs. retention, which produces more expected lifetime value?

Retention often wins because:
- Retained donors have already self-selected as people who give to you. The base rate of a repeat gift is far higher than a cold prospect's first gift.
- Small nonprofits typically have *terrible* retention (sector-wide first-year retention is roughly 20-25%, meaning ~75-80% of new donors never give again). When retention is that low, there's usually low-hanging fruit — a thank-you call, a decent welcome series — with high marginal return.
- Retention spending is often cheaper and less skill-dependent than running effective acquisition campaigns, which small orgs frequently do poorly.

## When acquisition is actually the right marginal dollar

Retention is not automatically the answer. Spend on acquisition when:

1. **Your donor base is shrinking or too small to sustain you.** You cannot retain your way out of an inadequate base. If attrition is mathematically outrunning any plausible retention improvement, you need new blood. Retention is a *rate*; acquisition sets the *level*.
2. **Your retention is already good** (say, 60%+ for repeat donors). Diminishing returns kick in — squeezing retention from 65% to 68% may cost more than it returns, while you have untapped acquisition channels.
3. **You have a genuinely underexploited, efficient channel** — a board member offering warm introductions, a matching-gift opportunity, an event with a natural audience. A cheap acquisition channel can beat expensive retention efforts.
4. **You're new or pivoting** and simply don't have enough donors for retention to be the binding constraint.

## What this framing gets wrong (the critical part)

Treating this as a binary is itself a mistake. Some pushback on the premise:

- **They're not independent.** Bad retention makes acquisition look expensive because you're constantly refilling a leaky bucket. Fixing retention *improves your acquisition economics*. So the "either/or" can be a false choice.
- **"Donor" is too coarse a unit.** A marginal dollar spent retaining your *major* donors is completely different from retaining $20 lapsing donors. Segment first. The highest-return dollar in most small nonprofits is often retaining/upgrading the top ~10% of donors who provide most of the revenue — that's technically "retention" but it behaves nothing like the mass-market version.
- **Marginal dollar ≠ marginal effort.** The best return might not be either — it might be improving your *donation processing*, your *data hygiene*, or your *ask amounts*. Don't let the framing of the question trap you into two options when a third is better.

## What I'd actually do

1. **Get your numbers.** Calculate your first-year and multi-year retention rate and your cost-per-acquired-donor. Most small nonprofits don't know these. You cannot answer this question without them, and I'd be skeptical of any decision made without them.
2. **Apply the diagnostic:** Is retention below ~40%? Almost certainly fix retention first — the marginal return is high and cheap. Is retention decent but the base is shrinking? Lean acquisition. Is the base adequate and retention decent? Look at *upgrading* existing donors, which often beats both.
3. **Protect the base before growing it.** As a tiebreaker, retention losses compound silently while acquisition failures are visible, so retention tends to be under-invested. But this is a lean, not a law.

**Bottom line:** For a typical small nonprofit with weak retention, the marginal dollar most likely goes to retention — but the answer genuinely flips if your base is too small or your retention is already strong. Pull your retention rate and CPA before deciding; the "right" answer is embarrassingly sensitive to numbers you probably already have but haven't calculated.
