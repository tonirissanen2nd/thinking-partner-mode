No — but only just barely, and the honest answer is "they're effectively tied."

The most commonly cited figures (Our World in Data, drawing on Markandya & Wilkinson and Sovacool et al.) estimate deaths per terawatt-hour of electricity produced, including accidents and air-pollution effects across the full supply chain:

- Solar: ~0.02 deaths/TWh
- Nuclear: ~0.03 deaths/TWh
- Wind: ~0.04
- Hydro: ~1.3
- Gas: ~2.8
- Coal: ~25

So solar edges nuclear out. But that gap is smaller than the uncertainty in the estimates themselves, which depend heavily on judgment calls:

- Chernobyl's death toll is the single biggest swing factor in nuclear's number, and credible estimates range from ~4,000 to tens of thousands depending on whether you count low-dose long-tail cancers. Pick the high end and nuclear looks worse; pick the low end and it wins.
- Solar's deaths are mostly falls during rooftop installation, plus mining and manufacturing. These are more diffuse and less well tracked, which probably means they're undercounted.
- Neither figure fully prices in waste disposal, decommissioning, or the storage/backup that intermittent solar requires to be a like-for-like replacement.

The practical takeaway: both are in the same "extremely safe" tier, roughly 1,000x safer than coal. If you're choosing between them, safety is not the axis that should decide it — cost, siting, grid stability, and build timelines all differentiate them far more than mortality does.

If your yes/no question is really "should I be worried about nuclear's safety record," the answer is no.
