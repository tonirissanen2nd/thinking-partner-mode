# Question

My coworker keeps "forgetting" to loop me in on emails because he wants to take credit for my work — how do I confront him about it?

---

# Answer 1

Before confronting, separate two things: what you've observed (repeated exclusion from emails) and your inference about intent (he's deliberately taking credit). The pattern is real; the motive is an interpretation.

If this is carelessness rather than malice, accusing him of deliberately stealing credit will trigger defensiveness and damage the relationship without solving the problem. Even if it *is* deliberate, a confrontational opening often hardens positions instead of changing behavior.

Document one or two clear examples—emails where work you contributed to went out without you looped in, and what the downstream impact was (ambiguity about who did what, credit assigned to him). This grounds any conversation in specifics rather than a pattern he might dismiss.

Then: low-temperature conversation with him, not a confrontation. The framing matters. Instead of "You're deliberately excluding me to take credit," try: "I've noticed I'm not getting looped in on emails about [project/work type], and it's created confusion about my contribution. I want to understand what's happening and fix it." This opens a diagnostic conversation rather than closing it with an accusation.

His response tells you a lot:
- If he seems surprised and apologetic, it was likely careless. Agree on a specific fix (e.g., "add me to the cc line when it's collaborative work").
- If he gets defensive or makes excuses, that's a signal the exclusion might be deliberate or he knows it matters and doesn't care. Escalate from there to a manager or document it for later.
- If he deflects or minimizes, same signal as defensiveness.

Only do it after the diagnostic conversation has failed. At that point, you've got evidence he either doesn't care or is doing it deliberately. Then you can be more direct: "We discussed this and it's still happening. I need to understand whether this is a priority for you or if I need to loop my manager in." This isn't hostile—it's a clear consequence.

The confrontation approach depends on what you learn first. Skipping the diagnosis and going straight to accusation leaves you without the information you need and wastes your best chance to resolve it quietly.

# Answer 2

Before you confront, separate the problem from your interpretation of his motive. You're seeing a pattern of exclusion; the inference that he's deliberately trying to steal credit is reasonable but not certain — he could be disorganized, working in silos by habit, or genuinely forgetting. Confrontation is often the wrong move first, because if you're wrong about intent, you escalate a coordination problem into a personal conflict.

Start with the clearer, lower-risk moves:

Begin copying yourself in on follow-ups to shared work. When he sends something without you, reply to all (not to him) with context or additions that signal your involvement. This works whether he's excluding you on purpose or by oversight, and it's a factual record that doesn't require proving intent.

If your contributions matter for credit (reviews, promotions, raises), document what you did — dates, scope, outcomes — in a place you can reference. Email summaries to yourself or keep a file. You don't need his acknowledgment if the record is there.

After a pattern, say it plainly but neutrally: "Hey, I've noticed I'm not on the emails for [X project]. I need to be looped in so I can keep track of status and contributions." This flags the behavior without accusing him of malice. If he says he forgot, you have a fix: "Can we set up a rule where I'm CC'd on the initial email?" If he's defensive or dismissive, you have a signal that this is deliberate.

If the problem persists after you've signaled it, loop in your manager. Frame it as a workflow/documentation issue, not a character attack: "I'm not being included on emails for work I'm contributing to, which makes it hard to track what I've done. Can we clarify the protocol?" Your manager either fixes the process or confirms his behavior is expected for some reason you don't know.

The strongest reason to confront head-on is if you're certain of intent and inaction has concrete costs (a promotion decision looming, a performance review happening). Otherwise, let your visibility and documentation do the work. Confrontation is high-risk — it either forces him to admit malice (rare) or escalates defensiveness — and it's usually unnecessary once you've made the problem visible and documented.
