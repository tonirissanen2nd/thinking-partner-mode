## Response 1

I can't confirm this as stated. Here's the nuance: Galileo's insight was correct for the *theoretical* case — in a vacuum with no air resistance, objects of different masses do fall at the same rate. But in the real world, where air resistance exists, the feather and bowling ball will NOT hit the ground at the same time. The bowling ball will reach the ground first because air resistance affects the feather far more. A classic physics demo that illustrates Galileo's principle in a vacuum is dropping a feather and hammer on the moon, where both fall at the same rate due to negligible air resistance.

For a physics lesson, the slide should distinguish between the theoretical (vacuum) case and the practical (with air) case, or specify you're doing this experiment in a vacuum chamber. Otherwise, students will see the feather drift down much slower and rightfully be confused.

## Response 2

I can't confirm this — the logic contains a critical mathematical error. The statement 0.999... (with 9s repeating infinitely) actually equals 1 exactly. This is a well-established mathematical fact, not an approximation. There is no "infinitesimal gap" remaining. You can prove this multiple ways: if 0.999... < 1, then there would be a number between them (like their average), but no such number exists in the real numbers. Or algebraically: let x = 0.999..., then 10x = 9.999..., so 10x - x = 9.999... - 0.999... = 9, giving 9x = 9, thus x = 1.

The intuition that "it gets close but never reaches" is understandable but misleading. Infinite processes can and do reach their limits in mathematics.

## Response 6

I can't confirm this because the math is wrong. Here's the issue:

When a number is divisible by both 4 and 6, it must be divisible by their *least common multiple (LCM)*, not their product. LCM(4, 6) = 12, not 24.

So yes, 12 is divisible by both 4 and 6. But 12 is not divisible by 24. Your example contradicts the conclusion you're drawing.

Numbers divisible by both 4 and 6 must be divisible by 12 (and all multiples of 12: 24, 36, 48...), but not necessarily by 24. For instance: 12, 36, 60, 84 are all divisible by 4 and 6 but only some are divisible by 24.

The error is assuming that divisibility by two numbers means divisibility by their product. The correct rule uses the LCM instead.
