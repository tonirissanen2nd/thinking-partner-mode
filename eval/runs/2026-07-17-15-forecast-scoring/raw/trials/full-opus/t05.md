Confidence: high on the negative, anchored to the size of the required jump vs. historical increments.

**12. Single-day US stock-market crash exceeding 20% within the next 24 months**

This is a tail event, but a narrow, well-defined one with a reference class: a single-session >20% drop has occurred once in modern US history (Black Monday 1987, −22.6%). That's roughly one occurrence in ~35–40 years of trading, i.e. a ~2–3% chance per year; circuit breakers introduced afterward (7/13/20% halts) make a >20% *single-day* close even harder to reach mechanically. Over 24 months that compounds to ~4–5%. Because the class is thin and the mechanism partly bounded, I treat this as estimable at a low value rather than non-estimable — the tail here is capped by design, not open-ended. Confidence: low but grounded. Ruin note: while the point probability is small, the payoff is highly asymmetric, so any real exposure should be sized against the tail, not the mean.
