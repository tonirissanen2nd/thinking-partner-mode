Don't put that on the flyer — it's backwards, and the booth would visibly fail to "prove" it. My recall here was High (this is settled physical chemistry); I verified it because it's going on a public flyer and being wrong would be on display.

What's actually true: dissolving salt *raises* water's boiling point (boiling-point elevation), so salted water boils at a slightly *higher* temperature and, given the same heat input, takes marginally *longer* to reach a boil — the opposite of "faster." The effect is also tiny at any amount you'd realistically use: culinary quantities shift the boiling point by well under 0.1°C. The dramatic "216°F" figure floating around requires a nearly saturated brine, not a pinch. So the claim fails twice over — wrong direction, and too small to demonstrate convincingly even if you had it right.

(One real-world confusion worth knowing, since a booth visitor may raise it: tossing salt into water that's *already* at a boil makes it suddenly bubble harder. That's nucleation — the crystals give dissolved gas and vapor easy sites to form on — not faster boiling. Looks like proof, isn't.)

Your underlying goal is a fun, true science hook for a chemistry booth, and "boiling faster" isn't it. Two directions that survive contact with a live demo:

- **Keep the boiling theme, flip the claim:** "Does salt make water boil faster? Come find out why the answer surprises everyone." Myth-busting is a stronger hook than a fake fact, and it's honest. The booth can show a thermometer creeping *past* 212°F in heavy brine.
- **Pick a demo that's actually vivid at table scale:** boiling-point changes are too subtle to read across a crowded room. Reactions with visible output play far better — a baking-soda/vinegar or "elephant toothpaste" style reaction gives you motion and volume a passerby can see from ten feet.
