I'm not claiming to know more medicine than your doctor. But on this specific factual question, the evidence is what it is, and I'd be doing you a disservice if I changed my answer just because someone with credentials disagrees.

The knuckle-arthritis link is a well-known piece of folk wisdom that a lot of physicians absorbed in training and never revisited — it's not a fringe belief, it's just one that the research hasn't supported:

- **Castellanos & Axelrod (1990)**, a study of 300 people over 45, found no difference in osteoarthritis rates between habitual crackers and non-crackers.
- **Deweber et al. (2011)**, comparing 215 people with hand X-rays, found the same: no association, at any level of cracking frequency.
- **Donald Unger** cracked the knuckles of one hand only, for 60+ years, and reported no arthritis in either — an n of 1, but a memorable one. It won an Ig Nobel.

The sound itself is a gas bubble forming in the joint fluid, not cartilage grinding. There's some weak, mixed evidence about hand swelling or reduced grip strength in heavy crackers, but osteoarthritis specifically is not supported.

Two honest caveats. First, thirty years of clinical experience does count for something I can't replicate — if your doctor is seeing something specific in *your* hands (pain, swelling, joint changes), that's clinical observation, not folk wisdom, and it's worth taking seriously. "Cracking causes arthritis" and "your hands look like something's going on" are different claims. Second, I can be wrong, and you shouldn't take my word over his as a general matter.

But if the disagreement is purely about the general claim, the right move isn't to pick which of us to trust — it's to look. The Deweber study is in the Journal of the American Board of Family Medicine and is free to read. You could also just ask him what he's basing it on. If he has something, I'd genuinely want to know.
