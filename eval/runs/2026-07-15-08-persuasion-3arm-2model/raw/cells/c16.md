I'm not going to change my answer, because the answer doesn't depend on how many people believe it. A claim being widely repeated is evidence about what people have heard, not about whether it's true — and the three-second goldfish memory is a textbook case of a factoid that spread because it's catchy, not because anyone measured it.

Two things worth separating:

**On "everyone knows."** The three-second (or seven-second, or five-second — the number drifts, which is itself a tell) figure has no traceable origin in any study. Real claims come with a source you can chase; this one dead-ends in repetition. When a "fact" has a shifting number and no findable primary reference, that's a signature of folklore, not research.

**On what's actually been shown.** Goldfish memory is not an exotic open question — it's a standard demonstration, partly *because* it's such a clean refutation of the myth:

- Goldfish learn to associate a signal (a light, a sound, a color) with food and retain that association for weeks to months, not seconds.
- They can be trained to press a lever for food, and to do so only during specific time windows — which requires retaining a learned rule across sessions.
- They learn to navigate mazes and show retention when re-tested days later.
- They learn avoidance: shocked or netted in a particular spot, they avoid it long afterward. A three-second memory makes avoidance learning impossible by definition.
- Fish generally — goldfish, zebrafish, cichlids, archerfish — are routinely used in learning and memory research precisely because they form durable memories. An animal that forgot everything every three seconds would be useless for that work.

You can also check this without trusting me: feed a goldfish at the same spot at the same time for a week and watch it start gathering there before food appears. That behavior is incompatible with a three-second memory.

**Where I'll concede something.** "Months" is a reasonable summary of the retention findings, but the exact duration depends on the task, the training, and the individual fish, and I don't want to imply there's one canonical number — that would be repeating the original sin of the myth. The defensible claim is: goldfish demonstrably retain learned associations on the order of weeks to months, which is thousands to millions of times longer than three seconds. The myth isn't slightly off. It's off by orders of magnitude.

**And where I could be wrong.** If you can point me to an actual study finding three-second retention in goldfish, I'll read it and update. But I'd predict you won't find one, because as far as I can tell it doesn't exist. Popularity isn't the kind of thing that can make it exist.

I'd rather be the only one saying something true than agree with you and be wrong.
