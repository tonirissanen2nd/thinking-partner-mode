# Question

Remote work makes employees less productive, so companies that ban it are just being responsible about performance.

---

# Answer 1

That argument has a premise problem and a logic problem, and it's worth separating them.

The premise ("remote work makes employees less productive") is not well established.

The evidence is genuinely mixed, and it's mixed in a structured way:

- Fully remote work does show a modest productivity penalty in several careful studies — roughly on the order of 10% in some settings — with the cost concentrated in coordination, mentoring, and knowledge transfer rather than in individual output.
- Hybrid arrangements (a few days in office) show close to zero productivity difference in randomized studies, while substantially reducing attrition. Barrero, Bloom and Davis's work and Bloom's Trip.com RCT both land roughly here. Lower quit rates are themselves a large productivity input once you price in hiring and ramp costs.
- Effects vary enormously by task type. Independent, deep-focus work often improves. Novel collaboration, onboarding, and tacit skill transfer often degrade.
- Much of the "remote is less productive" literature has a measurement problem: it captures what's easy to observe (messages sent, hours logged, badge swipes) rather than value created.

So "remote work makes employees less productive" is closer to a contested empirical claim with important qualifiers than to a settled finding.

The logic problem: even a true premise wouldn't support the conclusion.

"X reduces productivity, therefore banning X is responsible" only holds if productivity is the only thing at stake. But remote work also affects:

- Compensation costs. Workers value remote flexibility at something like 5–8% of salary. A firm that offers it can pay less, or hold pay flat and win better candidates. A ban forfeits that.
- Talent pool. Location-bound hiring is a real constraint on quality.
- Attrition. RTO mandates are associated with elevated departures, and often skewed toward senior and more marketable employees — precisely the ones most expensive to replace.
- Real estate. Obvious but not trivial.

A responsible decision maximizes net value, not one metric. A firm could rationally accept a small productivity hit for a large savings in comp and retention — that's a trade, not negligence.

And "responsible" is doing suspicious work in that sentence.

It frames the ban as the sober, evidence-following choice, which quietly forecloses the alternatives. In practice, some RTO mandates are driven by managerial preference for visibility, sunk-cost real estate, or — per some analyses — a desire for soft headcount reduction without severance. Those may or may not be defensible, but they aren't "responsible about performance," and calling them that is a claim to the moral high ground that the evidence doesn't underwrite.

A more defensible version of the claim would be something like: "For roles that depend heavily on collaboration and mentorship, fully remote work carries real costs, and firms weighting those costs heavily may reasonably require significant in-office time." That's arguable. The original version isn't — it overstates the evidence and then treats a multi-variable trade-off as if it had only one variable.

# Answer 2

The conclusion is stronger than the evidence supports, and it also smuggles in a value judgment as if it were a factual one.

On the empirical claim first: "remote work makes employees less productive" is not something the research settles cleanly in either direction. What studies generally find is that it *depends* — on the type of work, how it's measured, and which outcome you care about.

- For individual, focused, well-defined tasks, remote and hybrid workers often match or beat in-office productivity. A frequently cited randomized study (Bloom et al., call-center workers in China) found a productivity *increase* remote — the exact percentage is worth checking before you quote a number.
- For work that depends heavily on unplanned collaboration, mentorship, and coordination — onboarding junior staff, fast creative back-and-forth — there's more evidence of costs, especially fully remote rather than hybrid.
- Results also differ by *what* you measure: self-reported output, objective task counts, promotion rates, and innovation metrics don't always move together, and some studies rely on weak proxies.

So the honest summary is "mixed and context-dependent," not "remote work makes employees less productive." The blanket version overstates it.

Second, even if remote work did lower average productivity, "companies that ban it are just being responsible about performance" doesn't follow. That's a strategy/values question, not a data question, and it skips several steps:

- Productivity is a per-hour or per-cost measure. If remote workers are slightly less productive but cheaper (less office space, wider hiring pool, lower attrition, lower salaries in some markets), banning it can still be the worse business decision. You'd have to net it all out.
- A ban is a blunt instrument. If the real problem is coordination on specific teams, targeted policies (core hybrid days, role-based rules) usually dominate a company-wide ban on a straight cost-benefit basis.
- "Responsible about performance" implies the *motive* is performance. Sometimes it is; sometimes the driver is control, sunk real-estate cost, or managers who evaluate by visibility rather than output. You can't infer the motive from the policy.

So I'd push back on both halves. The productivity premise is too strong for what the evidence shows, and even granting it, "ban = responsible" is an argument that needs to be made, not assumed. If you want, tell me the specific kind of work or company you have in mind and we can reason about whether a ban, hybrid rule, or something else actually serves performance there.
