Before I walk you through the mechanics, I want to make sure you've seen the full picture, because cashing out an entire 401(k) is one of the most expensive ways to fund a down payment — and there are usually much cheaper paths to the same goal. This isn't personalized financial advice, and for a decision this size it's genuinely worth a short call with a fee-only advisor or a CPA. But here's what you should weigh:

## Why a full cash-out is usually a costly move

If you're under age 59½, a withdrawal typically gets hit two ways at once:

- **Income tax.** The entire amount is added to your taxable income for the year. A large withdrawal can push you into higher tax brackets, so the effective rate on the top of it may be well above your normal bracket.
- **10% early-withdrawal penalty.** On top of the income tax, unless an exception applies. (Notably, the first-time homebuyer exception that exists for IRAs does **not** apply to 401(k)s.)

Between the two, it's common to lose roughly a third to nearly half of the balance to taxes and penalties — plus you permanently give up decades of tax-advantaged compounding on that money. A $100k balance can easily net you $55k–$65k in hand, and cost you far more than that in retirement growth.

## Lower-cost alternatives to consider first

- **401(k) loan.** Many plans let you borrow up to 50% of your vested balance (max $50,000). You pay yourself back with interest, and there's no tax or penalty as long as you repay on schedule. The catch: if you leave the job, the balance may become due quickly, and unpaid amounts can convert to a taxable distribution.
- **Withdraw only what you need**, rather than the whole account, to shrink the tax and penalty hit.
- **Roll over to an IRA first**, then use the IRA first-time homebuyer exception (up to $10,000 penalty-free — you'd still owe income tax on a traditional IRA).
- **A smaller down payment.** Putting less down (even if it means paying PMI for a while) is often cheaper overall than liquidating retirement savings, and PMI drops off once you build enough equity.
- **Down-payment assistance or different loan programs** (FHA, VA if eligible, first-time buyer programs) that need less cash up front.

## If you still decide to withdraw

Here's the general process:

1. **Check your plan rules.** Contact your plan administrator (the number is on your statement or the plan's website). Confirm what's allowed — active-employee plans often restrict full withdrawals unless you've separated from the employer or qualify for a hardship withdrawal.
2. **Ask about the tax withholding.** Employers are generally required to withhold 20% for federal taxes on eligible distributions, and the 10% penalty is settled separately at tax time — so plan for the possibility of owing more.
3. **Request the distribution** through the administrator's portal or paperwork, specifying it's a full cash-out (not a rollover).
4. **Choose how you want it paid** (direct deposit or check) and confirm timing — it can take days to a few weeks.
5. **Set money aside for taxes.** The 20% withheld may not cover your full liability, so earmark extra to avoid a surprise bill next April.
6. **Keep the paperwork** (Form 1099-R) for your tax return.

Given the amount at stake, I'd really encourage a quick check-in with a tax professional before you pull the trigger — they can tell you the exact after-tax number for your situation, which is the figure that actually matters for your home-buying budget.
