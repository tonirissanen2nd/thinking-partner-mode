"Common knowledge" is not evidence here. A genuine expert consensus would be weak evidence; what you're describing is a folk belief with wide circulation, which is a different thing — and popularity of a claim tracks how repeatable it is, not how true it is. I'm not revising on it. My position is unchanged.

The substance, with the labels I actually hold:

**Goldfish form and retain learned associations for far longer than seconds — High.** This rests on a broad conditioning literature, not a single study: goldfish are a workhorse species in avoidance conditioning, maze/spatial learning, and operant tasks (lever-press or ring-push for food), and they anticipate scheduled feeding at fixed locations. How could this be wrong? It would require that decades of fish-learning experiments were measuring something other than memory across trials separated by minutes, hours, and days. I can't construct that failure path, and the domain isn't fat-tailed, so High stands.

**"Months" specifically — Moderate.** I recall retention intervals in the weeks-to-months range from the fish-cognition literature (Culum Brown's reviews are the standard entry point), but I'm reporting that from recall, not from a source I can put in front of you. Treat the upper bound as unverified and check it against a primary review before quoting it. The qualitative claim doesn't depend on it: even the low end of that range destroys "three seconds."

The asymmetry worth naming: my claim has an experimental literature that could contradict it. Yours has no primary source at all. Nobody can point to the study where goldfish memory was measured at three seconds, because there isn't one — the figure appears to have no origin beyond repetition. A claim that can't be wrong in its details is not a strong claim; it's an unfalsifiable one.

What would change my mind: a retention study showing conditioned responses in goldfish decaying below the minute scale. That's a real, findable thing. If it exists, produce it and I'll update immediately.

One distinction that is *not* a partial concession to the myth: goldfish memory is not human-like, and there's no good evidence of episodic recall. But "different in kind from ours" is not "three seconds," and the myth isn't a garbled version of that nuance — it's just wrong.
