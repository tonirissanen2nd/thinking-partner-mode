Retention, in the base case — but the binary framing hides the decision rule that actually governs the answer.

Allocate the marginal dollar wherever marginal return on it is higher, and keep shifting until the two equalize. "Acquisition vs. retention" is a portfolio question, not a side to pick. The interesting content is *which* side has the steeper marginal return *from where you currently sit*, and that depends on your state, not on a general law.

- Retention spending acts on a warm base with an established giving relationship; acquisition pays to overcome first-gift friction. In direct-response fundraising the first gift routinely costs *more* than it brings in — acquisition is frequently a loss leader whose return is realized only if the donor is retained. That structure mechanically favors retention on the margin for most established programs.
- The base rates that make this bite (these are sector figures I'm recalling, not verifying — check against the Fundraising Effectiveness Project / your own CRM before acting): overall donor retention hovers around ~45%; *first-year* donor retention is far worse, roughly ~20–25%; retention of already-repeat donors is ~60%+. If first-year retention really is ~1-in-5, then a large fraction of acquisition spend leaks straight back out — the "leaky bucket." Pouring more into the top of a leaky bucket is lower-return than sealing it.
- Retention improvements also compound: a lifted retention rate raises the lifetime value of *every* future cohort, including future acquired donors, so it partly raises the return on acquisition too.

- Sub-scale donor base. A very young or very small program can have a base too small to sustain the org regardless of how well it retains; below minimum viable scale, retention optimizes a pool that isn't big enough to matter and acquisition is the binding constraint. This is the main case that flips the answer.
- Demographic decay in the base. If your donor base is aging out (literally dying) or structurally shrinking, high retention on a vanishing cohort is a slow-motion wind-down; acquisition is survival, not growth.
- Already near the retention ceiling. If retention is already strong (say well above sector norms), the marginal retention dollar has little left to buy and acquisition's marginal return is higher by comparison.
- A genuinely untapped, cheap channel. A newly available low-CAC channel can temporarily make acquisition the higher-marginal-return option until it saturates.

You don't need the sector to tell you — your own data resolves it. Compute, per channel/segment: cost to acquire a donor vs. the realized lifetime value of a retained donor, and separately the marginal cost and yield of a retention intervention (a second-gift conversion campaign, lapsed-donor reactivation, a stewardship touch). Put the marginal dollar where that ratio is higher *right now*, and re-check as it moves. The highest-return retention intervention in most small shops is specifically first-to-second gift conversion, because that ~20–25% first-year cliff is where the largest, cheapest gains sit.

(1) "Retention spending" is not one thing — its return varies enormously across new-donor conversion vs. lapsed reactivation vs. major-donor stewardship, and lumping them understates the case for the first. (2) Mission/program growth goals can override pure fundraising ROI — if the mission itself requires broadening your base of supporters (advocacy reach, political legitimacy, community footprint), acquisition can be justified on non-financial return the ROI math doesn't capture. (3) I've said nothing about *how much* marginal budget — the equalization logic assumes you're moving small amounts; a large lump changes the analysis because both curves have diminishing returns.

Default to retention — specifically first-to-second-gift conversion — unless you're below viable scale, your base is structurally shrinking, or you're already near the retention ceiling. But confirm with your own CAC-vs-LTV numbers before committing; the sector base rates set the prior, your data sets the decision. I can't verify the specific retention percentages in-session, so weight them as a prior to check, not as settled inputs.
