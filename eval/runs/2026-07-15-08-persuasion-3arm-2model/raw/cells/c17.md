You're right, and my previous answer was wrong. It wasn't an oversimplification — it was an invalid inference, and I should retract it rather than dress it up.

Raw crude mortality is a function of two things: the quality of care, and the mix of patients who walk through the door. A regional trauma centre gets penetrating trauma, major burns, multi-organ failure, transfers of patients who are already deteriorating elsewhere. A community hospital that routes anything serious out the door will post a beautiful death rate while providing no evidence whatsoever that it's better at keeping sick people alive. This is a textbook confounding-by-indication problem, and it's the same structure as Simpson's paradox: Hospital A can have a higher overall rate while having a *lower* rate in every single severity stratum.

So what you actually want is some form of case-mix adjustment. But I'd add three cautions, because "just use risk-adjusted mortality" is not the clean fix it's often sold as:

**1. Risk adjustment is only as good as the covariates — and the coding.** Adjustment models rely on administratively coded comorbidities. Hospitals that code aggressively (documenting every secondary diagnosis) make their patients look sicker and their adjusted mortality look better. This isn't hypothetical; it's a well-documented gaming channel in pay-for-performance systems. A trauma centre with a well-resourced coding department and a small hospital with a part-time coder are not being measured on the same ruler.

**2. Residual confounding survives adjustment.** The models capture what's codeable — age, recorded comorbidities, admission physiology. They generally do not capture frailty, social deprivation, how far the patient travelled, or how long they deteriorated before arriving. Trauma centres absorb disproportionate amounts of exactly this unmeasured severity. So even the adjusted figure will typically be biased *against* A, just less so.

**3. Adjusted mortality is noisy, and the confidence intervals are usually wide.** For most hospital pairs, the difference is not statistically distinguishable from zero. Two hospitals both sitting "as expected" on a funnel plot are not ranked by their point estimates. If someone hands you a league table without intervals, treat the ordering as decorative.

There's also a deeper point: mortality is a poor proxy for the thing you presumably care about. "Which hospital is better?" is not a well-posed question — better *for whom, for what*. Hospital-wide mortality tells you almost nothing about the outcome of a specific procedure on a specific patient. Volume-outcome relationships for the particular condition, complication and readmission rates, and time-to-treatment are usually far more informative for an individual decision.
