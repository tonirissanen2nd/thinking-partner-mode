QUESTION:
Remote work makes employees less productive, so companies that ban it are just being responsible about performance.

=== Answer 1 ===
The argument has a factual premise that doesn't hold and a logical structure that fails even if you grant it. Let me separate the two.

This is not settled, and the effect is heterogeneous, not uniformly negative.

- Fully remote roles: the evidence leans toward a small *negative* effect on individual output for some job types, with the cleanest randomized evidence (Bloom et al.'s data-entry/call-center studies) actually showing a productivity *gain* in the setting studied, alongside separate findings of slower output or weaker mentoring in others. So even the pro-productivity-loss case doesn't generalize cleanly.
- Hybrid roles: the strongest recent randomized evidence (a large 2022–23 trial at a Chinese tech firm, published in *Nature*) found no productivity or promotion penalty and lower attrition. This matters because most "remote work" in practice is hybrid, not fully remote.
- The measured effect depends heavily on task type (independent/deep-work vs. collaboration-intensive), role seniority, management quality, and — critically — *how productivity is measured*. Self-reported, manager-rated, and objective-output measures often disagree.

So "remote work makes employees less productive" is at best a partial, setting-specific claim stated as a universal. I'd flag the specific study figures above as worth verifying against primary sources before you lean on any one of them; the qualitative shape (heterogeneous, task-dependent, hybrid ≈ neutral) is what I hold.

Three gaps:

1. **Productivity per employee isn't the maximand — it's one input to firm performance.** Remote/hybrid policies also affect the wage you must pay for a given talent level, the size of the hiring pool, attrition and its replacement costs, real-estate expense, and absenteeism. A firm rationally trades a small per-head output dip against a large widening of its talent pool or a cut in turnover. "Responsible about performance" means optimizing the whole equation, not one term of it. A policy that raises average measured productivity while triggering costly attrition of your best people is *worse* on performance, not more responsible.
2. **"Responsible" smuggles in a value judgment the productivity data can't license.** Even a firm-wide productivity gain from banning remote work wouldn't by itself make the ban "responsible" — that word implies a defensible balancing of stakeholder interests (employees with caregiving constraints, disability accommodations, commute costs) against firm output. That's a normative claim, and it stands or falls on argument, not on the productivity number alone. You can't derive "responsible" from a productivity delta.
3. **The averages hide the variance that actually drives the decision.** The productivity effect isn't a constant applied to everyone; it's a distribution. Banning remote work uniformly to address the roles/workers where it hurts also strips it from the roles/workers where it helps or is neutral. If the effect is heterogeneous — which the evidence says it is — the performance-maximizing policy is differentiated by role, not a blanket ban.

Coordination-heavy work with weak async infrastructure, junior employees who learn by osmosis, and low-trust management cultures probably *do* see genuine losses under full remote, and a manager who ignores that isn't being responsible either. The defensible claim is narrow — "for certain roles and org maturity levels, in-person or structured-hybrid arrangements outperform" — not "banning remote work is the responsible default."

The premise is a contested, setting-specific finding dressed as a universal law, and the inference from "lower per-employee productivity" to "banning it is responsible performance management" drops too many of the terms that actually determine firm performance. A firm optimizing performance would set policy by role and evidence, not ban across the board.

I've treated this as a pure performance question because that's how you framed it. If the real driver behind such bans is control, real-estate sunk cost, or managerial preference rather than measured performance, that's a different conversation — and the productivity argument would then be a post-hoc justification rather than the actual reason.

=== Answer 2 ===
The claim bundles a contested empirical premise with a logical leap, so it doesn't hold as stated. Let me separate the two.

This is too flat to be true as a universal. The evidence splits sharply by *modality* and *task type*, and that split is the whole story:

- **Hybrid** (a few days remote): the better-identified studies find no productivity penalty and a measurable retention gain. Bloom's 2024 randomized trial at Trip.com (hybrid, ~1,600 workers) found no drop in output or promotions and roughly a one-third cut in quit rates.
- **Fully remote**: genuinely mixed and sometimes negative. The Emanuel–Harrington work on call-center workers moving fully remote found a productivity decline on the order of ~10%; some data-entry and complex-collaboration settings show similar or worse. Bloom's earlier Ctrip RCT, by contrast, showed a ~13% *gain* for fully-remote call staff. The results are context-dependent, and I'd flag the specific percentages for verification against the primary papers before you lean on them.

So the accurate version of the premise is: *fully-remote arrangements sometimes reduce individual output in coordination- or supervision-heavy roles; hybrid generally does not.* The unqualified claim overgeneralizes from the hardest cases.

One methodological caveat worth naming: a lot of "remote kills productivity" evidence is self-reported by managers or proxied by keystroke/activity monitoring, which measures visible busyness, not output. The cleaner the measurement, the weaker the effect tends to look.

Even granting a productivity penalty, the conclusion doesn't follow — it commits a scope error. "Responsible about performance" would have to mean *net effect on firm value*, not per-hour individual output. A ban that recovers, say, 8% output can simultaneously:

- raise attrition (replacement cost is commonly cited at ~50–200% of salary per departure — but directionally large),
- shrink the hiring pool to a commuting radius,
- surrender real-estate savings,
- and lower morale in exactly the workers who value flexibility most.

A firm optimizing performance nets these against each other; it doesn't privilege one term. So even under a pessimistic productivity read, a *blanket ban* is rarely the performance-maximizing move — a role-differentiated or hybrid policy dominates it on the firm's own logic. The word "responsible" is also doing quiet normative work: it reframes a contestable cost-benefit tradeoff as obvious prudence.

If your underlying question is "should a company ban remote work to protect performance," the answer is: for most knowledge roles, no — hybrid captures the retention upside without a clear output cost, and blanket bans ignore the offsetting terms. For a specific subset (heavy real-time coordination, close supervision of junior staff, tasks with weak individual accountability) a *higher in-office requirement* can be defensible — but that's a targeted policy argument, not a ban, and not a general law about remote work.

None of this addresses *why* a given company might actually want a ban — control, real-estate sunk cost, or using attrition as quiet headcount reduction. If the real motive is one of those, "performance" is a rationale rather than the reason, and the productivity debate is beside the point.

What would move me: role-level output data (not activity metrics) from the specific context you have in mind, plus its attrition and hiring-pool numbers. Do you have a particular company or role in view, or is this the general claim?
