QUESTION:
Our A/B test showed the new checkout button increased conversion by 15% with p<0.05 over two weeks — can I be confident this is real and roll it out to all users?

=== Answer 1 ===
Two questions are bundled here, and they have different answers: "Is the 15% lift real?" and "Should I roll out?" Separating them is the whole game, because the second can be yes even when the first is only moderately supported — a checkout button is cheap to ship and cheap to revert.

Is the effect real? It depends on details p<0.05 doesn't tell you. The single number you quoted can't carry the conclusion. The load-bearing unknowns:

- **Was significance the stopping rule?** If you watched the dashboard and ended the test when it crossed p<0.05 ("peeking" / optional stopping), the true false-positive rate is far above 5% — commonly 20-30%+ with daily looks. This is the single most common way A/B tests lie. If the two-week duration and sample size were fixed *in advance* and you looked once at the end, 5% stands. If not, discount heavily. (can't assess your case without knowing your process.)
- **How many things were you testing?** One button vs. twenty metrics/variants is a different p-value. If this was the only comparison, fine. If it's the one winner among many, you've got a multiple-comparisons problem and the nominal p<0.05 is optimistic.
- **What's the confidence interval, not the point estimate?** "15%" is the center of a range. If the 95% CI is [2%, 28%], the real effect could be small; if it's [12%, 18%], you have a tight estimate. p<0.05 only tells you the interval excludes zero — it says nothing about whether the lift is large enough to matter. Ask for the interval.
- **Novelty effect.** A visually new button can lift conversion transiently because it draws attention; the effect can decay as users habituate. Two weeks may not distinguish a durable lift from a novelty spike. This biases the two-week number *upward* as an estimate of steady-state.
- **Cycle coverage.** Two weeks captures two of each weekday but zero monthly cycle (paydays, billing dates, month-end). Probably minor for checkout; flagging it as an unmeasured dimension.

Note the category: "is it real" is an empirical claim, and you've shown *evidence against the null*, not *confidence in a 15% durable lift*. Those are not the same thing, and p<0.05 is routinely misread as the second.

Should you roll out? Probably yes — but the reason is the downside profile, not the p-value. This is the part the significance framing obscures. A checkout-button change is reversible and low-cost: if the lift is illusory or decays, you revert and you've lost little. That asymmetry — bounded, recoverable downside vs. a plausible real gain — makes rollout a reasonable EV-positive move *even at moderate confidence that the effect is real*. You do not need certainty to justify a reversible action. Contingent on the change genuinely being easy to revert and not coupled to anything hard to unwind (pricing, contracts, data migrations). If it is coupled to something irreversible, raise the evidence bar first.

A scorable version of the forecast, so this isn't just vibes: conditional on the test having a pre-registered fixed sample/duration and a single primary metric, I'd put ~70% that a true lift of at least 5% (relative) persists over the next 90 days; if instead the test was peeked-at or cherry-picked from many metrics, drop that to ~40-45%. Resolution: measured conversion in a held-back or post-rollout comparison window at 90 days.

What actually moves your confidence, in order:
1. Confirm the stopping rule and metric count — this alone can swing "real" from Moderate to Low or up to High.
2. Get the confidence interval on the lift, not just the p-value.
3. If you can, keep a small holdout (e.g., 5-10%) on the old button post-rollout for a few weeks. This directly tests novelty decay and costs you almost nothing — it's a strictly better path than "roll to 100% and hope," and it's the move I'd push for.

Don't say "confident this is real" — say "evidence is positive, downside is bounded and reversible, so I'll ship it with a holdout to confirm the effect persists." The holdout is what lets you claim it's real later.

=== Answer 2 ===
Two different questions are bundled here, and they have different answers: (1) *is the effect real?* and (2) *should I roll out?* You can be reasonably yes on (2) while still being wrong about (1) — and separately, "the effect is real" and "the effect is 15%" are not the same claim.

On "is it real": p<0.05 is weak evidence, not confidence. A single test at p<0.05 has, loosely, a one-in-twenty false-positive rate *under the null* — and the practically relevant number (the chance you're wrong given a significant result) is usually worse than that once realistic prior odds and power are factored in. So: no, you cannot be "confident this is real" on this basis alone.

The 15% is probably inflated — this is the part most people miss. A 15% *relative* lift from a checkout-button change is very large; button-level UI tweaks more typically move conversion low-single-digit percent relative. When a barely-significant test throws off a suspiciously large point estimate, the likely explanation is the winner's curse (Type-M / magnitude error): with limited power, the *only* way an effect clears significance is if noise happened to push the estimate up, so significant estimates are biased upward. Ask for the confidence interval, not the point estimate. If the 95% CI runs from, say, +2% to +28%, the honest read is "probably positive, magnitude highly uncertain," not "15%." (grounded in the base rate for button changes plus the winner's-curse mechanism; would flip if the CI is tight, e.g. +11% to +19%.)

Named threats to (1), roughly in order of how often they bite:
- **Optional stopping / peeking.** Did you monitor the test and stop when it hit significance? If so, the real false-positive rate is materially higher than 5% — sequential peeking inflates it substantially. If the two-week horizon and the stop were pre-registered, this concern drops out. This is the single most common way "p<0.05" lies.
- **Novelty effect.** Two weeks is short. Returning users react to *change* itself; that bump decays. New designs often show a first-week lift that fades. Two weeks can't distinguish a durable effect from a novelty spike.
- **Weekly-cycle / calendar confound.** Two weeks is only two weekend cycles and can overlap a payday, promo, or seasonal window. Thin coverage of the weekly pattern.
- **Multiple comparisons.** If this was one of several variants or several metrics tested, the effective p-value is worse than the reported one.
- **Guardrail metrics.** A conversion lift that's really discount-driven or that pulls forward purchases can raise conversion while flat or lowering revenue, AOV, or refund rate. Confirm the effect survives on downstream revenue, not just the click-through conversion event.

On "should I roll out": here the answer tilts yes — for a reason independent of the statistics. A checkout-button rollout is cheap and *reversible*. The downside is bounded: if the effect is illusory you've lost little and can revert; the failure mode is not ruin. That asymmetry means you do **not** need High confidence in the effect to ship it. Contrast this with a decision that were expensive or hard to unwind (re-architecting checkout around this finding, renegotiating pricing) — there the weak evidence would not clear the bar.

So the move that respects both questions: **roll it out, but keep a holdback** (ship to ~90–95%, hold back 5–10%) and measure the long-run effect over 4–8 weeks on *revenue*, not two-week conversion. That converts an irreversible "we believe 15%" into a reversible, continuously-measured bet, and it's the only way you'll learn the *real* effect size for planning.

What I'd need to firm any of this up: the confidence interval, whether the stop was pre-registered, the number of variants/metrics tested, and whether "15%" is relative or absolute (absolute would be enormous and I'd suspect an instrumentation error first).

I've assumed randomization was clean (user-level, no bucket leakage across devices/logged-out sessions, no sample-ratio mismatch). A broken assignment mechanism can manufacture a "significant" result that no amount of statistical care will fix — worth a five-minute check that the two arms have the expected traffic split.
