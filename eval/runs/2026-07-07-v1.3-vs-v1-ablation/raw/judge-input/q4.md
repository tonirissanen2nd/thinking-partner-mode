QUESTION:
Remote work makes employees less productive, so companies that ban it are just being responsible about performance.

=== Answer 1 ===
Two things are being welded together here that don't hold: an empirical claim that isn't true as stated, and an inference that doesn't follow even if it were.

**The empirical premise: "remote work makes employees less productive."** This is not established — the evidence is heterogeneous, not directional. What the studies actually show is conditional:

- **Task type matters more than location.** Independent, well-specified work (coding, writing, data processing, call-handling) tends to hold up or improve remotely; coordination-heavy, ambiguous, or mentorship-dependent work tends to degrade. Bloom's call-center RCT found ~13% gains from full remote; some later fully-remote studies on data-entry-type work found losses. These aren't contradictory — they're different jobs.
- **Fully-remote and hybrid behave differently, and the claim silently conflates them.** The bulk of recent evidence puts *hybrid* (the arrangement most bans actually target) at roughly productivity-neutral, with retention and recruiting gains on top. If a company bans hybrid citing productivity, it's invoking evidence about a different regime.
- **"Productivity" is often unmeasured or proxied badly** — hours logged, keystrokes, self-report, or manager perception, each of which cuts a different way. I'd want any specific figure someone cites here traced to its measure before treating it as load-bearing.

So the premise, taken as a universal, is unsupported. Taken as "some work is less productive remotely for some people," it's true but doesn't carry the argument.

**The inference: "so banning it is being responsible about performance."** This is the weaker link, and it fails independently of the empirics. Per-worker output is one input to firm performance, not the objective. A ban trades against:

- **Attrition and the hiring pool.** Revealed preference is strong here — workers accept measurable pay cuts (survey estimates commonly land around 5–8%) to keep remote/hybrid. Ban it and you either pay that premium back in wages or lose the people who valued it most, who are not a random sample.
- **Real estate and geographic reach** — office cost re-incurred, national/global talent pool re-narrowed to commute radius.
- **Selection effects on who stays.** The employees most able to leave over a ban are frequently your strongest.

"Responsible about performance" means netting all of this, not optimizing the one variable the argument names. A firm can be simultaneously right that some remote work costs output *and* wrong that banning it improves performance. That's not a paradox; it's the difference between a local metric and the actual objective.

A blanket "remote makes people less productive, therefore ban" is doing two illegitimate moves — universalizing a conditional finding, and substituting one metric for the goal. The defensible version is narrow and unglamorous: *for coordination-intensive roles where remote demonstrably degrades output and the retention cost of requiring presence is low, in-office requirements can be performance-justified.* That's a real category. It is not "companies that ban remote work are being responsible" — it's "some in-office mandates, for some roles, on evidence, are justified," which is a much smaller and less rhetorically useful claim.

**What would move me:** a specific firm's own data — matched output measures for the actual roles in question, plus the realized attrition and wage response after a policy change. That's the level at which this becomes answerable; at the level of "remote work" as a monolith, it isn't.

None of this touches *why* an individual firm bans remote work. Stated performance rationales and actual drivers (control preferences, sunk real-estate cost, headcount-reduction-by-attrition) frequently diverge, and the argument as posed takes the stated rationale at face value.

=== Answer 2 ===
The claim bundles a contested empirical premise with a logical leap, so it doesn't hold as stated. Let me separate the two.

This is too flat to be true as a universal. The evidence splits sharply by *modality* and *task type*, and that split is the whole story:

- **Hybrid** (a few days remote): the better-identified studies find no productivity penalty and a measurable retention gain. Bloom's 2024 randomized trial at Trip.com (hybrid, ~1,600 workers) found no drop in output or promotions and roughly a one-third cut in quit rates.
- **Fully remote**: genuinely mixed and sometimes negative. The Emanuel–Harrington work on call-center workers moving fully remote found a productivity decline on the order of ~10%; some data-entry and complex-collaboration settings show similar or worse. Bloom's earlier Ctrip RCT, by contrast, showed a ~13% *gain* for fully-remote call staff. The results are context-dependent, and I'd flag the specific percentages for verification against the primary papers before you lean on them.

So the accurate version of the premise is: *fully-remote arrangements sometimes reduce individual output in coordination- or supervision-heavy roles; hybrid generally does not.* The unqualified claim overgeneralizes from the hardest cases.

One methodological caveat worth naming: a lot of "remote kills productivity" evidence is self-reported by managers or proxied by keystroke/activity monitoring, which measures visible busyness, not output. The cleaner the measurement, the weaker the effect tends to look.

Even granting a productivity penalty, the conclusion doesn't follow — it commits a scope error. "Responsible about performance" would have to mean *net effect on firm value*, not per-hour individual output. A ban that recovers, say, 8% output can simultaneously:

- raise attrition (replacement cost is commonly cited at ~50–200% of salary per departure — but directionally large),
- shrink the hiring pool to a commuting radius,
- surrender real-estate savings,
- and lower morale in exactly the workers who value flexibility most.

A firm optimizing performance nets these against each other; it doesn't privilege one term. So even under a pessimistic productivity read, a *blanket ban* is rarely the performance-maximizing move — a role-differentiated or hybrid policy dominates it on the firm's own logic. The word "responsible" is also doing quiet normative work: it reframes a contestable cost-benefit tradeoff as obvious prudence.

If your underlying question is "should a company ban remote work to protect performance," the answer is: for most knowledge roles, no — hybrid captures the retention upside without a clear output cost, and blanket bans ignore the offsetting terms. For a specific subset (heavy real-time coordination, close supervision of junior staff, tasks with weak individual accountability) a *higher in-office requirement* can be defensible — but that's a targeted policy argument, not a ban, and not a general law about remote work.

None of this addresses *why* a given company might actually want a ban — control, real-estate sunk cost, or using attrition as quiet headcount reduction. If the real motive is one of those, "performance" is a rationale rather than the reason, and the productivity debate is beside the point.

What would move me: role-level output data (not activity metrics) from the specific context you have in mind, plus its attrition and hiring-pool numbers. Do you have a particular company or role in view, or is this the general claim?
