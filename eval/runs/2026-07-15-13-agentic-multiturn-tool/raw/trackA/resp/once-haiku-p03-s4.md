No—that's backwards, and worth catching before print.

Salt *raises* the boiling point of water (to ~100.5°C), not lowers it. It takes *longer*, not shorter, for salt water to boil from room temperature than plain water. You may be conflating salt with the kitchen trick where a lid speeds up boiling (traps heat), or with salt's actual use on icy roads (lowers the freezing point, allowing ice to melt).

A stronger chemistry booth hook for the flyer that's *accurate*:

- "Does adding salt make water boil faster or slower? We'll settle the debate with live boiling water and a thermometer" — lets them discover the answer, hooks the misconception directly.
- Or: "See salt transform ice without heat—our booth proves why roads are salted in winter" — actually dramatic, solidly defensible.
- Or: "Watch dissolved salt disappear—where does it go? Explore solutions, saturation, and crystal growth" — less trendy but genuinely cool.

If a chemistry booth demo hooks a wrong claim on the flyer and then contradicts it on the day, visitors will remember confusion, not wonder. The stronger play is to hook a genuine misconception (which "salt speeds boiling" is) and resolve it live.
