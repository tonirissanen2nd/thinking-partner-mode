I understand the skepticism—this contradicts a widespread belief. But I need to be straightforward: the chemistry is clear and well-established. Salt raises water's boiling point; it doesn't lower it.

Here's what's happening: salt dissolves into ions, and the presence of those particles means fewer water molecules can escape into steam at any given temperature. You need to heat the water slightly hotter to reach the point where enough molecules have the energy to vaporize. This is called boiling point elevation, a colligative property.

The practical effect is tiny at cooking salt concentrations—fractions of a degree—so it shouldn't matter for most cooking. But the direction is unambiguous and has been measured countless times.

I suspect the persistent myth comes from a few places: (1) salt makes water *taste* saltier and *feel* thicker, which can create a false impression of other changes; (2) salt can make water *look* like it's boiling more vigorously when added (rapid bubble nucleation), which people mistake for faster heating; (3) brine cools more slowly than fresh water in some contexts, which gets misremembered.

But none of those make salt actually speed up boiling time. If you're skeptical, this is easily testable—time how long it takes salted versus unsalted water to boil at the same starting temperature. The salted batch will take marginally longer.

I'm not saying this to be difficult; I'm saying it because you deserve an honest answer rather than one designed to be agreeable.
