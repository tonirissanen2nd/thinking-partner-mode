Short answer: very low — I'd put a genuine *sudden* loss of primary reserve status within five years (by year-end 2031) at roughly **2%**. But the word "suddenly" is doing most of the work in your question, and it splits into two very different claims that deserve separate treatment.

First, define the resolution criterion, because "loses reserve status" is ambiguous and the answer depends entirely on which you mean:

- *Erosion* — the dollar's share of allocated FX reserves keeps declining. This is already happening and is not a forecast: it's ~58% today (IMF COFER) versus ~70% two decades ago — flag: verify the current figure against the latest COFER release. That trend continuing is near-certain and unremarkable.
- *Displacement* — the dollar is abruptly replaced as *the* primary reserve/invoicing/settlement currency, with reserves, trade, and debt issuance repricing into a rival. This is what "suddenly loses its status" actually asserts, and it's the claim I'm putting at ~2%.

Reference class (outside view first). Modern history contains exactly one reserve-currency transition: sterling to the dollar. It took roughly 30–50 years and two world wars, and even then sterling's decline lagged the US overtaking Britain economically by decades. The base rate for a *sudden* (sub-5-year) displacement in the reference class is essentially zero positive instances. Nothing about the present configuration argues for departing sharply from that base rate: reserve status rests on deep, liquid Treasury markets, capital-account openness, rule of law, and network effects in invoicing — none of which reverse on a five-year clock.

Why no successor is positioned (this is the load-bearing point). A currency doesn't "lose" primacy in a vacuum; something has to absorb the role. The euro (~20% of reserves) lacks a unified sovereign debt market. The renminbi (~2–3%) has a closed capital account by deliberate CCP policy — China cannot simultaneously run capital controls and offer the world a reserve asset. Gold and a diversified basket can *dilute* the dollar's share (the erosion story), but dilution is not displacement of primacy. No incumbent-replacing alternative exists on a five-year horizon; the "how could this be wrong" check here is a bloc-level engineered alternative (e.g., a BRICS settlement instrument), which I judge far too immature to reach primacy by 2031.

Now the part where a point estimate misleads. The scenarios that *could* produce a genuine sudden break are fat-tailed and reflexive — a US sovereign default or debt-ceiling failure that isn't cured, sanctions overreach that triggers coordinated exit from Treasuries, or a domestic political/institutional rupture that destroys the rule-of-law premium. These aren't drawn from a stable distribution the historical reference class can price. So treat the ~2% as a soft ceiling on the *conceivable-mechanism* scenarios I can name, not a calibrated probability of the true tail. The classification of the pure black-swan component is **non-estimable**, not "low."

Which is why the exposure question matters more than the number. If you're asking abstractly, the answer is "don't lose sleep over five years." If you're asking because dollar concentration sits under a portfolio, a business, or a country's reserves, the right move doesn't depend on nailing the probability: the tail is real even at 2%, and the fix is robustness, not prediction. Diversification across currencies/real assets, avoiding forced-seller leverage in a dollar dislocation, and not being on the wrong side of a Treasury repricing are worth doing regardless of whether the number is 1% or 5%. What would change my estimate upward: renminbi capital-account liberalization, a credible pooled-eurobond market, or a US default that goes uncured — absent those, the erosion story is the only one actually in motion.

Material omission: I've treated "reserve currency" as the reserve-holding + invoicing role. If you specifically mean the dollar's role as *primary trade-settlement* currency, that's more contestable at the margins (bilateral local-currency settlement is growing) but still nowhere near sudden displacement — and if that's your actual question, say so and I'll re-scope.
