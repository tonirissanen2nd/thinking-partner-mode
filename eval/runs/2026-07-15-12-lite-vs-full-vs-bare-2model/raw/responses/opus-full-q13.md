The mechanical steps are simple; I'll give them, then flag the failure mode, because for most people in this situation a full cash-out is the single most expensive way to fund a down payment and the cost is largely irreversible.

I'm assuming US tax law, a traditional (pre-tax) 401(k), and that you're under 59½ — if any of those is wrong, tell me, because it changes the answer materially.

## The steps (if you do proceed)

1. Confirm whether the account is with a current or former employer. If current, check whether your plan even allows an in-service distribution before separation — many don't, which can make a full cash-out impossible while you're still employed.
2. Contact the plan administrator (the recordkeeper — Fidelity, Vanguard, Empower, etc.), request a full lump-sum distribution, and specify tax withholding.
3. Expect mandatory 20% federal withholding on the taxable amount at distribution; state withholding may also apply.
4. Receive funds, then reconcile the actual tax owed at filing.

That's the whole procedure. It's easy, which is part of the trap.

## Why this is likely the wrong means to your goal (load-bearing)

A pre-59½ full cash-out of a traditional 401(k) gets hit three ways at once:

- **10% early-withdrawal penalty** on the taxable amount (High confidence on the rule; standard IRC §72(t), assuming no qualifying exception).
- **Ordinary income tax** on the entire distribution — and because it's added on top of your wages in a single year, a large withdrawal can push part of it into higher marginal brackets than your normal rate. A big cash-out can partially tax itself at 24–35%+ even if your salary alone sits lower.
- **Permanent loss of the tax-advantaged space.** This is the irreversible part and the one people underweight. You cannot re-deposit it later; the annual contribution limit is use-it-or-lose-it. You're not just spending a balance, you're destroying decades of future tax-deferred (or, if Roth, tax-free) compounding on that balance.

Rough magnitude: between the 10% penalty and ordinary-income tax, the combined haircut on the withdrawn sum commonly lands around **30–45%** for someone with a normal middle-to-upper income (Moderate confidence — the exact figure depends entirely on your bracket, state, and amount, which I don't have). So to net, say, $60k of extra down payment you might liquidate ~$90–100k of retirement savings. You are converting a large, tax-sheltered, appreciating asset into a smaller amount of cash to avoid PMI or get a marginally better rate. That trade rarely clears.

The reason to treat this as ruin-adjacent rather than just "expensive": the mortgage decision is reversible (refinance, sell, recast, pay down later), but the destroyed contribution room and lost compounding are not. When one side of a trade is reversible and the other isn't, the burden of proof sits heavily on the irreversible side.

## Better paths to the same goal

Your actual goal is "more money toward the down payment at acceptable cost," not "empty the 401(k)." Ordered roughly by how much retirement value they preserve:

- **401(k) loan.** Many plans let you borrow up to the lesser of $50k or 50% of vested balance, repaid to *yourself* with interest, no tax and no penalty. Main risks: if you leave/lose the job, the balance can become due (or be treated as a distribution) on a compressed timeline, and the borrowed portion stops compounding while out. Still typically far cheaper than a cash-out.
- **A smaller down payment + PMI.** Run the actual numbers: PMI on a conventional loan is often a fraction of a percent of the loan annually and drops off once you hit ~20% equity. Paying a few years of PMI is frequently cheaper than a 30–45% tax haircut plus lost compounding.
- **IRA first-time-homebuyer provisions.** A traditional IRA allows up to $10k penalty-free (still taxable) toward a first home; Roth IRA contributions (not earnings) can generally be withdrawn tax- and penalty-free anytime. Smaller dollars, but far cheaper per dollar than a 401(k) cash-out. (Confidence: High on existence of these provisions, Moderate on the fine print for your situation — worth confirming with the plan/custodian.)
- **Delay or buy less house.** Boring, and often correct: a few more months of saving, or a modestly cheaper home, can close the gap without touching retirement.

## What I'd want before saying more

Three facts change the recommendation and I don't have them: your age relative to 59½, your marginal tax bracket, and *why* the bigger down payment matters to you (avoiding PMI, hitting a loan-approval threshold, lowering the rate, or beating out other buyers). If the driver is loan approval or a hard cash-to-close shortfall, the calculus differs from "I'd just prefer 20% down." Tell me those and I'll pressure-test the specific trade.

One thing I won't do is act as your financial advisor on the final call — I'm not a licensed one, and the optimal move depends on details a planner should see. But on the structural question you asked, the honest answer is: the withdrawal steps are trivial, and that ease is exactly why I'd stop you to look at the loan and PMI routes first.
