Not answerable as a clean yes/no — but not because the question is empty. "Safer" hides a metric choice that flips the answer, so pin the metric and the honest reply is: **they're roughly tied, and both are among the safest sources we have.**

On the standard metric — deaths per unit of energy produced (accidents + pollution over the full lifecycle) — nuclear and solar land in the same ballpark: on the order of ~0.03 vs ~0.02 deaths per TWh respectively (Moderate confidence on the exact figures; these track the Our World in Data / Markandya-Wilkinson-Sovacool numbers and should be checked against a primary source before you quote them). Nuclear's toll is dominated by historical accidents and mining; solar's by falls during rooftop installation and manufacturing. The difference between them is small and sensitive to which studies and which time window you use — well within the noise. The robust, High-confidence claim is the comparative one: both are ~2-3 orders of magnitude safer than coal or oil, and that gap dwarfs any nuclear-vs-solar difference.

Two things the mortality average doesn't capture, and they cut in opposite directions:

- **Nuclear carries a fat tail.** A per-TWh death rate is a mean, and a mean cannot contain the tail of a rare catastrophic accident or a long-horizon waste-containment failure. This isn't a number I can honestly put a confidence level on — it's non-estimable, low-probability/high-consequence. So if "safer" means "lower expected deaths," they're tied; if it means "no plausible catastrophic downside," solar wins that specific dimension.
- **Solar's risk is distributed and reversible; nuclear's is concentrated and, in the tail, not.** That's the ruin distinction, and it's why reasonable people who agree on the mortality data still rank them differently — it's a values question about tail exposure, not a factual dispute.

So the direct answer depends on what you're optimizing:
- Expected mortality per unit energy → statistically tied.
- Worst-case / irreversibility → solar, because it has no catastrophic mode.
- "Should I worry about either relative to fossil fuels" → no; both are the safe options and the choice between them should ride on cost, grid fit, and siting, not safety.

Material omission worth naming: I've scoped this to human mortality/safety. Land use, waste, materials-supply chains, and lifecycle carbon are separate axes, and "safer" sometimes smuggles those in — if that's what you meant, say so and the comparison changes.
