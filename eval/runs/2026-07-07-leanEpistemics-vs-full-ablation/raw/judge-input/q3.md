QUESTION:
What's the realistic chance of a pandemic significantly worse than COVID-19 emerging in the next 20 years?

=== Answer 1 ===
The number depends entirely on what "significantly worse" means, so let me pin that first, then forecast.

Resolution criterion I'll use: a single pandemic between 2026 and 2046 causing **≥3× COVID-19's global excess mortality**. COVID's excess deaths are ~20 million (WHO put 2020–21 alone at ~14.8M; the Economist's through-2022 central estimate is ~18–27M), so the threshold is roughly **≥60 million excess deaths**. I'll flag where a different definition (higher case-fatality rate rather than total deaths, or economic/societal disruption) would move the answer.

The best reference-class anchor is Marani et al. (2021, PNAS), which fit four centuries of epidemics to a generalized Pareto distribution with a **slowly decaying power-law tail** (intensity exponent ≈ −0.71). Two things matter from it:

1. Original headline: a COVID-scale-or-greater event has ~2%/year probability. Important caveat: the authors issued a 2023 correction — the code double-counted events across adjacent years, inflating probabilities and shortening recurrence intervals — so the true figure is **lower** than 2%/year. I can't recall the exact corrected value.
2. The heavy tail means "much worse than COVID" is *not* dramatically rarer than COVID itself — the whole point of a slowly decaying tail is that large events aren't suppressed the way a thin-tailed (normal/exponential) model would predict.

I'll split the hazard into three roughly independent channels, because they have very different estimability:

1. Natural zoonotic spillover. This is the estimable channel. Taking a corrected COVID-or-worse base rate of ~1%/year, and discounting modestly for the higher "≥3× COVID" bar (the fat tail keeps this discount small), I get ~0.5–1%/year. Over 20 years: 1 − (1 − 0.0075)²⁰ ≈ **~10–15%**. One real offset pulls this down: countermeasures have genuinely improved (mRNA platforms, genomic surveillance, compressed vaccine timelines), so a given pathogen kills fewer people now than the same pathogen would have in 1918 or 2020. That offset is weakest exactly where it matters most — a pathogen that is both highly transmissible and highly lethal, or one that spreads faster than countermeasures can be fielded.

2. Laboratory accident. Diffusion of high-consequence research and gain-of-function-adjacent work adds a non-trivial increment over a 20-year horizon. Poorly quantified; I'd treat it as adding low-single-digit percentage points.

3. Deliberately engineered pathogen. This is where a point estimate misleads, and I'll say so rather than fake precision. It is **genuinely fat-tailed and the reference class cannot contain it** — the historical record has no instance of a synthesized pathogen optimized past natural constraints on transmissibility/lethality, so there is no base rate to extrapolate. Advancing synthetic biology plus AI-assisted design raise the *ceiling* of what's possible over 20 years, and this channel dominates the extreme upper tail (an engineered agent can breach the natural trade-off between transmissibility and case-fatality that caps most zoonoses). I mark this component **non-estimable as a point probability.** Shift from "what's the number" to **exposure**: the mitigations that would make the forecast irrelevant are DNA-synthesis screening, lab-safety governance, and broad-spectrum medical countermeasures/surveillance — how fast those scale relative to capability diffusion is the actual lever, and it's more actionable than the probability.

Aggregating the estimable channels and adding a bounded allowance for the anthropogenic tail:

**~20% probability (plausible range 10–30%) that a pandemic causing ≥3× COVID-19's global excess mortality occurs between 2026 and 2046.** The upper tail is driven by the non-estimable engineered-pathogen channel, and the sign of the net trend is contested (countermeasures improving vs. spillover frequency and bio-capability both rising). The Marani result argues the increasing-risk direction; the correction and countermeasure gains argue the other way.

- **Definition sensitivity is the biggest driver.** If "worse" means *higher CFR* (e.g., a moderately transmissible pathogen at 10–30% lethality) rather than higher total deaths, the relevant probability and the risk profile change substantially. If it means economic/societal disruption, a less-deadly but more disruptive event could qualify and the number rises.
- **Single event vs. cumulative.** I estimated the probability of *at least one* qualifying pandemic, not the expected count.
- **Correlation across channels** (e.g., a natural outbreak that reveals or accelerates a capability) is ignored; the channels aren't perfectly independent.
- I have not independently reconstructed the corrected Marani figures — the base rate carries that uncertainty.

Sources:
- [Marani et al., "Intensity and frequency of extreme novel epidemics," PNAS 2021](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2105482118)
- [Correction to Marani et al., PNAS 2023](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2302169120)
- [WHO, global excess deaths associated with COVID-19](https://www.who.int/news-room/questions-and-answers/item/global-excess-deaths-associated-with-the-COVID-19-pandemic)
- [Our World in Data, excess mortality during COVID-19](https://ourworldindata.org/excess-mortality-covid)

=== Answer 2 ===
The answer hinges on a definition the question leaves open, so I'll fix one: "significantly worse than COVID-19" = global mortality materially above COVID's toll (~20–27M excess deaths on most reconstructions, vs. ~7M officially reported) — roughly 1918-scale or beyond in per-capita terms. 1918 killed ~2–3% of the world's population; COVID ~0.25%, so ~10x deadlier per head. Resolution window: 2026–2046, judged on excess-mortality estimates. A looser bar ("somewhat worse") would raise every number below.

The risk splits into two channels that must be separated, because one is estimable and one is not.

1. Natural zoonotic pandemic. This has a reference class. Marani et al. (2021, PNAS) estimated the annual probability of an extreme novel epidemic at roughly 1918 scale in the low-single-digit percent per year, and argued it is rising with spillover pressure (land-use change, livestock density, air travel). Flag: I recall the paper's structure but not its exact figures — the ~2%/yr number should be checked against the primary source before you lean on it. Taking ~2%/yr as a central anchor: 1 − (0.98)^20 ≈ 33% over 20 years. I'd widen that to a ~20–40% range given base-rate uncertainty. The estimate rests on very few extreme events (a handful across ~4 centuries of usable data), so the base rate itself is poorly pinned, and the tail is heavy enough that the historical record can't contain it.

2. Engineered or lab-origin pandemic (accidental release from gain-of-function work, or deliberate misuse). Here a point probability would mislead. There is no historical reference class for a deliberate catastrophic engineered pandemic (zero events); the enabling technology — synthesis cost, AI-assisted design, openly published methods — is changing fast enough that even a good historical base rate wouldn't transfer; and the outcome is fat-tailed. This is non-estimable, not merely uncertain, so I won't attach a number to it. The useful question is not "what's the probability" but "what exposure makes the question moot": this tail is governed by dual-use research oversight and DNA-synthesis screening, not by anything a forecast changes.

Putting it together: the natural-channel figure (~20–40% over 20 years) is a floor, not the total. The engineered/accidental channel is non-estimable and, on most informed views, growing — so the true aggregate risk is higher than the natural-only number but cannot honestly be compressed into a single percentage. Anyone quoting a clean point estimate for the *total* (a "5%" or a "50%") is reporting false precision: the natural part is roughly estimable, the part that increasingly drives the tail is not.

What would move the natural estimate: a verified, updated base rate from the primary literature; shifts in spillover indicators; and demonstrated effect — up or down — of post-COVID surveillance and mRNA-platform response speed.

Omissions: (a) "emerging" vs. "becoming a full-blown pandemic" — I've read your question as the latter; (b) I've treated response capacity only lightly, yet faster medical countermeasures could genuinely cap severity in a way an 1918-anchored base rate doesn't capture (it cuts both ways — wider biotech capability also raises the engineered tail); (c) the whole answer is sensitive to the severity threshold — drop it from "1918-scale" to "modestly worse than COVID" and the natural-channel probability rises substantially.
