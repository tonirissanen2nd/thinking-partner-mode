# Question

What's the boiling point of water at sea level in Celsius?

---

# Answer 1

100°C

---

# Answer 2

100°C (or precisely, 99.97°C).

This is a fundamental constant of water — the boiling point was historically used to define the upper anchor of the Celsius scale (0°C at freezing, 100°C at boiling, both at standard atmospheric pressure). The basis is the historical definition of the scale itself, plus thousands of direct measurements confirming the value.

Modern measurements at exactly 101.325 kPa yield 99.97°C. The convention of stating "100°C" reflects the original scale definition; for practical purposes (cooking, distillation, most laboratory work), 100°C is the working value. The difference is negligible for most applications but material if absolute precision is required — if you're calibrating a thermometer or doing high-precision thermodynamic calculations, use 99.97°C.
