QUESTION:
What's the straight-line distance between New York City and London in miles?

=== Answer 1 ===
About 3,460 miles (roughly 5,570 km).

The figure depends on which endpoints you pick — "NYC" and "London" are areas, not points. Using standard reference coordinates (roughly Manhattan/JFK area to central London), the great-circle distance is ~3,450–3,470 miles. Any specific pair of points inside the two metros shifts the number by a few miles, not more.

One clarification on terms: "straight-line" here means the great-circle (surface) distance, which is what's almost always wanted. If you literally meant a chord through the Earth (tunnel distance), that's shorter — about 3,390 miles — but that's rarely the intended question.

=== Answer 2 ===
About 3,470 miles (roughly 5,570 km).

Two caveats on what "straight-line" means here:

- This is the great-circle distance along Earth's curved surface, not a true chord through the planet. A literal straight line tunneling through the Earth would be shorter (~5,540 km / ~3,440 km), but the surface great-circle figure is almost always what's wanted.
- The number shifts by a few miles depending on which reference points you pick (Times Square vs. JFK vs. city centroid; central London vs. Heathrow). The commonly cited value ranges roughly 3,450–3,470 miles depending on endpoints. For most purposes, "about 3,460–3,470 miles" is right.
