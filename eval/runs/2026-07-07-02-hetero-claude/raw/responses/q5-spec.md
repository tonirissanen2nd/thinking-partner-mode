Short answer: for most small nonprofits, the marginal dollar goes to **retention** — but that conclusion is conditional on one number you haven't given me, so treat the rule below as the actual answer and the default as a heuristic.

**The diagnostic variable is your current retention rate.** Marginal ROI on retention spending is high when retention is low and falling (lots of leak to plug cheaply) and collapses toward zero when retention is already near its ceiling. So the honest answer is "it depends on where you are on that curve," and the rest of this is how to locate yourself on it.

**Why retention usually wins the margin (Moderate confidence).** The core economics: acquisition typically *loses money on the first gift* — cost-to-acquire generally exceeds a first-time donor's first donation — and you only recoup through subsequent gifts. Retention spending (prompt thank-yous, showing where money went, a second-gift ask) is cheap relative to cold acquisition and acts on people who have already self-selected as willing to give. The compounding matters more than the per-dollar cost: donor lifetime value is roughly gift × frequency × (1 / churn rate), so retention improvements multiply the value of *every* donor including future acquired ones. This is why retention isn't just a competing use of the dollar — it sets the LTV that determines whether any acquisition spend is even solvent.

The empirical anchors here I hold at Moderate recall and would verify against the **Fundraising Effectiveness Project** before you act on them: overall US donor retention sits around 40–45%, first-time-donor retention is much worse (roughly 20–25%), and repeat-donor retention is far higher (~60%+). The gap between first-time and repeat retention is the whole game — getting a donor to their *second* gift is the highest-leverage transition in the funnel. Verify these figures; the direction is more robust than the specific numbers.

**When acquisition wins the marginal dollar instead:**
- **Your retention is already high** (say >65% and stable) — you're on the flat part of the retention curve, and marginal dollars find more return building the base.
- **The base is too small to matter yet** — a brand-new org with 30 donors doesn't have a retention problem to optimize; it has a scale problem. Compounding needs something to compound.
- **Demographic decay** — if your donors are aging out faster than replacement, retention only slows a structural decline; you need acquisition to survive regardless of ROI.
- **A capacity constraint on the acquisition side is temporarily cheap** — e.g., a matching-gift window, earned media, or a board member's network that lowers acquisition cost below its usual level.

**The reframe worth making (this is likely your actual XY problem).** "Acquisition vs. retention" is a partly false binary, and the highest-ROI marginal dollar for a small nonprofit is often *neither pure play*:
- **Reactivation of lapsed donors** — cheaper than cold acquisition (existing relationship, existing data) and usually under-worked. Often the single best marginal dollar, and it's frequently miscounted as "acquisition."
- **Upgrade / second-gift conversion** — moving existing donors up, or converting first-time to repeat, sits inside "retention" but is where the money actually is.

So the sharper question isn't "which funnel stage" but "which specific transition has the steepest untapped ROI right now" — and for small orgs that's most often the first-gift-to-second-gift and lapsed-reactivation transitions.

**What would change this answer:** your current first-time and repeat retention rates (the decisive input), your true fully-loaded cost-per-acquired-donor, and your time horizon. If you're optimizing net revenue over 3+ years, retention's compounding dominates; if you're solving a cash-survival problem this quarter, neither long-run funnel logic applies and you should be looking at major gifts or emergency appeals instead — a different question.

**Named omission:** I've treated "better" as maximizing net revenue / donor value over a multi-year horizon. If your objective is instead breadth of community support, advocacy reach, or board-mandated growth, the weighting shifts toward acquisition, and this whole analysis should be re-run against that objective. Tell me your retention rates and horizon and I can make this concrete rather than conditional.
