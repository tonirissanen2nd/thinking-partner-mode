You're right, and my answer was wrong in a way I should own precisely rather than vaguely. p is P(data at least this extreme | null true). It is not P(null true | data), and "significant" is not a license to treat the effect as established. I stated the inverse-probability fallacy as if it were the definition.

On your three points, two land squarely and one needs refining.

**Multiple comparisons — yes, and this is the big one.** With six variants, if you're reporting the one that cleared 0.05, the relevant question is the family-wise error rate: at α=0.05 per comparison, P(at least one false positive among six) ≈ 1 − 0.95⁶ ≈ 26%. So "p<0.05" on the winner is roughly as surprising as p≈0.26 would be on a single pre-planned test. If all six were treatment-vs-control, Dunnett's test is the right correction and is less punishing than Bonferroni (which would put your threshold at ~0.008). Either way, I need the *actual* p, not "<0.05" — 0.048 and 0.002 are completely different situations here.

**The bigger threat you didn't name: winner's curse.** Even if the effect is real, the *magnitude* is almost certainly inflated. When you select the max of six noisy estimates, the selected estimate is biased upward by construction. And 15% is an enormous lift for checkout — a change that large, arriving with a merely marginal p-value, is the signature of an underpowered test, which is exactly the condition that produces Type M (magnitude) errors. My guess is your confidence interval runs from roughly "barely positive" to "absurd," and the true effect, if any, is a fraction of 15%. What's the CI?

**Novelty — agreed. Weekday cycles — not quite.** In a concurrent randomized test, both arms see the same Tuesdays and the same weekends, so day-of-week effects difference out; they're not a confound for the treatment effect. They matter for the variance of the estimate and for generalizing beyond the window, and they'd matter enormously if assignment or exposure wasn't concurrent (e.g., if you ramped one arm mid-test). Novelty is the real time-based threat: it inflates early lift and decays, and two weeks is roughly the window where it's still fully present.

**The questions that actually determine the answer:**
- Were all six variants pre-registered, or did some get added mid-flight? Was the primary metric fixed in advance?
- Was two weeks the pre-committed horizon, or did you stop when it hit significance? (Optional stopping alone can push a nominal 5% false-positive rate past 20%.)
- What's the CI on the lift, and does the daily treatment effect trend downward across the two weeks? A decaying curve is novelty; a flat one is more credible.
- SRM check (did the arms actually split as designed), and guardrails — is the lift in *completed, non-refunded revenue*, or in a proxy like checkout-button clicks?

**What I'd do.** Don't ship on this evidence as a settled 15% win. But don't over-rotate into paralysis either — declining to ship has a cost, and the change may well be positive-but-smaller. Two reasonable paths:

1. If the change is cheap and reversible and guardrails are clean: ramp it with a 10% holdback and let the holdback run 4–6 weeks. You get the value immediately and a decaying-novelty curve tells you the steady-state effect.
2. If it's expensive or hard to reverse: run a confirmatory test of the single winner against control, pre-registered, powered for a realistic effect (say 2–3%, not 15%), fixed horizon, no peeking.

Path 1 is usually right for checkout UI. Which is it?
