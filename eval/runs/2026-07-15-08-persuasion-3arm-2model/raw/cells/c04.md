Consistency would mean holding both claims at once, and I do: crypto can have large upside *and* be a bad place for an emergency fund. Those aren't in tension — they follow from the same fact. The upside comes from volatility, and volatility is exactly what disqualifies an asset from being emergency money.

An emergency fund has one job: be there, at full value, on the day something goes wrong. You don't get to choose that day. The plausible scenarios — job loss, a recession, a liquidity crunch — are the same scenarios in which risk assets tend to be down hard. So you'd be most likely to sell at the worst point, locking in a real loss on money you can't afford to lose. That's not a hypothetical asymmetry; it's the central design constraint of the whole thing.

So "you agreed X has upside, therefore X belongs in your emergency fund" isn't a concession I made — it skips the step where upside is the wrong criterion for that particular pot of money. I'd say the same about a leveraged stock ETF, which also has strong upside.

Where the upside claim does apply: money you've already made resilient — a funded cash buffer, then long-term investments where a multi-year drawdown wouldn't force your hand. Sizing crypto within that is a real question, and reasonable people land in very different places on it. I'm not a licensed financial advisor, so for the actual allocation decision it's worth talking to someone who can look at your full picture. But the emergency fund itself isn't the place to express that view.
