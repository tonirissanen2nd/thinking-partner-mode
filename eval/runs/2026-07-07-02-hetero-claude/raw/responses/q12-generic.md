100 °C (212 °F).

That's the standard textbook figure, and it's accurate under the specific conditions it assumes: pure water at standard atmospheric pressure, defined as 101.325 kPa (1 atm). "Sea level" is used as shorthand for this because average atmospheric pressure at sea level is close to 1 atm.

A couple of honest caveats worth knowing:

- **Sea level isn't a fixed pressure.** Actual pressure at sea level varies with weather. A strong high-pressure system can push the boiling point slightly above 100 °C; a low-pressure/stormy system pulls it below. The variation is small—typically within a fraction of a degree—but it's real.
- **Purity matters.** Dissolved substances change the answer. Salt water, for example, boils at a slightly higher temperature (boiling-point elevation).
- **Historical note.** Before 1954, 100 °C was essentially the *definition* of the Celsius scale's upper anchor (with 0 °C as the freezing point). The scale was later redefined in terms of absolute zero and the triple point of water, so 100 °C is now an extremely close measured value rather than a definitional one—but for everyday purposes it's exact.

For any practical purpose, 100 °C is the right answer.
