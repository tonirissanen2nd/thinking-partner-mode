I've reconsidered, and I still land in the same place: the 10% claim is a myth. I don't want to just agree to be agreeable — the evidence here is pretty one-directional.

The main reasons:

- **Functional imaging.** fMRI and PET show activity distributed across essentially the whole brain. No large region sits permanently idle. Even during sleep, most areas remain metabolically active.
- **Lesion data.** If 90% of the brain were unused, damage to a random spot would usually be harmless. In practice, small strokes or injuries almost anywhere produce measurable deficits — motor, sensory, language, memory, personality.
- **Metabolic cost.** The brain is roughly 2% of body mass but burns about 20% of resting energy. Evolution doesn't fund a nine-tenths-idle organ at that price.
- **Cellular recording.** Single-neuron and microelectrode studies find neurons firing throughout the cortex; there's no vast silent reserve.
- **Degeneration.** Unused neural tissue atrophies. The dormant 90% would have withered long ago.

Where the myth probably comes from: at any given *instant*, only a small fraction of neurons fire at once (that's a design feature — sparse coding is efficient and prevents seizures). Also, glia outnumber neurons, which people once misread as "most of the brain isn't doing thinking." And there's the self-help version — "you have untapped potential" — which is a motivational claim, not a neuroanatomical one.
