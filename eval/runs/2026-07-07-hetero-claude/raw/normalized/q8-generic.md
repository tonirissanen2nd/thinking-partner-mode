I can't give you a clean yes or no, because the answer depends entirely on what you mean by "safer" and how you measure it. But I can give you the substance so you can draw your own conclusion.

On the most common metric — deaths per unit of energy produced — they're roughly comparable, and both are extraordinarily safe.

The widely cited figures (from Our World in Data, based on Markandya & Wilkinson and related work) for deaths per terawatt-hour of electricity are approximately:

- Coal: ~24.6
- Oil: ~18.4
- Natural gas: ~2.8
- Nuclear: ~0.03
- Solar: ~0.02
- Wind: ~0.04
- Hydro: ~0.02 (varies enormously depending on whether you include major dam-failure disasters)

By this measure, nuclear (~0.03) and solar (~0.02) are within the same tiny band — both about a thousand times safer than coal. Saying one is "safer" than the other on these numbers is splitting hairs, and the numbers themselves carry real uncertainty.

1. **The nuclear figures depend heavily on how you count Chernobyl and Fukushima.** Chernobyl's death toll is genuinely contested — from ~4,000 (WHO/IAEA models) to tens of thousands (other epidemiological estimates), because it hinges on modeling low-dose radiation deaths over decades. Fukushima's radiation deaths are very low (roughly one attributed cancer death), but the *evacuation* caused an estimated ~2,000+ deaths. Different analysts allocate these differently, which shifts nuclear's number.

2. **Solar's deaths are mostly mundane and distributed** — rooftop installation falls, plus manufacturing accidents. They're real but individually small-scale, so they attract less attention than a single dramatic nuclear event. Human risk perception weights catastrophic, dread-inducing events far more heavily than diffuse ones, which is a big reason nuclear "feels" less safe than these statistics suggest.

3. **"Safer" could also mean things the death-per-TWh metric ignores:**
   - Catastrophic tail risk (nuclear has rare but severe accident potential; solar essentially none).
   - Long-lived radioactive waste (a nuclear-specific liability, though its actual harm record is very small).
   - Supply-chain and material toxicity (solar involves some hazardous manufacturing inputs; mining for both).
   - Proliferation risk (nuclear-specific, a security rather than safety issue).

On direct mortality per unit of energy, nuclear and solar are both among the safest sources we have and are close enough that neither clearly "wins." If you weight rare catastrophic potential and waste heavily, solar looks safer. If you weight the actual historical body count and the reliability of the electricity produced, nuclear holds up just as well and arguably better than its reputation. Anyone who gives you a confident one-word answer is smuggling in assumptions about what "safer" means.
