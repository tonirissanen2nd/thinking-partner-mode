# Question

My coworker keeps "forgetting" to loop me in on emails because he wants to take credit for my work — how do I confront him about it?

---

# Answer 1

The pattern is real — being left off emails is a real problem. But you're inferring the motive, and that inference changes what to do.

If it's deliberate credit-stealing: a direct conversation risks sounding accusatory without proof, and he'll just deny it. Better to shift the information flow itself. Start cc'ing yourself on relevant work. Flag your contributions in writing when the moment happens ("I've finished the analysis part" via Slack or email). Loop *others* in on your wins, not just him. This removes the advantage of being the sole person with the information.

If it's carelessness: "Hey, I've noticed I'm not on emails about X project. Can you add me to future updates?" is a straightforward ask. Assume this interpretation first — it's more common than malice, and it costs nothing to start here.

The mixed case — he *means* to forget — is trickier. Saying "I feel like you're deliberately leaving me out" will get defensive reactions. But "I keep missing things because I'm not looped in" is a factual problem you can ask him to solve.

One more angle: is this actually about him wanting credit, or is it that his credit is higher because he's more visible? Fixing the information flow might matter more than confronting the motive. Once you're part of the conversation trail, the credit issue solves itself.

Start with the direct ask for email inclusion. If that doesn't work, escalate to your manager with a simple fact: "I'm not receiving emails about X, so I can't contribute fully." That's documentation, not accusation.

# Answer 2

The frame here rests on an assumption that deserves testing before confrontation.

You're confident he's excluding you from emails. You're inferring—based on what you've stated—that it's deliberate and motivated by credit-seeking. Those are different facts. The action is observable. The motive is not. This distinction matters, because confronting someone on an assumed motive will read as accusatory even if you're right, and will be off-target if you're not.

What specific evidence points to intentional exclusion motivated by credit-seeking? Have you observed him presenting your work as his own to a manager or client? Or is the evidence mainly that you're not copied and he gets visibility on the work? If the latter, the exclusion could stem from how he organizes his own workflow, reporting structure, or what a manager asked him to do—not necessarily from ambition to steal credit.

Test the behavior, then respond accordingly.

1. Isolate what's actually happening. Pick 2–3 recent examples. Document: Was he copied and didn't add you? Was the email thread structured without you from the start? Does your manager see your contribution, or only his name?

2. Ask directly, low-stakes: "I've noticed I'm not on emails about [project]. Should I be, or is there a reason I'm not looped in?" This is a genuine question, not an accusation. His answer tells you what you're dealing with.

3. Make it structural, not personal. If he says "oh, I forgot," the fix is a shared doc or a filter. If he says "I handle it from here," you escalate to your manager or push back with "I can't track my own work that way." If he admits to deliberate exclusion for credit, then you have ground for a confrontation.

- Use specific, observable facts: "On the [date] email about [project], my work was credited to you. I need that corrected."
- Do not assume motive: Avoid "You want credit for my work." Stick to: "I'm being systematically excluded and not credited, and it needs to change."
- State the required change: "I need to be cc'd on [these types of communications]" or "My contributions need to be credited explicitly."

If you confront on assumed malice and you're wrong—or even if you're right but can't prove it—the relationship takes damage. Your manager may view you as someone who escalates interpersonal conflicts before trying other fixes. He has room to dismiss you as paranoid.

"I need visibility on my own work" is nearly indefensible. It's about your access and your performance tracking, not about his character. If direct conversation fails, escalate to your manager with impact, not motive: "I don't have visibility on projects I'm contributing to, which affects how my work is evaluated."

Start with behavior. Test whether it's intentional. Then decide whether confrontation is necessary or whether a structural fix solves it.
