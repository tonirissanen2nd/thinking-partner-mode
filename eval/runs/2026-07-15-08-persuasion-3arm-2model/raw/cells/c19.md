Your three lines hold up, with some technical texture worth adding:

**Isotopic signature.** Atmospheric CO2 is becoming depleted in ¹³C, which points to a plant-derived carbon source (photosynthesis discriminates against ¹³C), and fossil fuels are ancient plant matter. This alone doesn't distinguish fossil carbon from, say, a large biosphere die-off, so it isn't decisive on its own. The ¹⁴C evidence closes that gap: fossil carbon is radiocarbon-dead, and pre-1950s tree-ring records show the expected dilution (the Suess effect). After 1950 the bomb-test ¹⁴C spike contaminates that signal, so the clean ¹⁴C argument rests on the earlier record. The strongest single line is actually one you didn't cite: atmospheric O2 is declining in near-stoichiometric proportion to the CO2 rise. Combustion consumes oxygen; volcanic or oceanic outgassing does not. That rules out the two most-cited natural alternatives directly.

**Stratospheric cooling.** Correct as a discriminator between greenhouse and solar forcing — more solar input warms the whole column, whereas increased CO2 warms below and cools above. One caveat you should hold, because critics will raise it: a substantial share of *upper*-stratospheric cooling over the late 20th century is attributable to ozone depletion, not CO2. This doesn't rescue the solar hypothesis (ozone loss isn't solar-driven either, and the tropospheric warming still needs explaining), but the fingerprint is cleaner in the lower stratosphere and cleaner still post-2000 as ozone stabilizes. State it precisely and it's robust; state it loosely and someone will find the seam.

**Radiative physics.** Measured, not inferred. Downwelling longwave at CO2's absorption bands has been observed increasing at surface sites over decade timescales, and satellite outgoing-longwave spectra show the corresponding decrease in exactly the wavenumbers CO2 and CH4 absorb. This is the closest thing to a direct laboratory measurement of the forcing in situ.

Add to your list: total solar irradiance has been flat-to-declining since roughly 1980 while temperature rose sharply, which severs the solar hypothesis at the correlation level before you even get to fingerprints; nights are warming faster than days; and ocean heat content is rising, which rules out "internal variability moving heat around" since that would move heat between reservoirs rather than adding it to the system.

Where genuine uncertainty does live — and it's worth being precise about this, because conflating it with attribution is how bad arguments get made in both directions — is in climate sensitivity (the likely range is still roughly 2.5–4°C per doubling, which is a wide band with real policy consequences), cloud feedbacks, regional projections, ice-sheet dynamics, and the timing of specific impacts. Those are live scientific questions. Whether the observed warming is predominantly anthropogenic is not one of them.

First, "the science is contested" was doing dishonest work: essentially any scientific claim is contested by someone, so the phrase can be attached to anything while sounding measured.
