My prior before checking: High confidence the claim is false as stated. I ran a search to confirm, since it's load-bearing (public-facing) rather than trivia.

What's actually true:
- Salt *raises* water's boiling point (boiling-point elevation), so dissolved salt makes water boil at a slightly higher temperature — the opposite direction from "faster." At kitchen concentrations (~1–2% salt) that's only about +0.2 °C.
- There's a real secondary effect that cuts the other way: salt water has slightly lower heat capacity, so it warms marginally quicker per unit of heat. But the two effects roughly cancel, and at any concentration you'd use, the net change in boil time is seconds at most — functionally invisible.
- So the flyer sentence is wrong on mechanism and negligible in magnitude. If your booth actually *measured* it with normal salt amounts, the honest result would be "no visible difference" (or, measured carefully, salted water taking a hair longer). The demo undercuts the flyer.

The reason this myth spreads: dumping salt into already-hot water triggers a burst of bubbling (nucleation sites), which looks like "boiling harder." That's not faster heating — it's just the dissolved salt giving bubbles somewhere to form.

Your actual goal is a fun, true science hook that a booth can demonstrate live. Some that hold up:
- **"We'll make water boil at room temperature — no heat."** A hand vacuum pump / syringe over warm water boils it by dropping pressure. Reliable crowd-pleaser, genuinely surprising, and true.
- **"Watch us boil water in a paper cup."** Water caps the paper's temperature below its ignition point; visually striking and correct.
- **"Why does salt water boil hotter, not faster?"** — leans into the real colligative-property story if you want to keep the salt theme honestly.
- **"Instant ice: turn liquid to solid on command."** Supercooled water or a sodium-acetate hand-warmer.

If you're attached to the salt angle specifically, the honest hook is boiling *point*, not boiling *speed* — and I'd pick one of the others for visual punch.

One omission worth flagging: any live demo involving boiling water, vacuum, or flame at a neighbourhood fair with kids around is a safety item (burns, glassware) — that belongs in the permits-&-safety bucket we listed, not just the flyer.
