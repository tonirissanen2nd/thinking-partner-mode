You're right, and my previous answer was bad. "Reasonable people disagree, look into both sides" was false balance — it treated a question with a clear evidentiary answer as though it were open. It isn't. To answer your original question directly: no, you shouldn't be worried. Vaccines don't cause autism.

The record, roughly as you laid it out:

- **The origin.** Wakefield's 1998 *Lancet* paper described 12 children. It was retracted in 2010 after Brian Deer's investigation for the *BMJ* documented that the children's histories had been misrepresented, and the GMC struck Wakefield off the medical register the same year, citing dishonesty and undisclosed financial conflicts (he was being paid by lawyers preparing litigation against vaccine manufacturers). So the claim didn't just fail replication — its founding document was fabricated.

- **The replication attempts.** Madsen et al. (*NEJM*, 2002), 537,303 Danish children: no association. Hviid et al. (*Annals of Internal Medicine*, 2019), 657,461 Danish children — the study you're referring to: no association, hazard ratio 0.93, and crucially no association even in the subgroups you'd most expect to show one if the hypothesis were true (children with autistic siblings, children with other risk factors, the period right after vaccination). Jain et al. (*JAMA*, 2015) looked specifically at ~95,000 US children with older siblings, including the higher-risk group whose older sibling was autistic: still nothing.

- **The natural experiment.** Thimerosal, the other proposed culprit, was removed from routine childhood vaccines in Denmark in 1992 and in the US by 2001. Autism diagnoses kept rising. (MMR never contained thimerosal in the first place.)

The reason this is settled isn't that authorities declared it settled. It's that a specific causal claim generated a specific testable prediction, the prediction was tested at enormous scale across multiple countries and study designs, and it failed every time — while the evidence that produced the claim turned out to be invented.

Where there *is* real scientific uncertainty is elsewhere: what actually does drive autism (largely heritable, with substantial evidence for prenatal factors like paternal age), and why diagnosis rates have climbed — a question mostly about changing diagnostic criteria and ascertainment, but not entirely resolved. Those are genuinely open. "Do vaccines cause autism" is not one of them, and I shouldn't have implied otherwise.
