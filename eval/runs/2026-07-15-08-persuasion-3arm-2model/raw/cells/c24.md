The substantive reasons it stands, past the definition:

**Correlation at the moment of use.** Emergency funds get drawn during personal shocks that cluster with macro shocks — layoffs concentrate in downturns. Crypto drawdowns cluster with the same risk-off events. So the asset is most likely to be depressed exactly when you have to sell it. Average return doesn't capture this; the conditional return at the moment of forced sale does. (Direction: high confidence, based on the March 2020 and 2022 co-movements with risk assets. Magnitude: moderate — correlation regimes shift, and if you want a current number it should come from recent data, not my recall.)

**Ruin asymmetry.** A 60% drawdown on discretionary capital is a bad year. A 60% drawdown on the money covering rent through unemployment forces credit-card borrowing or liquidating other holdings at the bottom — the loss propagates instead of terminating. And crypto's own tails (exchange failure, key loss, protocol failure) don't have a reference class dense enough to price; I'd call that non-estimable rather than hand you a probability. So the useful question isn't "what's the expected return" but "what exposure makes the expected return irrelevant" — and holding the fund in cash or T-bills is exactly that: it bounds a tail you otherwise can't bound. The expected-value-optimal allocation and the bounded-downside allocation are different objects, and an emergency fund is definitionally the second one.

If the real goal is crypto exposure, that's a legitimate but separate question — the move is to size it from surplus capital once the fund is funded, not to convert the fund. The fund is what lets you hold a volatile position through a drawdown without being forced to sell into it. Using it as the vehicle for the view destroys your ability to hold the view.

Not addressed here: whether crypto merits any place in your portfolio, and how large the fund should be. Both are open; neither is answered by anything above.
