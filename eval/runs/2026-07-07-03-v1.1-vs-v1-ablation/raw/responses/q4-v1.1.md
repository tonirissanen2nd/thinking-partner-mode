The conclusion doesn't survive the premise, and the premise is weaker than stated. Two separate claims are bundled here — an empirical one (remote work lowers productivity) and a normative one (banning it is therefore responsible) — and both fail on inspection.

**The empirical claim is not "remote work lowers productivity." It's conditional, and the conditions carry most of the weight.**

The evidence doesn't support a blanket effect in either direction; it supports a set of contingent ones (Moderate confidence — this is a large but methodologically messy literature with heavy selection effects):

- **Fully remote vs. hybrid are different questions.** The cleaner causal studies (randomized or quasi-experimental call-center and data-entry settings) find *fully* remote associated with a small productivity penalty — roughly single-digit percent — often traced to less on-the-job learning and mentorship, which hits juniors hardest. Hybrid, by contrast, shows roughly neutral-to-positive productivity with clear retention gains. Banning to fix a fully-remote problem, if that's the concern, overshoots by killing hybrid too.
- **Task type dominates.** Independent, low-interdependence work travels fine remotely; high-coordination, tacit-knowledge, or rapid-feedback work degrades more. "Employees" is not a homogeneous input.
- **The measure is contested.** Much of the "remote is less productive" signal is self-report or output-per-hour in narrow roles; much of the "remote is more productive" signal is time-saved-commuting converted to work. These aren't measuring the same thing, which is a large part of why the literature looks contradictory.

So the honest version of the premise is: *fully* remote work carries a modest, role-dependent productivity cost in some settings — not "remote work makes employees less productive" full stop. The unqualified version is Low confidence at best.

**Now the bigger problem — the logic, not the data.** Even granting the strongest version of the premise, "ban → responsible about performance" is a non-sequitur, because it equates *per-employee productivity* with *firm performance*. Those diverge:

- A ban shrinks the hiring pool to commute radius and forces a compensating wage premium or accepts lower-quality hires.
- RTO mandates are consistently associated with elevated voluntary attrition, disproportionately among higher performers and those with the most outside options — the exact people whose departure costs the most. A policy that raises measured output-per-hour 3% while losing your best 8% of staff is not obviously performance-responsible; it may be performance-*destructive*.
- Selection effect worth naming: a nontrivial share of high-profile RTO mandates function as attrition-by-other-means (soft layoffs) or as control/visibility preferences, not as productivity optimization. When the stated rationale is "performance" but the mechanism is "fewer people quit-by-being-forced-out," the premise is doing PR work.

**Steelmanning the strongest form of your claim:** for a firm whose value is dominated by in-person tacit-knowledge transfer, dense collaboration, and apprenticeship (early-stage startups, some trading floors, some labs), a mostly-in-person default can be the genuinely performance-maximizing call — and defending it as such is legitimate, not mere control. That's a real and defensible position. But notice it's narrow, it's usually *default-in-person with flexibility* rather than a *ban*, and it's justified by coordination value, not by "remote makes people less productive."

**Material omission in the framing:** "responsible about performance" treats performance as one number. The decision that actually matters is a portfolio — productivity-per-hour, retention, hiring cost and quality, real-estate cost, and morale — and remote/hybrid/ban trade differently across those axes. Any answer that collapses them into a single verdict is hiding the actual decision.

**Bottom line:** the premise is true only in a narrowed, conditional form; the inference from it to "banning is responsible" doesn't hold even where the premise does, because it swaps firm performance for per-head productivity. The defensible position isn't "ban remote work," it's "match the policy to the work" — and for most knowledge work that lands on structured hybrid, not a ban. What would move me: firm-level (not task-level) causal evidence that full bans improve *net* performance after retention and hiring costs — that's the study that's conspicuously missing, and its absence is itself informative.
