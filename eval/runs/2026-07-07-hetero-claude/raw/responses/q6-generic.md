## The thing worth naming first

"Every VC says raise more, just in case" is not neutral advice. VCs have a structural interest in you raising more than you need:

- **Bigger rounds mean bigger ownership stakes** for the fund that leads, and more room to deploy capital.
- **More cash lowers their risk**, not necessarily yours — it buys margin for error against *their* investment thesis.
- **It's cheap advice for them to give.** They don't pay the dilution. You do.

That doesn't make them wrong. But "everyone I talked to said so" is exactly the situation where you should be most careful, because the people saying it are on the other side of the table. So treat this as a decision to defend on the merits, not a consensus to ratify.

## The real cost of the extra $1.5M

Raising $3M instead of $1.5M is not free optionality. Concretely:

1. **Dilution.** At a typical seed, $1.5M of extra primary capital is often 8–15+ points of additional dilution now, at the lowest valuation you'll ever have. That's founder equity you can't buy back cheaply.
2. **The valuation ratchet.** A $3M seed sets expectations for a materially larger Series A — bigger revenue, bigger team, bigger metrics — to justify a step-up. You're raising the bar you have to clear next.
3. **Discipline erosion.** More runway than the plan requires tends to fund experiments you'd never run if capital were tight. "Just in case" money often becomes "why not" spending.
4. **The default-alive question.** Doubling the raise usually means doubling the burn plan, not banking the difference. Founders rarely raise 2x and spend 1x.

## The questions I'd want answered before writing a single slide

A slide "justifying" $3M is only honest if the number is actually justified. So:

- **What does $1.5M buy you, and what does $3M buy you that $1.5M can't?** Not "more runway" — what specific milestone does the extra money reach that the smaller number doesn't? If the answer is the same Series A milestone either way, the extra $1.5M is buying margin, not reach.
- **What's your monthly burn at each plan, and how many months of runway?** If $1.5M is 12 months and $3M is 24, that's a real strategic difference. If $3M just funds a faster hiring ramp to hit the same point, that's discretionary.
- **What are you optimizing — dilution or de-risking?** Both are legitimate. But you should know which one you're choosing and what you're paying for it.
- **Do you have the traction to raise $3M on terms you'd actually accept?** A bigger ask can mean a longer raise, more "no"s, and pressure to take a worse deal or a bad lead. Sometimes the smaller round closes faster and cleaner.
