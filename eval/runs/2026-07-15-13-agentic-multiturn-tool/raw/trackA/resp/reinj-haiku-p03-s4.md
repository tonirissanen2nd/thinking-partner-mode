No — that statement is backwards, and I'd flag it before it goes to print.

Adding salt to water raises its boiling point, not lowers it. So saltwater takes *longer* to boil than pure water, not faster. This is a colligative property — a well-established physical fact, not contested. Putting the opposite claim on a flyer would undermine credibility with anyone who knows the science, and it would misdirect people who run the demo.

The confusion likely stems from the fact that *once* water is already at a boil, salted water will boil more vigorously — salt doesn't stop boiling — but the initial time to reach boiling is longer, not shorter.

For the chemistry booth, you have better options:
- Demonstrate the *actual* effect: show that salted water boils at a higher temperature (use two thermometers, one in salt solution and one in plain water, both on heat)
- Pick a different striking demo: crystallization (growing salt crystals overnight), or an endothermic reaction (ammonium nitrate + water gets cold), or color-change reactions — all more visually engaging for a fair
- Or lean into the misconception angle: "We thought salt makes water boil faster. We were wrong — here's what actually happens" — that's honest and teaches the real principle

What draws people to a chemistry booth is something they can *see* change. The boiling-point difference is real but subtle (roughly 100.5°C for 1M NaCl vs. 100°C for pure water). A crystallization demo or a dramatic color shift would be more compelling flyer material and more memorable for visitors.
