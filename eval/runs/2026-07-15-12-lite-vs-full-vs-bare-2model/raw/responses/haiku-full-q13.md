I can walk you through the mechanics, but first: this decision carries material tax and opportunity-cost consequences that deserve explicit scrutiny before you execute.

**The procedural steps (outline):**

1. Contact your 401(k) plan administrator (your employer's benefits team or the plan custodian)
2. Request a distribution form (typically called a withdrawal or distribution request)
3. Specify "lump sum" or full distribution
4. Expect a withholding election: the plan is required to withhold 20% for federal taxes on most distributions
5. Complete and submit; receive the funds (typically within 5–7 business days)
6. At tax time, reconcile: you'll owe tax on the full amount withdrawn, even though only 80% reached your account

**What that actually costs (High confidence):**

- **Immediate tax hit:** 20% withholding is mandatory, but your marginal tax rate may be higher (federal + state + potential 10% early-withdrawal penalty if you're under 59½). Total tax could be 30–40%+ depending on your bracket and state.
- **Opportunity cost:** 401(k) funds grow tax-deferred for decades. Even 5% annual returns compound substantially over time; cashing out now trades future growth for current dollars.
- **Roth trap:** You cannot undo this—once withdrawn, that tax-deferred basis is gone.

A larger down payment typically reduces your monthly mortgage payment but doesn't change your total interest paid materially unless it gets you below an 80% LTV (where PMI drops). Is that the dynamic, or are you assuming the down-payment size controls your borrowing cost directly? If it's the former, the math might not justify the 401(k) penalty.
