Yes, you're essentially correct. Food safety experts including the CDC and USDA now recommend *not* washing raw chicken before cooking it, for exactly the reason you mentioned.

When you rinse raw chicken, the water doesn't kill the bacteria—it only removes some of it through mechanical action. More importantly, the splashing spreads bacteria-contaminated water droplets across your sink, countertops, hands, nearby utensils, and other surfaces. This increases the risk that bacteria will contaminate other foods or your hands.

The key point is that thorough cooking kills all the dangerous bacteria (salmonella, campylobacter, etc.) when chicken reaches an internal temperature of 165°F (74°C). Since proper cooking handles the safety issue anyway, washing introduces risk without providing meaningful protection.

So the best practice is to skip the rinse, place the chicken directly into the pan or oven, and ensure it cooks to the proper temperature. If you do accidentally splash raw chicken water, just clean and disinfect the affected surfaces.
