Before you move anyone's schedule, don't act on this number yet. A 40% higher churn rate on weekends is an interesting signal, but the leap from that observation to "reschedule the whole support team" skips several steps that could easily make the change useless or counterproductive.

1. **How is "weekend churn" defined?** Are you counting customers who *cancel* on Saturday/Sunday, or customers whose churn you *attribute* to a weekend experience? Many people cancel on weekends simply because that's when they have free time to deal with admin — the dissatisfaction may have built up during the week. If so, staffing weekends does nothing for the root cause.

2. **What's the base rate and sample size?** 40% higher relative to what absolute number? Going from 1.0% to 1.4% on low weekend volume can be statistical noise. Weekends are only ~2/7 of the calendar, so weekend cohorts are smaller and noisier. I'd want the raw counts and a significance check before treating this as real.

3. **Is it actually a support problem?** Churn correlates with lots of things that also cluster on weekends: billing cycles that renew Friday/Saturday, free-trial expirations, marketing cohorts acquired on certain days, B2C vs B2B usage patterns. The weekend correlation may be a proxy for *who* those customers are, not *when* they were served.

4. **Correlation vs. causation.** Even if weekend churn is genuinely higher and real, you have no evidence yet that support coverage is the lever. The implicit theory is "these people churned because they couldn't reach support." Do you have data showing weekend churners actually contacted support, waited too long, or went unanswered? If weekend churners never opened a ticket, more weekend agents won't help them.

- **Ticket data for weekend churners specifically.** Did they contact support? What were response/resolution times vs. weekday? Did unresolved weekend tickets predict cancellation?
- **A cohort breakdown** — segment by plan, tenure, acquisition channel, and geography to see whether "weekend" is really standing in for some other variable.
- **The counterfactual cost.** Shifting the *whole* team to weekends strips weekday coverage, where the majority of your volume and revenue conversations likely happen. You could trade a small, uncertain weekend gain for a real weekday degradation.

Don't reschedule the whole team. If the signal survives scrutiny, run a **bounded experiment**: add modest weekend coverage (a subset of agents, or extended hours) for a few weeks and measure whether weekend churn actually moves relative to a control period. That gives you causal evidence at a fraction of the disruption and risk.

Right now you have a suggestive correlation, not a validated cause, and the proposed fix is one of the most disruptive options available. I'd treat "40% higher weekend churn" as a hypothesis worth investigating, not a conclusion worth restructuring your team around.
