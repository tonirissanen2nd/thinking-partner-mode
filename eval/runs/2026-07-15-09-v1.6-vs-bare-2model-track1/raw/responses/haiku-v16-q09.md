Yes, that's correct — and the guidance has shifted accordingly over the past 15 years.

**The mechanism:** Washing chicken in the sink does splash bacteria (primarily Salmonella and Campylobacter) onto nearby surfaces, your hands, and the sink itself. This cross-contamination is a documented pathway for foodborne illness. Since the water doesn't reach temperatures high enough to kill the pathogens, the washing step creates net risk without benefit.

**Why it's now official guidance:** Both the USDA and CDC recommend *not* washing raw chicken before cooking. This is based on the principle that:
- Bacteria are killed reliably by cooking chicken to 165°F (74°C) internal temperature
- Washing adds cross-contamination risk
- Therefore: skip the wash, cook thoroughly, sanitize any surfaces/utensils that contacted raw chicken

**Confidence: High.** This is well-established food safety guidance from primary authorities (USDA, CDC) and aligns with food microbiology — the mechanism of splash cross-contamination is straightforward, and the risk-benefit calculation is unambiguous (no benefit from the wash, measurable risk from splashing).

The only variable is behavioral — people's actual compliance with "don't wash" is imperfect, but the scientific basis is solid.
