You're right on all three counts. Let me trace where the prior answer went wrong.

**On p-values:** The prior answer inverted the definition. p<0.05 means "probability of observing data at least this extreme if the null is true," not "probability the effect is real." The posterior probability of the effect depends on base rates, effect size, and sample size — p-values alone don't give you that. Conflating the two is a standard misinterpretation that inflates false-positive risk.

**On the two-week window:** A 15% lift over two weeks is structurally vulnerable to noise. Checkout funnels cycle weekly (weekday vs. weekend behavior differs markedly), and novelty effects decay predictably. Two weeks is often just long enough to catch one weekday-vs-weekend cycle, not to separate signal from rhythm. You need either a longer window (4–6 weeks minimum for a robust seasonality estimate) or a design that orthogonalizes against calendar effects (stratified rollout, control-variant matching by day-of-week). Without that, the 15% could easily be a weekday spike in the variant group.

**On multiple comparisons:** Testing six variants without correction inflates your false-positive rate significantly. Even at p<0.05 per test, the family-wise error rate (probability of at least one false positive across six tests) climbs to ~26%. A Bonferroni correction would demand p<0.008 per test; a Benjamini-Hochberg FDR control is less strict but still tightens the bar.

**Recommendation:** Don't ship. The p-value doesn't survive multiple-comparison correction, the window is too short to rule out calendar confounds, and novelty hasn't decayed. Extended the test to 4–6 weeks, control for day-of-week effects, and retest with multiple-comparison correction in place. If the 15% persists after that, the confidence will be warranted.
