Before the "how," a problem with the "what." Your question bundles an observable fact with an inferred motive, and the motive is the weakest part of the case — which matters, because it's what your chosen approach ("confront him about wanting to take credit") is built on.

Split them:

- **Observable:** He leaves you off threads where your work is discussed, and the work isn't attributed to you. This is verifiable and it's the thing that actually costs you.
- **Inferred:** He does it *deliberately, in order to* claim your work. This is a mind-reading claim about intent. You almost certainly can't prove it, and there are competing explanations that produce the identical pattern — disorganization, a mental "owner of the thread" heuristic that doesn't include you, genuine ambiguity about who's driving, or self-serving bias that's real but not consciously calculated. I'd put the probability that it's *conscious, strategic* credit-theft rather than motivated carelessness at no better than a coin flip on what you've described.

Why this distinction is load-bearing and not pedantry: **the two readings point to opposite actions, and one of them is close to irreversible.** If you open with the intent accusation ("you're cutting me out to steal credit") and you're wrong — or right but can't prove it — you've converted a fixable process problem into a personal war, in front of a coworker who now has a documented grievance against *you* and every incentive to freeze you out harder. That downside is asymmetric: the accusatory route's best case is marginally better than the process route's best case, but its worst case is much worse. When the payoff is lopsided like that, you play the bounded-downside move even if you privately believe the ugly version.

So the better goal isn't "confront him about his motive." It's **make your contribution structurally visible so his intent stops mattering.** Credit that's on the record can't be quietly absorbed regardless of why he was leaving you off.

Concretely:

1. **Fix the mechanism first, neutrally.** Ask to be added to the relevant threads/lists as a standing thing, framed as workflow, not grievance: "I keep landing outside these threads and then missing context — can you cc me on anything touching X so I'm not reconstructing it later?" A person acting in good faith complies instantly. A person who was doing it on purpose now has to actively *refuse*, which is far more diagnostic than any confrontation — and it's a test that costs you nothing to run.
2. **Create your own paper trail.** After you do a piece of work, send the summary/deliverable yourself to the stakeholders who matter (including his manager or the shared channel where appropriate). Not as a maneuver against him — as your normal operating practice. This is the single highest-leverage move: it makes attribution automatic and doesn't depend on his behavior at all.
3. **If it continues after step 1**, that refusal-to-comply *is* your evidence, and now you have a behavioral pattern instead of an accusation. That's the point at which you escalate — and you escalate to your manager, framed around visibility and workflow ("I've asked twice to be included on X and I'm still finding out after decisions are made; it's affecting my ability to do the work"), not around his soul.

If you do end up speaking to him directly, the script is behavior-and-impact only, zero motive: *"When the Henderson work went out and I wasn't on the thread, the client came back to me for details I hadn't seen — I need to be included on things I'm doing the work for."* Describe what happened and what it cost. The moment you say "because you wanted the credit," you've handed him the exit: he denies the intent, the conversation becomes about whether you're paranoid, and the actual problem never gets addressed.

Two things I'm not addressing that could change the calculus: **whether this is already affecting a review or promotion cycle** (if credit is about to be formally allocated, the timeline compresses and looping in your manager moves up), and **the local power dynamic** — if he's senior to you or tight with the manager, the escalation math shifts and the paper-trail route (step 2) carries even more of the weight. If either applies, tell me and I'd sequence this differently.

One caution against my own advice: if you have *specific* evidence of deliberate misrepresentation — he verbally claimed your work as his in a meeting, took your document and put his name on it — then this isn't a visibility problem, it's a conduct problem, and the neutral-process framing under-responds to it. Everything above assumes the honest read of your situation is "I'm being left off threads," not "I've watched him actively lie about authorship." Which one is it?
