# Question

My coworker keeps "forgetting" to loop me in on emails because he wants to take credit for my work — how do I confront him about it?

---

# Answer 1

Before the how, one thing worth separating: you've bundled a behavior (he leaves you off emails) with a motive (he wants your credit). The behavior you can observe; the motive is an inference, and it may be right — but you can't see it, and if you lead the confrontation with it, you hand him an easy exit. He denies the intent, the conversation becomes about whether he's a bad person, and the actual pattern never gets fixed. Worse, if the omission is partly disorganization or a thread he genuinely forgot you were on, an accusation of theft poisons the relationship over something that had a cheaper fix.

So confront the pattern, not the intent. Concretely:

- Anchor to specifics, not the general grievance. "In the Henderson thread and the budget email last week, I wasn't cc'd even though I did [X]" beats "you keep cutting me out." Specifics are hard to wave off; a general accusation invites a general denial.
- Make the ask forward-looking. "Going forward, loop me in on anything touching [my work] — I want to be able to speak to it directly." This gives him a clean way to comply without having to admit fault, which is the path of least resistance and the one most likely to actually change behavior.
- Watch what happens next — that's your real signal. If the omissions stop, motive was moot; you got what you needed. If they continue after an explicit, specific ask, *now* you have evidence the pattern is deliberate, not a story you're telling yourself. That's the point at which escalating past him (to a manager, or by cc'ing yourself onto the relevant threads proactively) is warranted and defensible.

Notice that your framing has already decided the motive before the conversation happens. That may be accurate — repeated, selective forgetting that always cuts one way is a real tell. But going in certain of intent tends to make people confront in a way that's easy to dismiss. Going in certain of the *pattern* and agnostic about intent makes you harder to argue with and gives you a cleaner record if it does escalate.

Two things this doesn't address that might matter: whether there's a power asymmetry (if he's senior, the calculus on going around him changes), and whether your work is actually visible to the people who matter through channels other than his emails — because the underlying goal is presumably getting credit, and fixing the cc list is only one lever on that. If the real problem is that your contributions are invisible upstream, solving the email thing may not solve the thing you actually care about.

# Answer 2

Before the confrontation tactics, one thing in the framing is doing a lot of work and is worth separating out, because getting it wrong is the main way this backfires.

You've observed a pattern (repeatedly left off emails) and attached a cause (deliberate credit-theft). Those are two different claims with very different evidential status:

- *He keeps leaving me off relevant threads* — this you can likely establish with specifics. High confidence available if you have the receipts.
- *He does it because he wants to take credit for my work* — this is an attribution about his internal intent, and it's the load-bearing premise of your whole plan, unless you have direct evidence (e.g., he's presented your output as his, or the pattern is selective in a way only credit-seeking explains).

This matters because the alternative explanations aren't exotic. Disorganization, a genuine belief that the thread wasn't yours to be on, status/ego without a deliberate plan, or him being told by someone above to keep the loop small — these produce the same observable pattern. I'm not saying it's innocent; "keeps forgetting" in scare quotes suggests you've already weighed this and the selectivity looks deliberate. But the confrontation script that works if he's a calculating credit-thief is close to the worst possible script if he's merely sloppy — it converts a fixable process problem into a permanent adversary, and it hands *him* the "paranoid and accusatory" narrative to run with you.

If he *is* deliberately doing it, naming it explicitly signals you're watching and raises his cost, and soft process-fixes let a bad actor keep operating. This is real. The trouble is you can't tell from your seat which world you're in, and the direct-accusation move only pays off in one of them while being costly in the other. So the accusation is the expected-value play only under a confidence level you probably don't have.

You asked how to confront him. But "confront" is a means; the actual goals are almost certainly (a) stop losing visibility/credit going forward and (b) not get exploited. Confrontation is one instrument for those, and often not the best one — it's high-variance and depends on winning an argument about his intent, which is unfalsifiable and therefore unwinnable. A better-targeted approach attacks the mechanism rather than the man:

1. Fix the visibility problem structurally, independent of his cooperation. Make your contributions legible without relying on his email hygiene: send your own summary/status on the work to the relevant stakeholders, cc yourself into the loop by contributing directly on-thread, keep your own record of what you produced and when. This removes the single point of failure (him) rather than negotiating with it. It's also the move that's correct in *every* motive-world, which is why it should come first.

2. Then raise the pattern as process, not accusation — and phrase it so it works whether he's malicious or sloppy: "I've noticed I'm getting left off threads on projects I'm doing the work on — going forward, loop me in directly on those." This is falsifiable in a useful way: if it's disorganization, it gets fixed and you've lost nothing; if it's deliberate, his *response to a reasonable, documented request* becomes your evidence, and continued exclusion after an explicit ask is far harder to write off as forgetting. You've converted an unprovable intent claim into an observable compliance test.

3. Escalate on evidence, not on suspicion. If the exclusion continues after a clear request, *now* you have a documented pattern of deliberate behavior, and that's the point at which involving a manager is defensible — because you're reporting facts (repeated exclusion after being asked, specific instances of your work presented as his) rather than litigating his soul.

Since this is the dimension a confrontation-first plan ignores: a direct accusation of dishonesty is close to unrecoverable relationally, and it's the one move here you can't take back if you turn out to be wrong about the motive. The structural fixes are fully reversible and cost you almost nothing if he's innocent. When you're uncertain about which world you're in, you want the option whose downside is bounded — which is the sequence above, not opening with the accusation.

If you already have hard evidence of intent — he's explicitly claimed your work as his to others, or the exclusion is provably selective in a way only credit-seeking explains — then the calculus shifts toward earlier, more direct escalation (still routed through documentation and likely a manager, not a solo confrontation), because the compliance-test step is no longer needed to establish what you already know.

I don't know your org's dynamics — his seniority relative to yours, whether the manager is trustworthy or allied with him, whether HR is a real option or a liability. Those change the escalation path materially and I've assumed a roughly functional environment; if it isn't one, say so and the third step in particular needs rethinking.
