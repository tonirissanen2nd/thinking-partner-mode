Yes, that's right — and it reflects the current mainstream food-safety guidance (the UK's NHS/FSA and the US CDC and USDA all advise against washing raw chicken).

The reasoning:

- **Washing doesn't decontaminate the meat.** The bacteria of concern (*Campylobacter*, *Salmonella*) are killed by heat, not water. Cooking chicken to a proper internal temperature (73–74°C / 165°F) reliably kills them. Rinsing does essentially nothing to reduce the bacterial load on the meat itself.

- **Washing actively spreads bacteria.** Water hitting the chicken's surface aerosolizes and splashes droplets onto surrounding surfaces — sink, faucet, countertop, nearby utensils, and food. Splashes can travel over half a meter. This is the mechanism that turns a contained hazard (raw meat you're going to cook) into a distributed one (contaminated surfaces that may touch ready-to-eat food).

So the net effect is negative: no benefit, plus a new cross-contamination pathway. The dominant real-world route to poultry-linked illness is cross-contamination of things that *won't* be cooked, not undercooking the chicken itself.

One clarification on the "kills anything" phrasing, since it's the part people most often get slightly wrong: the claim isn't that rinsing fails because bacteria are hard to rinse off — it's that rinsing is the wrong tool entirely. Even if it removed some surface bacteria, cooking handles what remains anyway, and the splash risk is pure downside.

What actually matters for safety, in rough priority order: cook to temperature (a thermometer beats visual doneness), keep raw chicken and its juices away from ready-to-eat food, and wash hands, utensils, and any surface that contacted the raw meat or its packaging with soap and hot water.

Two edge notes so this isn't overstated: (1) The guidance is about plain water rinsing. Some traditional preparations use an acid or salt treatment (e.g., soaking in lemon or vinegar); the splash-contamination concern still applies, and these aren't reliable substitutes for proper cooking. (2) Patting chicken dry with paper towels (then binning them and washing hands) is fine and is often preferred for browning — that's not "washing" in the sense the warning targets.
