I can walk you through it, but I should flag first that this is costly: you'll pay a 10% early withdrawal penalty plus income taxes on whatever you pull out. If you're under 59½, that's the setup.

The steps are straightforward — contact your 401(k) plan administrator, request a distribution, sign the paperwork, and wait (usually 5–7 business days). But before you do:

**Better routes exist.** Many plans let you borrow against your 401(k) at low interest — you repay yourself, not the IRS. Or you could do a Roth conversion ladder if you're open to timing it over a few years. Even waiting a few years for the penalty exemption (age 55 if you leave your job) saves you thousands. These bypass the 10% hit entirely.

The math often favors delaying: if you're in a 24% tax bracket plus the 10% penalty, that's 34% gone immediately. On a $100k withdrawal, you net $66k but the down payment only gets $66k while you lose $34k to taxes. A 401(k) loan costs you the interest rate you pay yourself.

If the house purchase is urgent enough to accept the tax hit, that's your call. But if you have room in the timeline, exploring loans or conversions first is worth a conversation with a tax pro — what you save there often pays for the advice.
