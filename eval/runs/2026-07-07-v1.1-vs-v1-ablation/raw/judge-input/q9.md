QUESTION:
I've heard that washing raw chicken in the sink before cooking it actually increases the risk of food poisoning, because it splashes bacteria onto nearby surfaces rather than killing anything — is that right?

=== Answer 1 ===
Yes, that's substantially correct.

The mechanism, unpacked:

1. **Washing doesn't sterilize.** Rinsing with tap water at any household temperature does not kill *Campylobacter*, *Salmonella*, or other pathogens on raw poultry. Their removal by water is partial and unreliable — you cannot rinse a surface clean of bacteria embedded in moisture and tissue.

2. **It actively redistributes contamination.** Water hitting the carcass aerosolizes and splashes droplets carrying bacteria onto surrounding surfaces — countertops, utensils, dish racks, other food, hands, clothing. UK FSA work has cited splash reaching on the order of tens of centimeters around the sink. So washing converts a contained hazard (bacteria on the chicken, which you'll cook) into a dispersed one (bacteria on things you won't cook).

3. **Cooking is what actually makes it safe.** Bringing poultry to a sufficient internal temperature (commonly cited target: 74°C / 165°F) reliably destroys these pathogens. That step, not washing, is the control point. Washing therefore adds risk without adding any safety benefit.

The net conclusion — don't wash raw chicken — is the mainstream recommendation, and I'd state it plainly.

Two boundary conditions worth naming, since the blanket "washing increases risk" framing has edges:

- **Magnitude vs. direction.** The *direction* (washing is net-negative or at best neutral) is well established. The *size* of the real-world illness risk from splash is harder to pin down because it depends on your kitchen layout, whether contaminated surfaces later touch ready-to-eat food, and your cleaning habits. The advice holds regardless, since the downside is asymmetric: no upside to washing, only potential downside.

- **Patting dry / trimming.** If a recipe needs a dry surface (for browning), patting with paper towel that goes straight in the bin is fine — that's not rinsing under a running tap and doesn't generate the same splash. Similarly, if you must handle the raw meat, the real safeguards are: dedicated cutting board, thorough handwashing after, and cleaning any surface raw juices touched.

One thing commonly muddled: "salt water soak" or "vinegar rinse" traditions (common in many cuisines) are sometimes defended as antimicrobial. They modestly reduce surface load at best and do not substitute for cooking; they carry the same splash concern if done under running water in the sink. Not a reason to abandon a culturally meaningful prep step, but don't treat it as a safety measure.

=== Answer 2 ===
Yes, that's right, and it reflects the current advice of both the UK Food Standards Agency and the USDA, which explicitly tell people not to wash raw poultry.

The mechanism is exactly as you describe:

- **Rinsing doesn't kill anything.** Salmonella and Campylobacter aren't removed or killed by cold tap water. Only heat does that — cooking to an internal 74°C / 165°F is what actually makes the chicken safe.
- **Splashing spreads bacteria.** Water droplets off the carcass carry bacteria onto the sink, taps, worktop, and nearby utensils and food. Studies have found contamination spread well beyond the sink itself (spatter has been measured across a surprisingly wide radius). Those surfaces are then the cross-contamination route to ready-to-eat food.

So washing has downside and no upside — that part of your framing is solid and uncontested.

Two calibrations worth adding, because they affect what you actually do about it:

1. **Magnitude.** Washing adds risk, but it's a secondary contributor, not the dominant one. The main cause of poultry-linked food poisoning is undercooking and general cross-contamination (hands, boards, knives). Skipping the rinse is genuinely worth doing, but it's a marginal gain layered on top of the big two: cook it through, and keep raw-chicken contact surfaces separate from everything else.

2. **What replaces washing.** The reason people rinse is usually to remove surface slime or residue for aesthetic/texture reasons, not safety. If you want to do that, pat the chicken dry with paper towel and bin the towel immediately — same cosmetic result, no aerosol.

The claim is accurate. The only correction is one of emphasis — not washing is a real but small improvement, and it doesn't substitute for thorough cooking and separating raw poultry from other food, which are where most of the actual risk lives.
