QUESTION:
I've heard that washing raw chicken in the sink before cooking it actually increases the risk of food poisoning, because it splashes bacteria onto nearby surfaces rather than killing anything — is that right?

=== Answer 1 ===
Yes, that's essentially correct, and it reflects the current guidance from major food-safety authorities including the US CDC and USDA, the UK's Food Standards Agency (FSA), and the NHS. They actively recommend *not* washing raw chicken. Let me lay out the reasoning, and also add a couple of caveats so you have the full picture.

Rinsing chicken under the tap does not remove or kill the bacteria that cause food poisoning — mainly *Campylobacter*, and also *Salmonella*. Water at tap temperature and pressure simply isn't capable of doing that. The only reliable way to kill these pathogens is heat: cooking the chicken thoroughly (to an internal temperature of 74 C / 165 F, until juices run clear and there's no pink meat). Proper cooking destroys the bacteria whether or not you washed the bird first, which means the washing step contributes nothing on the "killing" side of the ledger.

The concern is contamination spread. When water hits the surface of raw chicken, it can splash and aerosolize droplets containing bacteria. Research has shown these droplets can travel and land on surrounding surfaces — the sink and drain, adjacent countertops, utensils, dish sponges/cloths, and other foods left nearby (a salad or bread on the counter is a classic risk, since those won't be cooked afterward to kill anything transferred to them). This cross-contamination is a genuine route to illness. Washing raw chicken carries a real downside and no upside.

- **The splash distances are debated in specifics.** You'll sometimes see dramatic claims like "bacteria splash up to 3 feet / 50–60 cm." The general phenomenon of splash contamination is well established (notably a 2019 USDA study observing home cooks), but exact distances depend heavily on faucet height, water pressure, and sink design. Treat the specific numbers as illustrative rather than precise.
- **Washing isn't the biggest risk factor — undercooking and general cross-contamination are.** Not washing is good advice, but it's a relatively minor lever compared with cooking chicken thoroughly and keeping raw chicken separate from ready-to-eat foods. Don't let "I didn't wash it" create false confidence.
- **Some cultural and culinary practices differ.** Certain traditional preparations involve rinsing poultry (sometimes with salt, vinegar, or lemon). Food-safety agencies still advise against it. The mitigation, if someone does rinse, is to do it in a way that minimizes splash and then thoroughly clean and disinfect the sink and surrounding area with hot soapy water afterward — but the recommended default is simply not to rinse.
- **"Increases risk" is about relative risk, not certainty.** Plenty of people have washed chicken for years without getting sick, because thorough cooking still saves you and splash contamination doesn't always reach food or always transfer enough bacteria to cause illness. The point is that washing shifts the odds in the wrong direction for no benefit — not that it reliably causes food poisoning.

Skip the rinse. Take the chicken straight from packaging to pan/oven. Then focus on the things that actually matter:

1. **Cook it thoroughly** — 74 C / 165 F internal, no pink, clear juices. A meat thermometer is the reliable check.
2. **Prevent cross-contamination** — separate cutting board and utensils for raw chicken, don't let raw chicken or its juices touch ready-to-eat foods.
3. **Wash your hands** with soap and warm water after handling raw chicken, and clean any surfaces or tools that touched it.

If you're patting chicken dry for better browning (a legitimate cooking reason), use paper towels and bin them immediately rather than rinsing under the tap — you get the dryness without the splash.

=== Answer 2 ===
Yes, that's correct, and the reasoning holds up.

The mechanism: cooking chicken to an internal temperature of 74°C / 165°F reliably kills the bacteria that cause concern (Campylobacter, Salmonella). Washing does nothing to reduce that bacterial load — it's not hot enough, there's no antimicrobial action, and rinse water can't penetrate the meat. So the wash provides zero pathogen-reduction benefit; the cooking step already covers that entirely. This is settled food-safety guidance from the USDA, UK NHS/FSA, and others.

The added risk: droplet splash. Rinsing under a tap aerosolizes and splashes contaminated water onto the sink, adjacent surfaces, utensils, and nearby ready-to-eat foods. Anything in that splash zone is now a cross-contamination route to food that won't be cooked afterward — a salad prepped next to the sink, a chopping board, a dish towel. That's the actual poisoning pathway, and it's created rather than mitigated by washing. The splash-transfer mechanism has been demonstrated experimentally, e.g. USDA/NC State studies using fluorescent tracers and marker bacteria.

The "increases the risk of food poisoning" claim is directionally correct, though the magnitude isn't precisely quantified. The absolute increase in illness risk from washing depends on kitchen layout, what else is being prepped nearby, and cleaning habits — a person who washes chicken but keeps a scrupulously clean, isolated sink and cooks everything thoroughly may see little real-world effect. The point is that washing has upside of zero and downside greater than zero, so the expected value is unambiguously negative regardless of how large the downside is in any given kitchen. You lose nothing by skipping it.

Two things worth adding that the "washing" framing can obscure:

- The real controls are the ones doing the work: cook to temperature, and prevent cross-contamination from the raw meat and its packaging/juices generally (separate boards, wash hands and surfaces that touched raw chicken). Not-washing is a minor optimization on top of those; it isn't itself the thing keeping you safe.
- "Don't wash" applies to rinsing under running water specifically. Patting chicken dry with paper towel (then binning the towel and washing hands) is fine and actually improves browning — no splash, no aerosol.

Your understanding is right. Washing raw chicken adds a cross-contamination risk while providing no benefit, because the heat of cooking — not water — is what makes it safe.
