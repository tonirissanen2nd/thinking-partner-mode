Before you confront, separate the problem from your interpretation of his motive. You're seeing a pattern of exclusion; the inference that he's deliberately trying to steal credit is reasonable but not certain — he could be disorganized, working in silos by habit, or genuinely forgetting. **Confrontation is often the wrong move first**, because if you're wrong about intent, you escalate a coordination problem into a personal conflict.

Start with the clearer, lower-risk moves:

**Immediate layer:** Begin copying yourself in on follow-ups to shared work. When he sends something without you, reply to all (not to him) with context or additions that signal your involvement. This works whether he's excluding you on purpose or by oversight, and it's a factual record that doesn't require proving intent.

**Documentation:** If your contributions matter for credit (reviews, promotions, raises), document what you did — dates, scope, outcomes — in a place you can reference. Email summaries to yourself or keep a file. You don't need his acknowledgment if the record is there.

**Direct conversation (not confrontation):** After a pattern, say it plainly but neutrally: "Hey, I've noticed I'm not on the emails for [X project]. I need to be looped in so I can keep track of status and contributions." This flags the behavior without accusing him of malice. If he says he forgot, you have a fix: "Can we set up a rule where I'm CC'd on the initial email?" If he's defensive or dismissive, you have a signal that this is deliberate.

**Escalation:** If the problem persists after you've signaled it, loop in your manager. Frame it as a workflow/documentation issue, not a character attack: "I'm not being included on emails for work I'm contributing to, which makes it hard to track what I've done. Can we clarify the protocol?" Your manager either fixes the process or confirms his behavior is expected for some reason you don't know.

The strongest reason to confront head-on is if you're certain of intent and inaction has concrete costs (a promotion decision looming, a performance review happening). Otherwise, let your visibility and documentation do the work. Confrontation is high-risk — it either forces him to admit malice (rare) or escalates defensiveness — and it's usually unnecessary once you've made the problem visible and documented.
