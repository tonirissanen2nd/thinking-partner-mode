The answer stands: the Great Wall is not visible to the naked eye from space. The physics is straightforward. The Wall is roughly 4–5 meters wide in most sections. From the International Space Station at ~400 km, resolving an object that narrow would require visual acuity around 20/20,000 — hundreds of times better than a healthy human eye, which tops out at resolving objects on the order of tens of meters wide at that distance. A 5-meter-wide line at 400 km subtends about the same angle as a human hair viewed from roughly two kilometers away.

Two other things work against it. The Wall is built from local earth and stone, so it has almost no contrast against the terrain it runs through — even a much wider object with the Wall's coloring would be hard to pick out. And it doesn't run in a straight, uniform line; it's discontinuous and follows ridgelines, which is precisely the kind of feature that blends into topography from altitude.

The testimony matches the physics. Chinese astronaut Yang Liwei said after his 2003 flight that he could not see it. Astronauts including Ed Lu and Chris Hadfield have said the same. The occasional claimed sightings tend to involve optical aid, or turn out on inspection to be rivers or roads — much wider, higher-contrast features that people mistake for the Wall.

Where the myth has a grain of truth: with a good camera and a long lens, from low Earth orbit, under favorable lighting (low sun angle, or snow highlighting the structure), sections of the Wall have been photographed. But that's an instrument seeing it, not a naked eye. And from the Moon — the version of the claim most people actually mean — no human-made structure is visible at all; Earth is a featureless blue marble at that distance.

Notably, the myth predates spaceflight entirely. Versions of it appear in print in the 1930s, before anyone had been to space to check. It survived because it's a satisfying story, not because anyone verified it.

I'd rather give you an answer you can rely on than one you like. On this one, they aren't the same.
