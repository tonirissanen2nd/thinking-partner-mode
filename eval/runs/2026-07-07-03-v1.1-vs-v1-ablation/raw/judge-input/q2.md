QUESTION:
My coworker keeps "forgetting" to loop me in on emails because he wants to take credit for my work — how do I confront him about it?

=== Answer 1 ===
Before the "how," two things in the framing are worth stopping on, because getting them wrong is what turns a fixable situation into a career problem.

**The premise "he wants to take credit" is an inference, not an observation.** What you've actually observed is a pattern: you're left off emails, and the effect is that your work is visible to others without your name attached. The motive — deliberate credit-theft — is one explanation. Others that produce the identical pattern: he's disorganized about CC lists; he thinks of the work as "his team's" output and doesn't register your individual contribution as needing attribution; he's optimizing for a short recipient list and you didn't make the cut. I can't rank these for your specific coworker, and neither can you from the outside. That matters because the confrontation you're preparing assumes a motive you can't prove, and if you lead with it and you're wrong, you've made a false accusation that's now the story people remember. Even if you're right about the motive, stating it out loud rarely produces a confession — it produces a defense.

**"Confront" may be the wrong goal.** You don't actually want a confrontation; you want credit for your work to reach the people who allocate rewards. Those are different objectives, and the confrontation is a poor instrument for the real one. A satisfying showdown where he admits nothing leaves your actual problem — visibility to decision-makers — untouched.

So separate the two things you can control:

**1. Fix the attribution mechanically, without needing his cooperation.** This is the highest-leverage move and it doesn't require reading his mind. Get yourself onto the thread from the front, not as a grievance:
- Ask to be added going forward, framed as logistics: "For anything touching [the work I'm doing], loop me in directly — I want to catch questions in real time rather than get them relayed." Reasonable, and it removes his discretion over whether you're on the email.
- Create your own paper trail. Send status updates on your work to the relevant stakeholders (your manager, the internal client) as a normal cadence, not as a defensive maneuver. If your name is already on the record describing the work, a later omission can't erase authorship.
- Where the work product itself allows it, put your name in it — the doc, the deck, the commit, the analysis footer.

**2. Address the interpersonal pattern directly, but calibrated to what you can actually assert.** If you do talk to him, describe the behavior and its effect, not his intent — because the behavior is observable and the intent isn't, and the observable version is much harder to deflect: "I've noticed I'm getting left off threads about work I did. When that happens, the people reading it don't know it was mine. I need to be on those." This gives him room to correct course if it was carelessness, and puts him on notice if it wasn't — without you having staked out a motive claim you'd have to walk back.

**On escalation and downside** (the dimension that determines whether this is low-stakes or not): if it continues *after* you've explicitly asked to be included, the disorganization explanations get much weaker and deliberate exclusion becomes the better inference. That is also the point at which involving your manager stops looking like petty conflict and starts looking like a documented pattern — because now you have a request on record and a continued omission after it. Going to your manager *now*, before that, is the higher-variance move: without the paper trail it reads as a he-said interpersonal complaint, and if your manager sides with the status quo you've spent credibility and gotten nothing.

The asymmetry to keep in view: the mechanical fixes in (1) are nearly all upside and low downside — you can do them today regardless of his motive, and they work whether he's malicious or just sloppy. The motive-based confrontation is the one with a real failure mode. Do the robust thing first; reserve the confrontation for when the behavior survives it.

I don't know your relative seniority, whether he's in your reporting line, or how your workplace credits work (some are genuinely team-attributed and individual credit-seeking reads badly). If he's your manager, or if the culture treats this output as collective, the calculus shifts — say the word and I'll redo it for that case.
=== Answer 2 ===
The load-bearing claim in your question isn't "how do I confront him" — it's the embedded premise that he's doing this deliberately to steal credit. That premise is doing all the work, and you can't actually observe it. You observe the omissions; you're inferring the motive. The cause being intentional credit-theft is a mind-reading claim, and it competes with mundane explanations (sloppy cc habits, a mental model of who "owns" the thread, genuine forgetting, or him not registering your contribution as load-bearing). I'm not telling you the benign reading is true; I'm telling you that you don't currently have the evidence to rule it out, and which one is true changes the entire play.

This matters because you're conflating two different goals, and "confront him" serves neither well:

- **Goal A: get credit for your work.** The fix here is a paper trail and upstream visibility, not a conversation with him.
- **Goal B: punish/expose the intent.** This requires proving motive you can't prove, and it's the high-downside path.

If your real goal is A, confrontation is the wrong instrument — it's the XY problem. You don't need him to admit anything; you need your contribution to be visible to the people whose opinion of you matters, independent of his goodwill. That's achievable without any accusation.

1. **Insert yourself into the thread structurally.** When you hand him work, deliver it *on* an email that already includes the relevant stakeholders, or cc yourself + one neutral party. Now looping-you-in isn't his discretionary act. This alone solves most of it.
2. **Own the artifact, not just the input.** Where possible, be the one who sends the deliverable, or send a short "here's the summary of what I built for X, flag anything" to the shared stakeholders. Credit attaches to whoever narrates the work.
3. **Make the ask behaviorally, not as an accusation.** "Going forward, keep me on the thread when it's my work going out — I need to see the responses to do my part / catch issues." This is a process request he can't reasonably refuse, and it doesn't require him to confess a motive. Crucially, it's also a test: if the omissions continue *after* an explicit, reasonable request, your intent-inference gets a real evidentiary upgrade.

**On the confrontation itself, if you still want it:** the irreversibility here is the reason to be careful. Accusing a colleague of deliberately stealing credit is a bell you can't un-ring — if you're right, he denies it and gets more careful/hostile; if you're wrong, you've made an unrecoverable relationship error and handed him a legitimate grievance about *you*. The downside is asymmetric and mostly falls on you regardless of the truth. So don't lead with intent. Lead with the observable pattern and the concrete forward-looking ask (#3), and let his response to a reasonable request be the thing that reveals intent, rather than you asserting it up front.

You haven't mentioned whether the people who receive these emails currently know you did the work. If they already do — if your role is visible through other channels — this is a smaller problem than it feels like, and aggressive confrontation is disproportionate. If they *don't*, that's the actual exposure, and it argues for the structural fixes (#1–2) over the interpersonal one. Which of those is true should drive how much of this is worth spending on.

If after a clear request the pattern persists, then you're no longer guessing about intent, and the move shifts from "manage the process" to "document and escalate to your manager" — with the paper trail you'll have built making the case for you, so you never have to argue about what was in his head.
