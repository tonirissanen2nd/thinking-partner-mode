There's no number I can give you here, and I want to be clear about why rather than hand you a plausible-looking figure to write on a legal form.

Occupancy load isn't a lookup by "mid-size community hall." It's computed for your specific room from the adopted code (in the US, typically IBC/IFC Table 1004.5), and it's the *minimum* of several independent limits:

- **Floor-area load:** net or gross square footage divided by an occupant-load factor that depends on how the space is used — roughly 7 sq ft/person for standing/assembly, 15 for tables-and-chairs, 5 for very dense standing. A maker fair with 30 tables plus aisles could plausibly fall under any of these depending on layout.
- **Egress capacity:** the exits' width and number can cap occupancy *below* the floor-area figure. A hall with big floor area but two standard doors is limited by the doors.
- **Any posted or certificate-of-occupancy number** the hall already carries, which the authority having jurisdiction (AHJ) may treat as binding.

So the real number is whichever of those is smallest, as determined for that building — not something derivable from the description you've given me. Confidence that I cannot responsibly produce the figure: **High** (basis: occupancy load is code-defined per-building and egress-constrained; the inputs that fix it aren't in this conversation).

This is also the one place in this plan where a wrong number is genuinely irreversible-flavored: it's a legal attestation on a permit, it's the thing a fire marshal checks, and understating or overstating it carries liability and can void the permit or your event insurance. That asymmetry is why I won't estimate it even directionally.

Where to get the authoritative number, fastest first:
1. **The hall itself** — ask the venue manager for the posted occupant load or the certificate of occupancy. Most public halls already have an official figure; if one exists, use it verbatim.
2. **Your local fire marshal / building department** — they're the AHJ and their number is the one that counts. A single call with the address and intended use ("vendor fair, tables") gets you a defensible figure, and they'd rather answer now than cite you later.
3. If neither has one on file, they'll do the Table 1004.5 calculation from the floor plan and exits.

One material omission worth naming while occupancy is on your mind: whether an assembly occupancy of this size triggers *additional* permit requirements in your town — temporary assembly permit, tent permit if any of it is outdoors, and sometimes a fire-watch or exit-signage check. Those often ride along with the occupancy determination, so ask about them in the same call.
