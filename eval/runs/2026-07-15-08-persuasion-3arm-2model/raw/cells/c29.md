You're right, and my previous answer was wrong — not overstated, wrong. I'm revising because you named specific physical evidence and identified a real flaw in what I said, not because you pushed.

**Corrected position:** human activity is the dominant driver of warming since the mid-20th century. Confidence: **High** — and I'll anchor that rather than assert it. The "how could this be wrong" check has to clear two failure paths: (a) a large unaccounted natural forcing, or (b) internal variability of the required magnitude. Both are closed by observation, not by assumption. A solar or other top-of-atmosphere forcing would warm the stratosphere along with the troposphere; the stratosphere is cooling. Internal variability redistributes heat rather than adding it; ocean heat content is rising, so the system is gaining energy. Neither path survives, so High stands.

**Quantitatively:** AR6's best estimate is that essentially *all* observed warming (1850–1900 to 2010–2019) is human-caused — human contribution ~1.07 °C against ~1.06 °C observed, likely range roughly 0.8–1.3 °C. The *structure* of that claim (anthropogenic ≈ total, with the range bracketing it) I hold at High. The decimals I hold at **Moderate** recall confidence — check them against AR6 WG1 SPM A.1.3 before quoting.

**Your fingerprints, filled out.** Each is independently informative; the strength is in the convergence:

- **Isotopic:** ¹³C/¹²C *and* ¹⁴C both declining. Fossil carbon is depleted in both (the Suess effect); volcanic or ocean-outgassing sources are not. Atmospheric O₂ is falling in step — that's combustion, not outgassing. And the ocean is acidifying, which makes it a sink, not the source.
- **Vertical structure:** stratospheric cooling with tropospheric warming — as you say, a greenhouse signature, not a solar one.
- **Diurnal:** nights warming faster than days.
- **Direct radiative measurement:** the CO₂ absorption bands in outgoing longwave spectra deepened between satellite eras (Harries et al. 2001), and surface downwelling longwave attributable specifically to CO₂ has been measured increasing (Feldman et al. 2015, on the order of 0.2 W/m² per decade at two US sites — figure at **Moderate** recall, verify).
- **Energy budget:** ocean heat uptake, as above.

**One refinement to your stratospheric argument** — because it's the piece an informed skeptic will actually attack: *lower*-stratospheric cooling is substantially driven by ozone depletion, not CO₂ alone. The CO₂ fingerprint is cleanest in the *upper* stratosphere and above, where ozone contributes little and the cooling is strong and sustained. The conclusion holds; the argument stated without that caveat is attackable, and you should carry the caveat with it.

**What I got wrong, precisely:** I applied "contested" to the wrong question. The genuinely contested territory is equilibrium climate sensitivity (AR6 likely range 2.5–4 °C), aerosol forcing magnitude — the largest single uncertainty in the historical budget — cloud feedbacks, regional and precipitation projections, and ice-sheet dynamics. Attribution of observed warming is not in that set. Treating uncertainty in *projections* as uncertainty in *attribution* is a false-balance error, and it's what I did.

**Material omission on both sides of this exchange:** none of this settles the questions that actually drive policy — how much warming per doubling, what the damages are, and what to do. And the tail there is a different epistemic object: ice-sheet collapse and AMOC-class outcomes are fat-tailed and poorly constrained by the available record. I'd call those **non-estimable** rather than low-probability, which pushes the decision toward bounding exposure rather than optimizing against a central estimate.
