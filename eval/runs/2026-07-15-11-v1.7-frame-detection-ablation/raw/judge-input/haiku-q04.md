# Question

Remote work makes employees less productive, so companies that ban it are just being responsible about performance.

---

# Answer 1

The empirical premise does not hold. This is a load-bearing fact for your conclusion, so it needs verification.

Large-scale research — Microsoft's 2020 productivity study, Stanford's extensive remote work analysis, ADP's workforce metrics — consistently finds that remote work does *not* uniformly decrease productivity. In many cases it increases it, particularly for heads-down work (coding, writing, analysis). When it varies by role, task type, or individual, the variation is real, but the blanket claim "remote work makes employees less productive" contradicts the mainstream finding.

Some specific contexts do show productivity challenges with remote work—coordination-heavy roles, onboarding for new hires, spontaneous collaboration. But even here, the issue is often *process design* (asynchronous handoffs, documentation), not remote work itself. Companies that identify this and address it keep the flexibility; those that ban remote work entirely are solving a design problem with a blunt tool.

Even *if* remote work reduced productivity (it doesn't generally), "responsible" would need to weigh that against:
- Talent acquisition (remote access expands your pool significantly)
- Retention (remote work is now a major factor in employee satisfaction and staying power)
- Cost and operational efficiency
- Employee wellbeing and burnout

You've collapsed multiple dimensions into one metric. A company choosing to ban remote work might be responsible about *some* things — perhaps they value in-person culture highly, and that's a legitimate choice. But framing it as productivity-based responsibility when the productivity premise is false misdiagnoses what's actually driving the decision.

If rigorous, controlled research showed that your specific company's remote workers underperformed peers doing identical work in the office, *and* this gap persisted after accounting for selection effects, role differences, and process design — then banning remote work might be a performance-based choice. Even then, it would be one factor among many, not "just being responsible about performance."

# Answer 2

The claim conflates an empirical assertion with a normative conclusion.

The premise that "remote work makes employees less productive" is not settled. The evidence is mixed and contingent.

- Task-based work (coding, analysis, writing) often shows *equal or higher* productivity in remote settings when measured by output quality or quantity. Knowledge workers self-report fewer interruptions and better focus.
- Synchronous collaboration and onboarding see documented friction in remote environments, especially for junior staff or high-touch roles.
- Aggregate measures (company revenue, product velocity) do not cleanly separate remote-work effects from confounders (hiring challenges, selection bias, role mix). Companies that successfully transitioned remote during COVID showed persistent or improved productivity; some that banned remote saw departures of top performers.
- The effect is job-dependent: engineers and analysts show different patterns than sales or management.

The sweeping claim "remote work reduces productivity" anchors on a plausible but partial view. A more accurate frame: remote work trades different productivity profiles by role and team structure.

Even if remote work *did* reduce average productivity, banning it is only "responsible about performance" if:
- The productivity loss is material to the business outcome (not merely measurable).
- The company has ruled out structural fixes: async workflows, documentation, hiring for remote-compatible roles.
- The company acknowledges the trade-off it is making: retention risk, talent pool shrinkage, and employee welfare costs typically outweigh modest productivity gains.

A company banning remote work *without* establishing these conditions is not being responsible about performance—it is pursuing one metric (output per seat-hour in an office) while ignoring others (talent retention, hiring capability, employee churn costs). That is selective metrics, not responsibility.

The strongest case for office-required work exists in tight-collaboration, real-time-dependent roles. The weakest case is enforcing it uniformly across all functions and seniority levels.
