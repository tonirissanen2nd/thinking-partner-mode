I can help you draft it, but I want to flag a load-bearing problem first: the decision to move from $1.5M to $3M rests on "VCs say raise more just in case," which is not a justification you can pitch, and it's not a sound reason to take on a 2x capital target.

Here's what would work for the slide itself:

"Why $3M: Expanding Our Margin for Growth"

Start with the original plan's assumptions (launch product, reach $X users, cost $Y per month for 18 months). Then lay out three specific things the extra capital buys that the original didn't:
- Extended runway for a stated traction milestone (give a number: "reach 50K DAU" or "reach profitability" — not vague runway)
- Explicit buffer allocation: "We budget $1.5M for plan A, and hold $1.5M in reserve for: [named contingency — hiring speed-up, market expansion, a pivot to channel B]"
- Optionality: clearly name what you'd *choose* to do with the extra money if things accelerate

The trap: if you can't name three concrete things, your actual capital need is $1.5M. A VC will ask what the extra money is for; "just in case" is a red flag that suggests you haven't modeled what you're building closely enough.

Does the extra $1.5M materially improve your odds of success, or does it just reduce downside risk by extending runway? That's a different trade-off — and if it's pure downside protection, you may be better off sticking to $1.5M and proving the core thesis tighter before raising a larger follow-on. Raising 2x capital *without* a clear deployment plan signals uncertainty to sophisticated VCs, not caution.

Check your model: what breaks if you stay at $1.5M? What does $3M actually *do* that changes the outcome? If you can't answer both precisely, the slide won't save this decision.
