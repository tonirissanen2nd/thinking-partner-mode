For most small nonprofits, the marginal dollar goes to retention. But "better" here depends on where you sit on your own retention curve, and the honest answer is that this is a question you should be answering with your own numbers, not a general rule. Let me give you the general case and then the conditions that flip it.

**Why retention usually wins the marginal-dollar comparison**

The core argument is unit economics, and it's strong (this is an argument from structure, not a citation of a single study — treat it as such):

- Acquiring a donor costs money and typically loses money on the first gift. You recover the acquisition cost only through subsequent gifts. So an acquired donor who never gives again is often a net loss.
- Retention compounds. A retained donor costs far less to solicit again (you already have the relationship, the contact data, the giving history), and retained donors tend to give more over time and are the pool from which major gifts and bequests eventually come.
- Sector retention rates are chronically low — first-year donor retention is commonly cited in the roughly 20–30% range, repeat-donor retention closer to 60%. If your first-year retention is near the low end, you are pouring acquired donors into a leaky bucket, and acquisition spend partially subsidizes that leak.

Those retention figures are widely reported (e.g., Fundraising Effectiveness Project-type benchmarks) but I'd flag them as *Moderate* confidence for your purposes: they're sector aggregates, definitions of "retention" vary, and your organization's numbers can diverge sharply from the mean. Verify against your own donor file before betting on them.

**The frame problem: "acquisition vs. retention" is a false binary at the margin**

The two aren't independent, and treating them as a straight either/or will mislead you:

1. **You cannot retain donors you never acquired.** An organization that spends nothing on acquisition eventually retains its way down to zero as natural attrition (death, moving, life changes) outpaces its shrinking base. Retention is a multiplier on an acquisition-built base, not a substitute for it.
2. **The real question is marginal ROI on each, at your current spend levels** — where does the next dollar earn more? That's the decision-relevant framing, and it's answerable with your data.

**The decision rule I'd actually use**

Estimate, from your own donor database:
- First-year and multi-year retention rates
- Average donor lifetime value (LTV)
- Cost to acquire a donor (CAC)
- Cost to run a retention/stewardship touch, and its measured lift on renewal

Then: **if your first-year retention is below roughly 40%, the marginal dollar almost certainly belongs in retention** — you have a leaky-bucket problem and acquisition is subsidizing churn. Fixing retention also raises the LTV/CAC ratio, which makes future acquisition profitable. If your retention is already healthy (say 60%+ repeat) and your base is shrinking or stagnant in absolute donor count, the constraint has shifted to the top of the funnel and acquisition earns the marginal dollar.

Confidence that retention is the better default for a *typical* small nonprofit: **Moderate-to-High** — the unit-economics logic is robust and points one direction. Confidence that it's right *for you specifically*: **Low without your numbers**, because the answer hinges on where you are on your own retention curve, which I can't see.

**Ruin/irreversibility note (the asymmetry that should tilt you):** the two failure modes are not symmetric. Over-investing in acquisition while ignoring retention burns cash on donors who lapse — painful but recoverable; you can redirect spend next cycle. Letting your donor base atrophy through neglect is slower and harder to reverse, because rebuilding lapsed relationships and lost institutional donor knowledge is costlier than maintaining them. That asymmetry is a second, independent reason to favor retention as the default when you're unsure.

**Two things this answer doesn't resolve, that you should weigh:**
- **Organizational stage.** A brand-new nonprofit with a tiny base is a genuine exception — it may rationally over-index on acquisition simply to reach a viable scale, accepting near-term losses. The retention-first default assumes you already have a base worth defending.
- **Mission-growth goals vs. financial efficiency.** If your objective is rapid expansion of reach or constituency (not just revenue efficiency), acquisition may be worth funding past the point of narrow financial optimality. That's a values/strategy call, not a math error.

Bottom line: default to retention, but treat that as a hypothesis to confirm against your first-year retention rate and LTV/CAC — those two numbers will tell you which way your marginal dollar actually points, and they're worth pulling before you allocate.
