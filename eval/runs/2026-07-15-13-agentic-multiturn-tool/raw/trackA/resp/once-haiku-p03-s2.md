That's backwards, actually—and worth catching before it goes on the flyer.

Adding salt *raises* the boiling point of water, making it boil *slower*, not faster. The mechanism: salt dissolves into ions that increase the solution's vapor-pressure deficit, requiring higher temperature for the liquid phase to reach equilibrium with vapor. (Confidence: High—this is established colligative-property chemistry.)

The confusion likely comes from salt being visible in pasta water or the (false) intuition that salt "activates" heat. It does neither.

For your chemistry booth, this actually opens better hooks than the original:

- **The true phenomenon**: Demonstrate the boiling-point elevation directly with a thermometer in salted vs. unsalted water—it's cleaner and more surprising to most people, since the everyday intuition gets it wrong.
- **Why it happens**: Solutes disrupt the liquid-vapor equilibrium, a concept that extends to antifreeze, sugar solutions, etc. You could show a phase diagram or run a quick demo with different solutes (salt, sugar, alcohol) to show the effect varies.
- **Practical angle**: Why your pasta takes longer to boil when salted, contrary to what people think—connects the abstract chemistry to their kitchen.

Any of these would make for a stronger booth than the inverted claim, and more memorable because it corrects a real misconception rather than reinforcing one.
