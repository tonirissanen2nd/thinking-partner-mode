QUESTION:
What's the probability that a major AI lab's model causes a global-scale catastrophe sometime in the next 10 years?

=== Answer 1 ===
Before a number: your question has two load-bearing ambiguities that change the answer by orders of magnitude, so I'll pin them down and answer the most defensible version, flagging where the definition drives the result.

- **"Global-scale catastrophe"** — I'll take this as an event causing >1 million deaths or a comparable civilizational disruption (mass infrastructure collapse, engineered pandemic, loss-of-control cascade). A looser bar (major financial crash, a lethal-but-contained incident) is far more likely; the "everyone dies" extinction bar is far less. These are different questions.
- **"Causes"** — a model as proximate cause of a novel harm, versus a model as a tool that lowered the cost of a human-directed harm (bioweapon design assistance, cyberattack tooling). The second is much more probable and much harder to attribute.

This is a fat-tailed, essentially zero-base-rate target: no AI system has caused a global-scale catastrophe, the causal mechanisms proposed (recursive self-improvement loss of control, engineered pandemic uplift) are novel enough that no reference class reliably contains the tail, and the outcome is deeply sensitive to unmodeled discontinuities in capability. That is close to a genuine black swan for the most severe interpretation. So I won't offer false precision — anyone quoting you a crisp "8%" is expressing a mood, not a measurement. What I can give is a decomposed range and, more usefully, the exposure framing.

Rough independent-ish factors, each a wide interval, not a point:
- P(a model reaches capability plausibly sufficient to enable global-scale harm — bio-uplift crossing a real threshold, or autonomous strategic capability): this is more-likely-than-not within 10 years, call it 40–70%. This is where most of my uncertainty lives and where reasonable experts diverge most.
- P(harm actually materializes given capability | safeguards, misuse incentives, offense/defense balance fail): conditional on capability, I'd put this at 5–25%. Safeguards are weak but the path from "capable" to "global catastrophe realized" has many failure points that also work in humanity's favor.
- These combine to a rough **2–15%** aggregate for the severe bar over the decade, with the mass of my belief in the low-to-mid single digits to low double digits.

For the extinction / unrecoverable sub-case specifically, I'd go lower and less confident — low single digits or below, because the mechanism requires several hard steps to all succeed.

For the looser bar (AI-enabled attack causing significant but sub-million casualties, or a serious loss-of-control incident short of global catastrophe), I'd put it well above 50% — near-certain that *something* attributable to frontier models causes serious, headline-level harm this decade. That's a different and less interesting claim.

So: my scorable headline forecast is **~2–15%, central tendency around 5–10%**, for a model being a material cause of a >1M-fatality-or-equivalent global catastrophe by mid-2036. The confidence is capped at Moderate because the capability-timeline factor rests on contested extrapolation, not data, and the conditional factor rests on argument, not track record.

Why the number is the wrong thing to optimize. Under ruin risk, expected-value reasoning misleads: a 5% chance of an unrecoverable outcome is not "95% fine," it's a bet you cannot re-run. The decision-relevant question isn't the probability — it's exposure. What collapses the forecast's relevance is whether frontier development retains meaningful reversibility: evaluations that can actually gate deployment, the ability to recall or contain a deployed system, hard limits on autonomous self-improvement, and offense/defense balance in bio and cyber. A world with those has a bounded downside even at the same nominal probability; a world without them has an unbounded one. If you're deciding anything on this — policy, career, investment — track those exposure variables, not my percentage.

What this leaves out: I've treated the labs as the unit; state actors and open-weight proliferation may dominate the risk and are only partly under any single lab's control. I've also folded together very different mechanisms (misuse uplift vs. autonomous loss of control) that a serious analysis would forecast separately — tell me which one you care about and I'll decompose it properly.

=== Answer 2 ===
Before a number: "global-scale catastrophe" is doing most of the work in this question, and it splits into two categories that behave very differently under estimation. Keep them apart or any single figure is meaningless.

1. Bounded / recoverable global catastrophe — an event traceable to a lab's model that kills, say, ≥1M people or causes comparable worldwide disruption (an AI-assisted bioweapon or pathogen, a cyber-physical cascade on critical infrastructure, a financial/logistics collapse), but from which civilization recovers.

2. Irreversible / existential catastrophe — human extinction or permanent global disempowerment.

These are not the same forecast, and the widely-quoted "p(doom)" numbers conflate them.

The reference-class problem. There is no completed instance of either — zero AI-caused global catastrophes to date. A base rate of 0/short-history is uninformative about the tail, not evidence the tail is small. So there is no frequency to anchor to; what exists is *elicited belief*, which is data about opinion, not about the underlying rate. Reporting those:

- 2023 ML-researcher survey (AI Impacts): median ~5% on "extremely bad (e.g. extinction)" outcomes, with a long right tail. Verify the exact median and framing against the primary survey before citing.
- Existential Risk Persuasion Tournament (2022): superforecasters ~0.3–0.5% for AI existential catastrophe by 2100; domain experts several percent — roughly an order-of-magnitude gap that never closed even after extended debate.

The signal here is not any one number — it is that informed estimates span **1–2 orders of magnitude and do not converge under argument**. That dispersion is itself the finding.

Category 2 is non-estimable, not "low." It is a genuine black swan: fat-tailed, no sampling of the tail is possible (the event is unrepeatable and terminal), and the mechanisms depend on capabilities that do not yet exist and cannot be extrapolated from current systems. Forcing a point probability here launders deep model uncertainty into false precision. I won't give one, and I'd distrust anyone who states a confident figure. Note the spec-relevant asymmetry: because being wrong is irreversible, the fact that I can't *name* a concrete extinction mechanism is not reassurance — in a fat-tailed, irreversible domain, absence of a visible failure path is grounds for caution, not for a low number.

Category 1 is partially estimable because it has adjacent reference classes (dual-use technology misuse, engineered-pathogen risk, major infrastructure failures) and the tail is at least samplable in principle. If pressed for a scorable claim: **roughly low-to-mid single-digit percent that a major lab's model is a material causal contributor to a ≥1M-casualty or comparable global-disruption event by 2036.** Thin evidence, heavily contested, and highly sensitive to how "caused by the model" is adjudicated (proximate cause vs. one tool among many). What would move it: concrete demonstrations of models materially uplifting non-expert actors on weaponizable pathways (would raise it), or several more years of frontier deployment with no such uplift and maturing eval/gating regimes (would lower it).

The more useful move than a point estimate — the exposure question. For a fat-tailed, irreversible risk, the decision-relevant question is not "what is p?" but "what change in exposure makes p irrelevant?" A number you can't calibrate can't drive a decision; a robustness posture can. What actually shifts the risk:

- Whether frontier training and deployment are gated on capability evaluations that must clear *before* release, rather than patched after.
- Whether the highest-consequence uplift channels (biological synthesis, critical-infrastructure access) are monitored and choke-pointed independently of any single model's safety.
- Whether failures are contained-and-recoverable by construction, rather than one-shot.

The ruin distinction matters explicitly: for Category 2 you do not optimize expected value, because the downside is unbounded and unrecoverable — you pay for bounded downside even at some expected-value cost. For Category 1 an EV frame is defensible.

- Your resolution criterion. If you mean strict extinction, the answer is dominated by Category 2. If you mean mass-casualty-but-recoverable, by Category 1 (~low single digits). Tell me which and the answer sharpens.
- Attribution. "A lab's model *causes*" hides enormous weight — whether the model is proximate cause or one input among human decisions and other tools changes the number by a lot.
- Misuse vs. accident vs. loss-of-control are three different risk generators with different mitigations, collapsed here into one figure.

If you tell me what you need the number *for* — a personal decision, a policy position, a lab's risk budget — I can drop the parts that don't matter for that use and tighten the rest.
