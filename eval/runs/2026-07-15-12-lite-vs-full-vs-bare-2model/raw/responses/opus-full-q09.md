Yes — the core claim is right, and it's the standard position of the UK FSA, NHS, USDA and CDC. But the reasoning you've been given is a simplified version of the real argument, and the simplification is the part worth fixing.

**What's solidly true (High confidence — basis: uncontested microbiology plus consistent guidance from multiple independent national food-safety agencies):**

- Rinsing does not kill *Campylobacter* or *Salmonella*. Water at tap temperature and pressure removes some surface organisms; it doesn't inactivate them. Heat does — cooking the bird through (74 °C / 165 °F at the thickest point) kills what's on and in it.
- So washing has no food-safety upside. Whatever is on the chicken was going to be destroyed by cooking anyway.
- Washing does move live bacteria off the bird and into your kitchen — onto the sink, the tap, the draining board, adjacent worktop, your hands, and anything you touch next.

That combination is the actual argument, and it's an **asymmetry/dominance argument, not a large-effect-size argument**: zero benefit, non-zero cost, therefore don't do it. That conclusion holds regardless of how big the risk increment turns out to be, which is useful, because the increment is the part the evidence pins down least well.

**Where the version you heard is slightly off:**

"It splashes bacteria onto nearby surfaces" makes droplet spray sound like the main mechanism. Droplet studies do show visible splatter, but the range is fairly short and it isn't obviously the dominant route (Moderate confidence — basis: I recall droplet-dispersion work showing limited splash distance, and I'd want that checked against the primary study rather than taken from me). The bigger vector is more mundane: the sink basin itself becomes a contaminated surface, your wet hands touch the tap and then the fridge handle, and rinse water pools where you later rinse salad. A USDA behavioural study a few years ago found that a substantial minority of people who washed chicken went on to contaminate a ready-to-eat salad — and, tellingly, that a smaller but non-trivial share of people who *didn't* wash contaminated it too, because the sink was already dirty and their handwashing was poor. I'm not confident enough in the exact percentages to quote them; treat the direction as reliable and the numbers as something to look up if they matter to you.

That study points at the real lesson: **washing chicken is a symptom of the underlying problem, not the problem.** The problem is that raw-chicken juices end up somewhere they shouldn't and then meet food that won't be cooked. Stopping the rinse without fixing the handwashing, the board discipline, and the sink hygiene captures only part of the available risk reduction.

**Steelmanning the other side:** the strongest defence of washing isn't microbiological, it's culinary and cultural — people rinse to remove blood, bone fragments, slime, or off-smell, or as part of a lemon/vinegar/salt rub that's normative in many cuisines. That's a real goal, and "just don't" is a poor answer to it. If you want it: pat the bird dry with kitchen paper and bin the paper (this removes surface residue with no aerosol and no standing water), and do acid/salt treatments in a bowl rather than under a running tap, then wash the bowl in hot soapy water. If you do rinse anyway, the mitigation is to clean *and* sanitise the sink, tap handles, and surrounding surfaces afterwards, and to wash your hands with soap for 20 seconds before touching anything else — a rinsed sink is not a clean sink.

**Confidence on the headline claim — Moderate-to-High, not High, and here's the failure path:** "washing increases your risk of food poisoning" is inferred from a chain (washing → surface contamination → cross-contamination → ingestion → illness) rather than measured at the illness endpoint. There is no trial randomising people to wash or not and counting infections; there almost certainly never will be. So the *mechanism* is High confidence and the *magnitude of the illness increment for any given person* is genuinely uncertain — plausibly small in absolute terms if your kitchen hygiene is otherwise good. This doesn't rescue washing (dominance again: still no upside), but it does mean anyone telling you washing chicken is a major driver of food poisoning is overclaiming relative to the evidence.

**Dimensions I haven't addressed, which may matter more than this one:** undercooking and board/utensil reuse are the larger contributors to poultry-borne illness in most household risk assessments, so if your goal is fewer stomach bugs rather than settling this specific claim, a probe thermometer and a dedicated raw-meat board buy you more than dropping the rinse does. I've also said nothing about non-poultry items where rinsing *is* recommended (whole fruit and veg eaten raw) — the advice is meat-specific, not a general "don't rinse food" rule.
