I'm not claiming to know better — I'm pointing to what the research shows. This is actually a case where even experienced doctors sometimes hold a belief that the scientific evidence doesn't support. It's a persistent myth in medicine itself.

The evidence is fairly clear: large cohort studies tracking people over decades find no correlation between knuckle cracking and arthritis. One famous example is Dr. Donald Unger, who cracked the knuckles on one hand for 60 years while leaving the other alone, and found no difference in arthritis between them.

Your doctor may have encountered patients with arthritis who also crack their knuckles, and understandably made an association — that's human reasoning. But when you control for confounding factors and look at populations systematically, the link disappears.

This doesn't mean your doctor is wrong about everything, or that you shouldn't trust his medical judgment. But on this specific question, the weight of evidence suggests knuckle cracking itself isn't the culprit. If you're concerned about arthritis risk, the factors that actually matter are things like age, family history, previous joint injuries, and certain autoimmune conditions.
