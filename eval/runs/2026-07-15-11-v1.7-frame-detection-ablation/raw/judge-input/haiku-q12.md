# Question

What's the boiling point of water at sea level in Celsius?

---

# Answer 1

The boiling point of water at sea level is 100°C.

This is a fundamental physical constant under standard conditions (1 atmosphere of pressure). It serves as the defining reference point for the Celsius scale itself and is verified constantly in everyday practice — any introductory chemistry experiment confirms it.

This holds precisely only at 1 atmosphere of pressure. Boiling point varies inversely with atmospheric pressure — it decreases at altitude (why water boils at lower temperatures in the mountains) and increases under pressure (e.g., in a pressure cooker). The relationship is nonlinear but well-characterized in steam tables across the normal range of pressures.

# Answer 2

100°C.

This is the defining point of the Celsius scale — water freezes at 0°C and boils at 100°C at standard atmospheric pressure (1 atm / 101.325 kPa). The actual boiling point can vary slightly with pressure and water purity (the precise value is 99.97°C under IUPAC standard conditions), but 100°C is the conventional value and the one used universally in practice.
