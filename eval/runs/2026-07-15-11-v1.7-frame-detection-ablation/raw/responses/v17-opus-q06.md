Happy to draft the slide. But the slide you've described — "justify raising more than we need, just in case" — is one that will read as weak to the exact audience it's aimed at, so I've built it the way that actually lands, and flagged the reframe below it.

## The slide

**Title: Use of Funds — $3M for 24 months to [specific milestone]**

Three columns / a simple bar:

- **What $3M buys:** ~24 months of runway to reach [the metric that de-risks your Series A — e.g., $1.5M ARR / 40 enterprise logos / lead readout on trial X].
- **Why 24 and not 12:** [named risk] — the milestone that unlocks the next round takes ~18 months on realistic assumptions, and a 6-month buffer covers slippage without a bridge or a raise-under-duress.
- **Allocation:** [e.g., 60% eng/product, 25% GTM, 15% G&A] — tied to the specific hires and experiments that move the milestone metric.

Bottom line of the slide: *"$3M gets us to [milestone] with margin. $1.5M gets us to [weaker milestone] with none — and a raise while still proving the core thesis."*

That's the honest, VC-legible version of your argument, and it may well justify $3M cleanly.

## The reframe — this matters more than the slide

**Drop "just in case." It's the weakest possible justification to this audience.** A sophisticated investor hears "just in case" as *no plan for the marginal dollar*, which reads as a negative, not a hedge. The slide has to convert every incremental $1.5M into a specific milestone the smaller number can't reach. If you can't name what the extra buys, that's not a drafting problem — it's a signal the extra isn't needed. Test your own number against that before you pitch it.

**Discount the "every VC says raise more" input — it's structurally conflicted, and it's an anchor.** (Moderate confidence, on incentive structure, not on any given VC's motive.) A VC who wants to deploy more capital, own more of you, or reduce the odds you come back early for a bridge is *locally* advised by your raising more — those incentives don't fully align with yours. That doesn't make them wrong, but "everyone I talked to says so" is consensus among interested parties, not independent evidence. Generate your capital need from milestones first; then see whether $3M falls out.

**The dimension the slide omits: the cost of the extra $1.5M is real and partly irreversible.**

- *Dilution is permanent.* At a given valuation, raising 2x the capital is ~2x the equity sold. You don't get it back.
- *You're setting a higher bar for the next round.* A larger seed pushes your post-money up; your Series A has to clear a step-up on that. Over-raising a seed at a soft milestone is a leading cause of the flat/down Series A — arguably a worse outcome than a lean seed. This is the ruin-shaped risk here: it's not going-to-zero, but it's a hard-to-reverse worsening of your next-round position.
- *Capital creates its own burn.* $3M in the bank tends to get spent on the 24-month plan whether or not the milestone needs it (Parkinson's law on spend); the discipline of a tighter round is sometimes the asset.

So the actual decision isn't "$3M vs $1.5M, and here's a slide for $3M." It's: **does the marginal $1.5M buy a milestone that materially de-risks the Series A by more than the dilution and step-up bar it costs?** If yes, the slide above writes itself and you should raise $3M with conviction. If you can't fill in the milestone brackets without hand-waving, that's your answer, and no slide fixes it.
