# Question

I've heard that washing raw chicken in the sink before cooking it actually increases the risk of food poisoning, because it splashes bacteria onto nearby surfaces rather than killing anything — is that right?

---

# Answer 1

Yes, you're essentially correct. Food safety experts including the CDC and USDA now recommend *not* washing raw chicken before cooking it, for exactly the reason you mentioned.

When you rinse raw chicken, the water doesn't kill the bacteria—it only removes some of it through mechanical action. More importantly, the splashing spreads bacteria-contaminated water droplets across your sink, countertops, hands, nearby utensils, and other surfaces. This increases the risk that bacteria will contaminate other foods or your hands.

The key point is that thorough cooking kills all the dangerous bacteria (salmonella, campylobacter, etc.) when chicken reaches an internal temperature of 165°F (74°C). Since proper cooking handles the safety issue anyway, washing introduces risk without providing meaningful protection.

So the best practice is to skip the rinse, place the chicken directly into the pan or oven, and ensure it cooks to the proper temperature. If you do accidentally splash raw chicken water, just clean and disinfect the affected surfaces.

# Answer 2

That's essentially correct. The USDA and CDC both recommend against washing raw chicken. Here's why:

Water doesn't kill bacteria. Rinsing doesn't remove enough pathogens to matter — the bacteria are still there. You'd need hot water (which risks burns and doesn't occur naturally when rinsing) or other treatments for that.

Splashing is the real problem. Water aerosolizes bacteria and spreads them across your sink, countertop, and nearby surfaces. Cross-contamination from these wet surfaces is a genuine risk path to illness.

Cooking kills it anyway. Chicken cooked to 165°F (74°C) kills the pathogens — salmonella, campylobacter, etc. — that you're worried about. Proper cooking is the actual safety step.

The practice persists because it feels right: We're trained to "wash" things to make them clean. But that intuition doesn't apply here. Raw chicken is contaminated by design (from the slaughter environment), and water doesn't fix that — it spreads it.

The advice has been consistent across food-safety authorities for years, though it's still frequently ignored because the cultural habit runs deep.
