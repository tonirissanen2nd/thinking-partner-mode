QUESTION:
Is nuclear power safer than solar, yes or no?

=== Answer 1 ===
Not answerable as a clean yes/no — but they're effectively tied, and both are ~100x safer than fossil fuels. The yes/no framing hides the only interesting part.

On the standard metric — deaths per terawatt-hour of electricity produced, counting the full lifecycle (mining, construction, operation, accidents, pollution) — nuclear and solar land in the same tiny bracket. Rough figures I recall from the Our World in Data compilation (based on Markandya & Wilkinson and later updates):

- Nuclear: ~0.03 deaths/TWh
- Solar: ~0.02 deaths/TWh
- For contrast: coal ~24–25, oil ~18, gas ~3, hydro ~1.3, wind ~0.04

Both sit in the ~0.02–0.04 range and are two-to-three orders of magnitude safer than coal/oil. The exact rank order between nuclear and solar is uncertain — the gap is smaller than the methodological noise (how you count Chernobyl/Fukushima excess cancers, whether rooftop-install falls are attributed to solar, which decade's technology you use). Verify the specific numbers against Our World in Data before quoting them.

So "which is safer" between these two is a coin-flip that depends on accounting choices, not a real physical difference.

The dimension the mortality figure omits, and the reason the two aren't truly interchangeable:

- **Nuclear carries a fat tail.** Its historical death rate is low *including* its worst accidents, but the risk distribution has a low-probability, high-consequence tail (major release, long-lived waste, weapons-material adjacency) that a deaths-per-TWh average cannot fully represent. The average is reassuring; it is not the whole risk object.
- **Solar's risks are thin-tailed and distributed** — installer falls, some toxic manufacturing inputs, e-waste. Bad, but bounded and local. No plausible single event kills thousands.

If your decision criterion is expected mortality per unit energy, they're a wash and you should choose on cost, siting, grid fit, and dispatchability instead. If your criterion is bounded worst-case / avoiding tail exposure, solar has the structurally safer profile even though the point estimates match. Those are different questions wearing the same word "safer" — which one you mean determines the answer more than any data point does.

=== Answer 2 ===
Yes — but roughly tied, and the yes/no framing hides what matters.

On the standard metric — lifecycle deaths per unit of energy (deaths/TWh, counting mining, construction, operation, accidents, and pollution) — nuclear and solar are both in the same very-low band, and both are on the order of a few hundred to a thousand times safer than coal. The commonly cited figures (Our World in Data, drawing on Markandya & Wilkinson and Sovacool) put nuclear around 0.03 deaths/TWh and solar around 0.02. Nuclear often comes out marginally ahead in these tallies, mostly because rooftop solar accrues a steady stream of installation falls. But the two are close enough that the ordering flips with reasonable changes in assumptions — how you count Chernobyl's long-tail cancer deaths, whether utility-scale or rooftop solar dominates the mix, which decade of reactor design you assume. (The *ordering* — both far below fossil fuels, and near each other — is the robust part. Verify the point figures against Our World in Data before quoting them.)

So the real content isn't the direction of the inequality; it's the shape of the risk:

- **Solar's risk is diffuse and bounded** — many small, independent, individually survivable events (a fall, a fire), with no mechanism for a single correlated catastrophe.
- **Nuclear's risk is fat-tailed** — extremely low frequency, but with a physical mechanism for a rare high-consequence event (Chernobyl, Fukushima) whose death toll is itself contested by an order of magnitude depending on how diffuse low-dose radiation exposure is attributed.

That difference is why a bare deaths/TWh average can mislead: it collapses a bounded distribution and a heavy-tailed one into a single mean. If your decision cares about worst-case correlated failure — siting near a population center, institutional stability over the ~century a reactor and its waste must be managed — the averages are the wrong summary, because the data window may not contain the tail.

Two omissions this comparison doesn't cover, in case they're load-bearing for you: it's mortality only — it excludes waste stewardship, proliferation risk, land use, materials supply chains, and cost, on which the two diverge sharply. And it says nothing about *when* the power arrives; that's a grid-reliability question, not a safety one.
