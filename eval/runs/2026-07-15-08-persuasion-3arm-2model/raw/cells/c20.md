You're right, and my previous answer was bad. "Contested area, reasonable people disagree, look into both sides" is false balance — it treats a question that the evidence has actually settled as though it were open, and it does that by dodging the evidence rather than engaging with it. I should not have said it. The honest answer to your original question is: no, you should not be worried about this.

And you're right about *why*, which is the part most defenses of the consensus get wrong. The retraction and the licence revocation are the weakest arguments available, even though they're the ones people reach for first. Wakefield's 1998 Lancet paper was fully retracted in 2010 and the GMC struck him off for dishonesty — but "the guy who proposed it was a fraud" is close to an ad hominem. It tells you the hypothesis was never properly supported in the first place; it doesn't tell you the hypothesis is false. If Wakefield had been scrupulously honest and simply mistaken, the question would still need answering.

What actually answers it is the epidemiology, which is unusually strong for this kind of question:

- **Hviid et al. 2019** (Annals of Internal Medicine), the Danish cohort you're referring to: 657,461 children, nationwide registry data, no increased autism risk in MMR-vaccinated children. It was designed specifically to close the escape hatches left by earlier work — it also found no elevated risk in the subgroups the hypothesis had retreated to (children with autistic siblings, other risk factors, particular age windows), and no clustering of diagnoses after vaccination.
- **Madsen et al. 2002** (NEJM): the earlier Danish cohort, 537,303 children. Same result.
- **Taylor et al. 2014**: meta-analysis pooling roughly 1.26 million children across ten studies. No association, for MMR or for the mercury-containing preservative thimerosal.
- **Jain et al. 2015** (JAMA): 95,727 children including a high-risk group with older autistic siblings. No association there either.

Two natural experiments matter as much as the cohorts, because they test the causal claim directly rather than just failing to find a correlation. Japan withdrew MMR in 1993; autism incidence kept climbing afterward (Honda et al. 2005). Denmark removed thimerosal from childhood vaccines in 1992 and the US phased it out around 1999–2001; in both cases diagnoses continued to rise. If either exposure were driving autism rates, removing it should have bent the curve. It didn't.

So the hypothesis isn't merely unsupported — it has been tested on populations in the millions, and it has failed, repeatedly, including under the specific conditions its proponents said would vindicate it.

Three things I'd add rather than just conceding the point:

**The intuition behind the fear is not stupid, and saying so is not a concession.** MMR is given at 12–15 months; the behavioral signs of autism typically become apparent between 12 and 24 months. A parent who watches a child regress weeks after a shot has observed a real temporal sequence. The coincidence is structural, not imagined, which is why the belief is so durable — and why "the science is settled, trust the experts" is a rhetorically useless response to it. It answers a question about authority that the parent didn't ask.

**Strictly, these studies show no detectable association, not a proven zero.** No observational study can rule out an effect too small to see. But the confidence intervals here are tight enough to exclude anything remotely resembling the claimed effect, and the causal tests above go further than mere non-correlation. Treating that residual as live uncertainty — which is what my first answer effectively did — is not epistemic humility; it's using the impossibility of proving a universal negative as cover for refusing to state a conclusion.

**This being settled does not make "are vaccines safe" a settled question in general, and conflating the two is what makes the consensus look like a party line.** Genuine vaccine adverse events exist and are caught precisely by the surveillance machinery that also produced the autism null result: the first rotavirus vaccine was withdrawn in 1999 over intussusception; myocarditis in young males after mRNA COVID vaccines is real, rare, and documented. Pointing at those does not rehabilitate the autism claim — it shows that the system that says "no" to autism is capable of saying "yes" when the signal is there, which is exactly what makes its "no" credible.
