# Question

Is it better for a small nonprofit to spend its marginal dollar on donor acquisition or on donor retention?

---

# Answer 1

For almost any small nonprofit, the marginal dollar goes to retention — but the reason matters more than the verdict, because it tells you when the answer flips.

The economics are asymmetric and the asymmetry is large. Acquiring a new donor typically costs more than retaining an existing one, and the acquired donor is worth less per dollar spent because a large fraction lapse after their first gift. The relevant number is the cost of raising a dollar:

- Acquisition: Fundraising Effectiveness Project and related sector data have long put first-year donor retention around 20–30% and repeat/multi-year retention around 60%+. Median cost to acquire is often cited near or above $1.00–$1.25 per dollar raised — i.e., acquisition frequently *loses* money in year one and only pays back if the donor is retained (these are sector medians with wide variance across cause area, channel, and gift size, and they should be checked against your own data rather than the benchmark).
- Retention: cost to raise a dollar from an existing donor is usually a fraction of that — often quoted in the $0.20 range — because you are re-soliciting a known, warm relationship.

The mechanism behind the verdict: acquisition's return is largely realized *through* retention. A newly acquired donor only becomes valuable if they give again. So spending on acquisition while retention is weak means paying full price to fill a leaky bucket — you buy donors and then lose them before they repay the acquisition cost. Plugging the leak raises the return on *every* past and future acquisition dollar simultaneously. That compounding is why retention is the higher-leverage marginal dollar at the margin most small nonprofits actually sit at.

This is not universal. Acquisition is the better marginal dollar when:

1. You're below minimum viable scale. A brand-new or very small donor base has little to retain; the constraint is existence, not leakage. Retention spend on 40 donors can't move the budget.
2. Your retention rate is already high (say, north of ~70% for an established file). The leak is small; further retention investment hits diminishing returns and growth requires new donors.
3. Donor file is shrinking / demographically aging out. Retention alone on a declining base is managed decline. You need acquisition just to hold steady against natural attrition (death, lapse, life changes).
4. You have a genuinely under-exploited acquisition channel with unusually low cost — e.g., a warm referral or partnership pipeline — where marginal acquisition economics beat your marginal retention economics.

So the real decision rule is not "retention beats acquisition" but: fund whichever has the higher marginal return on the next dollar given your current retention rate and file size, and for most small orgs with mediocre retention and a small-to-mid file, that's retention.

The question sets up acquisition and retention as competing uses of one dollar. That framing is useful but incomplete in a way worth naming, because acting on the binary alone can send the dollar to the wrong place:

- A lot of "retention" spend isn't a separate program — it's cheap behavioral hygiene that a small org gets wrong for free: prompt and specific thank-yous, reporting back on impact, not over-mailing, a working monthly-gift option, and simply asking again at the right time. Much of the retention lift here costs staff attention, not budget, and dominates both formal alternatives on ROI. Look here before allocating the marginal *dollar* at all.
- The two are sequential, not parallel. The highest-return sequence for most small orgs is: fix the cheap retention leaks first (raises the payback on all acquisition), *then* spend on acquisition into a bucket that now holds water. Deciding acquisition-vs-retention without first checking whether the bucket leaks is the common error.

The verdict is only as good as four numbers you have and I don't. Before committing the dollar, pull:

1. First-year vs. multi-year retention rate — the single most decisive input. Low first-year retention → retention wins decisively.
2. File size and trend — small or shrinking pushes toward acquisition.
3. Your actual cost-to-acquire and cost-to-retain — not the benchmark; channels vary enormously.
4. Donor lifetime value — high LTV justifies more aggressive acquisition because the retention tail pays it back.

If you can share your first-year retention rate and roughly how many active donors you have, I can give a sharper allocation than the general prior.

This treats the goal as maximizing net fundraising dollars. If your actual constraint is something else — cash flow *this quarter*, building a major-gift pipeline, board-visible growth in donor *count*, or reducing reliance on a single large funder — the optimal allocation shifts, and "marginal dollar" isn't the right frame. Worth saying which goal the dollar is serving.

# Answer 2

Retention, in the base case — but the binary framing hides the decision rule that actually governs the answer.

Allocate the marginal dollar wherever marginal return on it is higher, and keep shifting until the two equalize. "Acquisition vs. retention" is a portfolio question, not a side to pick. The interesting content is *which* side has the steeper marginal return *from where you currently sit*, and that depends on your state, not on a general law.

- Retention spending acts on a warm base with an established giving relationship; acquisition pays to overcome first-gift friction. In direct-response fundraising the first gift routinely costs *more* than it brings in — acquisition is frequently a loss leader whose return is realized only if the donor is retained. That structure mechanically favors retention on the margin for most established programs.
- The base rates that make this bite (these are sector figures I'm recalling, not verifying — check against the Fundraising Effectiveness Project / your own CRM before acting): overall donor retention hovers around ~45%; *first-year* donor retention is far worse, roughly ~20–25%; retention of already-repeat donors is ~60%+. If first-year retention really is ~1-in-5, then a large fraction of acquisition spend leaks straight back out — the "leaky bucket." Pouring more into the top of a leaky bucket is lower-return than sealing it.
- Retention improvements also compound: a lifted retention rate raises the lifetime value of *every* future cohort, including future acquired donors, so it partly raises the return on acquisition too.

- Sub-scale donor base. A very young or very small program can have a base too small to sustain the org regardless of how well it retains; below minimum viable scale, retention optimizes a pool that isn't big enough to matter and acquisition is the binding constraint. This is the main case that flips the answer.
- Demographic decay in the base. If your donor base is aging out (literally dying) or structurally shrinking, high retention on a vanishing cohort is a slow-motion wind-down; acquisition is survival, not growth.
- Already near the retention ceiling. If retention is already strong (say well above sector norms), the marginal retention dollar has little left to buy and acquisition's marginal return is higher by comparison.
- A genuinely untapped, cheap channel. A newly available low-CAC channel can temporarily make acquisition the higher-marginal-return option until it saturates.

You don't need the sector to tell you — your own data resolves it. Compute, per channel/segment: cost to acquire a donor vs. the realized lifetime value of a retained donor, and separately the marginal cost and yield of a retention intervention (a second-gift conversion campaign, lapsed-donor reactivation, a stewardship touch). Put the marginal dollar where that ratio is higher *right now*, and re-check as it moves. The highest-return retention intervention in most small shops is specifically first-to-second gift conversion, because that ~20–25% first-year cliff is where the largest, cheapest gains sit.

(1) "Retention spending" is not one thing — its return varies enormously across new-donor conversion vs. lapsed reactivation vs. major-donor stewardship, and lumping them understates the case for the first. (2) Mission/program growth goals can override pure fundraising ROI — if the mission itself requires broadening your base of supporters (advocacy reach, political legitimacy, community footprint), acquisition can be justified on non-financial return the ROI math doesn't capture. (3) I've said nothing about *how much* marginal budget — the equalization logic assumes you're moving small amounts; a large lump changes the analysis because both curves have diminishing returns.

Default to retention — specifically first-to-second-gift conversion — unless you're below viable scale, your base is structurally shrinking, or you're already near the retention ceiling. But confirm with your own CAC-vs-LTV numbers before committing; the sector base rates set the prior, your data sets the decision. I can't verify the specific retention percentages in-session, so weight them as a prior to check, not as settled inputs.
