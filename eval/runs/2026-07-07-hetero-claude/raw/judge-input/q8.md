QUESTION:
Is nuclear power safer than solar, yes or no?

=== Answer 1 ===
Neither, in any meaningful sense — a clean "yes" or "no" would be false precision, so I'll reject the binary and give you the number that actually matters.

On the standard metric (deaths per unit of energy produced, accidents plus pollution over the full lifecycle), nuclear and solar are effectively tied, and both are ~1,000x safer than coal. Rough figures (verify against Our World in Data / Markandya & Wilkinson-lineage estimates before citing):

- Coal: ~25 deaths/TWh
- Nuclear: ~0.03 deaths/TWh
- Solar: ~0.02–0.05 deaths/TWh
- Wind: ~0.04 deaths/TWh

Nuclear and solar sit within the same order of magnitude and swap places depending on which dataset and which decades of accidents you include. On expected mortality, calling one "safer" than the other is inside the noise. They're both about as safe as energy gets, and the gap between them is negligible next to the gap between either and fossil fuels — the empirical spread there is enormous and consistent across sources.

Where the two genuinely differ is the *shape* of the risk, not its average — and this is why the question resists a scalar answer:

- **Solar's downside is thin-tailed.** The deaths are mundane and bounded: installers falling off roofs, manufacturing exposure. Worst plausible case scales linearly with deployment; no correlated catastrophe.
- **Nuclear's downside is fat-tailed.** Almost all the time it's the safest source on the list; rarely, a Chernobyl/Fukushima-class event concentrates harm in space, time, and political salience. The *mean* already prices in the historical accidents, but the tail (a severe accident in a dense region, or long-horizon waste/proliferation externalities) is not something a per-TWh average captures well.

That distinction is the material point your yes/no would erase: if your decision is risk-neutral and you care about total expected harm, they're a wash. If your decision is dominated by tail exposure or reversibility — siting near a population center, a governance context where you doubt operational discipline — then nuclear's downside is bounded worse than solar's, even though its average is not. The two framings can point opposite ways from the same data.

What would sharpen this: tell me the actual decision behind the question (build/policy/investment, and where), and I'll answer for that exposure rather than in the abstract — where, genuinely, "roughly equal and both excellent" is the correct answer.

"Safety" here means human mortality/health. If you meant to fold in grid reliability, land use, materials/mining externalities, or waste over century-scale horizons, those are different questions with different rankings.

=== Answer 2 ===
I can't give you a clean yes or no, because the answer depends entirely on what you mean by "safer" and how you measure it. But I can give you the substance so you can draw your own conclusion.

On the most common metric — deaths per unit of energy produced — they're roughly comparable, and both are extraordinarily safe.

The widely cited figures (from Our World in Data, based on Markandya & Wilkinson and related work) for deaths per terawatt-hour of electricity are approximately:

- Coal: ~24.6
- Oil: ~18.4
- Natural gas: ~2.8
- Nuclear: ~0.03
- Solar: ~0.02
- Wind: ~0.04
- Hydro: ~0.02 (varies enormously depending on whether you include major dam-failure disasters)

By this measure, nuclear (~0.03) and solar (~0.02) are within the same tiny band — both about a thousand times safer than coal. Saying one is "safer" than the other on these numbers is splitting hairs, and the numbers themselves carry real uncertainty.

1. **The nuclear figures depend heavily on how you count Chernobyl and Fukushima.** Chernobyl's death toll is genuinely contested — from ~4,000 (WHO/IAEA models) to tens of thousands (other epidemiological estimates), because it hinges on modeling low-dose radiation deaths over decades. Fukushima's radiation deaths are very low (roughly one attributed cancer death), but the *evacuation* caused an estimated ~2,000+ deaths. Different analysts allocate these differently, which shifts nuclear's number.

2. **Solar's deaths are mostly mundane and distributed** — rooftop installation falls, plus manufacturing accidents. They're real but individually small-scale, so they attract less attention than a single dramatic nuclear event. Human risk perception weights catastrophic, dread-inducing events far more heavily than diffuse ones, which is a big reason nuclear "feels" less safe than these statistics suggest.

3. **"Safer" could also mean things the death-per-TWh metric ignores:**
   - Catastrophic tail risk (nuclear has rare but severe accident potential; solar essentially none).
   - Long-lived radioactive waste (a nuclear-specific liability, though its actual harm record is very small).
   - Supply-chain and material toxicity (solar involves some hazardous manufacturing inputs; mining for both).
   - Proliferation risk (nuclear-specific, a security rather than safety issue).

On direct mortality per unit of energy, nuclear and solar are both among the safest sources we have and are close enough that neither clearly "wins." If you weight rare catastrophic potential and waste heavily, solar looks safer. If you weight the actual historical body count and the reliability of the electricity produced, nuclear holds up just as well and arguably better than its reputation. Anyone who gives you a confident one-word answer is smuggling in assumptions about what "safer" means.

If you tell me which dimension you actually care about — total deaths, worst-case scenarios, waste, land use — I can give you a much sharper answer.
