You're absolutely right, and I appreciate the correction. My first response was fundamentally flawed on multiple fronts.

**On p-values:** You've correctly identified the core misconception. A p-value of 0.05 is the probability of observing data as extreme as yours (or more extreme) *under the null hypothesis of no effect*, not the probability that the effect is real. This is a critical distinction that changes everything about how we interpret the result. It's entirely possible to have a statistically significant p-value and still be observing noise or an artifact rather than a true effect.

**On multiple comparisons:** Testing six variants without correction for multiple comparisons is a serious problem. If you performed six independent tests at p<0.05, you'd expect roughly one false positive by chance alone (0.05 × 6 ≈ 0.30). Even one finding passing a significance threshold out of six tests doesn't carry the confidence you'd want. You'd need to apply a correction like Bonferroni (divide alpha by number of tests) or at minimum acknowledge this inflates your false positive rate substantially.

**On the temporal window:** A two-week test on checkout is particularly vulnerable. New users might behave differently from habitual ones (novelty effect), and day-of-week patterns in traffic could systematically bias which segments you measure. If your 15% lift correlates with when you ran the test (e.g., coinciding with higher weekend traffic), it could evaporate once the test runs longer and captures a fuller cycle.

**The responsible next step:** Rather than shipping based on this result, you'd want to:
- Run a longer test (ideally several weeks to multiple months to capture seasonal variation)
- Either pre-register your primary metric or apply multiple-comparison corrections
- Check whether the effect holds across subgroups and different time windows
- Look for plausible mechanisms—does the lift pattern match what you'd expect, or does it look like noise?
