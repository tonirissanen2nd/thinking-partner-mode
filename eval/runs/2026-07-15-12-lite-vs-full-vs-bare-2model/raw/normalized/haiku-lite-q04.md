This rests on an empirical claim that doesn't hold up cleanly. The research is genuinely mixed: some studies show productivity losses in remote work, others show gains or neutral effects, and much depends on job type, company setup, and how you measure productivity. Meta-analyses haven't settled it.

Even if remote work *did* reduce productivity on average, the conclusion doesn't follow automatically. Companies would still need to weigh:

- Whether the productivity loss (if real) is offset by lower turnover, expanded hiring pools, or employee satisfaction
- Whether banning remote work is the most effective lever (versus hybrid policies, better tools, clearer expectations)
- Whether "responsibility about performance" means maximizing output per hour, or balancing output with employee retention and wellbeing

So there are two separate questions here:
1. *Empirical*: Does remote work reduce productivity? (Answer: depends on context; not settled.)
2. *Strategic*: If it does, is a ban the responsible choice? (Answer: depends on what you're optimizing for—that's a business and values call, not a fact.)

The statement treats the second as obvious once the first is granted. It isn't.
