# Question

I've heard that washing raw chicken in the sink before cooking it actually increases the risk of food poisoning, because it splashes bacteria onto nearby surfaces rather than killing anything — is that right?

---

# Answer 1

Broadly, yes — that's the mainstream food-safety position, and the reasoning you've given is basically the right one. The CDC, the USDA, and the UK's Food Standards Agency all advise against rinsing raw chicken, for exactly the reason you describe.

Rinsing doesn't sterilize anything. Campylobacter and Salmonella aren't sitting loosely on the surface waiting to be flushed off — they're adhered to the meat and lodged in its crevices. Cold tap water removes some, but nowhere near enough to matter. The thing that actually makes chicken safe is heat: cooking it through (165°F / 74°C at the thickest point) kills the bacteria regardless of what you did beforehand. So the rinse contributes nothing to the step that actually protects you.

Rinsing does move bacteria around. Water hitting meat generates aerosols and droplets. Studies using fluorescent tracers or harmless marker bacteria have found contamination on the sink basin and faucet, on surrounding counters, and on nearby dishes and utensils — with some detectable spread on the order of half a meter or more. If a colander of salad or a cutting board is sitting next to the sink, that's a real transfer route, and unlike the chicken, the salad never gets cooked.

A few honest caveats, since the messaging around this sometimes overstates its case:

- The sink gets contaminated either way. A well-known USDA/Drexel observational study found that participants who *didn't* wash their chicken still contaminated their sinks at high rates, just from handling the package and the meat. So washing isn't the only sin; sloppy handling generally is the problem. The advice "don't wash" is a cheap win, not a complete solution.
- Nobody has counted the illnesses. There's no study showing "X cases of campylobacteriosis per year are caused by chicken-washing." The recommendation rests on demonstrated contamination pathways plus the absence of any benefit — a sensible basis, but it's a risk-reduction argument, not a measured body count.
- It's a real culinary tradition, not just ignorance. Washing chicken (often with lemon, vinegar, or salt) is standard practice in many Caribbean, West African, Latin American, and South Asian kitchens, and food-safety campaigns have sometimes been clumsy about that. The acid doesn't meaningfully sterilize either, but if you're going to do it, doing it in a bowl rather than under a running tap, and then washing the bowl, the sink, and your hands thoroughly with hot soapy water, contains most of the splash risk.

Practically: skip the rinse. If you want dry skin for better browning, pat the chicken with paper towels and bin them — that's more effective than rinsing anyway, and it doesn't aerosolize anything. Then wash your hands, and clean anything the raw meat or its packaging touched.

# Answer 2

Yes — the core claim is right, and it's the standard position of the UK FSA, NHS, USDA and CDC. But the reasoning you've been given is a simplified version of the real argument, and the simplification is the part worth fixing.

- Rinsing does not kill *Campylobacter* or *Salmonella*. Water at tap temperature and pressure removes some surface organisms; it doesn't inactivate them. Heat does — cooking the bird through (74 °C / 165 °F at the thickest point) kills what's on and in it.
- So washing has no food-safety upside. Whatever is on the chicken was going to be destroyed by cooking anyway.
- Washing does move live bacteria off the bird and into your kitchen — onto the sink, the tap, the draining board, adjacent worktop, your hands, and anything you touch next.

That combination is the actual argument, and it's an asymmetry/dominance argument, not a large-effect-size argument: zero benefit, non-zero cost, therefore don't do it. That conclusion holds regardless of how big the risk increment turns out to be, which is useful, because the increment is the part the evidence pins down least well.

"It splashes bacteria onto nearby surfaces" makes droplet spray sound like the main mechanism. Droplet studies do show visible splatter, but the range is fairly short and it isn't obviously the dominant route. The bigger vector is more mundane: the sink basin itself becomes a contaminated surface, your wet hands touch the tap and then the fridge handle, and rinse water pools where you later rinse salad. A USDA behavioural study a few years ago found that a substantial minority of people who washed chicken went on to contaminate a ready-to-eat salad — and, tellingly, that a smaller but non-trivial share of people who *didn't* wash contaminated it too, because the sink was already dirty and their handwashing was poor. I'm not confident enough in the exact percentages to quote them; treat the direction as reliable and the numbers as something to look up if they matter to you.

That study points at the real lesson: washing chicken is a symptom of the underlying problem, not the problem. The problem is that raw-chicken juices end up somewhere they shouldn't and then meet food that won't be cooked. Stopping the rinse without fixing the handwashing, the board discipline, and the sink hygiene captures only part of the available risk reduction.

The strongest defence of washing isn't microbiological, it's culinary and cultural — people rinse to remove blood, bone fragments, slime, or off-smell, or as part of a lemon/vinegar/salt rub that's normative in many cuisines. That's a real goal, and "just don't" is a poor answer to it. If you want it: pat the bird dry with kitchen paper and bin the paper (this removes surface residue with no aerosol and no standing water), and do acid/salt treatments in a bowl rather than under a running tap, then wash the bowl in hot soapy water. If you do rinse anyway, the mitigation is to clean *and* sanitise the sink, tap handles, and surrounding surfaces afterwards, and to wash your hands with soap for 20 seconds before touching anything else — a rinsed sink is not a clean sink.

"washing increases your risk of food poisoning" is inferred from a chain (washing → surface contamination → cross-contamination → ingestion → illness) rather than measured at the illness endpoint. There is no trial randomising people to wash or not and counting infections; there almost certainly never will be. So the *mechanism* is clear, and the *magnitude of the illness increment for any given person* is genuinely uncertain — plausibly small in absolute terms if your kitchen hygiene is otherwise good. This doesn't rescue washing (dominance again: still no upside), but it does mean anyone telling you washing chicken is a major driver of food poisoning is overclaiming relative to the evidence.

Undercooking and board/utensil reuse are the larger contributors to poultry-borne illness in most household risk assessments, so if your goal is fewer stomach bugs rather than settling this specific claim, a probe thermometer and a dedicated raw-meat board buy you more than dropping the rinse does. I've also said nothing about non-poultry items where rinsing *is* recommended (whole fruit and veg eaten raw) — the advice is meat-specific, not a general "don't rinse food" rule.
