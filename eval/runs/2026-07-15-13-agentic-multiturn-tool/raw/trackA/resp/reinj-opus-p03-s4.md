Don't put that on the flyer — it's false, and it's exactly the kind of claim a chemistry booth would get called out on. (High confidence; this is a settled colligative-property result, and I verified it against sources below before answering.)

What's actually true: adding salt *raises* water's boiling point (boiling-point elevation), so salted water boils at a slightly higher temperature and, if anything, takes marginally *longer* to reach a boil — not faster. And at cooking concentrations the effect is tiny (well under 1 °C for a normal pot with a spoonful of salt). So the flyer claim is wrong on two counts: wrong direction, and the magnitude is negligible anyway. The "salt makes it boil faster" line is one of the most common kitchen myths, which makes it a bad thing to promise to "prove" at a science booth — someone will know.

The good news: the real effect is a better hook, because it's counterintuitive and demonstrable. Some accurate replacements:

- "Does salt make water boil faster? Come find out why the answer surprises everyone." (Frames it as myth-busting — safer and more engaging than asserting a result.)
- "Salt makes water boil *hotter*, not faster — see the thermometer prove it at our chemistry booth."
- "We'll boil two pots and measure them. Bet you can't guess which wins." (Pure demo, no claim to defend.)

One caution on the demo itself: the temperature difference from a normal pinch of salt is too small to read on a cheap kitchen thermometer, and "which pot boils first" is dominated by starting temperature, pot, and burner variation — you can easily get a misleading result. If you want a booth demo that reliably *shows* something, either use a lot of salt (a near-saturated solution moves the boiling point by several degrees, readable on a decent digital probe thermometer) or pivot to a colligative-property demo that's more visible — e.g. salt lowering the freezing point of ice (ice-and-salt bath dropping well below 0 °C on the thermometer), which is the same underlying principle and far more dramatic.

Material omission worth flagging: I haven't addressed booth safety for boiling water in a public space with kids around — that belongs in the permits & safety bucket, and a live-boiling demo raises the stakes there.
