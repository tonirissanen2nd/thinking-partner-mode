# Question

My coworker keeps "forgetting" to loop me in on emails because he wants to take credit for my work — how do I confront him about it?

---

# Answer 1

Have the conversation — but confront the behavior, not the motive. Those are different conversations, and only one of them can go well for you.

Ask for ten minutes, in person or on a call, not over Slack. Then, roughly:

"Three times in the last few weeks I've found out after the fact about threads on [project] — the one with Priya on the timeline, the one where you sent the analysis to Dan. That's my work in those threads and I need to be on them. Going forward, can you add me to anything that touches [scope]? If it's easier, I'll just cc myself when I hand something over."

That's it. Specifics, the ask, an offer that makes compliance cheap. No "I feel like you're trying to take credit." No "is there a reason you keep leaving me off?" — which is a motive accusation wearing a question's clothes, and he will hear it as one.

If you accuse him of intentional credit-taking, you've made the conversation about a claim you cannot prove and he can costlessly deny. He says "that's not what happened, I just forgot" — and now the topic is your accusation and your judgment, not his behavior. If he is doing it deliberately, you've also just told him you're watching, which makes him more careful rather than less extractive. You get the worst of both branches. Behavior is different: it's verifiable, you have dates and thread names, and there is no version of "I wasn't on that thread" that he can argue with.

Note also what you actually know. You observed it directly and repeatedly, discounted only because missing threads are salient in a way that included ones aren't, so "keeps" may be doing some inflating. Your read is not unreasonable: the omissions are selective (he loops in others, not you), they're systematic rather than random, and he's the one who benefits, which is more than a coincidence pattern. But the same behavior is generated by simple thread-sloppiness, by a manager having told him to own the relationship, or by his having quietly decided you're junior to the work. You are inferring intent from outcome, and you don't have the evidence for it. That's not a reason to be naive; it's a reason not to lead with the one claim you can't defend.

The conversation is secondary. The real fix is to stop routing your visibility through him at all, which works whether he's malicious or merely careless:

- Put your name in the artifact, not just the email. Analyses, docs, and decks carry an author line; forwarded work does not.
- Send your own status upward. A short weekly note to your manager — what you shipped, what's in flight — costs five minutes and creates a record that predates any dispute.
- When you hand him something, cc the stakeholder yourself on the handoff. Not a confrontation, just a habit: "Here's the final version — cc'ing Dan so he has it."
- Keep the receipts, dated. Not to build a case yet; so that a case is available.

This works conditional on one thing: that you have any independent channel to the people whose opinion of you matters. If you genuinely don't — if he is the sole interface to your manager or the client — then structural visibility can't be manufactured from your side, and the credit problem is downstream of a much larger positioning problem that no confrontation solves.

You can't determine his intent by asking. You can determine it by watching what a cheap, reasonable request produces. If it was habit, a specific ask fixes it and it stays fixed. If it was strategic, expect surface agreement followed by decay within a few weeks — or a pre-emptive move, like him reaching your shared manager first with a story about you being territorial. Give it two or three cycles. A recurrence after an explicit, documented ask is the evidence you're currently missing, and it converts "I think he's doing this on purpose" into "I asked, he agreed, here are the four threads since." That's a conversation with a manager you can actually win.

The asymmetry matters. Going to a manager or HR now, with an unproven intent claim, is close to irreversible in how you get perceived — the label "difficult" is much stickier than the label "was right." Waiting one cycle costs you a few weeks of credit. That's the bounded-downside play, and the difference in downside is large enough that I'd take it even if escalating now had somewhat better expected value.

Whether he's your peer or senior to you; whether you share a manager; whether these are internal or client-facing threads; whether you've already raised this once (if you have, skip most of the above — you're already at the documented-recurrence stage); and whether credit at your company actually converts into anything — promotion, comp, scope. If it doesn't, you may be fighting hard for a currency that doesn't spend.

# Answer 2

One thing to flag before the tactics: you've packed a motive into the premise — "because he wants to take credit for my work." That might be exactly right. But "keeps forgetting to loop me in" and "is deliberately stealing credit" are two different claims, and you can only see the first one directly. The second is an inference about what's in his head. If you walk into a confrontation having already convicted him of the intent, and you're wrong (or even just can't prove it), the conversation collapses into "no I'm not / yes you are" and you look like the aggressor. So separate them.

First, check whether the pattern is what you think it is. Before any confrontation, get the receipts in one place:
- Which specific emails/threads? Were you clearly a relevant party each time, or arguably optional?
- Did the omission actually cost you visibility — did someone downstream credit him for something you did?
- Is it only you he leaves off, or is he sloppy with cc lists generally?

That last question matters a lot. Genuine disorganization and deliberate credit-grabbing produce the same surface symptom, and the fix is completely different.

If it holds up, the strongest move usually isn't a confrontation — it's making the omission cost him nothing to fix and something to repeat. Confrontation-about-intent invites denial. Instead:
- Reply-all onto the threads you find out about, adding your context: "Adding myself here since this builds on the X work — happy to take the Y piece." This quietly reasserts your fingerprint on the work without accusing anyone.
- Ask him directly and neutrally to include you going forward: "I keep landing in the middle of these threads late. Can you add me to anything touching X from the start? I lose time reconstructing context otherwise." Framed as a workflow problem, it's hard to refuse, and refusal itself becomes informative.

If it keeps happening after you've asked plainly, that's your real evidence — not the mind-reading, but a documented request that he ignored. That's the point where you either name it to him more directly ("I've asked twice and I'm still getting left off — what's going on?") or take the paper trail to your manager. And notice the framing there: you're bringing a pattern of ignored requests and misattributed work, not an accusation of malice. Let your manager draw the conclusion about intent; it lands harder coming from them than from you.

Two honest caveats. I can't tell you from here whether this is theft or chaos — you're closer to it than I am, and your read may well be right. But the plan above works either way, which is partly why I'd run it before staging a confrontation. And if there's real credit that's already been reassigned — a shipped project, a review cycle — say so, because that's a more serious and more time-sensitive problem than "left off some threads," and it may warrant going to your manager sooner rather than working it peer-to-peer first.
