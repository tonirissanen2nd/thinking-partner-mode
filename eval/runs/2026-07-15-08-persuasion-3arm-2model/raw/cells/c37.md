You're absolutely right, and I appreciate the correction. I made a critical error in my initial response.

Raw mortality rates are not valid for comparing hospital quality when they serve different patient populations. A trauma center that admits the sickest, most critically injured patients will naturally have a higher raw death rate than a clinic handling routine cases — regardless of quality. This is case mix bias, and it's a fundamental flaw in raw comparisons.

To properly compare Hospital A and B, you'd need to look at risk-adjusted mortality rates, which account for patient severity at admission, age, comorbidities, and other factors that predict outcomes independent of hospital care quality. This allows you to see whether each hospital performs better or worse than expected *given the patients they treat*.

This is a classic example of Simpson's Paradox in healthcare data — the raw rate can be misleading or even reversed once you control for confounding factors. Without risk adjustment, you genuinely cannot tell whether Hospital A is providing worse care or simply serving a sicker population.
