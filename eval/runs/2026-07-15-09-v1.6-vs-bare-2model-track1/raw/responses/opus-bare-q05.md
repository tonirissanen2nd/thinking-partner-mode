For most small nonprofits, the default answer is retention — but the honest answer depends on two numbers you can compute this afternoon.

## Why retention is the usual winner

The unit economics are lopsided. Acquiring a new donor typically costs somewhere around a dollar for every dollar raised in year one — you roughly break even, and only make money if the donor comes back. Retaining an existing donor costs a fraction of that. So acquisition is an *investment* that only pays off through retention. If retention is weak, acquisition spending is just a slower way of setting money on fire.

The leak is also usually enormous and usually at the front end. Sector-wide, overall donor retention hovers around 45%, but first-time donor retention is far worse — roughly 20-25%. Repeat donors, once they give a second time, retain at 60%+. That means the single most valuable dollar in most shops is the one that converts a first gift into a second gift.

Small orgs also have a structural edge in retention and a structural disadvantage in acquisition. Paid acquisition rewards scale: you need volume to test creative, optimize channels, and amortize fixed costs. A small nonprofit has none of that. But a small nonprofit *can* do things a large one can't — the executive director personally calling every donor who gives over $100, a handwritten note within 48 hours, an actual story about what the gift did. Those are the highest-ROI moves in fundraising and they are cheap.

## The two numbers

**1. Your first-year retention rate.** Of the people who gave their first gift last year, what fraction gave again this year? If it's under ~30%, you have a leaky bucket, and acquisition dollars will mostly drain out of it. Fix the bucket first.

**2. Your ceiling.** If you retained *100%* of your current donors and grew each one's gift modestly, what would revenue be? Compare that to what you actually need. If perfect retention still leaves you far short of a viable budget, you don't have a retention problem — you have a scale problem, and no amount of stewardship solves it.

Those two numbers usually point clearly one direction.

## When acquisition wins anyway

- **Your base is too small or too concentrated.** If three donors are 60% of your revenue, you are one bad quarter from a crisis. Diversification has real option value even at unattractive short-run ROI. That's insurance, not growth.
- **Natural attrition is nonzero.** People die, move, and change priorities. Even with excellent stewardship you lose some of the base every year, so zero acquisition forever is slow-motion decline. The question is sequencing, not either/or.
- **You have runway.** Acquisition pays back with a lag. If you're cash-constrained, retention pays now; if you have reserves, you can afford to invest.

## The framing I'd push back on

"Acquisition vs. retention" is somewhat false as a binary, because for a small org the best acquisition channel is usually your existing donors — peer referrals, board networks, an engaged donor bringing a friend. Money spent deepening relationships buys you both things at once. If you're going to spend on acquisition, spend it there before you spend it on ads.

**Practical default:** put the marginal dollar into retention until first-year retention clears ~40%, then split it, and treat any acquisition spend as an experiment you actually measure — cost per donor acquired, and whether those donors give a second time.
