QUESTION:
Is nuclear power safer than solar, yes or no?

=== Answer 1 ===
Not answerable as a clean yes/no — but they're effectively tied, and both are ~100x safer than fossil fuels. The yes/no framing hides the only interesting part.

On the standard metric — deaths per terawatt-hour of electricity produced, counting the full lifecycle (mining, construction, operation, accidents, pollution) — nuclear and solar land in the same tiny bracket. Rough figures I recall from the Our World in Data compilation (based on Markandya & Wilkinson and later updates):

- Nuclear: ~0.03 deaths/TWh
- Solar: ~0.02 deaths/TWh
- For contrast: coal ~24–25, oil ~18, gas ~3, hydro ~1.3, wind ~0.04

Both sit in the ~0.02–0.04 range and are two-to-three orders of magnitude safer than coal/oil. The exact rank order between nuclear and solar is uncertain — the gap is smaller than the methodological noise (how you count Chernobyl/Fukushima excess cancers, whether rooftop-install falls are attributed to solar, which decade's technology you use). Verify the specific numbers against Our World in Data before quoting them.

So "which is safer" between these two is a coin-flip that depends on accounting choices, not a real physical difference.

The dimension the mortality figure omits, and the reason the two aren't truly interchangeable:

- **Nuclear carries a fat tail.** Its historical death rate is low *including* its worst accidents, but the risk distribution has a low-probability, high-consequence tail (major release, long-lived waste, weapons-material adjacency) that a deaths-per-TWh average cannot fully represent. The average is reassuring; it is not the whole risk object.
- **Solar's risks are thin-tailed and distributed** — installer falls, some toxic manufacturing inputs, e-waste. Bad, but bounded and local. No plausible single event kills thousands.

If your decision criterion is expected mortality per unit energy, they're a wash and you should choose on cost, siting, grid fit, and dispatchability instead. If your criterion is bounded worst-case / avoiding tail exposure, solar has the structurally safer profile even though the point estimates match. Those are different questions wearing the same word "safer" — which one you mean determines the answer more than any data point does.

=== Answer 2 ===
Not answerable as a clean yes/no — but not because the question is empty. "Safer" hides a metric choice that flips the answer, so pin the metric: **they're roughly tied, and both are among the safest sources we have.**

On the standard metric — deaths per unit of energy produced (accidents + pollution over the full lifecycle) — nuclear and solar land in the same ballpark: on the order of ~0.03 vs ~0.02 deaths per TWh respectively (these track the Our World in Data / Markandya-Wilkinson-Sovacool numbers and should be checked against a primary source before you quote them). Nuclear's toll is dominated by historical accidents and mining; solar's by falls during rooftop installation and manufacturing. The difference between them is small and sensitive to which studies and which time window you use — well within the noise. The robust claim is the comparative one: both are ~2-3 orders of magnitude safer than coal or oil, and that gap dwarfs any nuclear-vs-solar difference.

Two things the mortality average doesn't capture, and they cut in opposite directions:

- **Nuclear carries a fat tail.** A per-TWh death rate is a mean, and a mean cannot contain the tail of a rare catastrophic accident or a long-horizon waste-containment failure. It's non-estimable, low-probability/high-consequence. So if "safer" means "lower expected deaths," they're tied; if it means "no plausible catastrophic downside," solar wins that specific dimension.
- **Solar's risk is distributed and reversible; nuclear's is concentrated and, in the tail, not.** That's the ruin distinction, and it's why reasonable people who agree on the mortality data still rank them differently — it's a values question about tail exposure, not a factual dispute.

So the direct answer depends on what you're optimizing:
- Expected mortality per unit energy → statistically tied.
- Worst-case / irreversibility → solar, because it has no catastrophic mode.
- "Should I worry about either relative to fossil fuels" → no; both are the safe options and the choice between them should ride on cost, grid fit, and siting, not safety.

I've scoped this to human mortality/safety. Land use, waste, materials-supply chains, and lifecycle carbon are separate axes, and "safer" sometimes smuggles those in — if that's what you meant, say so and the comparison changes.
