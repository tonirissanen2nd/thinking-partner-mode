QUESTION:
Remote work makes employees less productive, so companies that ban it are just being responsible about performance.

=== Answer 1 ===
The claim rests on a premise that doesn't hold up, so the conclusion doesn't follow.

**"Remote work makes employees less productive" is not an established fact.** The research is genuinely mixed, and the direction of the effect depends heavily on how you measure it and what kind of work you're talking about.

- A well-known randomized controlled trial by Nicholas Bloom (Stanford) with Ctrip found remote call-center workers were *more* productive (about 13%), though half of that came from working more minutes per shift and fewer breaks, not faster work per minute.
- His later 2022–2024 research on *hybrid* work found no productivity loss and meaningfully lower attrition.
- On the other hand, some studies of *fully* remote work (e.g., a 2023 study of software engineers, and research on newly-remote workers during COVID) found productivity dips, more time in meetings, or slower skill development and mentorship for junior staff.
- Self-reported productivity, manager-perceived productivity, and objective output metrics frequently disagree with each other, which is a red flag that "productivity" is being measured inconsistently across studies.

**It depends on the role, the tasks, the individual, the management quality, and whether it's fully remote or hybrid.** Anyone stating flatly that remote work reduces productivity is overselling a contested body of evidence.

**Even if we granted the premise, the conclusion still wouldn't follow.** The argument assumes:

1. *That the productivity effect is uniform.* If remote work helps some roles and hurts others, a blanket ban is a blunt instrument that throws away the gains along with the losses.
2. *That productivity is the only relevant variable.* It isn't. Bans affect retention, hiring pool (you lose candidates who won't or can't commute or relocate), real-estate costs, employee wellbeing, absenteeism, and morale. A company optimizing purely on a contested productivity signal while ignoring, say, a spike in attrition of experienced staff is not obviously "being responsible about performance" — it may be trading a small, uncertain productivity gain for large, concrete costs.
3. *That "responsible" equals "maximizing measured productivity."* That's a value judgment smuggled in as if it were neutral. Responsibility to whom? Shareholders, employees, and customers can have divergent interests here.

There's also a worth-naming complication: some return-to-office mandates appear to be motivated less by productivity data than by justifying real-estate commitments, increasing manager visibility and control, or using attrition as a soft form of headcount reduction. When a company frames such a decision as "responsible about performance," that framing deserves scrutiny rather than acceptance at face value.

**Where the argument has a legitimate kernel:** it is reasonable for a company to care about productivity and to act on evidence. If a specific firm has *its own reliable internal data* showing that its particular teams do worse remotely, restricting remote work for those teams could be a defensible, evidence-based call. That's a narrow, empirical, context-specific claim — very different from the broad, universal one in the original statement.

The sentence as written is a syllogism with a shaky first premise and a leap in the second step. "Remote work reduces productivity, therefore banning it is responsible" doesn't survive contact with the actual evidence or with the other factors a responsible company has to weigh.

=== Answer 2 ===
The argument packs two shaky moves into one sentence: a contested empirical premise, and a leap from that premise to "responsible" that doesn't follow even if the premise were true.

This is not settled, and the direction depends heavily on how you measure and which work you mean. What the evidence actually shows:

- *Fully-remote individual output:* Mixed. The cleanest causal study is Bloom et al.'s randomized trial at a Chinese travel agency (2015), which found a ~13% productivity *gain* for call-center workers working from home. But a 2023 follow-up on data-entry-style work found fully-remote workers ~18% *less* productive than in-office. Both are narrow, task-specific settings — call-center and data-entry work generalize poorly to knowledge work.
- *Hybrid:* The strongest recent randomized evidence (Bloom et al., *Nature* 2024, at Trip.com) found hybrid work had *no* negative effect on productivity or promotion and cut attrition by roughly a third. Hybrid is where most of the actual policy fight is, and it's the case the "ban it" argument handles worst.
- *Self-reported vs. objective measures diverge:* Managers tend to rate remote workers as less productive while workers rate themselves as more so — a signal that some of the "less productive" belief is a monitoring/visibility artifact, not measured output.

So *sometimes, for some task types, under some measures.* Stated as a flat universal ("remote work makes employees less productive"), it's an overreach the evidence doesn't support. I'd flag the specific figures above as worth verifying against the primary papers before you lean on them.

Even granting the premise fully, "ban it, and that's responsible about performance" does not follow. Three breaks in the chain:

1. *Productivity is not the whole objective function.* A ban trades a productivity delta against retention, hiring pool, real-estate cost, and morale. The Trip.com study's headline finding was a large *attrition* reduction — if banning remote raises quit rates among your most mobile (often most valuable) employees, you can be more "responsible about performance" per-head while destroying performance in aggregate. "Responsible about performance" requires netting these, not counting one column.

2. *Ban is not the only, or best, response to a real productivity gap.* This is the core error. If some remote workers underperform, the targeted instrument is performance management — measure output, manage or coach the underperformers, set in-person norms for the collaborative work that genuinely needs it. A blanket ban is a low-resolution tool for a high-resolution problem: it also removes the arrangement from everyone it *helps* or is neutral for. Choosing the blunt instrument when a sharp one exists is closer to the opposite of "responsible."

3. *"Responsible" smuggles in the conclusion.* The word does normative work the argument hasn't earned. Whether a ban is responsible depends on whether it actually maximizes the firm's real objective under uncertainty — that's the thing in dispute, not a label you can attach by asserting a productivity effect.

The argument treats "remote work" as one thing. The relevant unit is task type × individual × measurement regime. Once you disaggregate, the universal claim dissolves into "certain work, certain people, certain metrics" — which is an argument for differentiated policy, not a ban.

The premise is, at best, partially and conditionally true, and the conclusion doesn't follow from it even where it holds. A firm genuinely optimizing performance would measure output and manage the tail, not ban the arrangement — a ban is more consistent with a preference for control and visibility than with a demonstrated performance case. If you want to actually defend a ban, the burden is to show that (a) the productivity loss is real *for your specific work*, and (b) no less-costly targeted intervention captures the same gain — and to net it against retention and hiring effects. Absent that, "responsible" is doing rhetorical work the evidence hasn't paid for.
