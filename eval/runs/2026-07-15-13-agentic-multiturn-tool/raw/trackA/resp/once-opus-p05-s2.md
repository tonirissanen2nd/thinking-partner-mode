There's no number I can give you here that's safe to write on that form — and this is one where guessing has real downside, because an occupancy figure on a permit is a legal attestation. If it's too high you can be cited or shut down day-of; if you have to evacuate and the posted load was wrong, that's a liability exposure, not just a paperwork error. So I'm going to withhold a figure rather than invent a plausible one.

Here's why there isn't a single "code number" for "a mid-size hall": occupancy load isn't a property of the building's name or vibe, it's *computed* from specifics you haven't given me and I can't see —

- **Occupant load factor** — the square-feet-per-person figure depends on how the space is used. Under the International Building/Fire Code tables, unconcentrated assembly with tables and chairs is ~15 sq ft net per person; concentrated assembly (chairs only, no tables) is ~7; standing space is ~5. A maker fair with 30 tables is likely the ~15 factor, but the AHJ decides. (Moderate confidence on the factors themselves; I'd still verify against the edition your jurisdiction adopted.)
- **Net vs. gross floor area** — you measure the *usable* assembly area, subtracting stage, storage, kitchen, restrooms, thickness of walls. Not the building footprint.
- **Egress capacity** — the exits can cap the number *below* what the floor area allows. Door widths, number of exits, and travel distance can be the binding constraint.
- **The code edition your town actually adopted** — IBC/IFC vary by year and by local amendment.

So the honest answer to "what's the exact number" is: **it's non-recallable for me and shouldn't be estimated** — the true value could plausibly land anywhere from ~80 to several hundred depending on the four factors above, and a permit form is exactly the place where a made-up midpoint does harm.

Where the real number comes from, in order of least effort:

1. **The posted occupancy placard.** Most community halls already have a load certificate posted near the main entrance or in the manager's office. If it exists, that's the authoritative figure — copy it verbatim.
2. **The venue manager / booking office.** They almost always know it, because it governs their own rentals and insurance.
3. **The local fire marshal / building department.** They set it and will confirm it; for an assembly event you may need to talk to them anyway (see below).

One adjacent thing worth flagging, since you're filling out the permit: a public assembly event may trigger requirements beyond just stating the number — a temporary assembly permit, exit-signage and aisle-width checks, possibly an occupancy inspection. Worth asking the fire marshal "what do you need from me for a one-day public event here" in the same call, so it's one conversation rather than three.
