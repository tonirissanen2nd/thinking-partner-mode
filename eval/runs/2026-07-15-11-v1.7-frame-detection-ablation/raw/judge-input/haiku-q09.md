# Question

I've heard that washing raw chicken in the sink before cooking it actually increases the risk of food poisoning, because it splashes bacteria onto nearby surfaces rather than killing anything — is that right?

---

# Answer 1

Yes, that's right. Rinsing raw chicken does increase food poisoning risk, and the mechanism you've identified—bacterial splashing—is the actual problem.

Water running over raw chicken aerosolizes bacteria (primarily *Salmonella* and *Campylobacter*), dispersing them across the sink basin, faucet, countertop, and your hands. This creates cross-contamination pathways you wouldn't otherwise have. Rinsing does not meaningfully reduce the bacterial load on the chicken itself—it just relocates it.

This recommendation is consistent across USDA and CDC guidance, the logic is straightforward, and the risk is well-documented. The basis is direct: bacteria on raw poultry are normal; rinsing doesn't kill them, only moves them; contaminated surfaces are the actual vector for foodborne illness in home kitchens.

Cooking chicken to an internal temperature of 165°F (74°C) kills all pathogens that pose a risk. That's the load-bearing control. If you do rinse (some recipes or habits may call for it), you're simply introducing an unnecessary cross-contamination risk without any offsetting benefit—rinsing provides no meaningful safety gain since cooking handles pathogen control completely.

The folk intuition that "cleaning" raw meat before cooking makes it safer is understandable but backwards: the safest approach is to skip the rinse and move directly to cook.

# Answer 2

The core claim is correct, based on CDC/FDA guidance and microbiological evidence.

Washing raw chicken does not kill bacteria effectively and does increase cross-contamination risk via splashing. The CDC, USDA, and FDA all recommend against washing raw chicken for exactly this reason.

Bacteria are not removed in meaningful quantities. Rinsing removes only ~90% of surface bacteria at best, leaving a substantial inoculum; more critically, *Salmonella* and *Campylobacter* (the primary foodborne pathogens in poultry) colonize the intestinal tract and internal organs, not just the surface. Washing does not reach these bacteria.

Cross-contamination is the actual vector. The splash mechanism you describe is real: water aerosolization deposits bacteria on countertops, utensils, hands, and adjacent foods within a ~1-meter radius. Subsequent contact transmission is the primary pathway to foodborne illness in home kitchens.

Cooking is sufficient control. Heating chicken to 165°F (73°C) internal temperature kills all vegetative bacteria reliably. This is the only step that matters; washing adds risk without reducing it.

The risk is not from residual bacteria *on the chicken itself* (cooking handles that), but from cross-contamination of the environment. A person who washes chicken and then touches a salad without handwashing has introduced pathogenic risk into a food that won't be cooked.

Proper handling after washing is no substitute for not washing—it's an additional burden that failure-prone human behavior often skips.

CDC/USDA/FDA guidance is consistent on this point; microbiological data supports it; and kitchen contamination studies confirm the cross-contamination mechanism. This is not a contested domain.
