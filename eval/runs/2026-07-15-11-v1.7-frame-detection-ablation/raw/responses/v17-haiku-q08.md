No. But the question itself suppresses relevant structure.

"Safer" has at least two independent dimensions where they trade off:

**Deaths per unit of energy (empirical, High confidence):** Nuclear is substantially safer. Peer-reviewed meta-analyses show nuclear at ~0.07 deaths per terawatt-hour, solar at ~0.2–0.4 (manufacturing accidents, installation falls, mining hazards). This is a factual win for nuclear and rests on regulatory and epidemiological data.

**Catastrophic tail risk (empirical, Moderate confidence):** Solar is safer. Nuclear carries low-probability, high-consequence scenarios (Fukushima, Chernobyl demonstrate "extremely rare" is not "impossible"); solar's worst cases are localized fires or toxic decommissioning events. Quantifying true nuclear accident probability requires modeling assumptions that are contested, so this claim is not High confidence, but the directional pattern is clear.

**Long-term waste disposal (empirical, Low to Moderate confidence):** This is genuinely contested. Nuclear waste is concentrated and well-characterized, but permanent storage for 10,000 years is unfalsifiable in-session. Solar creates diffuse toxic e-waste; recycling infrastructure is immature. Neither is demonstrably safer — they displace the risk across time and to different populations. The empirical basis here is thin.

**The frame suppresses a choice:** If you measure by "expected deaths in the next 20 years," nuclear wins decisively. If you measure by "maximum downside to a single city if something goes catastrophically wrong," solar wins. The question asks you to pick, but the answer depends on which risk dimension you weight — a preference, not a fact.

The honest conclusion: Nuclear is safer on the most common metric (deaths per energy unit). Solar is safer if you prioritize bounded downside. Neither framing is wrong; they're incommensurable. A yes-or-no answer substitutes your preference for evidence.
