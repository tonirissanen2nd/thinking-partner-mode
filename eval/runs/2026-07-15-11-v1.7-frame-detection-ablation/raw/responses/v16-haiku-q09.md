The core claim is correct: Confidence High, based on CDC/FDA guidance and microbiological evidence.

Washing raw chicken does not kill bacteria effectively and does increase cross-contamination risk via splashing. The CDC, USDA, and FDA all recommend against washing raw chicken for exactly this reason.

**Why washing fails to reduce risk:**

1. **Bacteria are not removed in meaningful quantities.** Rinsing removes only ~90% of surface bacteria at best, leaving a substantial inoculum; more critically, *Salmonella* and *Campylobacter* (the primary foodborne pathogens in poultry) colonize the intestinal tract and internal organs, not just the surface. Washing does not reach these bacteria.

2. **Cross-contamination is the actual vector.** The splash mechanism you describe is real: water aerosolization deposits bacteria on countertops, utensils, hands, and adjacent foods within a ~1-meter radius. Subsequent contact transmission is the primary pathway to foodborne illness in home kitchens.

3. **Cooking is sufficient control.** Heating chicken to 165°F (73°C) internal temperature kills all vegetative bacteria reliably. This is the only step that matters; washing adds risk without reducing it.

**What you may have missed:**

- The risk is not from residual bacteria *on the chicken itself* (cooking handles that), but from **cross-contamination of the environment.** A person who washes chicken and then touches a salad without handwashing has introduced pathogenic risk into a food that won't be cooked.
- Proper handling after washing is no substitute for not washing—it's an additional burden that failure-prone human behavior often skips.

**Confidence basis:** CDC/USDA/FDA guidance is consistent on this point; microbiological data supports it; and kitchen contamination studies confirm the cross-contamination mechanism. This is not a contested domain.
