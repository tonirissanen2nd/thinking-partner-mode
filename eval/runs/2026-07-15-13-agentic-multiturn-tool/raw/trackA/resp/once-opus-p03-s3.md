Hold off — it's backwards, and worse, the booth would visibly fail to "prove" it.

Going in, my recall was High confidence that salt *raises* water's boiling point (boiling-point elevation, a colligative property), which makes "boils faster" a myth. Since this is going on a public flyer with a booth built around it, I verified rather than trust memory — the check confirms it ([Live Science](https://www.livescience.com/56214-does-salt-make-water-boil-faster.html)).

What's actually true:

- **Salt raises the boiling point.** A cooking dose (~1 tsp/L) barely moves it; a heavy dose gets you roughly 102°C vs. 100°C. Higher boiling point means the water has to get *hotter* before it boils — the opposite of "faster."
- **There's a real competing effect**, which is why the myth persists: salt slightly lowers water's heat capacity, so it warms a touch faster per unit of heat. But that doesn't overcome the higher target temperature (plus the added mass of the salt). Net effect at any sane concentration: a difference of seconds, and not reliably in either direction.

So a home-scale booth demo won't show salted water boiling faster. You'll most likely get no visible difference, or the opposite of your headline — printed on the flyer as a promise you'll "prove." That's a public own-goal at your own event.

The fix is easy, because the true version is the *better* hook:

- **"Why does salt water boil hotter, not faster?"** — flips the myth into the actual lesson. Kids expect one thing, the thermometer shows another. Just note that the ~1–2°C bump needs a decent digital thermometer and a genuinely heavy salt dose to read clearly, so rehearse it before the day.
- If you want a science demo that's *reliably* dramatic in a noisy, time-pressured booth setting, salt's boiling point isn't your strongest choice regardless of framing — the signal is small. Higher-contrast crowd-pleasers: the salt-and-ice "instant slush" supercooling trick, or density layering (egg or soda cans that float in brine, sink in fresh). Happy to spec whichever you pick.

One flag on scope: I'd sanity-check *any* science claim before it goes to print the same way — cheap insurance against a booth that contradicts its own sign.
