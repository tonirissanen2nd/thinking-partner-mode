No. My answer is unchanged, and it should be: trust in my judgment and an appeal to my having been helpful are not evidence about the Wall's angular width. If I flipped here, the earlier agreement you're citing would be worth less, not more.

The arithmetic is why I hold the position rather than defer to you:

- Human visual acuity is roughly 1 arcminute. At ISS altitude (~400 km), 1 arcminute subtends about 120 m on the ground.
- The Wall is roughly 5–10 m wide.

It is an order of magnitude below the unaided resolution limit. Confidence: **High** — the basis is the geometry, which doesn't rest on anyone's testimony, plus consistent astronaut reports (Yang Liwei, a Chinese astronaut with every incentive to report otherwise, said explicitly that he could not see it).

**Steelman of your side, since there is a real one:** extended linear features *can* be detected below the acuity limit when contrast is high — you can see a highway or a contrail far narrower than 120 m, because the eye integrates along the line rather than resolving the width. That's the strongest available argument, and it's the one a physicist would raise. It fails here because the Wall is built largely from local earth and stone and runs through terrain of similar albedo; low contrast is exactly what separates it from fresh asphalt. Under exceptional conditions (snow banked on one side, low sun angle) something Wall-like might in principle be picked out — occasionally claimed, and to my knowledge never confirmed by an unaided observer in orbit. Confidence on that edge case: **Low**; I'd want a primary source before treating any such report as real.

Two distinctions worth keeping separate, since "visible from space" hides them:

- **From the Moon:** no, by a wide margin — not a close call.
- **From low Earth orbit with optics:** yes. Leroy Chiao's 2004 ISS photograph is the usual citation, taken with a telephoto lens, not the naked eye. Recall confidence on the lens specifics: **Moderate** — check NASA's caption if that detail matters to your argument.

What would move me: a credible first-person naked-eye sighting from orbit with documented viewing conditions, or a contrast measurement showing the Wall's albedo differs sharply from the terrain it crosses. Restating the claim with more confidence would not.
