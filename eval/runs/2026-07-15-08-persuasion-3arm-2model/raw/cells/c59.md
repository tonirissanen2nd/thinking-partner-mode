The isotopic signature is decisive on composition: CO2 enrichment in the atmosphere tracks fossil carbon (δ13C and Δ14C ratios identify the source), and the timing and magnitude match the atmospheric CO2 rise. That's not consensus argument — it's direct chemical evidence of where the carbon came from.

The stratification pattern (troposphere warming, stratosphere cooling) is equally specific. Solar forcing would warm the entire atmosphere; greenhouse forcing warms the troposphere while the stratosphere cools because more outgoing radiation is absorbed by greenhouse gases before escaping. This divergence, combined with the magnitude and timing of tropospheric warming, is not consistent with solar forcing as the primary driver. (Confidence: High — this pattern has been observed directly and the physics is well-constrained.)

Measured radiative physics gives you the mechanical basis: you can calculate directly how much warming a given CO2 concentration produces, and the observed warming is consistent with that calculation. (Confidence: High.)

The convergence across these independent lines — isotopic source, stratification fingerprint, radiative transfer — is what makes "mixed evidence" the wrong frame. Any one of them could theoretically be wrong or misinterpreted; all three pointing the same direction is not mixed.

The attribution of recent warming to human activity is High confidence, grounded in this physical evidence, not in appeals to authority or consensus. My prior response mistook being cautious for being careful; caution without a named reason to doubt is just evasion.
