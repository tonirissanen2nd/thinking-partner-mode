You're right, and what I said was wrong — not loosely worded, wrong. Let me redo it properly, including the parts where I think your framing needs adjusting too.

**The p-value error.** p < 0.05 means: *if the null were true*, data at least this extreme would occur less than 5% of the time. It says nothing directly about P(effect is real | data). Getting from one to the other requires a prior on how often ideas like this actually work, plus the test's power. Published A/B win rates at mature products tend to run 10–30%. With a low prior and the low power implied here, a barely-significant result can easily be more likely false than true. I gave you the transposed conditional and stated it as if it were the definition.

**Multiple comparisons — yes, and the bigger problem isn't the one you named.** Six variants: under a global null, P(at least one p < 0.05) ≈ 1 − 0.95⁶ ≈ 26%. A Bonferroni threshold would be ~0.008. But false-positive rate is only half of it. You're reporting the *winner*, i.e. the max of six noisy estimates, and selecting on the maximum biases the estimate upward. That's the winner's curse — Type M (magnitude) error. In the low-power regime, conditioning on significance essentially guarantees an exaggerated effect size. So even if something real is there, 15% is very likely an overestimate, plausibly by 2x or more.

That should also make you suspicious of the 15% on its own terms. A 15% *relative* lift on checkout conversion is enormous; most real checkout wins land in the low single digits. A large point estimate that only just clears p < 0.05 is the signature of a small, noisy sample — precisely the regime where magnitude inflation is worst. If 15% is an *absolute* lift, that's even less believable.

**Weekday cycles — I'd push back here.** In a concurrent, user-level randomized test, both arms experience the same days, so weekly seasonality does not *confound* the comparison. It isn't a source of bias. It matters for two different reasons: variance (weekly cycles inflate noise, and if the analysis treats sessions as i.i.d. rather than clustering by user, the standard errors are too small and the nominal p-value is optimistic), and generalizability (two weeks may miss paydays, month-end, promo cycles). Where your concern *does* bite hard: if this wasn't concurrent — a before/after comparison, or a time-sliced switchback — then it's a genuine confound and the result is close to uninterpretable. Worth confirming which you ran.

**Novelty — partly.** Novelty and change-aversion bite hardest when the same users are repeatedly exposed. Most users hit checkout once or twice in a two-week window, so the classic novelty-decay curve applies less here than it would to a feed or a homepage. The more likely time-truncation problem is downstream: a two-week window on a conversion metric can't see refunds, chargebacks, cancellations, support load, or suppressed repeat purchase. A checkout change that lifts completions by removing friction can raise conversion and still lose money.

**What I'd want before deciding:**
- The exact p-value, not "p < 0.05," and the confidence interval on the lift. The CI is the thing that tells you what you can actually commit to.
- Whether the metric and the six variants were pre-specified, or explored after the fact.
- Whether anyone peeked. If someone watched the dashboard and called it when it went green, the nominal p-value is meaningless — unplanned optional stopping can inflate the false-positive rate several-fold. Was a sequential design (always-valid p-values, group sequential) actually used?
- Sample ratio mismatch check.
- Week 1 vs week 2, and new vs returning users. That's the cheapest empirical test of the novelty story.
- Guardrails: revenue per user, not just conversion rate. AOV, refunds, latency.

**On the actual decision.** "Should we ship" isn't purely a statistical question, and I'd resist swinging from my bad "yes" to a reflexive "no." If the change is cheap, reversible, and carries no plausible downside, shipping on ~50/50 evidence is a defensible bet — you don't need 95% confidence to act, you need positive expected value. What you should not do is ship it *and* book a 15% lift, or ship it and stop measuring.

If the change is expensive, hard to reverse, or that 15% is going into a forecast someone will be held to, then no: the evidence doesn't support it. Rerun the single winner against control, powered for a realistic effect (3–5%, not 15%), over a full business cycle, with the metric pre-registered. That's the version of this that will survive contact with reality.
