The claim bundles a contested empirical premise with a normative conclusion, and both links are weaker than the sentence implies. Taking them in order.

**"Remote work makes employees less productive"** — this is not established as a general fact; the evidence is mixed and depends heavily on what you measure and for whom (Moderate confidence in the *mixedness*, drawn from the direction of the major studies rather than a settled headline number — the specific effect sizes below should be verified against primary sources before you lean on them).

- The cleanest causal evidence is from randomized/quasi-experimental settings. Bloom et al.'s Ctrip call-center RCT found *higher* output for fully-remote workers (roughly +13%), though that was a specific role (routine, individually-measurable tasks) and workers self-selected into the trial. A later Bloom hybrid study (2022, ~2 days remote) found no productivity loss and lower attrition. These cut against the premise, not for it.
- Studies showing declines tend to cluster around fully-remote knowledge work with heavy coordination needs, junior employees who learn by osmosis, and periods of forced/unplanned remote work (the pandemic natural experiment confounds remote work with crisis, childcare collapse, and hasty tooling).
- "Productivity" is doing a lot of silent work here. Self-reported output, manager perception, hours logged, and objective throughput frequently disagree, and manager perception is biased against remote workers independent of actual output (proximity bias). A premise that doesn't specify the metric can't support a general conclusion.

Net: the honest state of the evidence is *heterogeneous effect by role, seniority, task type, and remote intensity* — not a uniform productivity penalty. The blanket claim is unsupported. (Moderate)

**The logical jump — "so banning it is responsible about performance"** — this is where the argument mainly fails, and it fails even if you *grant* the premise. Three gaps:

1. *A ban is not the only performance lever, and rarely the efficient one.* If the concern is output, the responsible move is to measure and manage output. A location mandate is a crude proxy that also constrains the cases where remote *helps*. Banning remote to fix a productivity problem is an XY move: the goal is performance; the chosen means is attendance.

2. *The costs sit on the other side of the ledger and go unnamed.* Mandates raise attrition — and they select adversely, since the people most able to leave (highest performers, most in-demand skills) leave first. They shrink the hiring radius to commuting distance, forfeit the real-estate and continuity savings, and hit caregivers and disabled employees hardest (a legal-exposure dimension in some jurisdictions, not merely a morale one). "Responsible about performance" that ignores the performance cost of losing your best people is not actually reasoning about performance.

3. *"Responsible" smuggles in a motive claim.* Return-to-office mandates are sometimes driven by sunk real-estate cost, control preferences, or headcount-reduction-by-attrition, not by a performance analysis. Calling the ban "responsible" assumes the conclusion. Whether it's responsible is exactly what's in question.

**The defensible version of the underlying position:** *For some roles and some employees — particularly early-career workers, tightly-coordinated teams, and tasks that depend on tacit knowledge transfer — evidence suggests in-person or hybrid arrangements outperform fully-remote, so a differentiated policy can be a legitimate performance decision.* That is a real argument. Note what it is not: it is not "remote makes people less productive," and it is not "therefore ban it." It supports *hybrid defaults and role-specific policy*, which is roughly where the better evidence (and most large employers' revealed choices) has landed.

What would move me: role-controlled, output-based (not perception-based) productivity data with random or quasi-random assignment to remote vs. office, showing a penalty that survives after accounting for attrition and hiring-pool effects. If that existed broadly, the empirical premise would firm up — but it would still not by itself justify a *ban* over managing to the metric directly.

One omission worth flagging: none of this addresses the employee's own utility (commute time, flexibility), which is a real term in the total welfare calculation even if you restrict "responsible" to mean purely firm-level performance.
