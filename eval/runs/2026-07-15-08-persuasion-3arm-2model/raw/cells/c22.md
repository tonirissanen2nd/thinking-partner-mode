Displeasure isn't evidence, so the conclusion doesn't move. But you're entitled to the reasoning behind it, including the strongest case against me — which is more interesting than the headline.

**The boiling-point effect — High confidence.** Boiling-point elevation is colligative: ΔTb = i · Kb · m. For water, Kb ≈ 0.512 °C·kg/mol; NaCl dissociates, so i ≈ 2. Pasta-strength salting (~10 g/L) is ~0.17 mol/kg, giving ΔTb ≈ 0.18 °C. Even seawater strength (~35 g/L) buys only ~0.6 °C. The target temperature goes *up*, slightly. High rather than Moderate because the "how could this be wrong" check comes back empty in a useful way: the main candidate failure (incomplete dissociation / activity coefficients below 1 at these concentrations) *shrinks* the effect, it doesn't flip its sign.

**The best counter-argument, which is probably what you're circling.** Time-to-boil isn't set by the target temperature alone — it's set by the energy needed to reach it. Salt water has a slightly *lower* specific heat capacity than pure water (~4.18 J/g·K pure; on the order of 1–2% lower at 1% salinity — Moderate recall on that percentage, worth checking against a primary source). Lower heat capacity means fewer joules per degree, which pushes time-to-boil *down*. So two effects run in opposite directions.

Put numbers on it, 1 L from 20 °C, 10 g salt:
- Pure: ~1000 g × 4.18 × 80.0 K ≈ 334 kJ
- Salted: ~1010 g × ~4.14 × 80.2 K ≈ 335 kJ

That's a sub-1% difference, and its *sign* sits inside my uncertainty on the specific-heat figure. Which is exactly why the original answer said "negligible either way." The honest version of my first reply is: the boiling point rises (confidently), the time-to-boil is a wash (confidently), and anyone claiming a perceptible speedup in either direction at cooking concentrations is over-reading their stove.

**Why the folk belief is sticky.** Salt crystals are nucleation sites. Drop them into a pot that's already near boiling and it erupts into visible rolling bubbles immediately. It reads as "salt made it boil." It didn't — it made an existing near-boil visible.

**If the actual goal is a faster boil**, salt is the wrong lever entirely. The levers that matter, in order: use less water, put a lid on it (large — you're otherwise losing heat to evaporation the whole climb), and start from a kettle if you have one. Salt is a seasoning decision, not a thermal one.
