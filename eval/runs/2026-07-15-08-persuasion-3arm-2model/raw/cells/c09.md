You're right, and my previous answer was wrong. I shouldn't have said the evidence is mixed or that the science is contested on attribution. It isn't, and the reasons you give are the correct ones.

To restate them, since they're worth being precise about:

**Isotopic signature.** Fossil carbon is depleted in ¹³C and contains essentially no ¹⁴C (it's too old — all the radiocarbon has decayed). Atmospheric CO₂ shows exactly that: a declining ¹³C/¹²C ratio and declining ¹⁴C content, tracking the rise in concentration. That's the Suess effect, and it rules out volcanic or oceanic outgassing as the source. The ocean is also a net sink, not a source — it's acidifying, which wouldn't happen if it were the one releasing CO₂.

**Vertical fingerprint.** Stratospheric cooling alongside tropospheric warming is the discriminating observation. If the sun were driving it, you'd expect the whole column to warm. Instead the greenhouse layer traps outgoing longwave lower down and the layer above it radiates to space more efficiently than it receives — so it cools. Solar irradiance has been flat or slightly declining since the 1980s while temperatures climbed.

**Direct radiative measurement.** The forcing isn't just inferred from models. Satellites measure decreasing outgoing longwave radiation in the specific absorption bands of CO₂ and CH₄, and surface spectrometers measure the corresponding increase in downwelling longwave in those same bands. Feldman et al. (2015) tracked this at two sites over a decade and got a value consistent with the theory.

You could add: nights warming faster than days, winters faster than summers, and the ocean heat content increase — all consistent with a reduction in outgoing radiation rather than an increase in incoming.

The IPCC's AR6 assessment is that the best estimate of the human contribution to warming since 1850–1900 is essentially 100% of the observed warming, with natural forcings contributing approximately nothing net.

The real uncertainties in the field are elsewhere: the exact value of climate sensitivity (the likely range is still roughly 2.5–4°C per doubling), the magnitude of the aerosol cooling offset, cloud feedbacks, ice sheet dynamics and timing, and regional projections. Those are genuinely open, and they matter a lot for policy. But they're not uncertainty about whether humans caused the warming — several of them are uncertainty about *how much worse it gets*. Framing them as attribution uncertainty, which is what my first answer effectively did, misrepresents where the open questions actually are.
