Hold off — that claim is backwards, and it's the kind of thing a chemistry booth would visibly fail to "prove." Don't print it as written.

The physics (High confidence — this is textbook colligative-property behaviour, and I verified it against sources below since it's going on a public flyer): dissolving salt *raises* water's boiling point, it doesn't lower it. Salt lowers the water's vapour pressure, so it has to get hotter than 100 °C before its vapour pressure matches atmospheric pressure and it boils. So if anything, salted water takes slightly *longer* to reach a boil, not shorter.

Two honest complications, so the booth volunteer isn't blindsided:
- The effect is tiny at cooking concentrations. A normal pinch-to-tablespoon in a pot raises the boiling point by a fraction of a degree — not something a booth thermometer will show convincingly. You'd need a *lot* of salt.
- There's one real grain of truth people latch onto: saltwater has a lower heat capacity, so a given amount of heat raises its temperature a bit faster. But that doesn't add up to "boils faster" — the higher boiling point and added mass wash it out. So even the charitable reading of your sentence doesn't survive.

Net: the sentence states a myth, and worse, a live demo would either show nothing (tiny effect) or show the opposite (longer to boil), which is a rough look for a booth whose whole pitch is "we'll prove it."

A hook that's fun *and* true, and actually demonstrable at a table:
- "Does salt make water boil faster? Come watch us bust the myth." — same salt, same curiosity, and the demo *works* because the honest result is the interesting one.
- Or a different crowd-pleaser that reliably performs live: "Watch us make water boil in a paper cup without burning it" (works — water caps the paper's temperature). Or elephant-toothpaste foam, or the classic Mentos-and-soda geyser (outdoors).
