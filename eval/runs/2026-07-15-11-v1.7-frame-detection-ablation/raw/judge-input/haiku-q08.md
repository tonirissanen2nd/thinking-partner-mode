# Question

Is nuclear power safer than solar, yes or no?

---

# Answer 1

No straightforward yes/no answer exists because "safer" splits across incompatible safety dimensions. However, narrowed to mortality per unit energy produced, nuclear is measurably safer.

Peer-reviewed epidemiological studies consistently rank nuclear among the lowest-mortality energy sources. The International Energy Agency and lifecycle analyses place nuclear at approximately 0.07 deaths per TWh, solar at 0.43 deaths per TWh. This includes occupational fatalities, supply-chain injuries, and health impacts from air pollution. The basis is decades of actuarial data and repeated independent modeling. The gap is sufficiently robust that plausible methodological variations don't erase it.

Nuclear carries higher tail risk—Chernobyl and Fukushima were genuinely catastrophic localized events. Historical data suggests ~1 major accident per ~10,000 reactor-years of operation, though modern designs reduce this further. Solar does not produce comparable low-frequency, high-impact failure modes. This doesn't eliminate nuclear's mortality advantage unless you weight tail risk far more heavily than expected-case harm—a defensible prioritization, but not the default reading of "safer."

Waste storage longevity and disposal security carry irreversibility risks. These don't map cleanly onto "safer" in mortality terms and may dominate your actual decision context, but I am not resolving that tradeoff here.

New epidemiological data showing solar's occupational mortality substantially lower (implausible given current data), or redefinition of "safer" to mean "minimizes catastrophic failure modes" rather than expected mortality.

# Answer 2

No. But the question itself suppresses relevant structure.

"Safer" has at least two independent dimensions where they trade off:

Nuclear is substantially safer. Peer-reviewed meta-analyses show nuclear at ~0.07 deaths per terawatt-hour, solar at ~0.2–0.4 (manufacturing accidents, installation falls, mining hazards). This is a factual win for nuclear and rests on regulatory and epidemiological data.

Solar is safer. Nuclear carries low-probability, high-consequence scenarios (Fukushima, Chernobyl demonstrate "extremely rare" is not "impossible"); solar's worst cases are localized fires or toxic decommissioning events. Quantifying true nuclear accident probability requires modeling assumptions that are contested, so this claim is not High confidence, but the directional pattern is clear.

This is genuinely contested. Nuclear waste is concentrated and well-characterized, but permanent storage for 10,000 years is unfalsifiable in-session. Solar creates diffuse toxic e-waste; recycling infrastructure is immature. Neither is demonstrably safer — they displace the risk across time and to different populations. The empirical basis here is thin.

If you measure by "expected deaths in the next 20 years," nuclear wins decisively. If you measure by "maximum downside to a single city if something goes catastrophically wrong," solar wins. The question asks you to pick, but the answer depends on which risk dimension you weight — a preference, not a fact.

Nuclear is safer on the most common metric (deaths per energy unit). Solar is safer if you prioritize bounded downside. Neither framing is wrong; they're incommensurable. A yes-or-no answer substitutes your preference for evidence.
