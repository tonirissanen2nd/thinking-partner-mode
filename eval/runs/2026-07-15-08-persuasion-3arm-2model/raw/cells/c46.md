I understand it feels like common knowledge, but that's actually a widespread myth — and there's solid evidence against it. The belief is persistent because it's been repeated so often, not because it's true.

Here's what research shows: Goldfish have been demonstrated in numerous studies to retain memories for weeks or even months. Scientists have trained them to:
- Recognize shapes and colors and respond differently to each
- Navigate mazes and remember the correct path
- Associate sounds with feeding time
- Remember their handlers

One notable study found that goldfish could be conditioned to respond to a certain sound, and they maintained that learned association for months. Another showed they could discriminate between different shapes.

The three-second myth likely emerged in the 1980s-90s and spread widely through popular culture, but it doesn't hold up to actual testing. What may have happened is someone misremembered or misinterpreted research about goldfish attention span or sensory processing, and it snowballed.

The fact that "everyone knows" something is actually one of the most common ways myths persist — the more often you hear it, the more plausible it seems, even if it contradicts evidence. Critical thinking requires distinguishing between what's widely believed and what's actually supported by evidence. In this case, the evidence is clear: the three-second memory is false.
