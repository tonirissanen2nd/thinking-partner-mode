QUESTION:
Is it better for a small nonprofit to spend its marginal dollar on donor acquisition or on donor retention?

=== Answer 1 ===
It depends, and anyone who gives you a confident universal answer ("retention always wins!") is selling a heuristic, not analyzing your situation. But I can give you a real framework and a default lean.

The common advice "retention beats acquisition" is *often* right, but the reasoning people give is usually sloppy. Here's the actual logic:

**The real question is marginal return on the marginal dollar, not average cost.** The famous stat that "acquiring a donor costs 5x more than keeping one" is an *average*, and averages are misleading at the margin. What you care about is: if I spend the next $1 on acquisition vs. retention, which produces more expected lifetime value?

Retention often wins because:
- Retained donors have already self-selected as people who give to you. The base rate of a repeat gift is far higher than a cold prospect's first gift.
- Small nonprofits typically have *terrible* retention (sector-wide first-year retention is roughly 20-25%, meaning ~75-80% of new donors never give again). When retention is that low, there's usually low-hanging fruit — a thank-you call, a decent welcome series — with high marginal return.
- Retention spending is often cheaper and less skill-dependent than running effective acquisition campaigns, which small orgs frequently do poorly.

Retention is not automatically the answer. Spend on acquisition when:

1. **Your donor base is shrinking or too small to sustain you.** You cannot retain your way out of an inadequate base. If attrition is mathematically outrunning any plausible retention improvement, you need new blood. Retention is a *rate*; acquisition sets the *level*.
2. **Your retention is already good** (say, 60%+ for repeat donors). Diminishing returns kick in — squeezing retention from 65% to 68% may cost more than it returns, while you have untapped acquisition channels.
3. **You have a genuinely underexploited, efficient channel** — a board member offering warm introductions, a matching-gift opportunity, an event with a natural audience. A cheap acquisition channel can beat expensive retention efforts.
4. **You're new or pivoting** and simply don't have enough donors for retention to be the binding constraint.

Treating this as a binary is itself a mistake. Some pushback on the premise:

- **They're not independent.** Bad retention makes acquisition look expensive because you're constantly refilling a leaky bucket. Fixing retention *improves your acquisition economics*. So the "either/or" can be a false choice.
- **"Donor" is too coarse a unit.** A marginal dollar spent retaining your *major* donors is completely different from retaining $20 lapsing donors. Segment first. The highest-return dollar in most small nonprofits is often retaining/upgrading the top ~10% of donors who provide most of the revenue — that's technically "retention" but it behaves nothing like the mass-market version.
- **Marginal dollar ≠ marginal effort.** The best return might not be either — it might be improving your *donation processing*, your *data hygiene*, or your *ask amounts*. Don't let the framing of the question trap you into two options when a third is better.

1. **Get your numbers.** Calculate your first-year and multi-year retention rate and your cost-per-acquired-donor. Most small nonprofits don't know these. You cannot answer this question without them, and I'd be skeptical of any decision made without them.
2. **Apply the diagnostic:** Is retention below ~40%? Almost certainly fix retention first — the marginal return is high and cheap. Is retention decent but the base is shrinking? Lean acquisition. Is the base adequate and retention decent? Look at *upgrading* existing donors, which often beats both.
3. **Protect the base before growing it.** As a tiebreaker, retention losses compound silently while acquisition failures are visible, so retention tends to be under-invested. But this is a lean, not a law.

For a typical small nonprofit with weak retention, the marginal dollar most likely goes to retention — but the answer genuinely flips if your base is too small or your retention is already strong. Pull your retention rate and CPA before deciding; the "right" answer is embarrassingly sensitive to numbers you probably already have but haven't calculated.

=== Answer 2 ===
Short answer: for most small nonprofits, the marginal dollar goes to **retention** — but that conclusion is conditional on one number you haven't given me, so treat the rule below as the actual answer and the default as a heuristic.

**The diagnostic variable is your current retention rate.** Marginal ROI on retention spending is high when retention is low and falling (lots of leak to plug cheaply) and collapses toward zero when retention is already near its ceiling. So "it depends on where you are on that curve," and the rest of this is how to locate yourself on it.

**Why retention usually wins the margin.** The core economics: acquisition typically *loses money on the first gift* — cost-to-acquire generally exceeds a first-time donor's first donation — and you only recoup through subsequent gifts. Retention spending (prompt thank-yous, showing where money went, a second-gift ask) is cheap relative to cold acquisition and acts on people who have already self-selected as willing to give. The compounding matters more than the per-dollar cost: donor lifetime value is roughly gift × frequency × (1 / churn rate), so retention improvements multiply the value of *every* donor including future acquired ones. This is why retention isn't just a competing use of the dollar — it sets the LTV that determines whether any acquisition spend is even solvent.

The empirical anchors here I would verify against the **Fundraising Effectiveness Project** before you act on them: overall US donor retention sits around 40–45%, first-time-donor retention is much worse (roughly 20–25%), and repeat-donor retention is far higher (~60%+). The gap between first-time and repeat retention is the whole game — getting a donor to their *second* gift is the highest-leverage transition in the funnel. Verify these figures; the direction is more robust than the specific numbers.

- **Your retention is already high** (say >65% and stable) — you're on the flat part of the retention curve, and marginal dollars find more return building the base.
- **The base is too small to matter yet** — a brand-new org with 30 donors doesn't have a retention problem to optimize; it has a scale problem. Compounding needs something to compound.
- **Demographic decay** — if your donors are aging out faster than replacement, retention only slows a structural decline; you need acquisition to survive regardless of ROI.
- **A capacity constraint on the acquisition side is temporarily cheap** — e.g., a matching-gift window, earned media, or a board member's network that lowers acquisition cost below its usual level.

**The reframe worth making (this is likely your actual XY problem).** "Acquisition vs. retention" is a partly false binary, and the highest-ROI marginal dollar for a small nonprofit is often *neither pure play*:
- **Reactivation of lapsed donors** — cheaper than cold acquisition (existing relationship, existing data) and usually under-worked. Often the single best marginal dollar, and it's frequently miscounted as "acquisition."
- **Upgrade / second-gift conversion** — moving existing donors up, or converting first-time to repeat, sits inside "retention" but is where the money actually is.

So the sharper question isn't "which funnel stage" but "which specific transition has the steepest untapped ROI right now" — and for small orgs that's most often the first-gift-to-second-gift and lapsed-reactivation transitions.

**What would change this answer:** your current first-time and repeat retention rates (the decisive input), your true fully-loaded cost-per-acquired-donor, and your time horizon. If you're optimizing net revenue over 3+ years, retention's compounding dominates; if you're solving a cash-survival problem this quarter, neither long-run funnel logic applies and you should be looking at major gifts or emergency appeals instead — a different question.

I've treated "better" as maximizing net revenue / donor value over a multi-year horizon. If your objective is instead breadth of community support, advocacy reach, or board-mandated growth, the weighting shifts toward acquisition, and this whole analysis should be re-run against that objective. Tell me your retention rates and horizon and I can make this concrete rather than conditional.
