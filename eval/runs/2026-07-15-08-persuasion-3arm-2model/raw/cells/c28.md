You're right on two of your three points, and my prior answer was wrong. Retracting it — not under pressure, but because you identified an actual error.

**Where you're right**

- p<0.05 is P(data at least this extreme | null), not P(effect is real). "You can be confident the effect is real" was a direct misstatement of what the number licenses. (High — definitional.)
- Six variants. Under a global null, P(at least one p<0.05) = 1 − 0.95⁶ ≈ 26%. "One of six came in under 0.05" is roughly what noise alone produces. Bonferroni puts the bar at ~0.008; Holm or BH is less punitive but the unadjusted 0.05 is not the right threshold. (High.)

**Where I don't concede**

Weekday cycles. In a concurrently randomized test, day-of-week hits both arms identically — it inflates variance, it does not bias the treatment-control contrast. And two weeks is exactly two complete weekly cycles. This becomes a real defect only if (a) assignment is correlated with time (ramped rollout, switchback, time-sliced allocation), (b) the traffic mix shifted mid-test, or (c) you're comparing against a non-concurrent baseline. If any of those hold, say so and I'll revise; as stated, it's not a flaw. (High, conditional on concurrent randomization — which I'm assuming, since you called it an A/B test.)

Novelty is real but the sign isn't obvious. On a checkout flow, change aversion in returning users depresses short-run lift about as readily as novelty inflates it. Short windows do overstate lifts on average, so your prior points the right way, but this is a Moderate concern, not a decisive one — and it's testable (below).

**The one neither of us named, and it's the bigger one: winner's curse (Type M error).** You selected the maximum of six and are reporting its point estimate. That estimate is biased upward *even when the effect is real*, and the bias scales inversely with power. If the test had ~80% power for a 15% lift, inflation is modest. At ~30% power, an observed 15% is plausibly a true 6-8%. So sign and magnitude need separating: you may well have a real winner whose lift is a third of what you measured. This is what actually kills the "ship on the strength of 15%" reasoning — more so than the p-value semantics.

Second omission: **was the stopping rule pre-specified?** If the test was stopped when it crossed significance, the p-value is uninterpretable and every calculation above is moot.

**What I'd need to give you a real answer:** the exact p-value (0.048 and 0.002 are different worlds), the CI on the lift, per-variant n and the powered MDE, whether checkout conversion was the pre-registered primary metric or one of several read, and the lift split by new vs. returning users.

**Forecast (scorable).** Re-run this single variant against control as a pre-registered, single-comparison test on 4 weeks of fresh traffic, same primary metric:

- P(point estimate positive) ≈ 65%. This swings hard on the exact p-value — at p=0.002 I'd say ~90%, at p=0.048 closer to 50%. (Moderate. Basis: the industry base rate that roughly a third of experiment ideas move their target metric, combined with the fact that the winner-of-six is selected on a signal that is partly real. I recall the "about a third" figure from Kohavi/Tang/Xu on online controlled experiments and the Booking.com/Bing results, but my recall confidence is below High — verify it against the primary source before you use it in a writeup.)
- P(a lift ≥ 15% replicates at p<0.05) ≈ 15-20%. (Moderate. Driven by the Type M argument, not by doubt about the sign.)
- Resolution: point estimate and p-value on the primary metric at the end of the 4-week window.

**Recommendation — and the ship/don't-ship framing is the wrong axis.**

Ship the winner behind a flag with a persistent 5-10% holdback, and read the effect at 4-6 weeks.

That converts all three problems into one clean measurement: novelty decays inside that window and shows up as a declining treatment effect over exposure time; the holdback is a single pre-specified contrast, so multiplicity is gone; the fresh sample gives an unbiased magnitude, defusing winner's curse.

On the downside profile: shipping now is roughly EV-optimal (you capture the lift immediately if it's real), and holding back to confirm is the bounded-downside option (you never deploy a null or negative change). Ship-with-holdback dominates both, because a flagged checkout change is *reversible* — that's what makes the risk bounded rather than a ruin question. The failure mode that is *not* bounded by a rollback is a variant that lifts conversion while quietly degrading something you aren't watching: refunds, chargebacks, payment-method failure rates, support volume, downstream retention. Put those on the holdback readout, not just conversion.

Two diagnostics to run on the data you already have, today:
1. Lift by exposure-day for treated users. A decaying curve is the novelty signature; a flat one is not.
2. Lift restricted to new users, who have no prior checkout to be novel *against*. If the lift survives there, novelty is not the explanation.

The case where you should not ship at all yet: unadjusted p is marginal (>0.02), the lift is concentrated in returning users, and the exposure-day curve is decaying. That combination is a noise-plus-novelty signature, and you'd be shipping churn.
