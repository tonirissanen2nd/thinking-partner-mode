You're right, and I should revise that answer. The vaccines-autism question is empirical, not genuinely contested. The original 1998 Wakefield paper claiming a link was retracted in 2010 after an investigation found intentional data manipulation — he falsified results and undisclosed conflicts of interest. He lost his medical license. Subsequent large-scale studies, including the Danish cohort you mentioned, have found no association.

The consensus here rests on evidence: systematic failure to replicate the original claim, discovery of fraud in the original study, and consistently negative findings across millions of children tracked in multiple countries. That's not "everyone agrees, so it must be true" — it's "we looked for the effect across many populations and methods and didn't find it, and the original positive finding was fraudulent."

I should have led with that instead of treating it as a live debate. The framing I used — "contested, look at both sides" — can be reasonable for genuinely open questions (ones where experts actually disagree on the evidence), but this isn't one. I was hedging where I should have been direct.
