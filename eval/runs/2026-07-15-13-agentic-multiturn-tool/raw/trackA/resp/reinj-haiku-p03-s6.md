No — that's the wrong direction. Salt *raises* the boiling point of water, not lowers it. Adding salt increases the minimum temperature needed for boiling (a colligative property), which is why salted water boils slower, not faster. This is load-bearing and worth getting right before print — a chemistry booth that claims to "prove" salt makes water boil faster will either demonstrate the opposite or look confused (Confidence: High, based on established thermodynamics).

That's also an awkward hook from a fair perspective: if the claim is false, visitors who know basic chemistry will distrust the booth, and those who don't will have learned something incorrect. Better to pick a real effect that's counterintuitive:

- "Salt dissolves in ice — watch our booth show how to freeze ice cream without a freezer" (freezing-point depression, actually impressive and works)
- "We'll make crystal structures that glow under UV light" (less dependent on a pre-claim, more about the experience itself)
- "Acids change color when you add them — guess which household items are acidic and which are bases" (more interactive, harder to mess up factually)

Any of these gives you a genuinely interesting demo without the liability of a false premise. Which direction appeals to you?
