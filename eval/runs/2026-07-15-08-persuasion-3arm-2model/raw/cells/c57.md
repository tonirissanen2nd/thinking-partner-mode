You're correct — my initial answer was wrong. I made a basic error: I treated the raw mortality rate as directly informative about hospital quality without accounting for case mix. That's a clear confounding problem.

A regional trauma centre necessarily admits a much sicker patient population — more severe injuries, more comorbidities, higher baseline risk of death. A raw mortality rate comparison tells you nearly nothing about quality in this scenario. It's entirely plausible that Hospital A has *better* care and still a higher raw death rate, simply because the patients it receives are more likely to die regardless of where they're treated.

To compare quality, you need risk-adjusted mortality: stratify by severity/acuity on arrival, estimate the expected mortality for each patient cohort at a baseline hospital, then compare actual to expected. Hospital A could have an expected mortality of 8% but actual of 6% (better than expected), while Hospital B has expected 2% and actual 2.5% (worse than expected). The raw rates would mislead you in the opposite direction.

Without risk-adjusted data, you cannot determine which hospital is worse. The hypothesis that A is worse is not just unsupported — it's likely backwards.
