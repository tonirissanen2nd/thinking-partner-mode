Hold off — that claim is a common myth, and it's backwards. Don't put it on the flyer as written.

My recall here is High confidence, and since this is going on public print I checked it rather than trust memory. The verification confirmed the recall:

- Dissolving salt **raises** water's boiling point (boiling-point elevation) — salted water boils at a slightly *higher* temperature (~102°C vs 100°C at seawater-strength concentrations), so it takes marginally *longer* to reach a boil, not shorter.
- There's a real competing effect that feeds the myth: salt water has a lower specific heat, so a given amount of energy raises its temperature a touch faster. But at cooking concentrations (a teaspoon or two per litre) both effects are a few seconds' worth — negligible, and the boiling-point term wins. You'd need north of ~20% salt by mass to see anything meaningful.

Net: "salt makes water boil faster" is false as stated, and — this is the part that matters for you — a booth *cannot* prove it. A live demo would show no visible difference, or the honest version would show salted water boiling a hair slower. Printing "our booth will prove it" sets up a demo that disproves your own flyer in front of the crowd.

The fix is easy, because the true version is a better hook anyway — myth-busting demos land well with a mixed-age fair audience. A few that are both correct and actually demonstrable at a table:

- **"Does salt make water boil faster? Come watch us bust the myth."** Two small pots, one salted, side by side on burners — the reveal is the fun. (Requires safe hotplates + supervision; folds into your permits/safety bucket.)
- **Salt lowers ice's melting point** — the true, visible cousin of your claim. Ice + salt demo, easy and safe, ties into why roads get salted.
- **Non-boiling chemistry that's flashier and safer for a public table:** baking-soda-and-vinegar reactions, red-cabbage pH indicator, or density-column layering. No open flame, no burn risk, no myth exposure.
