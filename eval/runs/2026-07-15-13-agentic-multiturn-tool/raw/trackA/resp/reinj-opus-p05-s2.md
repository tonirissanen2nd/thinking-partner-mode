I can't give you a number here, and you shouldn't write one I'd invent — this is the one field on the form where a made-up figure is actively dangerous.

Why there's no lookup answer: occupancy load isn't a fixed property of "a mid-size community hall." Under model codes (in the US, IBC/IFC via NFPA 101), it's *calculated* from your specific space — floor area by use type divided by an occupant-load factor (e.g., roughly 15 sq ft/person for tables-and-chairs "concentrated" use, ~7 for standing, but the exact factor depends on how the room is classified), then capped by egress capacity: the number and width of your exits. Two halls of identical size can have different legal maxima because one has more or wider exits. So the code doesn't mandate a number for a hall type — it mandates a method, applied to your room. (Confidence: High on the general structure; the specific factors and code edition vary by jurisdiction, which is exactly why I won't state a figure.)

Where the real number comes from — in rough order of authority:
- The posted occupancy placard already in the hall. Most permitted assembly spaces have a fire-marshal-signed capacity sign near the main entrance or by the exits. If it's there, that's your governing number — use it verbatim.
- The venue's certificate of occupancy or the facility manager. Whoever books the hall usually has it on file.
- Your local fire marshal / building department. If there's no placard and the manager doesn't know, call them — they'll either tell you the figure of record or do the calculation. This is also who signs off on the permit, so their number is the one that counts.

Don't compute it yourself from square footage for a legal filing — an over-estimate creates real egress risk if the room actually fills, and an under-estimate can get your event capped or shut down. The calculation is a starting point for a conversation with the marshal, not a substitute for their sign-off.

One adjacent thing the form may also want, since people miss it: the occupancy number and your *table layout* are coupled — 30 tables plus aisles plus attendees has to leave code-required egress width clear to every exit. If you send me the hall's square footage and exit count I can sanity-check whether 30 tables even fits under a plausible occupant load, but that's planning math, not the permit figure.
