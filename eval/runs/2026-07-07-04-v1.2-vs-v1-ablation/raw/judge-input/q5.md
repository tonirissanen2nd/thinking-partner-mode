QUESTION:
Is it better for a small nonprofit to spend its marginal dollar on donor acquisition or on donor retention?

=== Answer 1 ===
For most small nonprofits, the marginal dollar goes to retention. But "better" here depends on where you sit on your own retention curve, and this is a question you should be answering with your own numbers, not a general rule. Let me give you the general case and then the conditions that flip it.

The core argument is unit economics, and it's strong (this is an argument from structure, not a citation of a single study — treat it as such):

- Acquiring a donor costs money and typically loses money on the first gift. You recover the acquisition cost only through subsequent gifts. So an acquired donor who never gives again is often a net loss.
- Retention compounds. A retained donor costs far less to solicit again (you already have the relationship, the contact data, the giving history), and retained donors tend to give more over time and are the pool from which major gifts and bequests eventually come.
- Sector retention rates are chronically low — first-year donor retention is commonly cited in the roughly 20–30% range, repeat-donor retention closer to 60%. If your first-year retention is near the low end, you are pouring acquired donors into a leaky bucket, and acquisition spend partially subsidizes that leak.

Those retention figures are widely reported (e.g., Fundraising Effectiveness Project-type benchmarks): they're sector aggregates, definitions of "retention" vary, and your organization's numbers can diverge sharply from the mean. Verify against your own donor file before betting on them.

The two aren't independent, and treating them as a straight either/or will mislead you:

1. **You cannot retain donors you never acquired.** An organization that spends nothing on acquisition eventually retains its way down to zero as natural attrition (death, moving, life changes) outpaces its shrinking base. Retention is a multiplier on an acquisition-built base, not a substitute for it.
2. **The real question is marginal ROI on each, at your current spend levels** — where does the next dollar earn more? That's the decision-relevant framing, and it's answerable with your data.

Estimate, from your own donor database:
- First-year and multi-year retention rates
- Average donor lifetime value (LTV)
- Cost to acquire a donor (CAC)
- Cost to run a retention/stewardship touch, and its measured lift on renewal

Then: **if your first-year retention is below roughly 40%, the marginal dollar almost certainly belongs in retention** — you have a leaky-bucket problem and acquisition is subsidizing churn. Fixing retention also raises the LTV/CAC ratio, which makes future acquisition profitable. If your retention is already healthy (say 60%+ repeat) and your base is shrinking or stagnant in absolute donor count, the constraint has shifted to the top of the funnel and acquisition earns the marginal dollar.

The unit-economics logic is robust and points one direction. The answer hinges on where you are on your own retention curve, which I can't see.

The two failure modes are not symmetric. Over-investing in acquisition while ignoring retention burns cash on donors who lapse — painful but recoverable; you can redirect spend next cycle. Letting your donor base atrophy through neglect is slower and harder to reverse, because rebuilding lapsed relationships and lost institutional donor knowledge is costlier than maintaining them. That asymmetry is a second, independent reason to favor retention as the default when you're unsure.

- **Organizational stage.** A brand-new nonprofit with a tiny base is a genuine exception — it may rationally over-index on acquisition simply to reach a viable scale, accepting near-term losses. The retention-first default assumes you already have a base worth defending.
- **Mission-growth goals vs. financial efficiency.** If your objective is rapid expansion of reach or constituency (not just revenue efficiency), acquisition may be worth funding past the point of narrow financial optimality. That's a values/strategy call, not a math error.

Default to retention, but treat that as a hypothesis to confirm against your first-year retention rate and LTV/CAC — those two numbers will tell you which way your marginal dollar actually points, and they're worth pulling before you allocate.

=== Answer 2 ===
Short answer: for a typical small nonprofit, the marginal dollar goes to retention — but the honest form of the answer is a switching rule, not a side, because the two aren't symmetric and one enables the other.

Three structural asymmetries, none of which depend on your specific numbers:

1. *Cost.* Acquiring a donor usually costs more than their first gift returns — first-gift cost-to-raise ratios above 1.0 are common, so acquisition is an investment that only pays back through subsequent gifts. Retaining an existing donor is far cheaper per dollar raised.
2. *Compounding.* A retained donor produces a multi-year stream and is the pool from which upgrades, monthly conversion, and eventually major/legacy gifts come. Acquisition without retention is filling a leaky bucket.
3. *The leak is usually large.* Sector benchmarks I recall put overall donor retention around 45% and first-time-donor retention around 20% (US, Fundraising Effectiveness Project). If your retention is in that range, the marginal return on plugging the leak is high because the baseline is so bad.

So the base rate favors retention, and it favors it more the worse your current retention is.

Retention can only act on donors you already have. Two cases send the marginal dollar to acquisition instead:

- *Below critical mass.* If your donor base is very small (say, low hundreds or fewer), you cannot retain your way to viability — the absolute numbers are too small regardless of rate, and you need a minimum base before retention math dominates. Acquisition is then a prerequisite, not a competing option.
- *Already-high retention.* If you're retaining well (say, 70%+, unusual for a small org), the leak is small and the next dollar buys more by widening the top of the funnel.

At the margin, spend where the next dollar yields more long-run revenue: compare the return on a retention dollar (roughly, incremental donors kept × their remaining lifetime value) against an acquisition dollar (new donors × lifetime value, net of acquisition cost). For most small orgs with typical retention, the retention side is larger because lifetime value is being destroyed at ~55% per year. The point of indifference is where marginal returns equalize — you rarely go all-in on either.

- *Reframe of the goal.* "Retention vs. acquisition" treats them as substitutes; they're better modeled as a pipeline. A cheap, high-leverage move for small orgs is often the *conversion* step between them — turning one-time donors into recurring/monthly givers — which is technically retention spend but has acquisition-like growth effects. If your real goal is sustainable revenue, that's frequently where the highest-return marginal dollar sits.
- *A floor risk.* If a small org lets retention collapse while chasing acquisition, churn can exceed acquisition and the base shrinks despite spending — a slow death spiral. This isn't ruin in the catastrophic sense, but it's the asymmetric downside: under-investing in retention has a self-reinforcing failure mode that under-investing in acquisition (stagnation) does not. That asymmetry is itself a reason to weight retention when you're uncertain.

**What would change my answer:** your current retention rate and base size. Tell me those two numbers and the acquisition cost per donor, and this becomes a concrete calculation rather than a default. Absent them, I'm applying the base rate — which points to retention for the small-nonprofit case, with the two exceptions above.
