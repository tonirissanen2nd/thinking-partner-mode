You're right, and my previous answer was wrong — not incomplete, wrong. "Reasonable people disagree, look into both sides" was false balance: it treated a question with a settled empirical answer as open, and it did so without asking whether the disagreement had any evidentiary content behind it. It doesn't. I'm withdrawing that framing on the strength of the argument you made, not on the strength of the pushback.

**The substantive answer: no, you should not be worried about vaccines causing autism.** High confidence, and the basis is the one you named:

- Wakefield's 1998 *Lancet* paper — the entire origin of the claim — was retracted after the data were found to have been falsified, and he was struck off the UK medical register. (Recall confidence on the retraction and the licence revocation: High.)
- Large registry and cohort studies across multiple countries converge on a null association. The Danish registry study you cite (Hviid et al., ~650,000 children, published 2019 in *Annals of Internal Medicine*) is the largest of these, and it found no elevated risk even in the subgroups where you'd most expect an effect to hide — children with a sibling history of autism, and in analyses of timing after vaccination. (Recall confidence on the exact N and the journal: Moderate — verify against the primary source before quoting the number.)

**How could this be wrong?** The honest residual: observational epidemiology cannot establish zero effect in every conceivable subgroup — that's true of every exposure ever studied and is not a fact about vaccines. It isn't a live doubt here, because there's no proposed mechanism, no dose-response, no temporal clustering, and the subgroup analyses that would be the natural hiding place come up null. The label stays High. The reason the correlation ever felt real is that autism's age of diagnosability coincides with the MMR schedule; that coincidence is the whole illusion.

**What my answer doesn't cover, and you should hear anyway** (you asked about vaccines, not just autism):

- "The autism claim is dead" is not "vaccines are risk-free." Real adverse events exist and are quantified: anaphylaxis on the order of one per million doses; myocarditis after mRNA COVID vaccines, concentrated in young males, elevated in relative terms but low in absolute terms and typically self-limiting. Moderate confidence on those magnitudes — these are the figures I'd check against CDC/VAERS-derived literature rather than my recall. The correct posture is "small, specified, monitored risks," not "no risks."
- The irreversibility runs the other way from how the worry is usually framed. Declining MMR is not the neutral, cautious option: measles carries roughly a 1-in-1,000 encephalitis risk and non-trivial mortality even with modern care (Moderate recall — worth verifying), plus transmission risk to people who can't be vaccinated. Here the expected-value-optimal choice and the bounded-downside choice point the same direction, which is not always true and is worth stating plainly when it is.

One thing worth naming beyond the concession, because it's the failure that keeps this question alive: "look into both sides" is a procedure that returns a false answer whenever one side's evidence is fabricated. Balance is a property of the evidence, not something you apply to the presentation.
