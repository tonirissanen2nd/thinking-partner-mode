You're right, and my first answer was wrong. Raw mortality confounds quality with case mix. A regional trauma centre admits penetrating trauma, major burns, complex transfers from smaller hospitals — patients with a high baseline probability of dying regardless of what anyone does. A community hospital that stabilises and transfers its sickest cases exports those deaths onto someone else's ledger. This is the standard selection problem, and it's why raw rates can rank hospitals in almost exactly the wrong order.

What actually bears on the question:

**Risk-adjusted mortality.** Observed deaths divided by deaths predicted from patient characteristics (age, comorbidities, injury severity, physiology on arrival). An O/E ratio below 1 means the hospital does better than expected given who walks through its doors. For trauma specifically, the usual instruments are TRISS or the more current TQIP benchmarking; for general medical admissions, CMS publishes 30-day risk-standardised mortality for particular conditions (heart attack, heart failure, pneumonia, COPD).

**Condition-specific, not hospital-wide.** "Is A worse" is nearly unanswerable as posed. Hospitals are not uniformly good or bad — the same institution can be excellent at cardiac surgery and mediocre at stroke care. The comparison only means something once you name the thing you'd be going in for.

**Volume for the relevant procedure.** For complex surgery and major trauma, the volume-outcome relationship is one of the more robust findings in health services research. High-volume centres tend to do better at the hard things.

Two caveats on the other side, so I don't overcorrect. Risk adjustment is imperfect and gameable — models rely on coded comorbidities, and hospitals that code aggressively look sicker on paper and therefore better on O/E. And adjustment can only condition on what's measured; unmeasured severity persists. So a risk-adjusted difference is evidence, not proof, and small differences between two hospitals are usually noise.
