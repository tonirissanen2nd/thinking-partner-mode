The answer hinges on a definition the question leaves open, so I'll fix one: "significantly worse than COVID-19" = global mortality materially above COVID's toll (~20–27M excess deaths on most reconstructions, vs. ~7M officially reported) — roughly 1918-scale or beyond in per-capita terms. 1918 killed ~2–3% of the world's population; COVID ~0.25%, so ~10x deadlier per head. Resolution window: 2026–2046, judged on excess-mortality estimates. A looser bar ("somewhat worse") would raise every number below.

The risk splits into two channels that must be separated, because one is estimable and one is not.

1. Natural zoonotic pandemic. This has a reference class. Marani et al. (2021, PNAS) estimated the annual probability of an extreme novel epidemic at roughly 1918 scale in the low-single-digit percent per year, and argued it is rising with spillover pressure (land-use change, livestock density, air travel). Flag: I recall the paper's structure but not its exact figures — the ~2%/yr number should be checked against the primary source before you lean on it. Taking ~2%/yr as a central anchor: 1 − (0.98)^20 ≈ 33% over 20 years. I'd widen that to a ~20–40% range given base-rate uncertainty. The estimate rests on very few extreme events (a handful across ~4 centuries of usable data), so the base rate itself is poorly pinned, and the tail is heavy enough that the historical record can't contain it.

2. Engineered or lab-origin pandemic (accidental release from gain-of-function work, or deliberate misuse). Here a point probability would mislead. There is no historical reference class for a deliberate catastrophic engineered pandemic (zero events); the enabling technology — synthesis cost, AI-assisted design, openly published methods — is changing fast enough that even a good historical base rate wouldn't transfer; and the outcome is fat-tailed. This is non-estimable, not merely uncertain, so I won't attach a number to it. The useful question is not "what's the probability" but "what exposure makes the question moot": this tail is governed by dual-use research oversight and DNA-synthesis screening, not by anything a forecast changes.

Putting it together: the natural-channel figure (~20–40% over 20 years) is a floor, not the total. The engineered/accidental channel is non-estimable and, on most informed views, growing — so the true aggregate risk is higher than the natural-only number but cannot honestly be compressed into a single percentage. Anyone quoting a clean point estimate for the *total* (a "5%" or a "50%") is reporting false precision: the natural part is roughly estimable, the part that increasingly drives the tail is not.

What would move the natural estimate: a verified, updated base rate from the primary literature; shifts in spillover indicators; and demonstrated effect — up or down — of post-COVID surveillance and mRNA-platform response speed.

Omissions: (a) "emerging" vs. "becoming a full-blown pandemic" — I've read your question as the latter; (b) I've treated response capacity only lightly, yet faster medical countermeasures could genuinely cap severity in a way an 1918-anchored base rate doesn't capture (it cuts both ways — wider biotech capability also raises the engineered tail); (c) the whole answer is sensitive to the severity threshold — drop it from "1918-scale" to "modestly worse than COVID" and the natural-channel probability rises substantially.
