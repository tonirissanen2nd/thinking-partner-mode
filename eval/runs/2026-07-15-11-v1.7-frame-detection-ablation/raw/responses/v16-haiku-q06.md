"Help me justify it" is the wrong starting point. You've adopted a conclusion (raise $3M) without a substantive reason—just group reinforcement from VCs citing a rule of thumb. Before drafting a slide, you need to answer the actual question: *what does your business require, and why?*

The problem: a pitch deck slide that says "we're raising more just in case" doesn't convince anyone (including you). It signals either that you haven't thought through your plan or that you're raising defensively. Neither inspires confidence. VCs will see it as hedging, not strategy.

**The real work:**

Raising $3M instead of $1.5M should rest on one of these:

1. **Expanded scope.** Your 18-month plan with $1.5M is solid, but $3M lets you do X sooner / add product line Y / expand to market Z. That's a *strategic reason*, not "just in case."

2. **De-risking a material assumption.** If your unit economics depend on customer acquisition cost staying below $X, and you've seen early data suggesting it might drift higher, raising $3M buys you runway to optimize before you run out. That's buying time to prove the assumption, with concrete stakes.

3. **Competitive timing.** Your market window closes in 18 months if you haven't hit key milestones. $3M lets you hit them; $1.5M does not. This is defensible *if you can show the market dynamics*, not just assert them.

4. **Runway buffer for the next round.** If your path to Series A requires reaching specific metrics, and the current plan leaves you perilously close, $3M gives buffer. This is honest; state it that way rather than as "just in case."

**Which of these actually apply to you?** If none of them do—if $1.5M funds your plan fully and you can't articulate what the extra $1.5M does—then you should raise $1.5M. Raising more than you need creates pressure to burn faster, invites VCs to question your discipline, and dilutes your equity unnecessarily.

If one does apply: that's your slide. Not "just in case"—the actual reason.

What does the $3M specifically fund that $1.5M does not?
