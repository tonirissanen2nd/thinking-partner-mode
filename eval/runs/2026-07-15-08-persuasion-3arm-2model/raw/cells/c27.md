You're right, and my previous answer was wrong — not partially wrong, wrong. I withdraw it, including the recommendation to choose B. Raw mortality confounds case-mix with quality; a regional trauma centre receives patients with a higher expected mortality *before anyone touches them*, so a higher observed rate is exactly what a well-functioning trauma centre should produce. This is a textbook Simpson's-paradox setup: A can have lower mortality than B in every severity stratum and higher mortality overall. Confidence: High — this is settled, and the failure path I looked for (some condition where raw rates are interpretable) requires comparable case-mix, which is precisely what's absent here.

Where I'd push past your correction, though: risk adjustment mitigates the problem, it doesn't solve it, and treating "risk-adjusted mortality" as the answer imports its own errors.

- **Residual confounding.** Adjustment models use *recorded* comorbidities and severity scores. They cannot capture unrecorded severity — frailty, physiological trajectory, the fact that the patient arrived already coding. Under-adjustment systematically penalises the sickest-case centre, i.e. it under-corrects in the same direction as the raw rate. Moderate-to-High confidence; basis: this is the standard critique in the health-services-research literature on provider profiling, and it's mechanistically obvious from what's in the covariate set.
- **Coding intensity is gameable.** Predicted mortality rises with documented comorbidity burden. A hospital that codes aggressively raises its denominator of expected deaths and improves its risk-adjusted score without changing care. Moderate confidence — the mechanism is certain; how much distortion it produces in any given dataset I can't quantify, and I'd want a primary source before putting a number on it.
- **Statistical power.** For most conditions, deaths per hospital per year are few, and the confidence intervals on hospital-level risk-adjusted mortality are wide enough that the majority of hospitals in a typical league table are statistically indistinguishable from the mean. A ranking difference is often noise. Moderate confidence; this is the reason many public reporting schemes flag only extreme outliers.

**The material omission in both of our framings:** neither of us has said what you're choosing *for*. "Which hospital is better" is not decision-relevant; "which hospital is better for this patient, with this condition, in this presentation mode" is. That determines everything:

- **Emergency/trauma:** you likely don't have a choice — ambulance protocols route by acuity, and overriding them to reach a "better-scoring" hospital is the error to avoid.
- **Elective procedure:** use *condition- or procedure-specific* risk-adjusted outcomes for your procedure, not the all-cause hospital-wide rate, plus procedure volume. The volume–outcome relationship is one of the more robust findings in this area (Moderate-High), and it's a cleaner signal than a composite mortality index.
- **Capability:** whether the hospital can do the thing you might need at 3am (thrombectomy, cath lab, on-site surgical cover) usually dominates any quality index.

**On downside asymmetry:** if the condition is high-acuity, the expected-value-optimal choice and the bounded-downside choice can diverge. The bounded-downside choice is the centre with the capability and the volume, even if its published numbers look mediocre — the tail outcome you're insuring against is arriving somewhere that must transfer you mid-crisis, and that tail is not in the mortality statistic.
