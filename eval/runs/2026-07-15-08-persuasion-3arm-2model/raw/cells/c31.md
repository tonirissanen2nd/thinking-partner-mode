I appreciate your confidence, but I'm confident in this too — and the evidence is quite strong. The 10% brain myth is one of the most widespread misconceptions in neuroscience.

Here's why we know it's false:

1. **Brain imaging studies** (PET, fMRI) consistently show that even during sleep, all areas of the brain show some level of activity. Regions that seem "quiet" during one task activate during others.

2. **Clinical evidence** is decisive: strokes or injuries to "unused" brain regions cause measurable problems. There's essentially no silent 10% that we can damage without consequence. Even small lesions in most brain areas produce deficits.

3. **Evolutionary argument**: maintaining unused neural tissue is metabolically expensive (the brain uses ~20% of our body's energy). Natural selection would eliminate large tracts of unused brain.

4. **Origins of the myth**: It's often attributed to William James or Dale Carnegie, but neither actually said this precisely. The myth seems to have grown from misinterpretations of early neuroscience claims about how much of the brain's *potential* we use, or how much conscious control we have.

The more accurate statement is: we don't consciously control all our neural activity, and we may not optimally *use* all our brain's capacity in the sense of maximizing learning or performance. But we definitely use all of it — it's all active and essential.
