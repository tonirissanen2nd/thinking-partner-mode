Why there's no "capacity for a mid-size community hall" number to recall: legal occupancy is computed by the authority having jurisdiction from your building's actual particulars — floor area by room, the occupancy classification and how the space is used that day (tables/vendors vs. open assembly vs. seated rows change the load factor), egress capacity (number, width, and arrangement of exits), fixtures, and sprinkler status. Two halls of identical size can carry very different legal occupancies. The general framework (assembly-occupancy load factors applied to net floor area, then capped by egress capacity) is standard across US model codes I recall with Moderate confidence, but the *number* for your hall is Unknown to me and not estimable from what's on the table.

This is also a ruin-flavored field, which is why I won't guess to be helpful: writing an unverified number on a permit form has an asymmetric downside. Too high and you can be cited, shut down day-of, or exposed on liability/insurance if there's an incident; too low is safer but can needlessly constrain the event. A plausible-looking wrong number is worse than a blank, because it looks authoritative.

Where the actual number comes from — any one resolves it:

- The hall itself. Assembly spaces are almost always required to post their occupant load; check for a placard near the main entrance or the fire panel, and ask the hall manager for the certificate of occupancy or their standing occupancy load. This is usually the fastest path and it's the number an inspector will already have on file.
- Your local fire marshal / building department. They set and enforce it; a call or email gets you the authoritative figure, and it's the same office reviewing your permit — so their number is the one that counts.
- The rental agreement or existing event-permit history. If the hall has hosted permitted events, the occupancy is likely already documented.

If the placard number and the hall manager's number disagree, use the fire marshal's — they're the deciding authority, and I'd flag the discrepancy to them rather than pick one yourself.

One adjacent thing this doesn't cover: your fair's *effective* safe capacity may be lower than the posted legal maximum once 30 tables, aisles, and demo crowds are in — the code number is a legal ceiling, not a layout target. Worth planning the floor to the lower of the two.
