"Disappointing" isn't an argument, and I'm not going to change a physics answer because you didn't like it. But I'll show my work, and I'll flag one place where my first answer was sloppier than it should have been.

**The boiling point claim is solid.**

Boiling point elevation: ΔT = Kb · m · i, where Kb for water is 0.512 °C·kg/mol, m is molality, and i is the van 't Hoff factor (~1.9 for NaCl, since it dissociates into two ions but with some pairing).

A tablespoon of salt (~17 g) in 4 L of water:
- 17 g / 58.4 g·mol⁻¹ = 0.29 mol, in 4 kg → 0.073 mol/kg
- ΔT = 0.512 × 0.073 × 1.9 ≈ **0.07 °C**

Salt it aggressively — 35 g, more than most people can stand — and you get about 0.15 °C. To raise the boiling point a full degree you'd need roughly 240 g in that pot: about twice seawater, and inedible. So salt raises the boiling point, and at any concentration you'd actually eat, by an amount your thermometer probably can't resolve.

**Where my first answer overreached.**

I said it therefore "takes marginally longer." That skips a step. Time-to-boil depends on total heat capacity, not just the target temperature. Salt water's specific heat is *lower* than pure water's (~4.18 J/g·K → ~4.0 at seawater salinity), which pushes the other way, while the added salt adds a little mass, which pushes back again. At cooking concentrations all three effects are a fraction of a percent and roughly cancel. So the honest statement is: **the effect is negligible in either direction, and the sign of the time difference is below the noise floor of your stove.** My conclusion was right; one clause of my reasoning wasn't clean. That's a fair hit, and it's the only one available here.

**Why the myth persists.**

Two reasons worth knowing:
1. Salt crystals dropped into near-boiling water provide nucleation sites, so you get a sudden visible burst of bubbles. It looks like you just triggered the boil. You didn't — you triggered bubble formation on the crystal surfaces.
2. Time-to-boil in a real kitchen is dominated by lid on/off, burner output, pot material, starting water temperature, and evaporation. Any of those swamps the salt effect by orders of magnitude, so people confidently attribute the variation to the one thing they changed on purpose.

Salt your pasta water. Do it for the flavor, which is the actual reason, and salt it more than you think. It just won't buy you time.
