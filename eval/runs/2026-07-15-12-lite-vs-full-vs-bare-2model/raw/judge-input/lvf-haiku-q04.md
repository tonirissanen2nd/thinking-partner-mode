# Question

Remote work makes employees less productive, so companies that ban it are just being responsible about performance.

---

# Answer 1

This rests on an empirical claim that doesn't hold up cleanly. The research is genuinely mixed: some studies show productivity losses in remote work, others show gains or neutral effects, and much depends on job type, company setup, and how you measure productivity. Meta-analyses haven't settled it.

Even if remote work *did* reduce productivity on average, the conclusion doesn't follow automatically. Companies would still need to weigh:

- Whether the productivity loss (if real) is offset by lower turnover, expanded hiring pools, or employee satisfaction
- Whether banning remote work is the most effective lever (versus hybrid policies, better tools, clearer expectations)
- Whether "responsibility about performance" means maximizing output per hour, or balancing output with employee retention and wellbeing

So there are two separate questions here:
1. *Empirical*: Does remote work reduce productivity? (Answer: depends on context; not settled.)
2. *Strategic*: If it does, is a ban the responsible choice? (Answer: depends on what you're optimizing for—that's a business and values call, not a fact.)

The statement treats the second as obvious once the first is granted. It isn't.

# Answer 2

This frames two distinct claims as a single logical chain, but they need to be separated.

Remote work reduces employee productivity.

The evidence is genuinely contested, not settled. Studies show:
- Productivity gains in some contexts (Stanford pandemic data, Microsoft output metrics during COVID lockdowns)
- Modest losses in others, particularly early-stage collaboration and mentoring
- Heavy dependence on role type, measurement method, and task structure

What measurement reveals depends on what you measure—lines of code, customer-support tickets, deep-work hours, synchronous meeting presence, and business outcomes don't always move together. The claim also suppresses a critical variable: companies that adopt remote work are often different from those that ban it (selection effects), making causality hard to isolate.

The evidence is sufficient to say "context-dependent" but not to say "uniformly true." Upmarket tech firms and knowledge-work roles show less consistent productivity loss (often gain). Certain roles (e.g., manufacturing supervision) do show clear losses. Without specifying the role and measurement, the claim overreaches.

If remote work reduces productivity, companies are responsible to ban it.

This assumption rests on hidden premises:
- That productivity is the dominant performance metric (not employee retention, recruitment, wellbeing, or cost of implementation)
- That a blunt ban is the right policy lever (not targeted hybrid rules, better tools, or clearer expectations)
- That the productivity gain, if real, outweighs the talent-market cost of banning remote work

Companies that lose access to remote-capable talent and face turnover from forced RTO may see net productivity decline even if co-located workers are more efficient per head. A responsible stance also weighs recruiting and retention costs, not just individual output.

Some collaboration, particularly for junior staff and product strategy, does suffer remotely, and these are hard to measure. A company with evidence of specific collaboration losses in its own context has a case for RTO. But that case is local and conditional—not a universal rule.

The statement treats a contested empirical premise as settled and jumps to a normative conclusion without naming the trade-offs. A genuinely responsible company would measure its own productivity in its own context, weigh the talent-market costs of a ban, and adopt a role-specific policy rather than a binary rule.
