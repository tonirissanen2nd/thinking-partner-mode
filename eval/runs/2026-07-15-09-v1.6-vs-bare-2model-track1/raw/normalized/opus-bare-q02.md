Before the confrontation question, it's worth separating two things that are getting bundled together in how you've framed this: the behavior (you're being left off emails, and work you did is landing without your name on it) and the motive (he's doing it deliberately to take credit).

The behavior is the part you can actually establish and act on. The motive is the part you probably can't prove, and it's also the part that will blow up the conversation if you lead with it. If you walk in saying "you're cutting me out to steal credit," he only has to deny the intent — which he can do sincerely or cynically, you'll never know which — and now you're in a he-said fight about his inner life rather than a fixable conversation about email threads. You lose either way.

So the practical move is: raise the pattern, skip the accusation, and make it awkward for him to keep doing it.

First, get concrete for your own sake. Write down specific instances: date, thread, what you contributed, who was on it, who wasn't. Three or four real examples is enough. This matters partly because you may find the pattern is thinner than it feels — people tend to remember every slight and forget the times they were included — and partly because if you do end up escalating, "it feels like this keeps happening" goes nowhere while a list goes somewhere.

Then talk to him directly, and low-key. Not a Serious Conversation. Something closer to:

"Hey — I noticed I wasn't on the thread with [X] about [project]. That's happened a few times now on things I've worked on. Can you loop me in going forward? I'd rather be in the thread than hear about decisions secondhand."

That's it. No motive, no grievance, just the ask. It's deliberately easy for him to say yes to. If it was genuine carelessness, it's fixed. If it wasn't, you've now put him on notice in a way he can't claim not to have heard, and he knows you're tracking it — which is often enough to change behavior on its own.

Watch what happens next. That's your real information. If it keeps happening after you've explicitly asked, that's no longer ambiguous, and you don't need to prove intent, because "I asked to be included and it kept not happening" is a legible problem you can take to your manager without ever having to psychoanalyze him out loud.

Meanwhile, stop relying on him as your channel. This is the part people skip because it feels like it lets him win, but it's the highest-leverage thing here. Make your work visible independently of whether he includes you: send your own status updates to the relevant people, reply-all onto threads you find out about, put your name on the docs, mention what you're working on in your one-on-ones with your manager. If your visibility depends on a coworker choosing to forward your name, you have a structural problem, and confronting him doesn't fix the structure.

One caveat on the escalation path: if you eventually do go to your manager, bring the pattern and the fact that you raised it with him, not the theory about his motives. "I asked to be included, it's still happening, and here are the threads" is credible. "He's trying to steal my work" makes you sound like the difficult one, even when you're right. Let the manager draw the conclusion; they usually will.
