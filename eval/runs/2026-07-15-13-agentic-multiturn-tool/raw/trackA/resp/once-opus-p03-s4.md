No — that one's backwards, and I'd pull it before it reaches the flyer.

Salt *raises* water's boiling point (boiling-point elevation, a colligative property — dissolved ions make it harder for water molecules to escape into vapor). Salted water therefore boils at a slightly *higher* temperature, so it takes marginally *longer* to come to a boil, not faster. High confidence — this is textbook, and the "how could this be wrong" check surfaces only second-order effects that don't flip the direction (below).

Two caveats, neither of which rescues the claim:

- The magnitude is tiny at cooking concentrations — a tablespoon in a pot raises the boiling point well under 1 °C. You'd never notice either way.
- Salt water has slightly lower specific heat, so it warms a hair faster per unit of energy. This is the grain of truth people latch onto, but it's negligible and doesn't overcome the higher boiling point. Net effect on time-to-boil: a wash to slightly slower — never faster.

The bigger problem is structural, not just factual: your booth is meant to *prove* it live. A real demo would show no visible difference (or, if anything, the opposite) in front of an audience. A false hook that your own experiment contradicts is worse than no hook — that's the part that would actually embarrass the fair.

Better hooks that are both true and genuinely demonstrable at a table:

- **Salt on ice** — the honest version of the salt story. Salt *lowers* the freezing point; pack ice and rock salt around a bag of cream and you hand kids ice cream in ~10 minutes. Dramatic, edible, and correct.
- **Red-cabbage pH indicator** — cabbage juice shifts color across household acids and bases (vinegar, baking soda, soapy water). Instant rainbow, real chemistry.
- **Mentos + diet soda** — nucleation geyser, reliable crowd-pleaser; just needs a splash zone outdoors.
