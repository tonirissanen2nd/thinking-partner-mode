I understand the skepticism—it's a common misconception. But I'm confident in this one: salt dissolved in water raises the boiling point, not lowers it. This is basic colligative properties (salt particles disrupt water molecules, requiring more heat to escape as vapor).

The confusion likely comes from salt being used in cooking contexts. People often *add* salt while water is already heating, then it boils and they assume salt made it faster. But if you measure carefully, salted water takes slightly longer to boil than pure water—the lag is just tiny at typical salt concentrations (1-2%), often masked by other variables like pot type or heating strength.

That said, you're right that salt affects *flavor* development and can help season pasta as it cooks, which is the actual reason chefs use it. The "faster boiling" idea got stuck in cooking lore, but the physics is clear: higher boiling point = slower boil.
