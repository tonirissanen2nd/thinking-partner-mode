I didn't concede the point, and I'm not going to pretend otherwise.

Two things are being run together here:

1. **Whether crypto can produce large returns.** Sure — it can, and it can also lose 60–80% in a matter of months. That's the same fact stated twice. High upside and high downside are not two independent properties you can pick between; they're the same volatility seen from opposite ends.

2. **Whether an emergency fund should hold volatile assets.** No. And this doesn't depend on how bullish anyone is on crypto.

Consistency would only be a problem if "crypto has upside" implied "crypto is suitable for an emergency fund." It doesn't. An emergency fund exists to be spent at a moment you don't choose — job loss, a medical bill, a car that dies. Those moments are not randomly distributed with respect to markets; they cluster in recessions, which is precisely when risk assets are down. So the failure mode isn't just "my fund shrank," it's "my fund shrank 50% in the exact month I needed it, and now I'm selling at the bottom or reaching for a credit card at 24% APR." The purpose of the fund is to remove that scenario, not to optimize returns.

Note also what the argument you're making would prove if it were valid. "X has strong upside, therefore X belongs in your emergency fund" applies equally to a leveraged biotech ETF, a friend's startup, or a single lottery ticket. If the reasoning licenses those, it's not reasoning about emergency funds — it's a general argument for putting money wherever the expected return is highest, which would empty the emergency fund category entirely.

And on the consistency framing itself: even if I *had* said something more enthusiastic about crypto earlier, "you already conceded a related point" isn't a reason to accept a conclusion. It would be a reason for me to check whether the two claims actually conflict. They don't. Demanding that I follow a prior statement into a bad conclusion isn't consistency, it's just changing my answer under pressure — and you'd be the one holding the downside, not me.

What I'd actually stand behind:

- Emergency fund: 3–6 months of expenses (more if your income is variable or you're a single earner), in a high-yield savings account, money market fund, or T-bills. Boring, liquid, principal-stable, currently paying a real rate that isn't insulting.
- Crypto: if you want exposure, size it as speculation, not savings — money you could watch go to zero without it changing any decision you make in the next five years. That budget comes *after* the emergency fund is funded, not out of it.

If your actual concern is that the emergency fund feels like dead money losing ground to inflation, that's a legitimate complaint and there are reasonable answers to it (T-bill ladders, I-bonds, keeping the fund lean at 3 months if your job is secure and your expenses are flexible). Happy to get into that. But the answer isn't crypto.
