QUESTION:
Is it better for a small nonprofit to spend its marginal dollar on donor acquisition or on donor retention?

=== Answer 1 ===
Short answer: for a typical small nonprofit, the marginal dollar goes to retention — but the honest form of the answer is a switching rule, not a side, because the two aren't symmetric and one enables the other.

Three structural asymmetries, none of which depend on your specific numbers:

1. *Cost.* Acquiring a donor usually costs more than their first gift returns — first-gift cost-to-raise ratios above 1.0 are common, so acquisition is an investment that only pays back through subsequent gifts. Retaining an existing donor is far cheaper per dollar raised.
2. *Compounding.* A retained donor produces a multi-year stream and is the pool from which upgrades, monthly conversion, and eventually major/legacy gifts come. Acquisition without retention is filling a leaky bucket.
3. *The leak is usually large.* Sector benchmarks I recall put overall donor retention around 45% and first-time-donor retention around 20% (US, Fundraising Effectiveness Project). If your retention is in that range, the marginal return on plugging the leak is high because the baseline is so bad.

So the base rate favors retention, and it favors it more the worse your current retention is.

Retention can only act on donors you already have. Two cases send the marginal dollar to acquisition instead:

- *Below critical mass.* If your donor base is very small (say, low hundreds or fewer), you cannot retain your way to viability — the absolute numbers are too small regardless of rate, and you need a minimum base before retention math dominates. Acquisition is then a prerequisite, not a competing option.
- *Already-high retention.* If you're retaining well (say, 70%+, unusual for a small org), the leak is small and the next dollar buys more by widening the top of the funnel.

At the margin, spend where the next dollar yields more long-run revenue: compare the return on a retention dollar (roughly, incremental donors kept × their remaining lifetime value) against an acquisition dollar (new donors × lifetime value, net of acquisition cost). For most small orgs with typical retention, the retention side is larger because lifetime value is being destroyed at ~55% per year. The point of indifference is where marginal returns equalize — you rarely go all-in on either.

- *Reframe of the goal.* "Retention vs. acquisition" treats them as substitutes; they're better modeled as a pipeline. A cheap, high-leverage move for small orgs is often the *conversion* step between them — turning one-time donors into recurring/monthly givers — which is technically retention spend but has acquisition-like growth effects. If your real goal is sustainable revenue, that's frequently where the highest-return marginal dollar sits.
- *A floor risk.* If a small org lets retention collapse while chasing acquisition, churn can exceed acquisition and the base shrinks despite spending — a slow death spiral. This isn't ruin in the catastrophic sense, but it's the asymmetric downside: under-investing in retention has a self-reinforcing failure mode that under-investing in acquisition (stagnation) does not. That asymmetry is itself a reason to weight retention when you're uncertain.

**What would change my answer:** your current retention rate and base size. Tell me those two numbers and the acquisition cost per donor, and this becomes a concrete calculation rather than a default. Absent them, I'm applying the base rate — which points to retention for the small-nonprofit case, with the two exceptions above.

=== Answer 2 ===
Short answer: for most small nonprofits, the marginal dollar should go to **retention** — but that's a default, not a law, and it's contingent on where your current retention rate sits. Let me give you the reasoning and the decision rule rather than just the verdict.

**Reframe the "either/or."** This isn't a binary allocation; it's a marginal one. The economically correct rule is to push dollars toward whichever channel has the higher *marginal* return per dollar until the two returns equalize. You almost never want 100% of anything. So the real question is: which channel is currently underfunded at the margin? For the typical small nonprofit, that's retention — for three reasons below.

1. **Unit economics.** Acquisition typically costs roughly $1.00–1.25 per $1 raised in year one — you often lose money on a donor's first gift and only profit if they give again. Renewal/retention costs a fraction of that (commonly cited near $0.20 per $1).

2. **Sector retention is low, so there's slack.** Overall donor retention runs around 45%, and first-year new-donor retention is far worse — roughly 20–30%. A low current rate means high-return, low-hanging fruit: moving first-year retention from 25% to 35% compounds through every future year of a donor's giving. If your retention were already excellent, this argument would weaken sharply.

3. **Behavioral default.** Small nonprofits instinctively chase new logos/donors and under-invest in stewardship (thank-yous, reporting back, converting one-time givers to monthly recurring). The marginal dollar tends to be underfunded precisely where attention is scarce.

**The strongest counter-argument:** You cannot retain donors you never acquired, and attrition is unavoidable — donors die, move, and change priorities regardless of stewardship. A retention-only posture means slow decline as your base leaks. This matters more for you than for a large org: with a small donor base, **concentration risk is real** — if a handful of donors carry your budget and one leaves, retention spend can't replace them. Acquisition is the only channel that grows the base and diversifies that risk.

There's also a tail consideration that resists simple ROI averaging: donor lifetime value is heavily skewed — a small number of major donors provide most revenue. Acquisition's payoff is partly lottery-like (you might bring in a transformational donor), so averaging its ROI understates its option value. This is a genuine reason not to zero out acquisition even when its average ROI looks worse.

- **New org or <~100–200 active donors:** acquisition first. You don't yet have a base worth optimizing, and retention math needs volume to matter.
- **First-year retention below ~40%:** retention almost certainly wins the marginal dollar. Fix the leaky bucket before pouring in more.
- **Retention already strong (repeat-donor retention 60%+ and healthy recurring base):** the marginal retention dollar hits diminishing returns; shift toward acquisition and reactivation.

**Ruin/downside note:** neither path is a true ruin scenario, but the downside profiles differ. Retention-only = bounded, predictable slow decline. Acquisition-only = you keep overpaying and never build loyalty, but you stay diversified. For a *small* org, the fragility to watch is base concentration, not overspending — which argues for never fully starving acquisition even when retention has the better ROI.

- **Reactivation of lapsed donors** is a third channel I've folded in above but that deserves its own line — it's often cheaper than fresh acquisition and you likely have a lapsed list already.
- **Recurring/monthly-giving conversion** is the single highest-leverage retention move (recurring donors retain far better) and may be a better home for the marginal dollar than either generic bucket.
- **The two channels aren't independent:** better retention raises lifetime value, which in turn justifies *more* acquisition spend. Improving retention can be the thing that makes acquisition worth funding.

What's your current first-year retention rate and roughly how many active donors do you have? Those two numbers move this from a default to a specific recommendation.
