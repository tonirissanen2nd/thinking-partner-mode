No. The word "always" is where this breaks. Higher crude (unadjusted) death rates do not reliably identify worse hospitals, and in some systematic cases they point the wrong way. This is well-established in health services research.

The core problem is that raw mortality confounds two different things: how sick the patients were on arrival, and how well the hospital treated them. Several mechanisms pull crude death rates away from quality:

- **Case mix / patient acuity.** A tertiary referral center, a Level I trauma center, or a transplant hospital receives the sickest, most complex patients — often transferred *because* they were failing elsewhere. Higher baseline risk mechanically produces higher death rates independent of care quality. This is the largest and most consistent confounder.
- **Selection at the door.** A hospital that declines high-risk surgeries will post better numbers than one that accepts them. Lower mortality can reflect *avoiding* hard cases, not treating them well — the opposite of a quality signal (documented as "risk-averse" behavior in cardiac surgery public-reporting studies).
- **End-of-life and palliative practice.** Hospitals with strong palliative/hospice programs may show *higher* in-hospital deaths because they let dying patients die in the ward rather than transferring them or prolonging futile care. Here a higher death rate can accompany better care.
- **Coding and documentation.** Mortality is usually reported as observed-to-expected ratios; "expected" depends on how thoroughly comorbidities are coded. Aggressive documentation inflates expected mortality and improves the apparent ratio without any change in real care.
- **Small-number noise.** For low-volume conditions, a few deaths swing the rate enormously. Much of the spread in rankings among small hospitals is statistical noise, not signal.

What the evidence *does* support: **risk-adjusted** mortality — standardized measures like HSMR (hospital standardized mortality ratio) or condition-specific 30-day mortality (AMI, heart failure, pneumonia) as used by CMS in the US and comparable national programs — correlates with quality more defensibly than crude rates. But "more defensibly" is not "definitively." Risk adjustment is imperfect: it can only correct for measured, coded variables, so unmeasured severity leaks through, and the models themselves are contested. The empirical literature on whether even HSMR tracks preventable death is genuinely mixed (this is a contested domain, and studies validating HSMR against case-note review of avoidable deaths have found weak associations).

So the accurate version of the claim:

- **Crude death rate → worse hospital:** unreliable, sometimes inverted. Reject.
- **Risk-adjusted, condition-specific, volume-adequate mortality → worse hospital:** a genuine but noisy signal, best read as one input among several, not a verdict.

Framing correction worth making explicit: mortality is a single outcome dimension. Quality is multidimensional — readmission rates, hospital-acquired infections, complication and failure-to-rescue rates, patient safety events, and access all matter, and they don't move together. A hospital can have middling mortality and excellent safety, or vice versa. Ranking by any one metric discards this.

I have not addressed (1) *which* specific metric a given league table or news story is using — the answer to your question changes entirely depending on whether it's crude, HSMR, or CMS 30-day, and that is the first thing to check on any actual claim you encounter; (2) the specific validation literature by name/year, which I'd want to verify against primary sources (the Lilford and Pronovost critiques of HSMR are the standard references, but treat that attribution as recall-level, not verified); and (3) survivorship/discharge effects (deaths shifted to hospice or post-acute settings escape in-hospital counts entirely).
