No — that claim is backwards and will undermine your booth's credibility. Don't put it on the flyer.

**What actually happens:** Adding salt to water raises its boiling point, meaning salt water boils at a temperature slightly *higher* than pure water (around 100.5°C vs 100°C). So salt makes water boil *slower*, not faster.

What salt does do — and this is probably where the confusion lives — is make water *appear* to boil more vigorously by increasing nucleation. When you add salt to already-boiling water, you see vigorous bubbling and splashing because the salt crystals provide surfaces for vapor bubbles to form. But that's a visual effect on already-boiling water; it doesn't speed up the initial boiling process.

**Why this matters for your flyer:** A chemistry booth's credibility is your asset. If you lead with an incorrect claim, fairgoers will test it, find it wrong, and lose confidence in the booth — especially if there are any skeptics or science-minded kids in the crowd. You'll spend the demo defending a false premise instead of exploring actual chemistry.

**Better hook for the flyer:** "Watch salt crystals trigger explosive bubbling in hot water" or "See how salt changes water's boiling point" — both are true and genuinely interesting. Or skip the salt angle and pick a different demo that's both correct and visually striking (vinegar and baking soda volcano, color-changing reactions, elephant toothpaste, slime, etc.).
