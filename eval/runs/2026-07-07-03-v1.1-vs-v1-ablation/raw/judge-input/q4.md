QUESTION:
Remote work makes employees less productive, so companies that ban it are just being responsible about performance.

=== Answer 1 ===
The claim bundles a contested empirical premise with a logical leap, so it doesn't hold as stated. Let me separate the two.

This is too flat to be true as a universal. The evidence splits sharply by *modality* and *task type*, and that split is the whole story:

- **Hybrid** (a few days remote): the better-identified studies find no productivity penalty and a measurable retention gain. Bloom's 2024 randomized trial at Trip.com (hybrid, ~1,600 workers) found no drop in output or promotions and roughly a one-third cut in quit rates.
- **Fully remote**: genuinely mixed and sometimes negative. The Emanuel–Harrington work on call-center workers moving fully remote found a productivity decline on the order of ~10%; some data-entry and complex-collaboration settings show similar or worse. Bloom's earlier Ctrip RCT, by contrast, showed a ~13% *gain* for fully-remote call staff. The results are context-dependent, and I'd flag the specific percentages for verification against the primary papers before you lean on them.

So the accurate version of the premise is: *fully-remote arrangements sometimes reduce individual output in coordination- or supervision-heavy roles; hybrid generally does not.* The unqualified claim overgeneralizes from the hardest cases.

One methodological caveat worth naming: a lot of "remote kills productivity" evidence is self-reported by managers or proxied by keystroke/activity monitoring, which measures visible busyness, not output. The cleaner the measurement, the weaker the effect tends to look.

Even granting a productivity penalty, the conclusion doesn't follow — it commits a scope error. "Responsible about performance" would have to mean *net effect on firm value*, not per-hour individual output. A ban that recovers, say, 8% output can simultaneously:

- raise attrition (replacement cost is commonly cited at ~50–200% of salary per departure — but directionally large),
- shrink the hiring pool to a commuting radius,
- surrender real-estate savings,
- and lower morale in exactly the workers who value flexibility most.

A firm optimizing performance nets these against each other; it doesn't privilege one term. So even under a pessimistic productivity read, a *blanket ban* is rarely the performance-maximizing move — a role-differentiated or hybrid policy dominates it on the firm's own logic. The word "responsible" is also doing quiet normative work: it reframes a contestable cost-benefit tradeoff as obvious prudence.

If your underlying question is "should a company ban remote work to protect performance," the answer is: for most knowledge roles, no — hybrid captures the retention upside without a clear output cost, and blanket bans ignore the offsetting terms. For a specific subset (heavy real-time coordination, close supervision of junior staff, tasks with weak individual accountability) a *higher in-office requirement* can be defensible — but that's a targeted policy argument, not a ban, and not a general law about remote work.

None of this addresses *why* a given company might actually want a ban — control, real-estate sunk cost, or using attrition as quiet headcount reduction. If the real motive is one of those, "performance" is a rationale rather than the reason, and the productivity debate is beside the point.

What would move me: role-level output data (not activity metrics) from the specific context you have in mind, plus its attrition and hiring-pool numbers. Do you have a particular company or role in view, or is this the general claim?

=== Answer 2 ===
The conclusion doesn't survive the premise, and the premise is weaker than stated. Two separate claims are bundled here — an empirical one (remote work lowers productivity) and a normative one (banning it is therefore responsible) — and both fail on inspection.

The evidence doesn't support a blanket effect in either direction; it supports a set of contingent ones:

- **Fully remote vs. hybrid are different questions.** The cleaner causal studies (randomized or quasi-experimental call-center and data-entry settings) find *fully* remote associated with a small productivity penalty — roughly single-digit percent — often traced to less on-the-job learning and mentorship, which hits juniors hardest. Hybrid, by contrast, shows roughly neutral-to-positive productivity with clear retention gains. Banning to fix a fully-remote problem, if that's the concern, overshoots by killing hybrid too.
- **Task type dominates.** Independent, low-interdependence work travels fine remotely; high-coordination, tacit-knowledge, or rapid-feedback work degrades more. "Employees" is not a homogeneous input.
- **The measure is contested.** Much of the "remote is less productive" signal is self-report or output-per-hour in narrow roles; much of the "remote is more productive" signal is time-saved-commuting converted to work. These aren't measuring the same thing, which is a large part of why the literature looks contradictory.

So the honest version of the premise is: *fully* remote work carries a modest, role-dependent productivity cost in some settings — not "remote work makes employees less productive" full stop.

Even granting the strongest version of the premise, "ban → responsible about performance" is a non-sequitur, because it equates *per-employee productivity* with *firm performance*. Those diverge:

- A ban shrinks the hiring pool to commute radius and forces a compensating wage premium or accepts lower-quality hires.
- RTO mandates are consistently associated with elevated voluntary attrition, disproportionately among higher performers and those with the most outside options — the exact people whose departure costs the most. A policy that raises measured output-per-hour 3% while losing your best 8% of staff is not obviously performance-responsible; it may be performance-*destructive*.
- Selection effect worth naming: a nontrivial share of high-profile RTO mandates function as attrition-by-other-means (soft layoffs) or as control/visibility preferences, not as productivity optimization. When the stated rationale is "performance" but the mechanism is "fewer people quit-by-being-forced-out," the premise is doing PR work.

For a firm whose value is dominated by in-person tacit-knowledge transfer, dense collaboration, and apprenticeship (early-stage startups, some trading floors, some labs), a mostly-in-person default can be the genuinely performance-maximizing call — and defending it as such is legitimate, not mere control. That's a real and defensible position. But notice it's narrow, it's usually *default-in-person with flexibility* rather than a *ban*, and it's justified by coordination value, not by "remote makes people less productive."

"Responsible about performance" treats performance as one number. The decision that actually matters is a portfolio — productivity-per-hour, retention, hiring cost and quality, real-estate cost, and morale — and remote/hybrid/ban trade differently across those axes. Any answer that collapses them into a single verdict is hiding the actual decision.

The premise is true only in a narrowed, conditional form; the inference from it to "banning is responsible" doesn't hold even where the premise does, because it swaps firm performance for per-head productivity. The defensible position isn't "ban remote work," it's "match the policy to the work" — and for most knowledge work that lands on structured hybrid, not a ban. What would move me: firm-level (not task-level) causal evidence that full bans improve *net* performance after retention and hiring costs — that's the study that's conspicuously missing, and its absence is itself informative.
