Retention, usually — but it depends on your position in the donor lifecycle.

Donor retention is almost always more cost-effective: acquiring a new donor costs $1–5 per $1 gained, while retaining an existing donor typically costs $0.20–$0.50 per $1 kept. Over a donor's lifetime, retention compounds; the average donor stays 5 years, and each year of retention adds value.

But acquisition makes sense if:
- You're undersized relative to your mission (early-stage nonprofit with low donor density in your market)
- Your retention is already good (70%+), so you've wrung most of the value from that cohort
- You have a new program or geographic market you're entering

Retention makes more sense if:
- You have high churn (below 40%), which signals donor dissatisfaction, bad communication, or poor stewardship — fix that first
- You're mature (10+ years) with an established base; your cost-per-dollar-raised will drop if you keep the donors you have

The real question isn't "which is better?" but "where are you stuck?" If donors are leaving after one gift, retention. If you're too small to move the needle on your mission, acquisition.

One specific thing to measure: your donors' lifetime value. If you don't know how long donors typically give or how much they give over time, you're flying blind. Calculate it (average gift × average retention years), then compare to your acquisition cost. If acquisition cost exceeds lifetime value, you're not gaining donors — you're losing money.
