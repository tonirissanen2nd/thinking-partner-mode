The argument has a factual premise that doesn't hold and a logical structure that fails even if you grant it. Let me separate the two.

**The empirical premise: "remote work makes employees less productive."**

This is not settled, and the honest summary is that the effect is heterogeneous, not uniformly negative (Moderate confidence — based on the body of RCT and quasi-experimental work through the mid-2020s, though the literature is contested and setting-dependent).

- Fully remote roles: the evidence leans toward a small *negative* effect on individual output for some job types, with the cleanest randomized evidence (Bloom et al.'s data-entry/call-center studies) actually showing a productivity *gain* in the setting studied, alongside separate findings of slower output or weaker mentoring in others. So even the pro-productivity-loss case doesn't generalize cleanly.
- Hybrid roles: the strongest recent randomized evidence (a large 2022–23 trial at a Chinese tech firm, published in *Nature*) found no productivity or promotion penalty and lower attrition. This matters because most "remote work" in practice is hybrid, not fully remote.
- The measured effect depends heavily on task type (independent/deep-work vs. collaboration-intensive), role seniority, management quality, and — critically — *how productivity is measured*. Self-reported, manager-rated, and objective-output measures often disagree.

So "remote work makes employees less productive" is at best a partial, setting-specific claim stated as a universal. I'd flag the specific study figures above as worth verifying against primary sources before you lean on any one of them; the qualitative shape (heterogeneous, task-dependent, hybrid ≈ neutral) is what I hold with Moderate confidence.

**The logical structure: even granting the premise, the conclusion doesn't follow.**

Three gaps:

1. **Productivity per employee isn't the maximand — it's one input to firm performance.** Remote/hybrid policies also affect the wage you must pay for a given talent level, the size of the hiring pool, attrition and its replacement costs, real-estate expense, and absenteeism. A firm rationally trades a small per-head output dip against a large widening of its talent pool or a cut in turnover. "Responsible about performance" means optimizing the whole equation, not one term of it. A policy that raises average measured productivity while triggering costly attrition of your best people is *worse* on performance, not more responsible.

2. **"Responsible" smuggles in a value judgment the productivity data can't license.** Even a firm-wide productivity gain from banning remote work wouldn't by itself make the ban "responsible" — that word implies a defensible balancing of stakeholder interests (employees with caregiving constraints, disability accommodations, commute costs) against firm output. That's a normative claim, and it stands or falls on argument, not on the productivity number alone. You can't derive "responsible" from a productivity delta.

3. **The averages hide the variance that actually drives the decision.** The productivity effect isn't a constant applied to everyone; it's a distribution. Banning remote work uniformly to address the roles/workers where it hurts also strips it from the roles/workers where it helps or is neutral. If the effect is heterogeneous — which the evidence says it is — the performance-maximizing policy is differentiated by role, not a blanket ban.

**The strongest version of your position**, to be fair to it: coordination-heavy work with weak async infrastructure, junior employees who learn by osmosis, and low-trust management cultures probably *do* see genuine losses under full remote, and a manager who ignores that isn't being responsible either. The defensible claim is narrow — "for certain roles and org maturity levels, in-person or structured-hybrid arrangements outperform" — not "banning remote work is the responsible default."

**Bottom line:** the premise is a contested, setting-specific finding dressed as a universal law, and the inference from "lower per-employee productivity" to "banning it is responsible performance management" drops too many of the terms that actually determine firm performance. A firm optimizing performance would set policy by role and evidence, not ban across the board.

One omission worth naming: I've treated this as a pure performance question because that's how you framed it. If the real driver behind such bans is control, real-estate sunk cost, or managerial preference rather than measured performance, that's a different conversation — and the productivity argument would then be a post-hoc justification rather than the actual reason.
