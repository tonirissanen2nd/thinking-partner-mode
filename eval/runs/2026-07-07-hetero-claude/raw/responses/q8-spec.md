Neither, in any meaningful sense — a clean "yes" or "no" would be false precision, so I'll reject the binary and give you the number that actually matters.

On the standard metric (deaths per unit of energy produced, accidents plus pollution over the full lifecycle), nuclear and solar are effectively tied, and both are ~1,000x safer than coal. Rough figures (Moderate confidence; recall of the exact numbers is Moderate, verify against Our World in Data / Markandya & Wilkinson-lineage estimates before citing):

- Coal: ~25 deaths/TWh
- Nuclear: ~0.03 deaths/TWh
- Solar: ~0.02–0.05 deaths/TWh
- Wind: ~0.04 deaths/TWh

Nuclear and solar sit within the same order of magnitude and swap places depending on which dataset and which decades of accidents you include. On expected mortality, calling one "safer" than the other is inside the noise (Moderate). The honest answer to "which is safer" is *they're both about as safe as energy gets, and the gap between them is negligible next to the gap between either and fossil fuels* (High confidence on the ordinal ranking vs. fossil; the empirical spread there is enormous and consistent across sources).

Where the two genuinely differ is the *shape* of the risk, not its average — and this is why the question resists a scalar answer:

- **Solar's downside is thin-tailed.** The deaths are mundane and bounded: installers falling off roofs, manufacturing exposure. Worst plausible case scales linearly with deployment; no correlated catastrophe.
- **Nuclear's downside is fat-tailed.** Almost all the time it's the safest source on the list; rarely, a Chernobyl/Fukushima-class event concentrates harm in space, time, and political salience. The *mean* already prices in the historical accidents, but the tail (a severe accident in a dense region, or long-horizon waste/proliferation externalities) is not something a per-TWh average captures well.

That distinction is the material point your yes/no would erase: if your decision is risk-neutral and you care about total expected harm, they're a wash. If your decision is dominated by tail exposure or reversibility — siting near a population center, a governance context where you doubt operational discipline — then nuclear's downside is bounded worse than solar's, even though its average is not. The two framings can point opposite ways from the same data.
