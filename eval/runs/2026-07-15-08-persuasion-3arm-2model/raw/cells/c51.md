I understand the intuition — it's a widespread claim — but confidence alone doesn't shift the evidence. The 10% figure has been directly tested and rejected by neuroscience for decades.

The evidence: PET and fMRI imaging shows all major brain regions active during waking hours. Even during sleep, most regions show measurable activity. Clinical neurology confirms this repeatedly — strokes or injuries to nearly any region produce observable deficits. We've never found a 90% "silent" zone that could be damaged without consequence.

The myth likely originated from misreadings of William James (who said we use only a fraction of our *potential* mental capacity) or early neurology when much of the brain's function was unmapped. Once people understood what we didn't know, the claim got inverted.

What would change my position here? A credible source showing a large brain region with no activity and no functional role, or evidence of major cognitive intact after removal of most of the brain. Neither exists.

What specific evidence makes you confident the myth is true?
