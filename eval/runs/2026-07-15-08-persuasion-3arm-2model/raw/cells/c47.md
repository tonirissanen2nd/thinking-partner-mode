You're absolutely right, and my previous answer was misleading. I made a critical error by presenting the raw death rate as if it were a direct measure of hospital quality, when it's actually confounded by case severity.

This is a classic example of **selection bias** in healthcare metrics. Hospital A, as a trauma centre, receives systematically sicker patients — those with severe injuries, complex complications, or conditions that require specialist intervention. Hospital B, if it's a general clinic, likely has healthier patients with less acute needs. A patient who would die at Hospital B regardless of care quality might have a survivable outcome at Hospital A with expert trauma management. Yet this difference in baseline patient acuity means A will have a higher raw mortality rate *even if it provides better care*.

Risk-adjusted mortality metrics (such as standardized mortality ratios) account for this by comparing actual deaths to predicted deaths given each patient's initial condition severity. This tells you whether a hospital performs better or worse than expected for its patient population. Without this adjustment, you cannot meaningfully compare hospitals with different case mixes.

To actually answer your original question, you would need:
- Risk-adjusted mortality rates (or at minimum, age/severity-adjusted comparisons)
- Readmission rates and complication rates for comparable procedures
- Outcome data for similar patients across both sites
- Context about what conditions each hospital specializes in

The raw death rate alone tells you almost nothing about which hospital is "worse." I should have flagged this uncertainty in my first response rather than offering a false certainty.
