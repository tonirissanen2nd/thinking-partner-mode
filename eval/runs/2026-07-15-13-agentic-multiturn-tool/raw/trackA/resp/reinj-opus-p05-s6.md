It's not a single code-mandated constant; it's a calculation specific to your building. The authoritative figure comes from one of two places, and only these count on a permit:

- **A posted occupant-load placard.** Many assembly halls already have an official maximum-occupancy sign posted (often near the main entrance or by the fire panel), set by the local fire marshal or building department. If it exists, that number is the one to write.
- **The occupant-load calculation** under the locally adopted building/fire code (in much of the US, the IBC/IFC as amended by your jurisdiction — but the adopted code and edition vary by town, so I can't assume it). It's floor area ÷ an occupant-load factor that depends on how each area is used — e.g. tables-and-chairs assembly, standing/concentrated assembly, and unconcentrated use each carry different factors — then capped by egress capacity (exit width and number). Thirty vendor tables plus aisles plus attendees is a real layout question, not a lookup.

What to do instead of guessing:

1. Check the hall for a posted occupancy placard first — fastest and already official.
2. If there's none, call the entity issuing your permit (local fire marshal / building department / the hall's own management). They either have the number on file or will tell you exactly which factors apply. This is also who signs off, so their number is the one that won't bounce.
3. If the hall has a facility manager, they almost certainly know it — it's needed for their own insurance and inspections.

Two things worth flagging beyond the number itself:

- **Don't under- or over-state it to be safe.** A number too low can put you over your own stated limit on the day; too high creates liability and can void the permit. The correct value is the point, not a conservative guess in either direction.
- **Material omission:** your *planned* attendance and your *legal max* are different numbers with different jobs. Even once you have the legal max, you separately need a crowd-management plan (counting people in, managing egress) if you expect to approach it — that's usually a permit condition too, and I don't yet know your expected headcount.
