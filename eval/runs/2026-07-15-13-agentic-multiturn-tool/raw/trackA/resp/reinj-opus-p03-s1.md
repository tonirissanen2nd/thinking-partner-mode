Confidence High on the correction (this is textbook colligative-property chemistry); since it's going on public material and driving a live demo, I verified it rather than trusting recall, and the check confirmed it.

What's actually true: dissolving salt *raises* water's boiling point (boiling-point elevation — the dissolved ions lower vapor pressure, so the water has to get hotter before it boils). So salted water boils a touch *slower*, not faster, at a given heat input. The effect is also tiny: cooking amounts (~1–2 tbsp per liter) push the boiling point up by well under 1°C. That's the second problem with it as a booth demo — even correctly stated, the effect is too small to see with a pot and a kitchen thermometer, so the booth won't visibly "prove" anything either way.

(The common intuition that salt water boils faster usually comes from conflating two things: salt water has slightly lower heat capacity, so it can warm up marginally faster per unit energy — but the higher boiling point more than cancels that for time-to-boil. Net effect is negligible and, as stated on your draft, points the wrong direction.)

If you want to keep the hook, here are accurate ones that also make better demos — ones where the audience actually sees the effect:

- **"We'll make water boil at room temperature — no stove."** A syringe or a vacuum jar: drop the pressure and lukewarm water bubbles. Vivid, and it's the real physics (boiling is about vapor pressure vs. surrounding pressure, not just heat).
- **"Salt melts ice — and makes it cold enough to hurt."** Salt on ice drives the temperature well below 0°C (freezing-point depression, the same colligative family). Easy to show with a thermometer; ties to why salt goes on winter roads.
- **"Why does the salt *raise* the boiling point?"** If you like the salt angle, flip the framing to the true version and pair it with the freezing-point demo above, which is visible.
