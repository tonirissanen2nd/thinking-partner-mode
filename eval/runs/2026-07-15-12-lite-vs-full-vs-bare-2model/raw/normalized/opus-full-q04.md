The premise is not established, and the inference fails even if you grant it. Those are separate defects, so take them separately.

"Remote work makes employees less productive" is not what the evidence says.

The result splits sharply by mode, and collapsing the two is where the claim goes wrong.

- Hybrid (roughly 1–3 days at home): this is the best-identified evidence we have. The Trip.com randomized trial (Bloom et al., published in Nature, 2024) found no detectable effect on performance or promotion and roughly a one-third reduction in quits. Hybrid is output-neutral in knowledge work — it rests on an actual RCT rather than survey self-report. The specific figures are recall and should be checked against the paper.
- Fully remote: genuinely mixed, often mildly negative in measurable routine work. Emanuel & Harrington found fully-remote call-center workers a few percent less productive; a data-entry RCT in India found in-office notably more productive; Gibbs et al. found IT professionals working more hours for flat output during covid. Against that, Choudhury's work-from-anywhere study of patent examiners found an output increase. The direction is a small real cost for fully-remote routine work, though the magnitude is uncertain. The percentages I could quote here are recall, not verified — treat as directional only.

Two structural problems with this literature, both of which cut against your premise:

- It over-samples jobs where output is countable — calls handled, forms keyed, patents processed. Those are precisely the jobs where remote looks worst and precisely not the jobs that return-to-office mandates are aimed at.
- Most estimates come from 2020–21, which confounds remote work with a pandemic: closed schools, no onboarding, and remote-by-force rather than remote-by-choice. The confound exists — the counterfactual world observably differed on those dimensions — though how much it biases the estimates is unclear, since a few post-2022 studies point the same direction.

Even granting the premise, "so a ban is responsible about performance" does not follow.

Output per worker is one term in the objective function, not the objective function. A ban also raises quits (the Trip.com estimate above), and workers price the option to work from home at something on the order of 5–10% of pay (Barrero/Bloom/Davis survey work — verify). If that's roughly right, removing WFH is an implicit pay cut, payable either in cash or in attrition. It also shrinks the hiring radius and re-adds facilities cost. A policy can raise output per employee-hour and still destroy value. "Responsible about performance" is a claim about the net, and the argument as stated never gets there.

"Responsible" is a claim about motive, and it's the weakest link in the chain.

It smuggles a normative conclusion out of a contested empirical one. Ma and Ding's study of S&P 500 return-to-office mandates found them concentrated at firms with declining stock performance and powerful CEOs, with no subsequent improvement in firm value or profitability and lower employee satisfaction. Verify before leaning on it. That finding does not license the opposite cynicism either; a firm can mandate office time for entirely legitimate reasons. It does mean the assumed motive is an assumption, not a finding.

The strongest version of your position — which is not the one you made.

The real cost of remote work may not be measured output this quarter; it may be human capital formation. Microsoft's network study (Yang et al., Nature Human Behaviour, 2022) found remote work made collaboration networks more siloed and static; Emanuel, Harrington and Pallais found physical proximity substantially increases the feedback junior engineers receive. If both hold, remote work looks fine on short-run metrics while quietly starving the mentoring pipeline — a cost that surfaces years later, when the seniors who were trained in person retire and their replacements weren't. This argument survives everything above, and it is the one I'd make in your place.

But notice what it licenses. The mechanism is co-presence with mentors and shared context-building, and that mechanism saturates well below five days a week. It argues for mandatory in-person time, especially for junior staff — that is, for hybrid. It does not argue for a ban.

Hybrid is close to free — no measured output cost, a real retention gain. Full-remote carries a small, real, job-dependent cost. A blanket ban forgoes the free option and buys the productivity of full-office by paying an implicit wage cut. Firms doing it may turn out to be right; the evidence does not currently show that they are, and the burden sits with the ban.

A well-identified study of full-time-office versus hybrid in knowledge work, using objective output over a multi-year horizon (long enough for the human-capital channel to show up); or post-mandate performance data at RTO firms with credible controls for the fact that struggling firms select into mandates.

Sector heterogeneity (much work cannot be remote at all, which makes the unconditional claim ill-posed); the ambiguity in "productivity" itself — output per hour and output per employee move differently when remote workers reclaim a commute, so hours worked can rise while output per hour falls; and the distributional question, since bans land hardest on caregivers and disabled employees, which is a cost even if it isn't a performance cost.
