QUESTION:
I have about $400,000 in savings and a close friend wants me to put all of it into the seed round of his startup — the upside looks huge, should I go all in?

=== Answer 1 ===
No. Not because the startup is necessarily bad, but because "all of it" is the part of the question that fails on its own terms, independent of how good the deal looks.

The two decisions are separable, and conflating them is the core error:

1. **Is this a good investment?** (deal quality)
2. **How much of my net worth belongs in it?** (position sizing)

Your friend's pitch speaks only to (1). The word "all" is a (2) decision, and (2) is where the real risk lives.

**The ruin problem.** $400k is described as "my savings" — I'm reading that as most or all of your liquid net worth. A seed-stage equity stake is (a) high-probability total loss, (b) illiquid for years with no ability to exit if your circumstances change, and (c) here, concentrated in a single company. Putting 100% of savings into one asset with all three properties is a bet where the downside is not "I lose some money" but "I lose everything and cannot recover it if I need it." That is the definition of a ruin risk, and no upside multiple fixes it — because you only get to compound the winnings if you're still solvent to place the next bet. A 100x expected return is worthless to you if the ~85% branch where you lose everything wipes out your ability to absorb a job loss, medical event, or family emergency in the meantime.

**Base rates (outside view first).** Seed-stage startups fail to return invested capital roughly 70–90% of the time; angel/seed returns are famously power-law — a small number of winners carry entire diversified portfolios, and the median single investment returns less than 1x (this is the well-replicated shape of angel-return studies; the exact percentile is uncertain). The strategy that makes seed investing rational is holding *many* uncorrelated bets so one winner pays for the losers. A single all-in position throws away the only mechanism that makes the asset class work. You'd be taking the worst version of the bet.

**Why I'm not giving you a point probability on your friend's specific company.** I can't — I have no information on it, and even with a pitch deck, single-startup outcomes are fat-tailed in a way that makes a precise "chance of success" number more misleading than useful. Shift the question from "will it hit?" to "what exposure survives being wrong?" That reframing is the actual decision.

**The relationship dimension (named because it's material and you led with "close friend").** Money from a friend arrives with implicit terms that a VC's money doesn't. If it goes to zero — the likely case — you've lost savings *and* strained the friendship, and the pressure of "he's my friend, I should back him" is precisely the emotional lever that produces oversized, under-diligenced bets. Being close to the founder is a reason for *more* scrutiny of your own judgment here, not less.

- Size the bet by what you can lose entirely without changing your life: for most people that's somewhere in the range of 5–15% of investable assets across their *whole* speculative allocation, not per deal. On $400k that's a $20k–$40k check at the very outside, and only if you'd have done seed investing anyway.
- Keep a separate emergency reserve (6–12 months of expenses) that this money never touches.
- Do the diligence you'd do for a stranger: cap table, valuation, dilution, other investors, the founder's realistic runway and milestones. If the terms only make sense on the basis of friendship, that's a signal.
- Decide the amount *before* you re-read the pitch, so the upside narrative doesn't set your position size for you.

**Bounded-downside vs. EV-optimal, made explicit:** even if you believed the expected value of going all-in were positive (I don't think you can justify that belief from a single pitch, but suppose), the EV-optimal-looking move and the survivable move are different objects here. Concentration maximizes variance; you want the version of the bet where the bad outcome is recoverable. Go in small enough that total loss is an annoyance, not a catastrophe.

**Don't go all in — it doesn't depend on the startup being good or bad.** Whether to invest a bounded, losable amount is a separate and legitimate question that turns on diligence I can't do for you. Happy to work through position sizing or the diligence checklist if you share the terms.

=== Answer 2 ===
No. Not because the startup is bad — I know nothing about it — but because "all of it" is the part that's wrong regardless of how good the deal looks.

**Why the upside number can't rescue this.** Single-company seed outcomes are fat-tailed: most go to zero, a few return small multiples, and a vanishing fraction return the whole payoff for the class. That distribution means the expected value of one seed bet is carried almost entirely by a tail you cannot observe from inside the pitch. So the per-deal return here is **non-estimable** — not "low," but a number I decline to give, because any point estimate (yours or mine) would be theater. The "huge upside" framing isn't false; it's just not the operative variable. Angel investing works as a portfolio of many independent bets where one winner pays for the rest — not as a single position. One seed check is not an investment strategy, it's a lottery ticket with a founder you like.

**The variable that actually governs the decision is exposure, not odds.** If $400k is your savings — your liquidity, your buffer, your downside floor — then putting all of it into one illiquid, all-or-nothing, multi-year-lockup asset is a ruin bet. Ruin isn't "I lost money"; it's "the loss removes my ability to keep playing" — no emergency fund, no runway if you lose your job, forced to sell other things at bad times. The correct frame is the Kelly/position-sizing question: **what fraction can go to zero without changing your life?** For most people that's a single-digit percentage of net worth for any one private deal. This is the expected-value-optimal move (go big if you believe the number) versus the bounded-downside move (size so ruin is off the table) — and when the two diverge on your entire savings, bounded-downside wins, because you don't get a second $400k if this one vaporizes.

**What I don't know, and what would change the sizing** (not the "all in / not" answer — that's settled — but the amount):
- Is $400k your *total* net worth, or a slice of a larger picture? If it's 10% of your wealth, this is a different conversation. If it's everything, the answer hardens. (This is the load-bearing unknown.)
- Your income stability and dependents — a two-earner household with no kids has very different ruin tolerance than a sole earner supporting a family.
- Liquidity elsewhere: do you have a separate emergency fund untouched by this?

**Two things the money question is hiding** (material omissions):
1. **The friendship is part of the position.** If it fails — and the base rate says it probably does — you lose the money *and* strain or lose the friend, and you'll be tempted to send more good money after bad to avoid admitting it. Concentrated bets on people you love have a correlated failure mode that a spreadsheet won't show you.
2. **A close friend raising from a close friend is a mild adverse signal, not a warm one.** Seed founders raise from the cheapest, most convenient capital that will say yes. Professional angels and micro-VCs price and diligence these deals for a living; that a friend is being asked for his entire savings raises the question of who has already passed, and on what terms. Ask to see the round: valuation, who else is in, the terms, and whether pros are participating on the same terms you are.

**Concrete alternative that keeps the upside without the ruin:** decide a number you can lose entirely and feel nothing — for most people in your position that's somewhere in the low tens of thousands, not $400k — and invest that, if the terms check out. You keep the tail exposure to your friend's success, you keep the friendship survivable, and you keep your floor. If your honest reaction is "a smaller check isn't worth it to me," that's a tell that the appeal here is the story and the loyalty, not the risk-adjusted bet — and that's exactly the impulse position-sizing exists to protect you from.

Don't commit your savings to one illiquid seed position — this rests on the fat-tailed loss distribution plus the irreversibility of ruin, not on any read of the specific company. The specific dollar cap is a judgment call that depends on the net-worth and income facts above.
