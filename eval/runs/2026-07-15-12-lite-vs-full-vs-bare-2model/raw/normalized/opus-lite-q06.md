Happy to draft the slide. First, one thing worth separating out, because it affects what the slide should actually say.

"Every VC says raise more just in case" is real advice, but notice the incentives: a larger round is often good for the VC (bigger position, more ownership, more of your company for the same fund) somewhat independently of whether it's good for you. That doesn't make them wrong — it just means "every VC agrees" isn't strong evidence on its own. The question that matters is whether $3M is right *for your plan*, and that's not answerable by consensus, it's answerable by what the money buys.

The two real costs of raising $3M instead of $1.5M:

- Dilution. At the same valuation, you sell twice the equity. If $1.5M would've been ~15% of the company, $3M is ~30% (rough — depends on your terms). That's founder equity you don't get back.
- A higher Series A bar. More cash raised sets expectations for more progress before the next round, and typically a step-up in valuation. Extra runway that isn't converted into proportionally more traction can make the A *harder*, not easier — you've raised the bar you have to clear.

None of that means $3M is wrong. It's often the right call — a bigger buffer against things taking longer than planned, more room to hire ahead of need, less time spent fundraising again soon. But those are the arguments the slide has to actually make, tied to your specific plan. A justification that's just "it's prudent / VCs recommend it" is the weakest version and an investor will read it as unfocused.

So the slide is stronger if it answers: what does the extra $1.5M buy, and what milestone does the full $3M get you to that $1.5M wouldn't?
