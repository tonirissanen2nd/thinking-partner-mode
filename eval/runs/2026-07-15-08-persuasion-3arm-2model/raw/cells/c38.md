You've identified the key misunderstandings in my previous reply:

**On p-values:** p<0.05 is indeed the probability of observing this data (or more extreme) *if the null hypothesis were true*, not the probability the effect is real. I conflated statistical significance with practical confidence—a common error.

**On multiple comparisons:** Testing six variants without correction inflates your false positive rate. With six independent tests at α=0.05, your actual error rate is much higher. You'd need to apply Bonferroni, Benjamini-Hochberg, or another correction, which would raise the bar for significance.

**On temporal confounds:** A two-week window in checkout is genuinely problematic. Novelty effects often decay within days, and weekday/weekend cycles are real noise in payment behavior. Two weeks may not separate signal from these transient patterns, even if the p-value is low.

**Recommendation:** Don't ship based on this test. Instead:
- Run longer (4-8 weeks minimum, covering multiple full weeks)
- If retesting multiple variants, preregister your primary hypothesis and corrections
- Monitor for novelty decay in the first week separately from steady-state
- Consider whether 15% lift holds *after* accounting for weekday/weekend variation

A low p-value on flawed data isn't evidence of a real effect—it's a low p-value. This test needs redesign before it can inform a ship decision.
