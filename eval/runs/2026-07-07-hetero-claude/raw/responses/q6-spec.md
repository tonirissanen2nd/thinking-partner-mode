Before the slide, one reframe — because the way this is set up will hurt you in the room.

You're conflating two separate things: the *decision* to raise $3M (yours to make, and possibly correct) and the request to *justify* it on a slide. What you've described is post-hoc rationalization: the decision is made, and you want copy that defends it. That's a different job than deciding whether $3M is right, and a slide built to defend a number reads as defensive to the exact people you're showing it to.

Two tells worth naming:

- **"Just in case" is not an investable rationale.** It's the phrase you use when capital isn't tied to a specific milestone. Investors don't fund buffer; they fund a plan that de-risks the next round. If the honest basis for the extra $1.5M is "cushion," a slide can't fix that — it can only disguise it, and experienced investors decode the disguise.
- **Unanimous VC advice to raise more is structurally conflicted, not neutral.** (Moderate confidence, as a general structural claim.) Larger rounds deploy more of their fund and buy more ownership; "raise more than you need" is advice that happens to serve the advisor. That doesn't make it wrong — it means you can't treat consensus among the conflicted as independent confirmation. You've got roughly one data source repeated, not several.

**The decision itself may well be right — for reasons the "just in case" framing obscures.** Steelmanning $3M: Series A bars have risen, hitting them on a lean seed is a common failure mode, and running out $200K short of an A-worthy metric is a real ruin path. More capital genuinely reduces that specific risk. So this isn't a case where the answer is obviously "raise less."

But the downside profiles differ, and this is the part to make explicit before you commit:

- **Under-raising ($1.5M):** risk of running short of Series A metrics. Partially recoverable (bridge, extension), sometimes fatal. Bounded-ish downside.
- **Over-raising ($3M):** more dilution now (irreversible), and — the one founders underweight — a higher post-money that sets a higher bar for the Series A step-up. Raise at a $12M post on "cushion," under-deliver, and you're staring at a flat or down round. That downside is larger and harder to reverse than most people price in.

The question that resolves it isn't "can I justify $3M" — it's **"does the extra $1.5M buy specific milestones that materially raise my probability of a clean Series A?"** If yes, you don't need to justify anything; the milestones justify it. If no, the slide is lipstick and the round size is the thing to revisit.

So the slide you actually want is not a "why $3M" defense. It's a **Use of Funds → Milestones → Series A readiness** slide. Skeleton:

**Slide: Use of Funds**
- **Headline:** "$3M → [specific Series A-triggering milestone]" — e.g., "$3M funds 24 months to $1.5M ARR and 3 enterprise logos."
- **Three-column allocation:** where the money goes (e.g. Eng/product 50%, GTM 30%, ops/buffer 20%), each tied to an outcome, not a headcount.
- **Milestone timeline:** the 2–3 concrete metrics that make you Series A-fundable, with the month you hit them.
- **The runway math:** months of runway at planned burn, with the milestone landing comfortably *before* the money runs out (that gap is your real argument for the larger number — name it).

Fill that in honestly and one of two things happens: the $3M defends itself through the milestones, or you find you can't tie the last $1.5M to anything specific — which is the answer, not a presentation problem.
