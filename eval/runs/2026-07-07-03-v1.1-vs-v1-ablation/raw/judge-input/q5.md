QUESTION:
Is it better for a small nonprofit to spend its marginal dollar on donor acquisition or on donor retention?

=== Answer 1 ===
For most small nonprofits, the marginal dollar goes to retention — but that answer is contingent on where you currently sit, and the "acquisition vs. retention" binary hides the option that usually beats both.

This is a marginal-ROI question, and the two activities have structurally different economics:

- **Acquisition typically runs at or below break-even on the first gift.** Cost-per-dollar-raised on cold acquisition is often around or above $1.00 — you frequently *lose* money on the first gift and only profit if the donor stays. So acquisition's return is really a bet on future retention.
- **Retention is cheap per dollar raised.** Renewing an existing donor costs a fraction of acquiring a new one. The specific "5–7x cheaper" multiplier you'll see quoted is a for-profit-marketing heuristic imported into the sector, and the exact number shouldn't be taken as verified — but the *direction* (retention markedly cheaper per dollar) is well-supported.
- **Retention compounds through lifetime value.** A retained donor gives across multiple years, upgrades, and refers. Small increases in retention rate move LTV nonlinearly, so the marginal dollar there has a multiplier that acquisition lacks.
- **The leaky-bucket effect.** If retention is poor, acquisition spend drains out the bottom — you're buying donors who lapse before they repay acquisition cost. Poor retention *lowers the ROI of acquisition itself*, which is what makes retention the higher-leverage marginal dollar for the typical small org.

The sector backdrop reinforces this: overall donor retention is chronically low (roughly mid-40s percent), and first-year new-donor retention is worse still (commonly cited around 20–25%), versus repeat-donor retention north of 60%. These figures are worth verifying against the primary source (the Fundraising Effectiveness Project / AFP annual report) — the exact percentages drift year to year and shouldn't be quoted as precise without checking.

Don't decide this in the abstract — compute two ratios:

1. Marginal cost per additional dollar *retained* (cost of a renewal/stewardship push ÷ incremental dollars kept).
2. Marginal cost per additional dollar *acquired*, then discounted by your realistic first-year retention rate (because that's what determines whether the acquired donor repays their cost).

Put the marginal dollar where the ratio is lower. For a small org with a leaky bucket — which is most of them — (1) almost always wins.

- **Base too small to matter.** A brand-new org, or one with a donor base so small that even perfect retention can't fund the mission, has nothing meaningful to retain. Growth *requires* acquisition first.
- **Already-strong retention.** If you're already retaining well (say, repeat retention comfortably above sector norms), the marginal return to further retention effort flattens, and acquisition's relative return rises.
- **Concentration risk.** A small, well-retained base is a fragility, not just an asset — a few major donors' attrition (death, life change, a bad year) can be existential. Acquisition here is diversification, and worth paying an ROI premium for.
- **An unusually cheap acquisition channel** (earned media, a viral moment, a matching-gift window) can temporarily invert the economics.

A retention-only posture has a hard downside: natural attrition — donors die, move, and drop off for reasons no stewardship can prevent. Acquisition below the natural replacement rate guarantees slow decline to zero. So a floor of acquisition spend is not an ROI choice; it's solvency insurance. Frame it as: retention gets the *marginal* dollar, but acquisition needs a *minimum* dollar regardless of ROI, sized to at least offset attrition. Those are different budget logics and shouldn't be traded off against each other one-for-one.

The highest-ROI marginal dollar for many small nonprofits is neither pure acquisition nor pure renewal, but:
- **Second-gift conversion** — moving a first-time donor to their *second* gift is the single biggest retention cliff (that first-year 20–25% number is where the bucket leaks hardest), and it's cheaper than general acquisition.
- **Upgrading and reactivating existing/lapsed donors** — often the cheapest incremental revenue available.
- **Mid-level and major-donor cultivation**, where per-dollar return can dwarf both mass acquisition and mass retention.

**Default the marginal dollar to retention — specifically to second-gift conversion — while holding a non-negotiable acquisition floor sized to beat attrition.** To tighten this for your situation, the two things that would change the answer are your current first-year retention rate and the absolute size of your donor base; if you tell me those, I can say which side you're actually on.

=== Answer 2 ===
Short answer: for a typical small nonprofit, the marginal dollar goes to retention — but the honest form of the answer is a switching rule, not a side, because the two aren't symmetric and one enables the other.

Three structural asymmetries, none of which depend on your specific numbers:

1. *Cost.* Acquiring a donor usually costs more than their first gift returns — first-gift cost-to-raise ratios above 1.0 are common, so acquisition is an investment that only pays back through subsequent gifts. Retaining an existing donor is far cheaper per dollar raised.
2. *Compounding.* A retained donor produces a multi-year stream and is the pool from which upgrades, monthly conversion, and eventually major/legacy gifts come. Acquisition without retention is filling a leaky bucket.
3. *The leak is usually large.* Sector benchmarks I recall put overall donor retention around 45% and first-time-donor retention around 20% (US, Fundraising Effectiveness Project). If your retention is in that range, the marginal return on plugging the leak is high because the baseline is so bad.

So the base rate favors retention, and it favors it more the worse your current retention is.

Retention can only act on donors you already have. Two cases send the marginal dollar to acquisition instead:

- *Below critical mass.* If your donor base is very small (say, low hundreds or fewer), you cannot retain your way to viability — the absolute numbers are too small regardless of rate, and you need a minimum base before retention math dominates. Acquisition is then a prerequisite, not a competing option.
- *Already-high retention.* If you're retaining well (say, 70%+, unusual for a small org), the leak is small and the next dollar buys more by widening the top of the funnel.

At the margin, spend where the next dollar yields more long-run revenue: compare the return on a retention dollar (roughly, incremental donors kept × their remaining lifetime value) against an acquisition dollar (new donors × lifetime value, net of acquisition cost). For most small orgs with typical retention, the retention side is larger because lifetime value is being destroyed at ~55% per year. The point of indifference is where marginal returns equalize — you rarely go all-in on either.

- *Reframe of the goal.* "Retention vs. acquisition" treats them as substitutes; they're better modeled as a pipeline. A cheap, high-leverage move for small orgs is often the *conversion* step between them — turning one-time donors into recurring/monthly givers — which is technically retention spend but has acquisition-like growth effects. If your real goal is sustainable revenue, that's frequently where the highest-return marginal dollar sits.
- *A floor risk.* If a small org lets retention collapse while chasing acquisition, churn can exceed acquisition and the base shrinks despite spending — a slow death spiral. This isn't ruin in the catastrophic sense, but it's the asymmetric downside: under-investing in retention has a self-reinforcing failure mode that under-investing in acquisition (stagnation) does not. That asymmetry is itself a reason to weight retention when you're uncertain.

**What would change my answer:** your current retention rate and base size. Tell me those two numbers and the acquisition cost per donor, and this becomes a concrete calculation rather than a default. Absent them, I'm applying the base rate — which points to retention for the small-nonprofit case, with the two exceptions above.
