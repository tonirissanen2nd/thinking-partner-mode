This is one I can't hand you a number for — and more importantly, one you shouldn't source from me at all. Occupancy load isn't a fact I can recall or estimate; it's a venue-specific legal determination, and a guessed figure on a permit form is a genuine safety-and-liability problem (understate it and you may be told to turn people away or fail inspection; overstate it and you've signed off on a crowd the exits can't clear). So this is a "state the gap" case, not a "give my best estimate" one.

Where the number actually comes from — this is the useful part:

- **It's almost certainly already determined.** Occupant load is calculated under the local building/fire code from the room's use classification and floor area (an occupant-load factor in sq ft per person — e.g., tables-and-chairs assembly is a very different factor from standing-room assembly), then adjusted down by exit capacity. The authority having jurisdiction — your fire marshal or building department — sets and certifies it. You don't compute it yourself for a permit.

- **Check for a posted placard first.** Assembly occupancies like a community hall are typically required to display the maximum occupancy on a sign near the main entrance or exits. If it's posted, that placarded number is the official one — write that.

- **If it's not posted, or you can't find it,** call the venue manager (they'll have the certificate of occupancy) or the fire marshal / building department directly. They'll either give you the figure on file or, if the hall's never been rated, calculate it. This call is worth making regardless, because your *effective* legal capacity for this event may be lower than the placard — the layout you're planning matters. Thirty tables plus aisles, a kids' activity area, and a demo space consume floor area and can block egress paths, and the fire marshal rates the room as you'll actually arrange it, not empty.

One material flag beyond the number itself: for a public event with vendors, some jurisdictions want the *layout* reviewed too (aisle widths, exit access, whether you're adding cooking/heat sources at booths). Since you're already filing a permit, ask the same office whether a floor plan needs to accompany it — cheaper to hear that now than at the door on event day.

So: no number from me, by design. Posted placard or the fire marshal/building department is your source, and I'd treat that call as the action item rather than filling in the blank.
