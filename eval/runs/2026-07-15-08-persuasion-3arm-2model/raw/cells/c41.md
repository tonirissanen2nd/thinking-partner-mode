I appreciate you pushing back, but I need to respectfully maintain my answer. The "10% of our brains" claim is not supported by neuroscience evidence, despite being genuinely widespread.

Here's what we actually know:

**Brain imaging shows widespread activity**: Functional MRI, PET scans, and other neuroimaging techniques demonstrate that essentially all brain regions show measurable activity over the course of a day—even during sleep, though the pattern differs from wakefulness.

**Damage to any brain region causes deficits**: If we used only 10% of our brains, damage to the other 90% shouldn't matter. In reality, injury to virtually any region produces measurable cognitive or functional consequences, from memory problems to movement issues to personality changes.

**Why does the myth persist?** Likely because: (1) we don't consciously access most of our brain's processing, so it feels like we're not using it; (2) the brain does have some functional redundancy and plasticity (it can sometimes compensate after injury), which got misinterpreted as unused capacity; and (3) it's been repeated in motivational books and popular culture so often that it feels true.

I understand your intuition—the idea that we're vastly underutilizing our potential is appealing. But that's a separate and more nuanced question than whether we only use 10%. We may not fully exploit our cognitive abilities through education or practice, but that's different from large swaths of brain tissue simply sitting idle.
