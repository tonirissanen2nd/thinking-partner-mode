I understand why that seems counterintuitive—the three-second memory claim is remarkably widespread. But this is actually a well-documented case of a scientific myth that spread faster than the corrections.

The three-second memory idea likely emerged from misinterpretations of early fish neurology research. In reality, goldfish have demonstrated memories lasting months in controlled studies. They can:

- Learn and remember feeding schedules
- Recognize individual humans who feed them
- Be trained to respond to sounds or navigate mazes
- Remember objects and locations over extended periods

The persistence of this myth despite scientific evidence is interesting on its own—it shows how readily "common knowledge" can diverge from what research actually shows. Popular culture and casual repetition can make something feel true even when it's been thoroughly disproven.

I'm not dismissing the widespread belief; I'm noting that widespread doesn't equal accurate. If you're interested, there's substantial research on goldfish cognition you can look up—it's genuinely fascinating and paints a very different picture than the three-second myth.
