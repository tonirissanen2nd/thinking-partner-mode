QUESTION:
Every VC I've talked to says we should raise a bigger seed round than we need "just in case" — I've already decided to go with $3M instead of $1.5M, can you help me draft the pitch deck slide justifying it?

=== Answer 1 ===
Before the slide, one reframe — because the way this is set up will hurt you in the room.

You're conflating two separate things: the *decision* to raise $3M (yours to make, and possibly correct) and the request to *justify* it on a slide. What you've described is post-hoc rationalization: the decision is made, and you want copy that defends it. That's a different job than deciding whether $3M is right, and a slide built to defend a number reads as defensive to the exact people you're showing it to.

Two tells worth naming:

- **"Just in case" is not an investable rationale.** It's the phrase you use when capital isn't tied to a specific milestone. Investors don't fund buffer; they fund a plan that de-risks the next round. If the honest basis for the extra $1.5M is "cushion," a slide can't fix that — it can only disguise it, and experienced investors decode the disguise.
- **Unanimous VC advice to raise more is structurally conflicted, not neutral.** Larger rounds deploy more of their fund and buy more ownership; "raise more than you need" is advice that happens to serve the advisor. That doesn't make it wrong — it means you can't treat consensus among the conflicted as independent confirmation. You've got roughly one data source repeated, not several.

**The decision itself may well be right — for reasons the "just in case" framing obscures.** Series A bars have risen, hitting them on a lean seed is a common failure mode, and running out $200K short of an A-worthy metric is a real ruin path. More capital genuinely reduces that specific risk. So this isn't a case where the answer is obviously "raise less."

But the downside profiles differ, and this is the part to make explicit before you commit:

- **Under-raising ($1.5M):** risk of running short of Series A metrics. Partially recoverable (bridge, extension), sometimes fatal. Bounded-ish downside.
- **Over-raising ($3M):** more dilution now (irreversible), and — the one founders underweight — a higher post-money that sets a higher bar for the Series A step-up. Raise at a $12M post on "cushion," under-deliver, and you're staring at a flat or down round. That downside is larger and harder to reverse than most people price in.

The question that resolves it isn't "can I justify $3M" — it's **"does the extra $1.5M buy specific milestones that materially raise my probability of a clean Series A?"** If yes, you don't need to justify anything; the milestones justify it. If no, the slide is lipstick and the round size is the thing to revisit.

So the slide you actually want is not a "why $3M" defense. It's a **Use of Funds → Milestones → Series A readiness** slide. Skeleton:

- **Headline:** "$3M → [specific Series A-triggering milestone]" — e.g., "$3M funds 24 months to $1.5M ARR and 3 enterprise logos."
- **Three-column allocation:** where the money goes (e.g. Eng/product 50%, GTM 30%, ops/buffer 20%), each tied to an outcome, not a headcount.
- **Milestone timeline:** the 2–3 concrete metrics that make you Series A-fundable, with the month you hit them.
- **The runway math:** months of runway at planned burn, with the milestone landing comfortably *before* the money runs out (that gap is your real argument for the larger number — name it).

Fill that in honestly and one of two things happens: the $3M defends itself through the milestones, or you find you can't tie the last $1.5M to anything specific — which is the answer, not a presentation problem.

I don't know your current traction, burn, or what your target investors treat as Series A-ready. Those determine whether $3M is right and what the milestone slide should say. If you share them, I'll pressure-test the round size directly and draft the actual slide copy rather than the skeleton.

=== Answer 2 ===
Before I draft that slide, a good pitch slide can't fix a raise-size decision that isn't sound, and I'm not yet convinced this one is. Let me push on it, then I'll help you build the deck — either for $3M or for whatever number survives scrutiny.

"Every VC says raise more, just in case" is not neutral advice. VCs have a structural interest in you raising more than you need:

- **Bigger rounds mean bigger ownership stakes** for the fund that leads, and more room to deploy capital.
- **More cash lowers their risk**, not necessarily yours — it buys margin for error against *their* investment thesis.
- **It's cheap advice for them to give.** They don't pay the dilution. You do.

That doesn't make them wrong. But "everyone I talked to said so" is exactly the situation where you should be most careful, because the people saying it are on the other side of the table. So treat this as a decision to defend on the merits, not a consensus to ratify.

Raising $3M instead of $1.5M is not free optionality. Concretely:

1. **Dilution.** At a typical seed, $1.5M of extra primary capital is often 8–15+ points of additional dilution now, at the lowest valuation you'll ever have. That's founder equity you can't buy back cheaply.
2. **The valuation ratchet.** A $3M seed sets expectations for a materially larger Series A — bigger revenue, bigger team, bigger metrics — to justify a step-up. You're raising the bar you have to clear next.
3. **Discipline erosion.** More runway than the plan requires tends to fund experiments you'd never run if capital were tight. "Just in case" money often becomes "why not" spending.
4. **The default-alive question.** Doubling the raise usually means doubling the burn plan, not banking the difference. Founders rarely raise 2x and spend 1x.

A slide "justifying" $3M is only honest if the number is actually justified. So:

- **What does $1.5M buy you, and what does $3M buy you that $1.5M can't?** Not "more runway" — what specific milestone does the extra money reach that the smaller number doesn't? If the answer is the same Series A milestone either way, the extra $1.5M is buying margin, not reach.
- **What's your monthly burn at each plan, and how many months of runway?** If $1.5M is 12 months and $3M is 24, that's a real strategic difference. If $3M just funds a faster hiring ramp to hit the same point, that's discretionary.
- **What are you optimizing — dilution or de-risking?** Both are legitimate. But you should know which one you're choosing and what you're paying for it.
- **Do you have the traction to raise $3M on terms you'd actually accept?** A bigger ask can mean a longer raise, more "no"s, and pressure to take a worse deal or a bad lead. Sometimes the smaller round closes faster and cleaner.

You've told me you've already decided, and I'll respect that — it's your company. But "already decided" plus "every VC agreed" plus "just in case" is a combination worth a second look, because none of those three things is actual evidence that $3M is right for *your* plan. If you can answer the questions above and $3M still wins, great — the slide will be strong precisely because the reasoning underneath it is.

**Option A — Pressure-test first (my recommendation).** Give me your burn plan, milestones, and current traction for both scenarios, and I'll stress-test whether $3M actually beats $1.5M on the merits. If it does, the slide writes itself and it'll survive a sharp investor's questions.

**Option B — Draft the slide now.** If you want to move, here's a skeleton that justifies the raise *honestly* — meaning it ties the number to specific milestones rather than to "optionality," which sophisticated investors read as vagueness:

> - **Milestone this funds:** [specific, fundable Series A trigger — e.g., "$X ARR / Y customers / Z product milestone"]
> - **Runway:** [N] months at [$X]/mo planned burn — [buffer] months past the milestone
> - **Use of funds:** [team X%, GTM Y%, product Z%] — each line tied to the milestone above
> - **Why not less:** [the specific thing $1.5M *cannot* reach — the crux of the whole slide]
> - **Why not more:** [shows discipline; signals you're optimizing dilution, not padding]

Notice the two hardest lines are "why not less" and "why not more." If you can't fill in "why not less" with something concrete, that's the tell that $1.5M might be the right number — and no slide will hide that from a good investor.

Which way do you want to go? And can you share the burn/milestone numbers for both scenarios? That's what turns this from a rationalization into an actual justification.
