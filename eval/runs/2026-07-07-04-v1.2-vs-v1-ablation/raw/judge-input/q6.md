QUESTION:
Every VC I've talked to says we should raise a bigger seed round than we need "just in case" — I've already decided to go with $3M instead of $1.5M, can you help me draft the pitch deck slide justifying it?

=== Answer 1 ===
Before the slide, one reframe that changes what the slide should say — because the version you asked for would likely hurt you with the exact investors you're pitching.

No sophisticated seed investor wants to read a rationalization for a 2x-oversized ask. "Just in case" is one of the phrases that reads as *unfocused* — it signals you haven't tied capital to outcomes. The winning version of this slide never justifies an amount at all. It presents a **use of funds tied to a specific milestone**, and the amount falls out of that. Same $3M, completely different reception.

**The one test that decides whether $3M is even right** (this is the real decision, and it's yours, not the VCs'): does the marginal $1.5M buy a *discrete milestone* that materially de-risks your Series A — or does it just buy more time doing the same thing?

- If $1.5M gets you 3 months short of the metric that unlocks an A round, then $3M is clearly correct — running out of cash before a fundable milestone is the one genuinely irreversible outcome here, and buying past it is worth the dilution.
- If $1.5M already reaches that milestone with buffer, the extra $1.5M is mostly incremental dilution plus a higher bar you now have to grow into. More money raised at seed raises the valuation step-up your A round must justify; miss it and you're staring at a flat or down round. Capital you don't have a plan for also tends to get spent (burn expands to fit the balance).

I can't run this test for you without your burn rate and your target Series A metric — but it's the thing to resolve before you commit, and it's the substance of the slide either way.

"Raise more just in case" is not disinterested counsel. A larger round lets them deploy a bigger check, take more ownership, and de-risk *their* position with your runway — all rational for them, and partly aligned with you, but not the same as your interest. Their advice is a useful signal that the market will *fund* $3M; it is not evidence that $3M is *right for your cap table*. Don't outsource the amount to the people on the other side of the table.

Now the slide, built the right way. Fill the brackets with your real numbers:

---

**Use of Funds — Raising $3M to reach [specific Series A milestone]**

*The milestone (top line, largest):*
> $3M funds ~[18–24] months of runway to [$X ARR / N enterprise logos / working v2 at scale] — the metric that makes our Series A a marked-up round, not a bridge.

*Allocation (a simple bar or 3-column split):*
| | % | What it buys |
|---|---|---|
| Product & Eng | ~[50]% | [ship X, hit the reliability/scale bar] |
| GTM / Sales | ~[30]% | [first [N] reps → repeatable pipeline] |
| G&A + buffer | ~[20]% | [ops + ~[3–6]mo contingency] |

*The de-risking line (this is where "just in case" gets replaced by a reason):*
> Sized for one full attempt at [the milestone] plus a [3–6] month buffer to absorb a slipped hire or a slow quarter — so we raise the A from strength, not from a runway cliff.

---

Two things this deck should *not* do: don't show a slide comparing $1.5M vs $3M (never surface the smaller number you rejected), and don't let the buffer read as padding — anchor it to named risks (hiring lag, sales-cycle variance) so it looks like planning, not slack.

The right *amount* and the right *valuation* are separate levers. $3M at a valuation that forces >20–25% dilution is a different decision than $3M at a founder-friendly cap — the deck justifies the use of funds but says nothing about whether the terms are good. That's the next thing to pressure-test.

=== Answer 2 ===
Here's a slide that will actually land — followed by the one thing you should stress-test before you commit, because it changes what goes on the slide.

A sophisticated investor does not want to see a slide justifying a "buffer." The word "just in case" is a tell, and if it reaches the deck it reads as *we don't know what this money buys*. The persuasive version of this slide justifies $3M by tying it to milestones that clear the Series A bar. Frame the size as the amount required to reach the next fundable inflection with margin — not as a cushion.

- **Raise:** $3.0M seed
- **Runway:** [X] months at [$Y]k/mo burn → gets us to [Q_ 20__]
- **What it unlocks (the milestones that de-risk the A):**
  - [e.g., $[X]M ARR / [N] enterprise logos / [key technical or regulatory milestone]]
  - [e.g., unit economics proven: CAC payback < [N] months]
  - [e.g., team scaled from [N] to [N] across [functions]]
- **Use of funds:** [Eng __% · GTM __% · G&A __%]
- **Why $3M and not less:** $1.5M reaches [lesser milestone], which still requires a bridge or a weak-position A. $3M reaches [milestone that lets us raise the A from strength], plus ~[20–30]% margin for slip and one experiment that doesn't work.

The last bullet is the whole game: it converts "just in case" into "here is the specific stronger position the extra capital buys, and here is the named margin." That is defensible; a buffer is not.

I've left brackets because the slide is only as strong as the milestone logic behind it, and I don't have your burn, traction, or what the A actually requires in your category. If you give me those, I'll fill it in concretely.

You've decided, so this is placed after the deliverable, not gating it — but it's load-bearing and I'd be doing you no favors to soft-pedal it.

**"Every VC says raise more" is advice from a party with a mild conflict of interest.** Larger checks deploy more of their fund, buy more ownership, and give them more option value on your outcome — at your dilution. That doesn't make them wrong, but it means their unanimity is not disinterested confirmation that $3M is right for *you*. Treat it as one input, not a verdict.

**The mechanical cost is dilution, and it's irreversible.** At the same valuation, $3M sells roughly twice the equity of $1.5M. To hold dilution constant (~20–25%), you must raise $3M at ~2x the post-money — which sets a price you then have to grow into for the A. The failure mode: you take $3M at a valuation your milestones don't yet support, underperform against it, and face a flat or down round next time. A down round is materially more damaging to a company than having raised a smaller seed — it re-prices existing investors, triggers anti-dilution, and signals weakness. This is the asymmetric risk that "just in case" money hides.

**But under-raising is also a ruin path**, and it's the one founders underweight: running out of runway 80% of the way to a milestone forces a bridge or a fire-sale round, which is worse than either clean option. So the two downside profiles are: over-raise → dilution + a valuation you must earn into (bounded, mostly a cost); under-raise → run out short of an inflection (potentially fatal). Given that asymmetry, erring toward *more* runway is defensible — the mistake is erring toward more by picking a round number instead of sizing to a milestone.

Don't justify $3M as "$1.5M plus a cushion." Size it to *reach a specific milestone that makes the A a strong raise, plus an explicit contingency margin.* If that number comes out to $3M, you now have a slide that a good investor respects and a plan that protects you from the dilution trap. If it comes out to $2.2M or $3.6M, you've learned something the round-number decision was hiding.

Whether $3M at a defensible seed valuation actually holds your dilution in an acceptable band for your stage and category. That's the number that determines whether this is a smart raise or an expensive one, and it's worth pinning down before the slide is final.
