Yes, that's right. Rinsing raw chicken does increase food poisoning risk, and the mechanism you've identified—bacterial splashing—is the actual problem.

**The mechanism:** Water running over raw chicken aerosolizes bacteria (primarily *Salmonella* and *Campylobacter*), dispersing them across the sink basin, faucet, countertop, and your hands. This creates cross-contamination pathways you wouldn't otherwise have. Rinsing does not meaningfully reduce the bacterial load on the chicken itself—it just relocates it.

**Confidence: High.** This recommendation is consistent across USDA and CDC guidance, the logic is straightforward, and the risk is well-documented. The basis is direct: bacteria on raw poultry are normal; rinsing doesn't kill them, only moves them; contaminated surfaces are the actual vector for foodborne illness in home kitchens.

**What matters:** Cooking chicken to an internal temperature of 165°F (74°C) kills all pathogens that pose a risk. That's the load-bearing control. If you do rinse (some recipes or habits may call for it), you're simply introducing an unnecessary cross-contamination risk without any offsetting benefit—rinsing provides no meaningful safety gain since cooking handles pathogen control completely.

The folk intuition that "cleaning" raw meat before cooking makes it safer is understandable but backwards: the safest approach is to skip the rinse and move directly to cook.
