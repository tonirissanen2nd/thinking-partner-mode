The empirical premise does not hold. This is a **load-bearing fact** for your conclusion, so it needs verification.

**What the evidence shows (High confidence):** Large-scale research — Microsoft's 2020 productivity study, Stanford's extensive remote work analysis, ADP's workforce metrics — consistently finds that remote work does *not* uniformly decrease productivity. In many cases it increases it, particularly for heads-down work (coding, writing, analysis). When it varies by role, task type, or individual, the variation is real, but the blanket claim "remote work makes employees less productive" contradicts the mainstream finding.

The steelman: Some specific contexts do show productivity challenges with remote work—coordination-heavy roles, onboarding for new hires, spontaneous collaboration. But even here, the issue is often *process design* (asynchronous handoffs, documentation), not remote work itself. Companies that identify this and address it keep the flexibility; those that ban remote work entirely are solving a design problem with a blunt tool.

**On the normative claim:** Even *if* remote work reduced productivity (it doesn't generally), "responsible" would need to weigh that against:
- Talent acquisition (remote access expands your pool significantly)
- Retention (remote work is now a major factor in employee satisfaction and staying power)
- Cost and operational efficiency
- Employee wellbeing and burnout

You've collapsed multiple dimensions into one metric. A company choosing to ban remote work might be responsible about *some* things — perhaps they value in-person culture highly, and that's a legitimate choice. But framing it as productivity-based responsibility when the productivity premise is false misdiagnoses what's actually driving the decision.

**What would change this:** If rigorous, controlled research showed that your specific company's remote workers underperformed peers doing identical work in the office, *and* this gap persisted after accounting for selection effects, role differences, and process design — then banning remote work might be a performance-based choice. Even then, it would be one factor among many, not "just being responsible about performance."
