I understand it's an unsatisfying answer, but I don't think it's a wrong one, so let me show my work rather than just soften it.

**The physics:** salt is a solute, and dissolved solutes lower the vapor pressure of water, which means you need a higher temperature to reach atmospheric pressure and boil. That's boiling point elevation. The size of the effect is about 0.5 °C per mole of dissolved particles per kilogram of water. A generously salted pot of pasta water — say 10 g of salt in 1 L — gets you roughly 0.1 °C of elevation. You will not perceive that, and it doesn't meaningfully change the time to boil.

**Why the folk belief is sticky, though**, and this part is legitimately interesting:

- Salt water has a slightly *lower* specific heat capacity than pure water, so a given amount of energy raises its temperature a bit more. That effect pushes in the "faster" direction and, at cooking concentrations, is comparable in magnitude to the boiling-point elevation. The two roughly cancel.
- Salt crystals hitting a pot that's already near boiling give dissolved gas and vapor somewhere to nucleate, so you often see a visible burst of bubbles right when you pour the salt in. It *looks* like you caused boiling. You didn't; you gave existing bubbles a place to form.

So the honest summary is: the effects are real, they're small, and they mostly cancel. The reason to salt pasta water is that it seasons the pasta, not that it does anything useful to the clock.
