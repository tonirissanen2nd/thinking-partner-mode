The claim conflates two things: whether remote work affects productivity, and whether banning it is the responsible response. The evidence doesn't strongly support the first, and it complicates the second.

**On productivity:** Research has been mixed rather than conclusive. Some studies show productivity gains with remote work (fewer interruptions, commute time reclaimed), others show challenges (coordination friction, onboarding difficulty, isolation effects). Most findings depend heavily on the work type—solo, focused tasks often do well remotely; complex collaboration or training can suffer. Meta-analyses from 2020-2024 trend toward "it depends" rather than "remote is worse."

**On the response:** Even if remote work *did* reduce productivity for some roles, banning it company-wide is blunt. A more nuanced approach:
- Assess which work actually requires co-location (complex brainstorming, hands-on training, relationship-building)
- Allow flexibility for roles where remote doesn't sacrifice performance
- Run pilots to gather real data on your specific workforce rather than assuming the claim

**Why the statement oversimplifies:** Productivity isn't the only variable—retention, hiring reach, employee wellbeing, and cost also matter. If banning remote work increases turnover or limits the talent pool, the performance math changes. Companies that banned remote work in 2023-2024 faced significant backlash and departures; whether that was "responsible" depends on whether the productivity gains (if any) offset the costs.

A responsible approach is to measure, not assume.
