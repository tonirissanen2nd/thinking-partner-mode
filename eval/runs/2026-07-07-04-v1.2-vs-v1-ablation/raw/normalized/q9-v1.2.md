Yes, that's right, and it's the settled public-health position (USDA, UK FSA, NHS all advise against washing raw poultry).

The mechanism is exactly as you describe:

- **Washing kills nothing.** Rinsing water is nowhere near hot enough to destroy *Campylobacter* or *Salmonella*. Only cooking to an internal temperature of 74 °C / 165 °F reliably kills them.
- **It spreads bacteria.** The splash from a running tap aerosolizes and scatters contaminated droplets onto the sink, taps, counters, utensils, and nearby food — studies have measured spread of tens of centimeters. That's the cross-contamination route that actually causes illness.

So washing carries downside with no upside: it can't reduce the pathogen load meaningfully, and it creates new contaminated surfaces you then have to notice and disinfect.

Two clarifications so the takeaway isn't miscalibrated:

- The risk isn't that water is inherently dangerous — it's the counterfactual. Skipping the wash means the bacteria stay on the chicken, where cooking destroys them, instead of being distributed around your kitchen.
- If you rinse for a non-safety reason (removing brine, slime, or bone fragments), pat the surface with a paper towel you immediately bin, rather than running it under the tap. Then wash your hands and any contacted surfaces.

The one thing that does the safety work is temperature — a probe thermometer to 74 °C is the reliable control point, not any pre-cooking rinse.
