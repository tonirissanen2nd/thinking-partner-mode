Here are 15, sorted roughly from wordplay-driven to concept-driven:

1. **Overdue** — plays on both library fines and the coffee you were overdue for. Doubles as a cheeky brand voice ("You're Overdue for a break").
2. **The Marginalia** — the notes readers scribble in margins; intimate, bookish, a little erudite.
3. **Grounds for Fiction** — coffee grounds plus "grounds for" as in justification. Leans clever; risks being too pun-forward for some.
4. **Spine & Steam** — book spines and steaming espresso; clean two-noun rhythm that reads as a design system.
5. **Dog-Eared** — the folded-page corner; warm, worn-in, unpretentious. Signals a used/secondhand vibe more than new releases.
6. **Foreword** — a book's opening section, homophone of "forward"; minimal, modern, works as a one-word sign.
7. **Bean There, Read That** — maximally punny. High memorability, low sophistication — a deliberate bet, not a safe one.
8. **The Appendix** — the back-of-book section, also the "extra room" connotation; slightly dry, works if the brand is witty.
9. **Percolate** — coffee brewing method and the way ideas percolate while reading. Single word, verb-as-name, ownable.
10. **Chapter & Verse** — the idiom for exhaustive detail; literary without being about coffee at all, which is fine if books lead.
11. **Ink & Instant** — alliterative pairing of print and quick coffee; slightly self-aware about being a hybrid.
12. **The Reading Roast** — "roast" as coffee and as a lively reading/critique; playful, event-friendly (roast nights).
13. **Loose Leaf** — loose-leaf tea/paper and loose-leaf binder pages; soft, tactile, a bit gentler than the coffee-forward names. Note: reads more tea than coffee.
14. **Volume** — book volumes and the volume of a pour/a room's buzz; austere, confident, hard to own in search results.
15. **Steeped** — steeped tea and steeped in stories; evocative, but again tilts toward tea over coffee.

A few notes worth flagging, since a name is a decision you'll live with:

- **The dimension these don't address is availability.** Short common-word names (Foreword, Volume, Percolate, Overdue) are the most elegant but the hardest to trademark and to rank for online — there's likely existing coffee or book businesses on several. Before falling for one, check your local trademark register and domain/handle availability. That's the load-bearing constraint I can't resolve from here.
- **Pun density is a real tradeoff.** Items 3, 7, and 12 are stickier but cap your perceived sophistication; if you want to court a design-conscious crowd, they'll read the pun as trying too hard. The single-word names age better.
- **Tea drift:** 13 and 15 are strong names but semantically point at tea, not coffee — a mismatch if espresso is the anchor.
