The fight itself wasn't your failure — it was probably overdue. Financial disagreements almost always carry weight far beyond the money: control, security, different values about risk or what "taking care of things" means. That your emotions are running this hot suggests something deeper touched a nerve, not that you did something wrong.

The specific frame you're stuck on — "I failed as a provider" — deserves to be separated from the actual conflict. There's a real disagreement that needs addressing (the money question, the spending pattern, whatever triggered it). That's the work to do together. But whether you're *good at providing* and whether you *handled a disagreement well* are different questions, and the first shouldn't be decided by last night.

What actually happened: You and your partner hit a friction point. You responded the way you did, they responded their way, and neither of you was at your best (that's just how big fights go). That's not the same as failing at your role or failing them.

The useful path forward is not to convince yourself you didn't mess up — you may have said something harsh, or shut down, or missed something they needed — but to get specific about what you actually regret and what you'd do differently. That's actionable. "I'm a failure" is a feeling, not a place to build from.

When you're ready, a straightforward approach: acknowledge the specific thing that didn't land well, ask them what you missed about where they were coming from, and tell them what you actually want to build toward on the money question (not a defense, but a direction). That moves it from "who failed" to "what do we need to solve."

You aren't your worst moment in a fight. You're the person who feels this much about getting it right.
