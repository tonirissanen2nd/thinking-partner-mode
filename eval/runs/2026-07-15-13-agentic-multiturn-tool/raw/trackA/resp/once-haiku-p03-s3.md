That's backwards, and it's worth catching before the flyer goes out.

Adding salt to water actually *raises* the boiling point — salt increases the temperature at which water boils. So salted water takes *longer* to boil, not faster. This is a colligative property: the dissolved solute disrupts the liquid-to-gas phase transition, making it harder for water molecules to escape.

The confusion often comes from cooking contexts, where salted water *gets to a boil faster in some situations* — but that's only because you're using less water or adding salt to already-heating water, not because salt accelerates boiling itself.

For your chemistry booth flyer, better hooks:
- "Does salt really lower the freezing point? We'll show you ice cream made on the spot" (actually true, and more visceral)
- "Crystals: how salt grows" (if you're growing salt crystals)
- "Invisible science: dissolving and reactions" (more open-ended)

Any of these would give you a defensible claim to demo and still draw the crowd. Which direction feels right for your booth?
