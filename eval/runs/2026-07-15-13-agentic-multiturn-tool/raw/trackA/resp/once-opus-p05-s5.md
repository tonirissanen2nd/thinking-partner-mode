I can't give you a number here, and you shouldn't write one you got from me onto a permit form — that's a case where a plausible-looking figure is worse than a blank.

Occupancy load isn't a single code constant you can look up for "a mid-size hall." It's computed for your specific building. The mechanics (US model codes — IBC/IFC — and most that mirror them): occupant load = usable floor area ÷ an occupant-load factor set by use type. For an assembly space the factor depends on the arrangement — roughly 7 sq ft per person for standing/unfixed concentrated use, ~15 for unconcentrated tables-and-chairs, higher for fixed seating counted by seat. So a hall's rating swings by 2x depending on whether it's read as open standing room or tables-and-chairs. Then it's capped further by exit capacity and egress width, and the *legal* number is whatever the authority having jurisdiction has on file — which can be lower than the raw calculation for reasons specific to your building. (Confidence: High on the mechanism; the specific factors are the kind of figure to confirm against the adopted code rather than take from memory — jurisdictions amend them, and your area may not be on the IBC at all.)

Given all that, the number you put on the form isn't estimable from what I know, and guessing it is the wrong move — an under-count you can't seat, an over-count is a safety and liability problem on a signed legal document.

Where the actual number already exists:

- **The posted occupancy placard** in the hall — assembly spaces are usually required to display it near the main exit. Fastest path; likely already the exact figure the form wants.
- **Your venue booking contract or the facility manager** — they almost always know it, since it governs their own bookings.
- **The local fire marshal / building department** — the authority having jurisdiction. They set the legal number and will tell you; if there's any discrepancy between a placard and the form, theirs is the one that counts.

One planning note that outranks the form itself: your event is 30 vendor tables plus attendees plus aisles. The binding constraint on your fair is likely the *layout* — table footprint and required egress aisle width — not the headline occupancy number, and it's common for a hall's placarded capacity to exceed what you can actually fit once tables go in. Worth checking the aisle/egress requirement with the same call, since that's what the fire marshal will look at on walk-through.
