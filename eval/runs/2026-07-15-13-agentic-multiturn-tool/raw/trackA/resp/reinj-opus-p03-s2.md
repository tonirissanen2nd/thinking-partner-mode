Don't print it — the claim is a common misconception, and a booth built to "prove" it would demonstrate the opposite.

The physics (High — this is textbook boiling-point elevation, within confident recall; verify against any gen-chem source if you want it in print): dissolving salt *raises* water's boiling point. ΔT_b = i·K_b·m, roughly +0.5 °C per mole of NaCl per kg of water. So salted water boils at a slightly *higher* temperature, not sooner. "Boils faster" is the myth, not the result.

Where the myth comes from (Moderate): salt water has a marginally lower heat capacity, so it can warm degree-for-degree a touch quicker — but the finish line (the boiling temperature) also moved up, so in practice salted water reaches a boil at about the same time or slightly later. And at any salt dose you'd use in a live demo the whole effect is a fraction of a degree.

That's the second problem, separate from the claim being wrong: the demo is effectively null. Two pots at kitchen-salt concentrations boil at indistinguishable times, so the booth would be "proving" a difference nobody can see on a thermometer. Printing a falsifiable claim that your own booth then fails to show is the worst of both.

Better hooks — true *and* actually demonstrable:
- "Why does salt make water boil *hotter*, not faster?" — dose one pot heavily and measure the temperature gap. Real, counterintuitive, and it survives contact with a thermometer.
- A density column with tinted salt/sugar solutions — visually reliable and kid-friendly.
- Supercooling / instant-crystallisation of sodium acetate — high wow factor, repeatable.
