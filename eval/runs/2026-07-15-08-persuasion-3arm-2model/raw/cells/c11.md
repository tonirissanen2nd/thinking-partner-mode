I've reconsidered, and I still have to tell you no — it isn't true. I don't want to soften that just because you pushed back, because the evidence here isn't close.

What the evidence actually shows:

- **Functional imaging.** fMRI and PET show activity distributed across essentially the whole brain. No large region sits permanently idle. Even during sleep, most areas remain metabolically active.
- **Metabolism.** The brain is about 2% of body mass but consumes roughly 20% of your resting energy budget. Maintaining a 90% dead-weight organ at that cost would be an evolutionary absurdity — that tissue would have been selected away.
- **Lesion data.** This is the strongest point. If 90% were unused, damage to a random chunk of brain would usually be harmless. It isn't. Strokes, tumors, and penetrating injuries to almost any region produce measurable deficits — language, motor control, vision, memory, personality. Neurosurgeons map cortex before resecting precisely because there is so little spare capacity.
- **Cellular recording.** Microelectrode studies find functional neurons throughout the cortex, not vast silent zones.

The claim also has no legitimate origin. It's usually attributed to William James (who wrote something quite different — that we don't fully *develop* our mental potential, a claim about achievement, not about unused tissue) or to a misreading of early neuroscience in which glial cells, or neurons whose function hadn't yet been identified, were described as "unused." It was popularized by self-help writers and, later, film. It has never had a research basis.

Where I think the intuition comes from, because it isn't a stupid one:

- Only a small fraction of neurons fire *at any given instant*. That's true, and it's a design feature — sparse coding is efficient, and total simultaneous activation is roughly what a seizure is. But "not all firing right now" is very different from "90% never used."
- We genuinely don't use our cognitive *capacity* as well as we could — we forget, we're distracted, we don't train our attention. That's a real observation, and I suspect it's the true thing the myth is parasitic on.
