Hold on — don't print that. The premise is wrong, and it's wrong in exactly the way that sinks a booth built to "prove" it.

Adding salt does not make water boil faster. It raises the boiling point (boiling-point elevation, a colligative property): dissolved salt lowers water's vapor pressure, so salted water must reach a slightly *higher* temperature before it boils. Directionally, salt makes water boil hotter, i.e. if anything marginally *later*, not sooner. Confidence: High — this is settled textbook chemistry, not a contested or time-sensitive figure.

Two real effects worth having straight, since a sharp attendee may raise them:
- The boiling-point rise is tiny at culinary doses — order of +0.5 °C per mole of NaCl per kg of water, so a tablespoon in a liter is a fraction of a degree. Confidence on that exact coefficient: Moderate; verify it against a chem reference before you put any number in print.
- Salt water has slightly lower specific heat, so it can warm marginally faster per degree — this is the grain of truth the myth grows from. But it's swamped by the boiling-point rise and by realistic salt amounts; the net at demo scale is negligible and does not point in the "faster" direction as a headline.

Why this matters past pedantry: the flyer says the booth will *prove* it. A side-by-side pot demo won't — best case it shows no visible difference, worst case the salted pot lags and someone with a thermometer says so out loud. That's a printed, public claim failing at the one booth whose whole draw is that the science works. The asymmetry is the point: fixing a line now costs nothing; walking it back on the day, in front of the crowd, is the expensive, irreversible version.

Better hooks that are both true and demo cleanly:
- Flip it into the lesson: "Does salt make water boil faster? Come find out why the answer surprises people." The honest answer *is* the demo, and the curiosity gap does the same work the false claim was doing.
- If you'd rather a guaranteed crowd-pleaser, pick an effect that visibly fires every time: supercooled water freezing on contact, a density-column "rainbow," or the classic baking-soda-and-vinegar eruption.
