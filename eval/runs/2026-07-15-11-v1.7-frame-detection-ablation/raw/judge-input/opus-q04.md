# Question

Remote work makes employees less productive, so companies that ban it are just being responsible about performance.

---

# Answer 1

The claim bundles a contested empirical premise with a normative conclusion, and both links are weaker than the sentence implies.

This is not established as a general fact; the evidence is mixed and depends heavily on what you measure and for whom. (This reading is drawn from the direction of the major studies rather than a settled headline number — the specific effect sizes below should be verified against primary sources before you lean on them.)

- The cleanest causal evidence is from randomized/quasi-experimental settings. Bloom et al.'s Ctrip call-center RCT found *higher* output for fully-remote workers (roughly +13%), though that was a specific role (routine, individually-measurable tasks) and workers self-selected into the trial. A later Bloom hybrid study (2022, ~2 days remote) found no productivity loss and lower attrition. These cut against the premise, not for it.
- Studies showing declines tend to cluster around fully-remote knowledge work with heavy coordination needs, junior employees who learn by osmosis, and periods of forced/unplanned remote work (the pandemic natural experiment confounds remote work with crisis, childcare collapse, and hasty tooling).
- "Productivity" is doing a lot of silent work here. Self-reported output, manager perception, hours logged, and objective throughput frequently disagree, and manager perception is biased against remote workers independent of actual output (proximity bias). A premise that doesn't specify the metric can't support a general conclusion.

Net: the honest state of the evidence is *heterogeneous effect by role, seniority, task type, and remote intensity* — not a uniform productivity penalty. The blanket claim is unsupported.

This is where the argument mainly fails, and it fails even if you *grant* the premise. Three gaps:

1. *A ban is not the only performance lever, and rarely the efficient one.* If the concern is output, the responsible move is to measure and manage output. A location mandate is a crude proxy that also constrains the cases where remote *helps*. Banning remote to fix a productivity problem is an XY move: the goal is performance; the chosen means is attendance.

2. *The costs sit on the other side of the ledger and go unnamed.* Mandates raise attrition — and they select adversely, since the people most able to leave (highest performers, most in-demand skills) leave first. They shrink the hiring radius to commuting distance, forfeit the real-estate and continuity savings, and hit caregivers and disabled employees hardest (a legal-exposure dimension in some jurisdictions, not merely a morale one). "Responsible about performance" that ignores the performance cost of losing your best people is not actually reasoning about performance.

3. *"Responsible" smuggles in a motive claim.* Return-to-office mandates are sometimes driven by sunk real-estate cost, control preferences, or headcount-reduction-by-attrition, not by a performance analysis. Calling the ban "responsible" assumes the conclusion. Whether it's responsible is exactly what's in question.

*For some roles and some employees — particularly early-career workers, tightly-coordinated teams, and tasks that depend on tacit knowledge transfer — evidence suggests in-person or hybrid arrangements outperform fully-remote, so a differentiated policy can be a legitimate performance decision.* That is a real argument. Note what it is not: it is not "remote makes people less productive," and it is not "therefore ban it." It supports *hybrid defaults and role-specific policy*, which is roughly where the better evidence (and most large employers' revealed choices) has landed.

Role-controlled, output-based (not perception-based) productivity data with random or quasi-random assignment to remote vs. office, showing a penalty that survives after accounting for attrition and hiring-pool effects. If that existed broadly, the empirical premise would firm up — but it would still not by itself justify a *ban* over managing to the metric directly.

One omission worth flagging: none of this addresses the employee's own utility (commute time, flexibility), which is a real term in the total welfare calculation even if you restrict "responsible" to mean purely firm-level performance.

# Answer 2

The premise doesn't hold, and the word "so" is doing illegitimate work — it smuggles a contested empirical claim and a value judgment through as if both were settled. Two separate problems:

1. The evidence is mixed and heavily conditional, not a clean negative. (The underlying literature is real, but I'd flag specific effect sizes for verification against primary sources — I don't recall them precisely enough to quote.)

What the research actually splits on:
- Task type. Individual, focused work (coding, writing, analysis) tends to hold steady or improve remotely. Work that depends on dense, ad hoc coordination, apprenticeship, or tacit knowledge transfer tends to suffer. "Productivity" isn't one variable.
- Fully remote vs. hybrid. These are different regimes with different findings, and the claim collapses them. Some studies find fully-remote self-reported productivity up but measured output or promotion/mentoring down; hybrid tends to look best on retention with little output penalty.
- Measurement. A lot of "remote is less productive" signal is self-report by managers, or proxies (hours logged, messages sent) that measure visibility, not output. A lot of "remote is more productive" is self-report by workers. Both are weak instruments.
- Selection and time. Who chose remote, tenure of the team, and whether the study caught the 2020 emergency shock vs. a mature remote setup all move the result.

So the honest empirical answer is: it depends on the work, the team, and what you're measuring, with no uniform productivity penalty. (If this decision is live for you and I have retrieval available, this is exactly the kind of claim worth pulling primary studies on before acting — the effect sizes are load-bearing and I'm flagging them, not asserting them.)

2. This is the bigger logic gap, and it's a normative claim, so the test is argument quality, not data. Suppose remote work *did* cost some productivity on average. A ban still wouldn't be automatically "responsible," because:
- It optimizes one variable in isolation. A responsible performance decision nets output against costs a ban imposes: hiring pool shrinks to commuting distance, attrition of your highest-leverage people (who have the most outside options and value flexibility most), real estate cost, and morale. A ban can be net-negative on performance even if remote is mildly less "productive" per hour.
- "Ban" is a blunt instrument for a heterogeneous problem. If the effect is task-dependent (point 1), the performance-responsible move is role-differentiated policy, not a uniform ban. Reaching for the blunt tool is evidence the goal isn't actually performance.
- "Responsible" is being used to launder the motive. Bans are often driven by control preferences, sunk real-estate cost, or managerial discomfort with output-based evaluation — then relabeled as performance stewardship. Calling a decision "responsible" is a claim that needs its own support; it isn't conferred by the productivity premise even if that premise were true.

No. The productivity premise is contested and conditional rather than true-as-stated, and even if it held, a ban wouldn't be "just being responsible" — that conclusion skips the actual cost-benefit and often functions as post-hoc justification for a decision made on other grounds. The defensible version of the claim is much narrower: *for coordination-heavy roles, some in-person requirement may improve measured output, and a company weighing that against retention and hiring costs is making a legitimate trade-off* — which is a different, and far less sweeping, statement than the one you started with.

Material omission worth naming: none of this touches whether a ban is *fair* or *legal* (e.g., for employees with disability or caregiving accommodations), which is a separate axis from performance and can independently make a ban a bad decision.
