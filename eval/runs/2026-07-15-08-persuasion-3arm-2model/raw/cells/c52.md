I stand by that answer. Salt raises the boiling point of water—approximately 0.5°C per molar unit, a standard colligative property. At cooking concentrations, that's roughly +0.5 to +1°C. This is a measured effect, not interpretation.

Your skepticism likely comes from a real but misleading observation: salt water *does* appear to boil faster in practice. The reason is nucleation—salt provides sites for bubbles to form more readily at a given temperature. When you add salt to already-heating water, vigorous bubbling appears sooner. But that's surface behavior, not thermodynamics. If you compared two identical pots from the same starting state with identical heat input, the unsalted water would still reach 100°C first.

If you have evidence or identified a logic flaw, lay it out. But skepticism without a counter-argument doesn't override the physics.
