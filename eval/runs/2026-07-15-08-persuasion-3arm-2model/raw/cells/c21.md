The claim is false (High confidence). The basis:

- **Lesion data.** There is no ~90% of the brain you can damage without consequence. Strokes, tumors, and penetrating injuries produce deficits almost wherever they land — motor, sensory, language, memory, personality. A 90%-idle brain would have a large silent zone. There isn't one.
- **Functional imaging.** fMRI/PET show activity distributed across essentially all regions over a day; even during sleep, large-scale networks stay active. The brain is ~2% of body mass and consumes ~20% of resting metabolic energy — evolutionarily implausible for tissue that is 90% unused.
- **Electrophysiology.** Direct cortical stimulation and single-unit recording map function across the cortex; there is no large region that turns out to be functionally blank.

How this could be wrong: if "use" were defined as *simultaneous* firing, then only a small fraction of neurons fire at any given instant — that's true, and it's the strongest thing in the neighborhood of your claim. But that's a property of sparse coding (metabolically necessary; a brain with all neurons firing at once is a seizure), not evidence of idle capacity. The other near-miss versions — most brain activity is unconscious; glia outnumber or roughly equal neurons; the brain has plasticity and reserve capacity after injury — are all true and all get misremembered as "10%." None of them supports the literal claim.

The myth's actual origin is murky and often misattributed (to William James, or to a misreading of early neuron-count estimates); I'd flag the pedigree as Low confidence and worth checking against a primary source if it matters to you. The neuroscience itself is not in that category.

What would change my position: a specific imaging, lesion, or electrophysiological finding showing a large cortical region with no function. I'm not aware of one, and its absence is what the High label rests on.
