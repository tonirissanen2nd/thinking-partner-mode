Short answer: no. For the general (largely vitamin-D-replete) population, "supplementation lowers all-cause mortality" is **not** a solid, well-established finding. It's a claim with a real but weak and contested evidentiary base, and the strongest study designs largely fail to confirm it. Confidence: **High** that this is not settled science; **Moderate** on the specific reasons below.

This is an empirical causal claim, so the question is whether the evidence supports causation, not just association. Three layers of evidence, in ascending order of reliability:

**Observational studies** consistently show low vitamin D levels tracking with higher mortality. But this is exactly where you'd expect confounding and reverse causation: people who are chronically ill, frail, obese, or housebound have low vitamin D *because* of poor health and low sun exposure — the low level is a marker, not necessarily a cause. This layer is close to worthless for establishing that supplementing *raises* survival. (High confidence this is the dominant flaw in the observational signal.)

**Randomized controlled trials** are the relevant test. The largest, VITAL (Manson et al., NEJM 2019, ~25,000 US adults, 2000 IU/day, ~5 yr), found no significant reduction in all-cause mortality, nor in its primary cancer/cardiovascular endpoints. Other large RCTs (e.g. the D-Health trial in older Australians, and VIDA in New Zealand) similarly found no all-cause mortality benefit. This is the crux: in trials of broadly unselected, mostly replete populations, the effect is essentially null. (High confidence on VITAL's null all-cause result; I'm recalling effect direction, not exact figures.)

**Meta-analyses** are where the "lowers mortality" claim mostly comes from. The Cochrane review (Bjelakovic et al., 2014) did report a small all-cause mortality reduction with vitamin D3 — but it was driven heavily by trials in elderly, often institutionalized women, i.e. a deficient, high-risk subgroup, not the general public. Several later meta-analyses find the signal, where it survives, concentrated in **cancer mortality** over long follow-up rather than all-cause mortality, and shrinking as larger, cleaner trials are added. (Moderate confidence; I'd flag the exact pooled risk ratios as something to verify rather than take from me — I won't invent a number.)

What this adds up to: the honest reading is that any mortality benefit is (a) small at best, (b) plausibly real mainly in people who are genuinely **deficient** or elderly/institutionalized, and (c) not demonstrated in the general, replete population that headlines imply. The single biggest omitted dimension in most reporting — and the one that reconciles the conflicting studies — is **baseline vitamin D status**: correcting a real deficiency is a different intervention from topping up someone already sufficient, and the "general population" framing collapses the two.

On acting on it — note the asymmetry, because it changes the decision even under uncertainty:

- **If you're deficient** (low measured 25-OH-D, limited sun, dark skin at high latitude, older, housebound): correcting it at standard doses (~800–2000 IU/day) is cheap, low-risk, and defensible on bone/fall-and-broader-deficiency grounds, even though the *mortality* claim specifically is unproven. This is the bounded-downside option.
- **If you're replete**: supplementing for a mortality benefit is acting on evidence that the best trials don't support. Low doses are still low-risk, so the downside is mostly the false confidence, not physical harm.
- **The one real ruin-side caveat**: high-dose/megadose vitamin D (chronic tens of thousands of IU) can cause hypercalcemia, and some trials of large intermittent boluses have shown *increased* fall/fracture risk. "More is better" is affirmatively wrong here. The safe zone is standard daily dosing, not aggressive loading.

Bottom line: treat it as "correcting deficiency is sensible; general-population life extension is not established." If your real goal is to reduce mortality risk, vitamin D is at best a minor lever aimed at a specific deficient subgroup — worth a 25-OH-D test to know which group you're in, rather than blanket supplementation on the strength of the headline.
