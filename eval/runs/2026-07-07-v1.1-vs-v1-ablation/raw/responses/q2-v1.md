Before the "how," two things in the framing are worth stopping on, because getting them wrong is what turns a fixable situation into a career problem.

**The premise "he wants to take credit" is an inference, not an observation.** What you've actually observed is a pattern: you're left off emails, and the effect is that your work is visible to others without your name attached. The motive — deliberate credit-theft — is one explanation. Others that produce the identical pattern: he's disorganized about CC lists; he thinks of the work as "his team's" output and doesn't register your individual contribution as needing attribution; he's optimizing for a short recipient list and you didn't make the cut. I can't rank these for your specific coworker (Unknown — I have no read on him), and neither can you from the outside. That matters because the confrontation you're preparing assumes a motive you can't prove, and if you lead with it and you're wrong, you've made a false accusation that's now the story people remember. Even if you're right about the motive, stating it out loud rarely produces a confession — it produces a defense.

**"Confront" may be the wrong goal.** You don't actually want a confrontation; you want credit for your work to reach the people who allocate rewards. Those are different objectives, and the confrontation is a poor instrument for the real one. A satisfying showdown where he admits nothing leaves your actual problem — visibility to decision-makers — untouched.

So separate the two things you can control:

**1. Fix the attribution mechanically, without needing his cooperation.** This is the highest-leverage move and it doesn't require reading his mind. Get yourself onto the thread from the front, not as a grievance:
- Ask to be added going forward, framed as logistics: "For anything touching [the work I'm doing], loop me in directly — I want to catch questions in real time rather than get them relayed." Reasonable, and it removes his discretion over whether you're on the email.
- Create your own paper trail. Send status updates on your work to the relevant stakeholders (your manager, the internal client) as a normal cadence, not as a defensive maneuver. If your name is already on the record describing the work, a later omission can't erase authorship.
- Where the work product itself allows it, put your name in it — the doc, the deck, the commit, the analysis footer.

**2. Address the interpersonal pattern directly, but calibrated to what you can actually assert.** If you do talk to him, describe the behavior and its effect, not his intent — because the behavior is observable and the intent isn't, and the observable version is much harder to deflect: "I've noticed I'm getting left off threads about work I did. When that happens, the people reading it don't know it was mine. I need to be on those." This gives him room to correct course if it was carelessness, and puts him on notice if it wasn't — without you having staked out a motive claim you'd have to walk back.

**On escalation and downside** (the dimension that determines whether this is low-stakes or not): if it continues *after* you've explicitly asked to be included, the disorganization explanations get much weaker and deliberate exclusion becomes the better inference. That is also the point at which involving your manager stops looking like petty conflict and starts looking like a documented pattern — because now you have a request on record and a continued omission after it. Going to your manager *now*, before that, is the higher-variance move: without the paper trail it reads as a he-said interpersonal complaint, and if your manager sides with the status quo you've spent credibility and gotten nothing.

The asymmetry to keep in view: the mechanical fixes in (1) are nearly all upside and low downside — you can do them today regardless of his motive, and they work whether he's malicious or just sloppy. The motive-based confrontation is the one with a real failure mode. Do the robust thing first; reserve the confrontation for when the behavior survives it.
