Not better than a doctor — better than an unsupported claim, which is what "thirty years of experience" is on this particular question. Experience is real evidence about things a clinician can observe directly: what hand pain looks like, which patients get worse, what a joint exam finds. It is weak evidence about a population-level causal link between a habit and a disease, because the clinician never sees the counterfactual. He sees the knuckle-crackers who came in with sore hands; he does not see the knuckle-crackers who never came in. That's the classic selection problem, and no amount of tenure fixes it. If you want me to update, the thing to bring back is what he's citing — a study, not a career.

What the literature shows (Moderate-High confidence in the direction, based on convergent case-control and cohort data plus an implausible mechanism):

- The proposed mechanism doesn't route to osteoarthritis. The crack is cavitation — gas coming out of solution in the synovial fluid as the joint capsule is stretched, plus the associated pressure change. It isn't cartilage loading. Osteoarthritis is a cartilage/subchondral bone disease. There's no established pathway from a gas bubble to cartilage degradation. (High confidence on the mechanism of the sound itself; the imaging work behind it is fairly recent and I'd verify the details.)
- Case-control work comparing habitual crackers to non-crackers has not found elevated rates of hand osteoarthritis. I recall a US case-control study around 2011 (deWeber et al., I believe, a few hundred subjects) reporting no association, and an older self-experiment — one man cracked the knuckles of one hand only for roughly sixty years and found no difference between hands at the end. Flag: my recall on the author names, sample sizes, and dates is Moderate, not High. Those are checkable against primary sources and you should check them rather than take my numbers.

Confidence, split, because "no association" hides two different claims: **High** that knuckle cracking is not a substantial cause of osteoarthritis. **Moderate** that it has no effect at all. The failure path for the strong version is real — the evidence is observational, samples are modest, cracking frequency is usually self-reported and recall-biased, and no one is going to run a randomized trial. A small effect could hide inside that. What would change my mind: a prospective cohort with imaging-confirmed OA outcomes and non-self-reported exposure, showing a dose-response relationship. Nothing I know of does.

The steelman of your doctor, which you should actually put to him: there is at least one older study (Castellanos & Axelrod, ~1990, my recall is Moderate) reporting that habitual crackers had more hand swelling and lower grip strength. That is contested and confounded — habitual cracking may be a marker for anxiety, occupational hand use, or existing joint laxity rather than a cause of anything. But it is a real finding, and it is not the same claim as arthritis. If what he means is "cracking is associated with hand problems," that's defensible and weakly supported. If he means "it causes arthritis," that's the specific claim the literature doesn't support.

One thing I'm not addressing, because you didn't ask and it changes the answer: if your knuckles crack involuntarily, or the cracking is painful, that's a different question entirely — pain-on-cracking can indicate an actual joint problem, and it's worth his examination rather than my literature summary. The null result covers voluntary, painless cracking.
