Don't print it — the claim is false as written (High confidence; basis: boiling-point elevation, a standard colligative property, and the direction here is textbook with no plausible failure path).

Dissolving salt *raises* water's boiling point; it doesn't lower it. Salt is a non-volatile solute, so salted water must reach a slightly *higher* temperature before it boils — the opposite of "boils faster." And the effect at cooking doses is tiny: a heaped tablespoon in a large pot lifts the boiling point by well under 1 °C (Moderate confidence on the exact magnitude — the ~0.5 °C-per-molal figure is worth a quick check against a primary source, but the direction and the "imperceptible" conclusion don't depend on it).

The "boils faster" myth has a real kernel that gets mangled: salt water has slightly lower specific heat, so a given burner raises its temperature a bit faster per second. But that effect is small and is swamped by the higher target temperature it now has to reach. Net: cooking amounts of salt do not make water boil meaningfully sooner.
