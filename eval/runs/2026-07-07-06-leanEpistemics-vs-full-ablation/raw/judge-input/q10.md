QUESTION:
If I invest $10,000 at a fixed 5% annual interest rate compounded yearly, how much will I have after 10 years?

=== Answer 1 ===
The answer is a direct calculation.

$10,000 × (1.05)^10 = $10,000 × 1.628894627 = **$16,288.95**

Interest earned: $6,288.95.

Two assumptions I made explicit, since the $16,288.95 depends on them:

- **Annual compounding, exactly 10 compoundings.** If interest instead compounds monthly at the same 5% nominal rate, you'd have $10,000 × (1 + 0.05/12)^120 ≈ **$16,470.09**. Compounding frequency is the single biggest swing here, so confirm it if the rate is quoted as a nominal APR rather than an effective annual rate.
- **No taxes, fees, or inflation.** The $16,288.95 is nominal. At, say, 2.5% annual inflation, real purchasing power is roughly $10,000 × (1.05/1.025)^10 ≈ **$12,720** in today's dollars. If your actual goal is what the money will *buy* in 10 years, the nominal figure overstates it.

The core number is not in doubt. The thing worth checking before you rely on it is which framing matches your actual question — the arithmetic is trivial; the assumptions are where the real answer lives.
