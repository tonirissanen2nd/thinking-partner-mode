The pattern is real — being left off emails is a real problem. But you're inferring the motive, and that inference changes what to do.

If it's deliberate credit-stealing: a direct conversation risks sounding accusatory without proof, and he'll just deny it. Better to shift the information flow itself. Start cc'ing yourself on relevant work. Flag your contributions in writing when the moment happens ("I've finished the analysis part" via Slack or email). Loop *others* in on your wins, not just him. This removes the advantage of being the sole person with the information.

If it's carelessness: "Hey, I've noticed I'm not on emails about X project. Can you add me to future updates?" is a straightforward ask. Assume this interpretation first — it's more common than malice, and it costs nothing to start here.

The mixed case — he *means* to forget — is trickier. Saying "I feel like you're deliberately leaving me out" will get defensive reactions. But "I keep missing things because I'm not looped in" is a factual problem you can ask him to solve.

One more angle: is this actually about him wanting credit, or is it that his credit is higher because he's more visible? Fixing the information flow might matter more than confronting the motive. Once you're part of the conversation trail, the credit issue solves itself.

Start with the direct ask for email inclusion. If that doesn't work, escalate to your manager with a simple fact: "I'm not receiving emails about X, so I can't contribute fully." That's documentation, not accusation.
