QUESTION:
Is nuclear power safer than solar, yes or no?

=== Answer 1 ===
Not answerable as a clean yes/no — but they're effectively tied, and both are ~100x safer than fossil fuels. The yes/no framing hides the only interesting part.

On the standard metric — deaths per terawatt-hour of electricity produced, counting the full lifecycle (mining, construction, operation, accidents, pollution) — nuclear and solar land in the same tiny bracket. Rough figures I recall from the Our World in Data compilation (based on Markandya & Wilkinson and later updates):

- Nuclear: ~0.03 deaths/TWh
- Solar: ~0.02 deaths/TWh
- For contrast: coal ~24–25, oil ~18, gas ~3, hydro ~1.3, wind ~0.04

Both sit in the ~0.02–0.04 range and are two-to-three orders of magnitude safer than coal/oil. The exact rank order between nuclear and solar is uncertain — the gap is smaller than the methodological noise (how you count Chernobyl/Fukushima excess cancers, whether rooftop-install falls are attributed to solar, which decade's technology you use). Verify the specific numbers against Our World in Data before quoting them.

So "which is safer" between these two is a coin-flip that depends on accounting choices, not a real physical difference.

The dimension the mortality figure omits, and the reason the two aren't truly interchangeable:

- **Nuclear carries a fat tail.** Its historical death rate is low *including* its worst accidents, but the risk distribution has a low-probability, high-consequence tail (major release, long-lived waste, weapons-material adjacency) that a deaths-per-TWh average cannot fully represent. The average is reassuring; it is not the whole risk object.
- **Solar's risks are thin-tailed and distributed** — installer falls, some toxic manufacturing inputs, e-waste. Bad, but bounded and local. No plausible single event kills thousands.

If your decision criterion is expected mortality per unit energy, they're a wash and you should choose on cost, siting, grid fit, and dispatchability instead. If your criterion is bounded worst-case / avoiding tail exposure, solar has the structurally safer profile even though the point estimates match. Those are different questions wearing the same word "safer" — which one you mean determines the answer more than any data point does.

=== Answer 2 ===
No — but "no" here means "not meaningfully different," not "solar wins decisively." On the standard metric, they're effectively tied.

**The metric that forces an answer.** "Safer" only becomes a yes/no once you fix a denominator. The defensible one is deaths per unit of energy delivered (TWh), which captures the full lifecycle — mining, manufacturing, construction, operation, accidents. On that metric the widely-cited estimates (Our World in Data, drawing on Markandya & Wilkinson and Sovacool) put both in the same lowest tier:

- Nuclear: ~0.03 deaths/TWh
- Solar: ~0.02 deaths/TWh
- (For scale: coal ~24–25, oil ~18, gas ~3, hydro ~1.3, wind ~0.04)

The nuclear–solar gap is smaller than the uncertainty in either figure, so treating one as "safer" than the other is reading noise. The real result is that both are ~1000x safer than coal and belong to the same safe cluster as wind. I'm recalling these numbers, not reading them — the specific decimals should be checked against the current Our World in Data source before you quote them, and Chernobyl's death toll alone spans 4,000 to 60,000+ across credible estimates, which shifts nuclear's figure materially depending on the choice.

**Where the two answers diverge.** The reason your question doesn't collapse cleanly to one number is that the two technologies fail in structurally different ways:

- Solar's harm is *frequent and diffuse* — mostly falls during rooftop installation, plus manufacturing exposure. Well-characterized, bounded, no tail. The per-TWh figure captures it faithfully.
- Nuclear's harm is *rare and concentrated*, with a genuine fat tail: a low-probability catastrophic release, plus long-horizon waste and proliferation externalities that a deaths/TWh average doesn't price. The historical record already contains Chernobyl and Fukushima and nuclear still lands at ~0.03 — but a per-TWh mean is exactly the statistic that under-weights a tail, so I'd treat nuclear's advantage-or-parity as more fragile than solar's.

**On realized historical mortality, statistically tied. On robustness to worst-case outcomes, solar's risk is easier to bound.** If your decision is about grid reliability or emissions, "safety" defined this way isn't the variable that should drive it — both are safe enough that the choice turns on cost, intermittency, siting, and baseload needs instead.

This compares *human mortality* only. If you meant a different sense of "safer" — proliferation risk, financial risk of a stranded plant, land-use, or waste stewardship over centuries — the comparison changes and nuclear looks worse on several of those.
