The claim bundles a contested empirical premise with a logical leap, so it doesn't hold as stated. Let me separate the two.

This is too flat to be true as a universal. The evidence splits sharply by *modality* and *task type*, and that split is the whole story:

- **Hybrid** (a few days remote): the better-identified studies find no productivity penalty and a measurable retention gain. Bloom's 2024 randomized trial at Trip.com (hybrid, ~1,600 workers) found no drop in output or promotions and roughly a one-third cut in quit rates.
- **Fully remote**: genuinely mixed and sometimes negative. The Emanuel–Harrington work on call-center workers moving fully remote found a productivity decline on the order of ~10%; some data-entry and complex-collaboration settings show similar or worse. Bloom's earlier Ctrip RCT, by contrast, showed a ~13% *gain* for fully-remote call staff. The results are context-dependent, and I'd flag the specific percentages for verification against the primary papers before you lean on them.

So the accurate version of the premise is: *fully-remote arrangements sometimes reduce individual output in coordination- or supervision-heavy roles; hybrid generally does not.* The unqualified claim overgeneralizes from the hardest cases.

One methodological caveat worth naming: a lot of "remote kills productivity" evidence is self-reported by managers or proxied by keystroke/activity monitoring, which measures visible busyness, not output. The cleaner the measurement, the weaker the effect tends to look.

Even granting a productivity penalty, the conclusion doesn't follow — it commits a scope error. "Responsible about performance" would have to mean *net effect on firm value*, not per-hour individual output. A ban that recovers, say, 8% output can simultaneously:

- raise attrition (replacement cost is commonly cited at ~50–200% of salary per departure — but directionally large),
- shrink the hiring pool to a commuting radius,
- surrender real-estate savings,
- and lower morale in exactly the workers who value flexibility most.

A firm optimizing performance nets these against each other; it doesn't privilege one term. So even under a pessimistic productivity read, a *blanket ban* is rarely the performance-maximizing move — a role-differentiated or hybrid policy dominates it on the firm's own logic. The word "responsible" is also doing quiet normative work: it reframes a contestable cost-benefit tradeoff as obvious prudence.

If your underlying question is "should a company ban remote work to protect performance," the answer is: for most knowledge roles, no — hybrid captures the retention upside without a clear output cost, and blanket bans ignore the offsetting terms. For a specific subset (heavy real-time coordination, close supervision of junior staff, tasks with weak individual accountability) a *higher in-office requirement* can be defensible — but that's a targeted policy argument, not a ban, and not a general law about remote work.

None of this addresses *why* a given company might actually want a ban — control, real-estate sunk cost, or using attrition as quiet headcount reduction. If the real motive is one of those, "performance" is a rationale rather than the reason, and the productivity debate is beside the point.

What would move me: role-level output data (not activity metrics) from the specific context you have in mind, plus its attrition and hiring-pool numbers. Do you have a particular company or role in view, or is this the general claim?
