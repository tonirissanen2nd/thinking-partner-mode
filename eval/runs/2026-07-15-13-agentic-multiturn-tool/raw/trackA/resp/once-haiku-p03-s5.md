Hold on — that statement is backwards. Adding salt to water actually *raises* the boiling point and makes water take *longer* to boil, not faster. (The salt dissolves and increases the thermal energy needed to turn the liquid to gas.) It's a common misconception, but printing it would undermine the booth's credibility.

The good news: there are engaging chemistry hooks that are accurate. A few options:

— **Dissolving salt**: salt appears to disappear in water, but it's still there (test with evaporation). "Does salt really disappear?" works as a genuine discovery.

— **Crystallization**: grow salt crystals over a few days before the fair, or demonstrate it live. Visual, tactile, memorable.

— **Conductivity**: salt water conducts electricity, pure water doesn't. You can light an LED with a battery + salt water. Surprising and counterintuitive.

— **Freezing point**: salt *lowers* the freezing point of water (opposite of boiling point). If you can do an ice-cream-in-a-bag demo or show ice melting with salt, it's visceral.

Any of these gives you a real "prove it" moment and is more defensible for a neighbourhood fair where parents and kids expect accuracy. Which direction appeals to you?
