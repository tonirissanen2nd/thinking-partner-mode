# Question

I've heard that washing raw chicken in the sink before cooking it actually increases the risk of food poisoning, because it splashes bacteria onto nearby surfaces rather than killing anything — is that right?

---

# Answer 1

Yes, that's right, and it's the standard food-safety guidance from bodies like the US CDC/USDA and the UK's NHS and FSA.

- Washing doesn't kill anything. Bacteria like *Salmonella* and *Campylobacter* aren't rinsed off in any meaningful way by tap water, and the water certainly doesn't kill them. Only cooking to a safe internal temperature does that (poultry: 74°C / 165°F).
- Splashing spreads them. Running water off raw chicken can aerosolize and splash contaminated droplets onto surrounding surfaces — countertops, utensils, other food, your hands, sponges — up to a fair distance around the sink. That's the actual mechanism of added risk: you take contamination that was confined to the meat and scatter it around your prep area.

So the net effect is that washing creates a cross-contamination hazard while providing no benefit. The advice is simply: don't wash it. Pat it dry with paper towel if you want a better sear (then bin the towel), and rely on cooking temperature for safety.

- If you *do* wash chicken for some reason, thoroughly cleaning and disinfecting the sink and any splashed surfaces afterward reduces the risk — the danger is the un-cleaned splatter, not the water itself.
- "Increases the risk of food poisoning" is directionally correct, but I'd put it as *introduces an avoidable cross-contamination risk* rather than implying it's a leading cause of illness on its own. The magnitude of real-world harm from this specific habit is harder to pin down than the mechanism is — the mechanism is well established; precise attributable case numbers I'd want to check rather than assert.

# Answer 2

Broadly, yes — that's the mainstream food-safety position, and the reasoning you've given is basically the right one. The CDC, the USDA, and the UK's Food Standards Agency all advise against rinsing raw chicken, for exactly the reason you describe.

Rinsing doesn't sterilize anything. Campylobacter and Salmonella aren't sitting loosely on the surface waiting to be flushed off — they're adhered to the meat and lodged in its crevices. Cold tap water removes some, but nowhere near enough to matter. The thing that actually makes chicken safe is heat: cooking it through (165°F / 74°C at the thickest point) kills the bacteria regardless of what you did beforehand. So the rinse contributes nothing to the step that actually protects you.

Rinsing does move bacteria around. Water hitting meat generates aerosols and droplets. Studies using fluorescent tracers or harmless marker bacteria have found contamination on the sink basin and faucet, on surrounding counters, and on nearby dishes and utensils — with some detectable spread on the order of half a meter or more. If a colander of salad or a cutting board is sitting next to the sink, that's a real transfer route, and unlike the chicken, the salad never gets cooked.

A few honest caveats, since the messaging around this sometimes overstates its case:

- The sink gets contaminated either way. A well-known USDA/Drexel observational study found that participants who *didn't* wash their chicken still contaminated their sinks at high rates, just from handling the package and the meat. So washing isn't the only sin; sloppy handling generally is the problem. The advice "don't wash" is a cheap win, not a complete solution.
- Nobody has counted the illnesses. There's no study showing "X cases of campylobacteriosis per year are caused by chicken-washing." The recommendation rests on demonstrated contamination pathways plus the absence of any benefit — a sensible basis, but it's a risk-reduction argument, not a measured body count.
- It's a real culinary tradition, not just ignorance. Washing chicken (often with lemon, vinegar, or salt) is standard practice in many Caribbean, West African, Latin American, and South Asian kitchens, and food-safety campaigns have sometimes been clumsy about that. The acid doesn't meaningfully sterilize either, but if you're going to do it, doing it in a bowl rather than under a running tap, and then washing the bowl, the sink, and your hands thoroughly with hot soapy water, contains most of the splash risk.

Practically: skip the rinse. If you want dry skin for better browning, pat the chicken with paper towels and bin them — that's more effective than rinsing anyway, and it doesn't aerosolize anything. Then wash your hands, and clean anything the raw meat or its packaging touched.
