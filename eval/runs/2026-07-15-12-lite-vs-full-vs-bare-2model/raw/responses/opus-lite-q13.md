Before the steps, the harder question: cashing out an entire 401(k) for a down payment is one of the more expensive ways to fund a house, and it's worth being sure you want to do it before I walk you through how.

Here's why, and these are the load-bearing facts to pin down for your specific situation:

**What a full cash-out costs you**
- **Income tax.** The whole withdrawal gets added to your taxable income for the year. A large balance can push you into a higher bracket, so a chunk of it is taxed at your top marginal rate — federal plus state if your state has income tax.
- **10% early-withdrawal penalty** if you're under 59½. This is on top of the income tax, and there's no first-home exception for 401(k)s. (There *is* a limited first-home exception for IRAs — up to $10,000 — but not for 401(k)s. If someone told you your retirement account has a homebuyer exception, that's the IRA rule being misapplied.)
- **Lost compounding.** Money pulled out now stops growing tax-deferred for the rest of your working life. For a balance with decades left to grow, the foregone growth usually dwarfs the tax and penalty.

Put together, it's common for a taxpayer under 59½ to net somewhere around half to two-thirds of the balance after tax and penalty — the exact fraction depends entirely on your bracket and state, so I can't hand you a precise number without those. That's the figure to actually run before deciding.

**Routes that get you a bigger down payment for less**
- **401(k) loan** (if your plan allows one): borrow up to 50% of the vested balance or $50,000, whichever is less, and pay yourself back with interest. No tax, no penalty, and the money stays invested-ish. The catch: if you leave or lose the job, the balance may come due quickly, and you're paying it back with after-tax dollars.
- A **smaller down payment** with private mortgage insurance (PMI). PMI feels like waste, but it's often far cheaper than the tax-plus-penalty hit, and it drops off once you reach ~20% equity.
- **First-time buyer / low-down-payment programs** (FHA, VA if you qualify, state housing-agency loans), which can shrink how much cash you need up front.
- If part of your goal is just hitting 20% to avoid PMI, compare that PMI cost head-to-head against what the cash-out actually costs. Often the down payment isn't worth destroying the retirement account for.

I'm not a licensed financial advisor, and the right call depends on your age, bracket, plan rules, and how the rest of your retirement savings looks — worth a conversation with a fee-only advisor or a CPA who can run your actual numbers.

**If, after weighing that, you still want to withdraw the full balance**, the mechanics are roughly:
1. Confirm you've left the employer or that your plan allows in-service withdrawals — many plans won't let you take a full distribution while you're still employed there.
2. Contact your plan administrator (the record-keeper listed on your statement) and request a full distribution / lump-sum withdrawal.
3. Decide on tax withholding. Cash distributions are subject to a mandatory 20% federal withholding, which may or may not cover what you'll actually owe — you could still have a bill (or the penalty) come tax time.
4. Complete their distribution form, specifying a cash payout rather than a rollover.
5. Receive the funds, and set aside enough to cover the tax and penalty so you're not caught short next April.
