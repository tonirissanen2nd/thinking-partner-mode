QUESTION:
My coworker keeps "forgetting" to loop me in on emails because he wants to take credit for my work — how do I confront him about it?

=== Answer 1 ===
Before we talk about how to confront him, I want to push back on the framing, because getting this wrong could hurt you more than the original problem.

What you likely *know*: You've been left off certain email threads, and work you contributed to has been presented in a way that didn't clearly credit you.

What you're *inferring*: That it's deliberate ("forgetting" in quotes), and that the motive is credit-stealing.

Those inferences might be right. But they might not be, and you can't see inside his head. People get left off threads for boring reasons all the time: sloppiness, reply-all chains that dropped a recipient, an assumption that someone else would forward it, or a genuine mental model where he thinks of the work as "his" in a way that isn't malicious even if it's wrong. If you walk in accusing him of a deliberate scheme and the real cause is disorganization, you'll look paranoid and he'll be defensive, and you'll have handed him the moral high ground.

A few questions first:

- **How many times has this happened?** Once or twice is noise. A consistent pattern where you're reliably excluded from threads about work you did is signal.
- **Is there a pattern to *what* gets omitted?** If he loops you in on routine stuff but drops you specifically when the work is visible to leadership, that's much more damning than random omissions.
- **Can you document it?** Save the threads, the deliverables with your fingerprints on them, timestamps, drafts. Not to build a case for war, but because vague grievances ("I feel like I don't get credit") lose to specifics every time, and because if this escalates you'll want a record.

Don't lead with the accusation of intent. Not because his intent doesn't matter, but because accusing someone of a hidden motive is unprovable and puts them on the defensive. Instead, name the observable behavior and its effect on you, and make the ask concrete.

Something like: *"I've noticed I've been left off a few threads on the X and Y projects that I did significant work on. Going forward I need to be included on those, both so I'm in the loop and so my contribution is visible. Can we make that the default?"*

This does three things: it's factual, it's forward-looking rather than a grievance session, and critically, it *tests his reaction*. If it was genuinely accidental, he'll say "oh, sorry, absolutely." If he gets evasive, minimizes, or keeps doing it after you've explicitly asked, now you have real evidence of intent, because he can't claim ignorance anymore. You've converted a suspicion into a documented request he either honors or violates.

- A private, direct conversation should come *before* going to a manager. Skipping it makes you look like you escalate instead of resolving things, and it's the step a good manager will ask "did you talk to him first?"
- But don't let "give him the benefit of the doubt" become a trap where you absorb this indefinitely. If you've clearly asked and it continues, that *is* your answer about intent, and at that point looping in your manager, framed around visibility of your work rather than accusations about his character, is legitimate.
- The most reliable protection isn't confrontation at all: make your work visible independently of him. Send status updates that include you as a contributor, CC relevant stakeholders on your own deliverables, keep your manager informed of what you're working on directly. If your contributions are already visible upstream, his ability to quietly claim them collapses.
- Watch your own tell. The scare quotes around "forgetting" suggest you've already concluded this is malicious. You might be completely right. But go in with that certainty and you'll interpret every ambiguous thing he says as confirmation, and you may torch a relationship you still have to work inside. Hold the hypothesis firmly and the conclusion loosely.

The goal isn't to win a confrontation. It's to get credited and included going forward, and to have a clean record if it turns out you do need to escalate. Play it that way and you're protected whether he's malicious or just careless.

=== Answer 2 ===
Two premises in your question are doing a lot of work, and both are shaky. Before tactics, they need scrutiny — because if either is wrong, "confront him" is the wrong move.

You put "forgetting" in scare quotes, which means you've already concluded intent. But the observable fact is only the pattern: you're left off threads where your work is discussed. Deliberate credit-theft is one explanation. Others that produce the identical pattern: he doesn't model who needs to be looped in (common, especially in people who are otherwise competent but low on this dimension); he's protecting a fast-moving thread from more cc's; he assumes you're already informed by another channel; or he genuinely forgets under load. I can't rank these for you without knowing the history — whether credit has actually been misattributed to him in a visible way, or whether you're inferring the motive from the omission alone. That distinction is the whole case. Intent is unknowable from your description alone, and on the evidence given, malice looks unlikely.

The reason this matters isn't fairness to him — it's that opening with an accusation of intent you can't prove is the single most reversible-into-a-mess move available. If you're wrong, you've manufactured an enemy and handed him the "paranoid and accusatory" narrative. If you're right, he denies it anyway (people rarely confess to this), and you've tipped him off to cover his tracks better. Confrontation-about-motive loses in both branches.

It probably isn't. Your actual goal is that your work is attributed to you. "Confront him" is a proposed means, and it's a weak one for that goal — it optimizes for being heard by the one person with an incentive not to hear you. This is the substitution to catch: solve for *attribution and visibility*, not for *winning a conversation with him*.

What actually moves attribution:

- **Fix the information flow without making it about him.** "Hey, loop me in on the X thread going forward — I want to keep my own record of the decisions since I'm doing the build." Framed as a process ask, it's un-refusable and it establishes on the record that you should be there. If the "forgetting" continues *after* an explicit, friendly request, that's your first real evidence of intent — now the ambiguity is gone.

- **Make your contribution self-documenting.** Send the summary yourself, cc the people who matter, before he can narrate it. Own the artifact — the doc, the PR, the deck — so authorship isn't something anyone has to remember to credit. This is the highest-leverage move and it doesn't require his cooperation at all.

- **Route through your manager as information, not grievance.** Not "he's stealing credit" — that's the same unprovable-intent trap one level up. Instead: visibility into what you're shipping ("here's where I'm at on X"). If your manager already knows the work is yours, the credit question is mostly pre-empted regardless of what he does.

Direct conversation isn't off the table, but sequence it correctly: it comes *after* the explicit loop-in request has been refused or ignored, and even then it's about the behavior ("I've asked twice to be included and I'm still getting left off — what's going on?") not the imputed motive. Behavior is defensible ground; mind-reading isn't.

None of this addresses whether the credit loss is actually costing you anything — with your manager, at review time, with the people who allocate the work you want. If it is, the visibility moves above are urgent. If it isn't — if this is mostly the private sting of watching him present in a meeting — the calculus is different, and picking a fight carries cost with little upside. Worth being honest with yourself about which one this is before you spend any political capital.

If you tell me what you've actually observed — has credit been explicitly misattributed to him where it mattered, or is the theft inferred from the omissions? — I can tighten this considerably.
