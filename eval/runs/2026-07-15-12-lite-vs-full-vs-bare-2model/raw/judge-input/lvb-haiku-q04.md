# Question

Remote work makes employees less productive, so companies that ban it are just being responsible about performance.

---

# Answer 1

This rests on an empirical claim that doesn't hold up cleanly. The research is genuinely mixed: some studies show productivity losses in remote work, others show gains or neutral effects, and much depends on job type, company setup, and how you measure productivity. Meta-analyses haven't settled it.

Even if remote work *did* reduce productivity on average, the conclusion doesn't follow automatically. Companies would still need to weigh:

- Whether the productivity loss (if real) is offset by lower turnover, expanded hiring pools, or employee satisfaction
- Whether banning remote work is the most effective lever (versus hybrid policies, better tools, clearer expectations)
- Whether "responsibility about performance" means maximizing output per hour, or balancing output with employee retention and wellbeing

So there are two separate questions here:
1. *Empirical*: Does remote work reduce productivity? (Answer: depends on context; not settled.)
2. *Strategic*: If it does, is a ban the responsible choice? (Answer: depends on what you're optimizing for—that's a business and values call, not a fact.)

The statement treats the second as obvious once the first is granted. It isn't.

# Answer 2

The claim conflates two things: whether remote work affects productivity, and whether banning it is the responsible response. The evidence doesn't strongly support the first, and it complicates the second.

Research has been mixed rather than conclusive. Some studies show productivity gains with remote work (fewer interruptions, commute time reclaimed), others show challenges (coordination friction, onboarding difficulty, isolation effects). Most findings depend heavily on the work type—solo, focused tasks often do well remotely; complex collaboration or training can suffer. Meta-analyses from 2020-2024 trend toward "it depends" rather than "remote is worse."

Even if remote work did reduce productivity for some roles, banning it company-wide is blunt. A more nuanced approach:
- Assess which work actually requires co-location (complex brainstorming, hands-on training, relationship-building)
- Allow flexibility for roles where remote doesn't sacrifice performance
- Run pilots to gather real data on your specific workforce rather than assuming the claim

Productivity isn't the only variable—retention, hiring reach, employee wellbeing, and cost also matter. If banning remote work increases turnover or limits the talent pool, the performance math changes. Companies that banned remote work in 2023-2024 faced significant backlash and departures; whether that was "responsible" depends on whether the productivity gains (if any) offset the costs.

A responsible approach is to measure, not assume.
