I haven't conceded that point, and there's no inconsistency. Let me clarify the distinction.

Saying crypto may have upside potential is different from saying it's suitable for an emergency fund. These aren't contradictory—they address different questions entirely.

An emergency fund serves a specific purpose: it needs to be accessible *when you need it*, and it needs to retain its value to cover actual expenses. Crypto fails on both counts. It's volatile—if an emergency hits during a market downturn, your $10,000 fund might be $6,000. It's also illiquid during crises—exchanges go down, networks congest, or regulations tighten precisely when everyone needs access. Your emergency fund losing half its value or becoming inaccessible defeats the entire purpose.

Separately, crypto might be a reasonable *speculation* or long-term investment with higher risk and higher potential returns. That's a different decision for different money.

The error in your argument is treating "X has upside potential" as logically equivalent to "X is suitable for emergency reserves." Those are different claims. I can consistently believe that rental properties have strong returns while also thinking they're terrible for an emergency fund, because the fund's purpose requires different properties than an investment vehicle.

What I'm being consistent about is this: don't confuse an asset's return potential with its suitability for a specific financial goal.
