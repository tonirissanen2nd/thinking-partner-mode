Short answer: not yet — the number you have doesn't license this decision, and the decision as framed skips two links in the chain.

**The frame problem.** "Shift the whole team to weekends" presupposes that weekend churn is *caused by* weekend support gaps. Your data, as described, only establishes a time-of-week correlation. There are two unestablished links between "churn is 40% higher on weekends" and "more weekend support fixes it":

1. **Is the 40% real, or an artifact?** Before treating it as a fact, I'd want to see how it's constructed, because the most common versions of this metric are measurement artifacts:
   - *Attribution timing.* If churn is dated to when the cancellation is *logged*, weekends catch self-serve cancels that people defer to their free time — the event surfaces on Saturday but wasn't "caused" that day. Weekday cancels may also route through a retention step (a rep, a save offer) that weekend cancels skip, which mechanically inflates the weekend rate without any support-quality story.
   - *Denominator and base rate.* Weekends are 2/7 of the week (~29%). "40% higher rate per day" needs a clean per-day denominator to mean anything; it's easy to conflate "more cancels land on weekends" with "the daily hazard is higher."
   - *Sample size.* If monthly churn is in the dozens-to-low-hundreds, a 40% weekday/weekend gap can sit inside the noise band. I'd want the absolute counts and a significance check before acting.

2. **Even if the 40% is real and causal-looking, is support the mediator?** The load-bearing question is: *what fraction of weekend churners had a support interaction that was slow, unresolved, or unavailable?* If most weekend churn is self-serve — people reviewing subscriptions on their day off and clicking cancel without ever contacting you — then more weekend staff changes nothing, because support was never in the loop. This is the single piece of data that would flip the decision, and it's absent from what you've described.

**The XY problem.** Your goal is reducing churn; "reschedule the support team" is one hypothesized means. Lead with the goal. The clean test is a mediation cut: of customers who churned on a weekend, how many opened a ticket / hit chat / tried to reach you, and what happened? That query probably already exists in your data and settles more than the 40% does.

**On the action itself — asymmetry of downside.** Moving the *whole* team is the high-variance choice, and its downsides are not symmetric with its upside:
- Weekday volume is almost certainly where the bulk of your support load sits (especially if you have any B2B mix). Pulling coverage there to chase a weekend hypothesis can create a real weekday regression to fix a possibly-illusory weekend one.
- Schedule upheaval carries staff-morale and attrition cost that's slow and expensive to reverse.

Contrast with the bounded-downside version: **add or reallocate a small amount of weekend coverage as a pilot**, keep weekday coverage intact, and measure weekend churn against a control period. Same directional bet, capped cost, reversible. If weekend support is genuinely the lever, a pilot shows it; if it isn't, you've spent little. The expected-value-maximizing move and the low-regret move point the same way here — do the small version first.

1. Pull the mediation cut (weekend churners who contacted support and were underserved). If that number is small, stop — this isn't a staffing problem.
2. Sanity-check the 40%: absolute counts, per-day denominator, significance, and whether cancel-timing is logged vs. effective.
3. Only if 1 and 2 both hold, run a weekend-coverage *pilot* — not a full reschedule.

One thing I'm *not* doing is arguing you're wrong that weekends matter — the pattern may well be real and worth acting on. The disagreement is narrower: "40% higher weekend churn" is not yet evidence that "reschedule the whole team" is the fix, and the full reschedule is the most expensive, least reversible way to test the idea.
