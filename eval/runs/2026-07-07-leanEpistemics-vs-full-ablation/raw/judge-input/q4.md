QUESTION:
My contractor says we've never had an inspector check this kind of interior wall removal, so pulling a permit is just a waste of time and money — should I skip it?

=== Answer 1 ===
Don't skip it, and notice that your contractor's argument doesn't actually bear on the decision.

The frame is off. "An inspector has never checked this kind of work" is an empirical claim about enforcement frequency. Whether to pull a permit is a question about required process, liability, and structural risk. Those are different axes. Low enforcement probability is not evidence that a permit is worthless — it's evidence that you *could* get away with not having one. Not the same thing.

Break the actual stakes into three, because they don't move together:

**1. Structural risk — the load-bearing dimension.** The central question with interior wall removal is whether that wall carries load (roof, floor above, ceiling joists). If it does and it's removed without a properly sized beam and adequate bearing points, the failure modes are sag, cracking, or in the bad case partial collapse — an injury/fatality risk, and irreversible. This is the reason to care, and it's independent of whether an inspector ever shows up. The permit/inspection process exists precisely to force a second set of eyes on that beam calc before the wall is gone. If your contractor's read on "load-bearing or not" is wrong, the permit is the mechanism that catches it; skipping it removes the one external check. (Structural risk is the dominant concern here — this is what wall-removal permits are fundamentally about. Whether *your specific* wall is load-bearing needs eyes on the framing; "the contractor says it's fine" is not the same as an engineered determination.)

**2. Your contractor has a conflict of interest on this exact question.** Skipping the permit saves *him* time, fees, and — more to the point — scrutiny of his own work. That doesn't make him wrong, but it means his advice to skip isn't disinterested, and it's the party with the conflict who's recommending the option that reduces oversight of himself. Weight it accordingly. (This is the standard incentive; I can't see his specific motives.)

**3. The downside asymmetry — this is the deciding factor.** Compare the two error cases:
- Pull the permit and it "wasn't needed": you're out permit fees and some scheduling delay. Bounded, small, recoverable.
- Skip it and it mattered: unpermitted structural work commonly voids the relevant portion of homeowner's insurance if it causes damage; it must be disclosed at resale in most U.S. jurisdictions and routinely blows up sales or forces retroactive permitting (open the wall back up, inspect, sometimes redo to code); and if a load-bearing error was missed, you own the structural failure with no engineered record to fall back on.

The expected *cost* of the permit is a few hundred dollars and a delay. The tail of skipping it is uncapped. When one path has bounded downside and the other has a fat, irreversible tail, you take the bounded path even if the bad outcome is unlikely — the low probability the contractor is implicitly citing doesn't rescue you, because the loss isn't proportional to the probability.

- Permit *requirements* are jurisdiction-specific. I can't tell you from here whether your locality requires a permit for this specific work — some exempt purely non-structural, non-electrical partition removal. Verify against your local building department (one phone call or their website), not against your contractor. If it's genuinely a non-load-bearing partition with no electrical/plumbing/HVAC in it, the requirement may legitimately not apply — but confirm that with the AHJ, not with the person who'd rather not file.
- The insurance-void and resale-disclosure points are the general pattern in U.S. residential practice; your policy language and state's disclosure law are the primary sources. Verify these against your actual policy and state law rather than treating my summary as ground truth.

**One material thing your question didn't raise:** if there's any electrical, plumbing, or HVAC in or behind that wall, that pulls in additional permit/inspection requirements independent of the structural question, and those are the ones that most often surface at resale.

The permit's value is a cheap external check against an irreversible structural mistake plus insurance and resale protection — none of which depends on how often an inspector historically shows up. Confirm whether it's actually required with your building department, and if it is (or if the wall is or might be load-bearing), pull it. The "waste of time and money" framing only looks right if you ignore the tail.

=== Answer 2 ===
No — skip the permit only if you're willing to absorb the downside yourself, and here that downside includes an irreversible one.

The core problem is that your contractor's argument is a non-sequitur. "We've never had an inspector check this" is an empirical claim about enforcement frequency. "So a permit is a waste" is a normative claim about whether you should comply. The first doesn't support the second. Rarely-enforced and not-required are different things, and the gap between them is exactly where you're exposed. Note also that the contractor is not a neutral party: a permit puts a third party in a position to check his work and slow his schedule. That doesn't make him wrong, but it means his conclusion needs independent support it isn't getting.

For a genuinely non-structural partition — no load path, no electrical, no plumbing, no HVAC in the wall — some jurisdictions don't require a permit at all, and in that case he'd be right that pulling one is pure friction. So the real question isn't "permit vs. no permit" as a matter of principle; it's **which category this wall is in**, and that is a factual question neither of you has resolved in what you've told me.

That factual question is the whole ballgame because of the asymmetry:

- **If the wall is load-bearing** (or hides electrical/plumbing/HVAC), removing it wrong risks structural failure, fire, or water damage — a low-probability but high-severity, hard-to-reverse outcome. This is the dimension the "waste of time and money" framing ignores entirely. You cannot un-remove a wall that turns out to have been holding up the floor above.
- **If it's a true non-load-bearing partition**, the permit costs you a few hundred dollars and some delay, and buys you the items below anyway.

The expected-value-optimal move and the bounded-downside move diverge here. Skipping the permit is probably cheaper in the average case. Pulling it is the bounded-downside move — it caps your worst case — and when the worst case is structural or life-safety, you want the bounded-downside option, not the EV-optimal one.

Beyond safety, a permit is load-bearing for three things people forget until they matter:

1. **Insurance.** Unpermitted structural work that later causes damage is a common basis for claim denial. (This varies by policy and jurisdiction; check your specific homeowner's policy language rather than take this as settled.)
2. **Resale.** Most jurisdictions require you to disclose unpermitted work, and buyers'/appraisers' agents flag it; it can kill or discount a sale, or force you to permit it retroactively at higher cost.
3. **Liability if you're not the last owner or occupant** — you inherit the risk that a future problem traces to this work.

Don't relitigate "permit or not" with the contractor. Ask him one concrete question — **is this wall load-bearing, and does it contain any electrical, plumbing, or HVAC?** If he can't answer definitively, that itself is a reason to get a structural engineer or your building department to weigh in before anything is removed. If it is load-bearing, a permit isn't optional friction; it's the mechanism that gets the beam/header sizing checked by someone whose job isn't finishing your job fast.

Two things I can't resolve from here and that change the answer: your **jurisdiction's actual permit rules** (some genuinely exempt non-structural partition removal) and whether the wall is structural. Both are verifiable — a call to your local building department settles the first in one conversation, and it's worth making before you rely on the contractor's read.
