Before we talk about how to confront him, I want to push back on the framing, because getting this wrong could hurt you more than the original problem.

**Start by separating what you know from what you're inferring.**

What you likely *know*: You've been left off certain email threads, and work you contributed to has been presented in a way that didn't clearly credit you.

What you're *inferring*: That it's deliberate ("forgetting" in quotes), and that the motive is credit-stealing.

Those inferences might be right. But they might not be, and you can't see inside his head. People get left off threads for boring reasons all the time: sloppiness, reply-all chains that dropped a recipient, an assumption that someone else would forward it, or a genuine mental model where he thinks of the work as "his" in a way that isn't malicious even if it's wrong. If you walk in accusing him of a deliberate scheme and the real cause is disorganization, you'll look paranoid and he'll be defensive, and you'll have handed him the moral high ground.

So a few honest questions first:

- **How many times has this happened?** Once or twice is noise. A consistent pattern where you're reliably excluded from threads about work you did is signal.
- **Is there a pattern to *what* gets omitted?** If he loops you in on routine stuff but drops you specifically when the work is visible to leadership, that's much more damning than random omissions.
- **Can you document it?** Save the threads, the deliverables with your fingerprints on them, timestamps, drafts. Not to build a case for war, but because vague grievances ("I feel like I don't get credit") lose to specifics every time, and because if this escalates you'll want a record.

**On the confrontation itself:**

Don't lead with the accusation of intent. Not because his intent doesn't matter, but because accusing someone of a hidden motive is unprovable and puts them on the defensive. Instead, name the observable behavior and its effect on you, and make the ask concrete.

Something like: *"I've noticed I've been left off a few threads on the X and Y projects that I did significant work on. Going forward I need to be included on those, both so I'm in the loop and so my contribution is visible. Can we make that the default?"*

This does three things: it's factual, it's forward-looking rather than a grievance session, and critically, it *tests his reaction*. If it was genuinely accidental, he'll say "oh, sorry, absolutely." If he gets evasive, minimizes, or keeps doing it after you've explicitly asked, now you have real evidence of intent, because he can't claim ignorance anymore. You've converted a suspicion into a documented request he either honors or violates.

**Where I'll be blunt:**

- A private, direct conversation should come *before* going to a manager. Skipping it makes you look like you escalate instead of resolving things, and it's the step a good manager will ask "did you talk to him first?"
- But don't let "give him the benefit of the doubt" become a trap where you absorb this indefinitely. If you've clearly asked and it continues, that *is* your answer about intent, and at that point looping in your manager, framed around visibility of your work rather than accusations about his character, is legitimate.
- The most reliable protection isn't confrontation at all: make your work visible independently of him. Send status updates that include you as a contributor, CC relevant stakeholders on your own deliverables, keep your manager informed of what you're working on directly. If your contributions are already visible upstream, his ability to quietly claim them collapses.
- Watch your own tell. The scare quotes around "forgetting" suggest you've already concluded this is malicious. You might be completely right. But go in with that certainty and you'll interpret every ambiguous thing he says as confirmation, and you may torch a relationship you still have to work inside. Hold the hypothesis firmly and the conclusion loosely.

The goal isn't to win a confrontation. It's to get credited and included going forward, and to have a clean record if it turns out you do need to escalate. Play it that way and you're protected whether he's malicious or just careless.
