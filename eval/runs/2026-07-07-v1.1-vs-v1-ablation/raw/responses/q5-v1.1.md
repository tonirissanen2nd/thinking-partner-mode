For most small nonprofits, the marginal dollar goes to retention — but that answer is contingent on where you currently sit, and the "acquisition vs. retention" binary hides the option that usually beats both.

**The core logic (why retention wins at the margin).** This is a marginal-ROI question, and the two activities have structurally different economics:

- **Acquisition typically runs at or below break-even on the first gift.** Cost-per-dollar-raised on cold acquisition is often around or above $1.00 — you frequently *lose* money on the first gift and only profit if the donor stays. So acquisition's return is really a bet on future retention.
- **Retention is cheap per dollar raised.** Renewing an existing donor costs a fraction of acquiring a new one. The specific "5–7x cheaper" multiplier you'll see quoted is a for-profit-marketing heuristic imported into the sector and I'd treat it as *low confidence* on the exact number — but the *direction* (retention markedly cheaper per dollar) is well-supported (moderate-to-high confidence).
- **Retention compounds through lifetime value.** A retained donor gives across multiple years, upgrades, and refers. Small increases in retention rate move LTV nonlinearly, so the marginal dollar there has a multiplier that acquisition lacks.
- **The leaky-bucket effect.** If retention is poor, acquisition spend drains out the bottom — you're buying donors who lapse before they repay acquisition cost. Poor retention *lowers the ROI of acquisition itself*, which is what makes retention the higher-leverage marginal dollar for the typical small org.

The sector backdrop reinforces this: overall donor retention is chronically low (roughly mid-40s percent), and first-year new-donor retention is worse still (commonly cited around 20–25%), versus repeat-donor retention north of 60%. I'm flagging these figures for verification against the primary source (the Fundraising Effectiveness Project / AFP annual report) — I recall the magnitudes with moderate confidence but the exact percentages drift year to year and shouldn't be quoted as precise without checking.

**A decision rule you can actually apply.** Don't decide this in the abstract — compute two ratios:

1. Marginal cost per additional dollar *retained* (cost of a renewal/stewardship push ÷ incremental dollars kept).
2. Marginal cost per additional dollar *acquired*, then discounted by your realistic first-year retention rate (because that's what determines whether the acquired donor repays their cost).

Put the marginal dollar where the ratio is lower. For a small org with a leaky bucket — which is most of them — (1) almost always wins.

**When acquisition wins instead (the real exceptions).**
- **Base too small to matter.** A brand-new org, or one with a donor base so small that even perfect retention can't fund the mission, has nothing meaningful to retain. Growth *requires* acquisition first.
- **Already-strong retention.** If you're already retaining well (say, repeat retention comfortably above sector norms), the marginal return to further retention effort flattens, and acquisition's relative return rises.
- **Concentration risk.** A small, well-retained base is a fragility, not just an asset — a few major donors' attrition (death, life change, a bad year) can be existential. Acquisition here is diversification, and worth paying an ROI premium for.
- **An unusually cheap acquisition channel** (earned media, a viral moment, a matching-gift window) can temporarily invert the economics.

**Ruin/floor consideration.** A retention-only posture has a hard downside: natural attrition — donors die, move, and drop off for reasons no stewardship can prevent. Acquisition below the natural replacement rate guarantees slow decline to zero. So a floor of acquisition spend is not an ROI choice; it's solvency insurance. Frame it as: retention gets the *marginal* dollar, but acquisition needs a *minimum* dollar regardless of ROI, sized to at least offset attrition. Those are different budget logics and shouldn't be traded off against each other one-for-one.

**The omission worth naming — it's not actually a binary.** The highest-ROI marginal dollar for many small nonprofits is neither pure acquisition nor pure renewal, but:
- **Second-gift conversion** — moving a first-time donor to their *second* gift is the single biggest retention cliff (that first-year 20–25% number is where the bucket leaks hardest), and it's cheaper than general acquisition.
- **Upgrading and reactivating existing/lapsed donors** — often the cheapest incremental revenue available.
- **Mid-level and major-donor cultivation**, where per-dollar return can dwarf both mass acquisition and mass retention.

If I had to compress this to one line: **default the marginal dollar to retention — specifically to second-gift conversion — while holding a non-negotiable acquisition floor sized to beat attrition.** To tighten this for your situation, the two things that would change the answer are your current first-year retention rate and the absolute size of your donor base; if you tell me those, I can say which side you're actually on.
