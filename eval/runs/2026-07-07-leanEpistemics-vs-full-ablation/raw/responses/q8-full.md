Two different questions are bundled here, and they have different answers: (1) *is the effect real?* and (2) *should I roll out?* You can be reasonably yes on (2) while still being wrong about (1) — and separately, "the effect is real" and "the effect is 15%" are not the same claim.

**On "is it real": p<0.05 is weak evidence, not confidence.** A single test at p<0.05 has, loosely, a one-in-twenty false-positive rate *under the null* — and the practically relevant number (the chance you're wrong given a significant result) is usually worse than that once realistic prior odds and power are factored in. This is Moderate-at-best evidence that *some* positive effect exists, and that's before the specific threats below. So: no, you cannot be "confident this is real" on this basis alone.

**The 15% is probably inflated — this is the part most people miss.** A 15% *relative* lift from a checkout-button change is very large; button-level UI tweaks more typically move conversion low-single-digit percent relative. When a barely-significant test throws off a suspiciously large point estimate, the likely explanation is the winner's curse (Type-M / magnitude error): with limited power, the *only* way an effect clears significance is if noise happened to push the estimate up, so significant estimates are biased upward. **Ask for the confidence interval, not the point estimate.** If the 95% CI runs from, say, +2% to +28%, the honest read is "probably positive, magnitude highly uncertain," not "15%." (Moderate confidence the true effect is smaller than 15% — grounded in the base rate for button changes plus the winner's-curse mechanism; would flip if the CI is tight, e.g. +11% to +19%.)

**Named threats to (1), roughly in order of how often they bite:**
- **Optional stopping / peeking.** Did you monitor the test and stop when it hit significance? If so, the real false-positive rate is materially higher than 5% — sequential peeking inflates it substantially. If the two-week horizon and the stop were pre-registered, this concern drops out. This is the single most common way "p<0.05" lies.
- **Novelty effect.** Two weeks is short. Returning users react to *change* itself; that bump decays. New designs often show a first-week lift that fades. Two weeks can't distinguish a durable effect from a novelty spike.
- **Weekly-cycle / calendar confound.** Two weeks is only two weekend cycles and can overlap a payday, promo, or seasonal window. Thin coverage of the weekly pattern.
- **Multiple comparisons.** If this was one of several variants or several metrics tested, the effective p-value is worse than the reported one.
- **Guardrail metrics.** A conversion lift that's really discount-driven or that pulls forward purchases can raise conversion while flat or lowering revenue, AOV, or refund rate. Confirm the effect survives on downstream revenue, not just the click-through conversion event.

**On "should I roll out": here the answer tilts yes — for a reason independent of the statistics.** A checkout-button rollout is cheap and *reversible*. The downside is bounded: if the effect is illusory you've lost little and can revert; the failure mode is not ruin. That asymmetry means you do **not** need High confidence in the effect to ship it. Contrast this with a decision that were expensive or hard to unwind (re-architecting checkout around this finding, renegotiating pricing) — there the weak evidence would not clear the bar.

So the move that respects both questions: **roll it out, but keep a holdback** (ship to ~90–95%, hold back 5–10%) and measure the long-run effect over 4–8 weeks on *revenue*, not two-week conversion. That converts an irreversible "we believe 15%" into a reversible, continuously-measured bet, and it's the only way you'll learn the *real* effect size for planning.

What I'd need to firm any of this up: the confidence interval, whether the stop was pre-registered, the number of variants/metrics tested, and whether "15%" is relative or absolute (absolute would be enormous and I'd suspect an instrumentation error first).

**Material omission I'm flagging rather than resolving:** I've assumed randomization was clean (user-level, no bucket leakage across devices/logged-out sessions, no sample-ratio mismatch). A broken assignment mechanism can manufacture a "significant" result that no amount of statistical care will fix — worth a five-minute check that the two arms have the expected traffic split.
