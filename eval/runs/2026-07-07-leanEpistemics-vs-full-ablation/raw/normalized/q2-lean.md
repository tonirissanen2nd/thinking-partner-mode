The word doing the work in your question is "suddenly." Two very different scenarios hide inside it, and they have opposite odds — so separate them before attaching a number.

Scorable forecast (the "suddenly" version). Probability that, by mid-2031, the USD undergoes an *abrupt* loss of primary reserve status — I'll define that as its share of allocated global FX reserves falling below ~25% within any 12-month window, or a disorderly event (US default/hyperinflation) that visibly ends its role as the default settlement and reserve asset: **~1-2%.** This is a thin-tailed-reference-class problem wearing fat-tailed clothing (more below).

Reference class first (outside view). Reserve-currency transitions are rare and slow. Sterling ceded to the dollar over roughly 1914-1956 — two world wars, a bankrupting of the incumbent, and it still took ~40 years. There is no clean historical case of an incumbent global reserve currency being displaced *abruptly* absent the issuing state's military/fiscal collapse. The base rate for "sudden" over a 5-year window is near zero; I'm pricing in a small premium above zero for today's specific stressors, not departing from the base rate without a reason.

Decomposition (inside view). A sudden loss requires roughly the conjunction of:

1. *A scalable alternative that can absorb trillions in reserves.* Currently absent. The euro is structurally fragmented (no joint safe asset at scale); the RMB is non-convertible under capital controls and China runs a closed capital account precisely to retain policy control; gold and crypto lack the depth and institutional plumbing to hold official reserves at scale. No ready substitute exists on a 5-year horizon.
2. *A triggering shock* — a US sovereign default, uncontrolled inflation, or sanctions overreach severe enough to make holders flee. Individually low-probability over 5 years, though not negligible (debt-ceiling brinkmanship and sanctions-driven diversification are real).
3. *Fast, coordinated exit* by holders who each face a collective-action problem: dumping dollars craters the value of your own remaining stockpile. This incentive is what makes reserve status "sticky" and is why erosion, when it comes, is gradual.

Multiply three low-to-moderate, roughly-independent factors and the conjunction lands in the low-single-digit-percent range — consistent with the outside view.

The frame you should probably be using instead. "Suddenly loses reserve status" is close to a black-swan framing, and forcing a crisp point estimate on the genuine tail can mislead. The live, *scorable* question is gradual erosion: the dollar's share of allocated reserves has already drifted from ~72% (2000) to the high-50s%/~58% today — I'm confident on the direction and rough magnitude; verify against the latest IMF COFER release before citing it. Continued slow erosion of *share* over the next five years is **Moderate-to-High probability**; loss of the *dominant* position (dropping below the largest single share) is Low. That is a different, more likely, and more decision-relevant claim than "sudden collapse."

Exposure over point estimate. Because the sudden-collapse tail is small but not zero and hard to price, the useful move isn't refining the 1-2% — it's asking what exposure makes the number irrelevant to you. If you're a saver or investor, the hedge against even a low-probability dollar shock (currency diversification, real assets, some gold) is cheap relative to the tail it covers, and you'd hold it regardless of whether the true probability is 1% or 4%. That asymmetry — bounded, cheap protection against an unbounded but unlikely downside — matters more than the exact odds.

What I'm leaving out: the mechanism by which reserve status and the dollar's role in trade invoicing / offshore dollar funding reinforce each other (network effects that further raise the switching cost); and the possibility that "reserve status" fractures into a multipolar arrangement rather than one currency replacing another — a real medium-term scenario that my binary resolution criterion doesn't capture. If your actual question is about that multipolar drift rather than sudden collapse, say so and I'll re-scope the forecast.
