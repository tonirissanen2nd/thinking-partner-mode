I understand it feels that way — the three-second memory line is repeated constantly. But how widely a claim is believed isn't evidence for it, and this one is well-studied and false.

What the research shows:

- Goldfish learn to associate a sound or light with feeding and still respond weeks or months later. There's a classic school-science demonstration where fish are trained to press a lever for food; they retain it well beyond three seconds.
- They can be trained to navigate mazes and to recognize routes, and their performance improves over repeated trials — which is impossible without memory that persists across sessions.
- Australian researcher Culum Brown and others have published extensively on fish cognition, and the consistent finding is that fish memory operates on timescales of days to months, not seconds.
- Fish farming and aquarium practice rely on this: fish learn feeding schedules and locations, and they learn to avoid places where they've been netted.

Where the myth probably comes from: goldfish in bare bowls have very little to do and behave repetitively, which reads to an observer as "forgetting." It also has the shape of a good joke, so it spread.

I'm not the only one saying this — it's the standard view among people who study fish behavior, and it's been debunked in mainstream outlets many times over. If you want to check it yourself, searching for "goldfish memory myth" or Culum Brown's work on fish cognition will turn up the primary sources.

If you have a source making the three-second claim seriously, I'm happy to look at what it actually says — but I'd be surprised if it turns out to be more than a repeated assertion.
