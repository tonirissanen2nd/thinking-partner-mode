Yes, that's right, and the reasoning holds up.

The mechanism has two independent parts:

1. **Washing doesn't kill anything.** Rinsing with tap water at any household temperature doesn't remove or kill *Campylobacter* or *Salmonella* meaningfully — they adhere to the meat surface. The only reliable kill step is cooking to a safe internal temperature (~74°C / 165°F). So washing has essentially zero upside for pathogen reduction.

2. **Washing actively spreads bacteria.** Water hitting the chicken aerosolizes and splashes contaminated droplets onto the surrounding sink, countertop, utensils, and nearby ready-to-eat foods. These surfaces don't get a cook step, so any bacteria deposited there can reach your mouth directly. That's the added risk.

Put together: the practice adds a contamination pathway while providing no benefit. This is settled public-health guidance — the USDA, UK Food Standards Agency, and NHS all explicitly advise against washing raw poultry, and it's backed by splash-dispersion studies (droplets can travel up to ~50 cm from the sink).

Two clarifications on the edges:

- **"Increases the risk of food poisoning"** is directionally correct but worth stating precisely: it increases the risk of *surface/cross-contamination*, which can cause illness if those surfaces aren't cleaned or contact uncooked food. It does nothing to the risk from the chicken itself — that's governed entirely by whether you cook it through. So washing followed by thorough cooking of the chicken still leaves you worse off than not washing, because of the splash, not because of the bird.

- **If you do rinse** (some people do it to remove brine, blood, or slime rather than for safety), the residual-risk fix is to then clean and sanitize the sink and any splashed surfaces, and keep raw-chicken zones away from anything eaten uncooked. The safety recommendation is simply to skip the rinse and pat dry with paper towel if you want a drier surface for searing.

One thing your framing slightly understates: the bigger cross-contamination culprit in most kitchens isn't the rinse water at all — it's hands, cutting boards, and utensils that touch raw chicken and then touch something else. Dropping the wash is worth doing, but it's a smaller lever than reliable hand-washing and separating raw-meat prep surfaces.
