# Question

My coworker keeps "forgetting" to loop me in on emails because he wants to take credit for my work — how do I confront him about it?

---

# Answer 1

The pattern is real — being left off emails is a real problem. But you're inferring the motive, and that inference changes what to do.

If it's deliberate credit-stealing: a direct conversation risks sounding accusatory without proof, and he'll just deny it. Better to shift the information flow itself. Start cc'ing yourself on relevant work. Flag your contributions in writing when the moment happens ("I've finished the analysis part" via Slack or email). Loop *others* in on your wins, not just him. This removes the advantage of being the sole person with the information.

If it's carelessness: "Hey, I've noticed I'm not on emails about X project. Can you add me to future updates?" is a straightforward ask. Assume this interpretation first — it's more common than malice, and it costs nothing to start here.

The mixed case — he *means* to forget — is trickier. Saying "I feel like you're deliberately leaving me out" will get defensive reactions. But "I keep missing things because I'm not looped in" is a factual problem you can ask him to solve.

One more angle: is this actually about him wanting credit, or is it that his credit is higher because he's more visible? Fixing the information flow might matter more than confronting the motive. Once you're part of the conversation trail, the credit issue solves itself.

Start with the direct ask for email inclusion. If that doesn't work, escalate to your manager with a simple fact: "I'm not receiving emails about X, so I can't contribute fully." That's documentation, not accusation.

---

# Answer 2

Here are some strategies for addressing this situation professionally:

- Document the pattern: Keep records of your contributions, email threads, and dates when you weren't included
- Consider intent: Is this deliberate exclusion or poor communication habits? This affects your approach
- Check if others notice the pattern too—it may be obvious to management already

- Choose a private, calm moment
- Use "I" statements: "I've noticed I'm not being included on emails about projects I'm working on"
- Avoid accusations: Instead of "you're taking credit," say "I need to be looped in so my contributions are visible"
- Be specific: Reference concrete examples ("Like the Tuesday meeting email and the client update yesterday")
- Ask clarifying questions: "Is there a reason you've been leaving me off these threads?"

- CC yourself proactively on relevant emails when possible
- Send follow-ups summarizing your contributions: "Just to confirm, here's what I completed on this task..."
- Loop in your manager or HR if the pattern continues—frame it as a communication/documentation issue
- Keep your own records to protect yourself during reviews or if credit disputes arise

- Establish clear communication norms with this coworker about project coordination
- Use shared project management tools or channels where contributions are automatically visible
- Build relationships with stakeholders so your work is known independently

The goal is to address the behavior without escalating unnecessarily, but don't let it continue if the direct approach fails. Your contributions deserve recognition.
