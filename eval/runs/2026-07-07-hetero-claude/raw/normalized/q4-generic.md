The claim rests on a premise that doesn't hold up, so the conclusion doesn't follow.

**"Remote work makes employees less productive" is not an established fact.** The research is genuinely mixed, and the direction of the effect depends heavily on how you measure it and what kind of work you're talking about.

- A well-known randomized controlled trial by Nicholas Bloom (Stanford) with Ctrip found remote call-center workers were *more* productive (about 13%), though half of that came from working more minutes per shift and fewer breaks, not faster work per minute.
- His later 2022–2024 research on *hybrid* work found no productivity loss and meaningfully lower attrition.
- On the other hand, some studies of *fully* remote work (e.g., a 2023 study of software engineers, and research on newly-remote workers during COVID) found productivity dips, more time in meetings, or slower skill development and mentorship for junior staff.
- Self-reported productivity, manager-perceived productivity, and objective output metrics frequently disagree with each other, which is a red flag that "productivity" is being measured inconsistently across studies.

**It depends on the role, the tasks, the individual, the management quality, and whether it's fully remote or hybrid.** Anyone stating flatly that remote work reduces productivity is overselling a contested body of evidence.

**Even if we granted the premise, the conclusion still wouldn't follow.** The argument assumes:

1. *That the productivity effect is uniform.* If remote work helps some roles and hurts others, a blanket ban is a blunt instrument that throws away the gains along with the losses.
2. *That productivity is the only relevant variable.* It isn't. Bans affect retention, hiring pool (you lose candidates who won't or can't commute or relocate), real-estate costs, employee wellbeing, absenteeism, and morale. A company optimizing purely on a contested productivity signal while ignoring, say, a spike in attrition of experienced staff is not obviously "being responsible about performance" — it may be trading a small, uncertain productivity gain for large, concrete costs.
3. *That "responsible" equals "maximizing measured productivity."* That's a value judgment smuggled in as if it were neutral. Responsibility to whom? Shareholders, employees, and customers can have divergent interests here.

There's also a worth-naming complication: some return-to-office mandates appear to be motivated less by productivity data than by justifying real-estate commitments, increasing manager visibility and control, or using attrition as a soft form of headcount reduction. When a company frames such a decision as "responsible about performance," that framing deserves scrutiny rather than acceptance at face value.

**Where the argument has a legitimate kernel:** it is reasonable for a company to care about productivity and to act on evidence. If a specific firm has *its own reliable internal data* showing that its particular teams do worse remotely, restricting remote work for those teams could be a defensible, evidence-based call. That's a narrow, empirical, context-specific claim — very different from the broad, universal one in the original statement.

The sentence as written is a syllogism with a shaky first premise and a leap in the second step. "Remote work reduces productivity, therefore banning it is responsible" doesn't survive contact with the actual evidence or with the other factors a responsible company has to weigh.
