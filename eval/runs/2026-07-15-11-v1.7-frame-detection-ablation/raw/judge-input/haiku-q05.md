# Question

Is it better for a small nonprofit to spend its marginal dollar on donor acquisition or on donor retention?

---

# Answer 1

For most small nonprofits, donor retention is the better marginal investment, but the answer depends on your current retention rate and donor base maturity.

The core tradeoff is cost-efficiency. Acquiring a new donor typically costs 5–7× more than retaining an existing one—this is well-documented across nonprofit research. Retention also has compounding value: donors who stay tend to increase their gifts over time, while each acquisition dollar buys you a single new relationship at its baseline gift size. For a small nonprofit with limited operating budget, this math generally favors retention.

However, this conclusion flips if your retention rate is unusually high (above 70%) or if your donor base is so small that growth through acquisition is existential. If you're retaining most donors already, acquisition becomes relatively more attractive. If your donor base is severely underdeveloped, you may need scale before retention spending yields returns.

- Whether acquisition and retention costs scale linearly or have step-function economies (most nonprofits see efficiency gains at scale, which affects the marginal decision).

If your donor base is very young or small, acquiring new donors may be a higher-leverage use of marginal dollars—each acquisition builds the base on which retention later operates. Calculate or estimate your donor retention rate and average gift lifecycle value. If retention exceeds 65% and your donor base is above 200, retention spending is almost certainly better. If retention is below 45% and your base is under 100, the case for acquisition is stronger—though even then, a small retention investment often pays. If you're between these points, the decision is close and may hinge on whether you have existing retention infrastructure to build on.

# Answer 2

For most small nonprofits, retention wins for the marginal dollar. But the strength of this conclusion depends heavily on your current retention rate and donor composition.

Retention spending typically yields better ROI because:

- Acquiring a new donor averages $1.25–$1.50 per dollar raised (Charity Navigator, Blackbaud data), while retention costs run $0.20–$0.40 per dollar retained. A 5-year donor retention program at $0.30/dollar outperforms acquisition at $1.35/dollar by a factor of ~4.5x over the lifetime value calculation.

- Retained donors give repeatedly. A donor you keep for 5 years might contribute $5,000 total; a new donor acquired for the same cost may give $1,200 and then churn. The lifetime value calculation *always* favors existing donors unless they are functionally at zero retention risk.

- You know who your existing donors are and what they've given. Acquisition is noisier — cost per acquired donor varies wildly by channel, and conversion rates are unpredictable. Retention spending has tighter feedback loops.

This rests on nonprofit sector data from multiple sources (Giving USA, CCS Fundraising, the 2024 AFP fundraising benchmarks). The cost structure is well-documented. The limitation is that most studies pool nonprofits of different sizes and mission types; true cost ratios for *your* nonprofit might differ by 20–30%.

- You already retain at 80%+ annually. If your retention rate is this high, marginal retention spending hits diminishing returns. The question flips toward acquisition or growth.

- Your donor base is dangerously narrow. If 50% of revenue comes from three donors, acquisition to diversify may be strategically essential, even if it's economically suboptimal in the short term. This is a ruin-risk question, not a pure ROI one.

- You've deprioritized retention badly. If your nonprofit hasn't invested in thank-you calls, renewal communications, or donor segmentation, *some* initial retention spending is cheap and high-yield. But once retention basics are in place, the math reverts to retention-favoring.

- You're in a growth phase where scale matters more than margin. Some nonprofits deliberately overspend on acquisition early because reaching a certain revenue threshold unlocks grants, partnerships, or board attention. This is strategic, not optimal on paper.

- Evidence that your retention cost is $0.80+/dollar retained: The math flips. Verify this against your own data before accepting sector averages.
- A proven, replicable acquisition channel at <$1.00/dollar cost: Some nonprofits find peer-to-peer fundraising or corporate giving programs with unusually low CAC. If you have one, it might justify reallocation.
- Your current retention rate is already 85%+ annually: The margin to gain is smaller; growth may matter more.

Allocate the marginal dollar to retention first, conditional on: (1) your retention rate is below 75% annually (room to move), and (2) you've already implemented basic retention mechanics (timely acknowledgment, annual reporting to donors, segmented communication). If both conditions hold, a dollar spent improving retention yields 2–3x the return of a dollar spent on acquisition.

Once retention is optimized, shift marginal dollars to acquisition.
