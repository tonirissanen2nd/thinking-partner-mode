Yes, that's substantially correct. High confidence, resting on the convergent position of major food-safety authorities (US FDA/USDA, UK Food Standards Agency, NHS) and the underlying microbiology.

The mechanism, unpacked:

1. **Washing doesn't sterilize.** Rinsing with tap water at any household temperature does not kill *Campylobacter*, *Salmonella*, or other pathogens on raw poultry. Their removal by water is partial and unreliable — you cannot rinse a surface clean of bacteria embedded in moisture and tissue.

2. **It actively redistributes contamination.** Water hitting the carcass aerosolizes and splashes droplets carrying bacteria onto surrounding surfaces — countertops, utensils, dish racks, other food, hands, clothing. UK FSA work has cited splash reaching on the order of tens of centimeters around the sink. So washing converts a contained hazard (bacteria on the chicken, which you'll cook) into a dispersed one (bacteria on things you won't cook).

3. **Cooking is what actually makes it safe.** Bringing poultry to a sufficient internal temperature (commonly cited target: 74°C / 165°F) reliably destroys these pathogens. That step, not washing, is the control point. Washing therefore adds risk without adding any safety benefit.

The net conclusion — don't wash raw chicken — is the mainstream recommendation, and I'd state it plainly.

Two boundary conditions worth naming, since the blanket "washing increases risk" framing has edges:

- **Magnitude vs. direction.** The *direction* (washing is net-negative or at best neutral) is well established. The *size* of the real-world illness risk from splash is harder to pin down — Moderate confidence — because it depends on your kitchen layout, whether contaminated surfaces later touch ready-to-eat food, and your cleaning habits. The advice holds regardless, since the downside is asymmetric: no upside to washing, only potential downside.

- **Patting dry / trimming.** If a recipe needs a dry surface (for browning), patting with paper towel that goes straight in the bin is fine — that's not rinsing under a running tap and doesn't generate the same splash. Similarly, if you must handle the raw meat, the real safeguards are: dedicated cutting board, thorough handwashing after, and cleaning any surface raw juices touched.

One thing I'd flag as commonly muddled: "salt water soak" or "vinegar rinse" traditions (common in many cuisines) are sometimes defended as antimicrobial. They modestly reduce surface load at best and do not substitute for cooking; they carry the same splash concern if done under running water in the sink. Not a reason to abandon a culturally meaningful prep step, but don't treat it as a safety measure.
