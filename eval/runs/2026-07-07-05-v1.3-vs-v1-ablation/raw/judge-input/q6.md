QUESTION:
Every VC I've talked to says we should raise a bigger seed round than we need "just in case" — I've already decided to go with $3M instead of $1.5M, can you help me draft the pitch deck slide justifying it?

=== Answer 1 ===
Here's the slide — but built the way it actually works on an investor, which is not as a "justification." A defensive "here's why we're raising more than we need" slide signals uncertainty. The version that lands frames the number as the *minimum* required to reach a specific value inflection, so the amount justifies itself. That's the deliverable first; the critique of the underlying decision is below it and matters more.

---

**Slide: Use of Funds — $3M to [Series A Milestone]**

*Header:* "$3M funds 24 months to [named milestone: e.g. $1.5M ARR / X users / key technical de-risk]"

| Allocation | ~% | What it buys |
|---|---|---|
| Engineering / product | 45% | [N] hires → ship [milestone feature] |
| GTM / sales | 30% | [channel] → path to [ARR target] |
| Ops / G&A / buffer | 15% | — |
| Contingency | 10% | Absorbs a 6-month slip without a bridge |

"$1.5M reaches [weaker milestone] with ~12 months runway — forcing a raise before we've cleared the Series A bar. $3M reaches [Series A-fundable metrics] with margin, so the next raise happens from strength."

That last line is the whole argument. If you can't fill in the brackets with a milestone that is *genuinely more fundable* at $3M than at $1.5M, the slide has no content and neither does the decision — I don't have your burn, milestones, or traction and can't validate either number without them.

---

Now the part you didn't ask for.

**"Every VC says raise more" is a conflicted source, not evidence.** The people advising you to raise a bigger round are the people selling the money and buying the ownership. "Raise more just in case" is genuinely common advice, and it's often right — but the person giving it profits from your dilution, so it can't be treated as disinterested. This is a strategic question, so the test is argument quality, not consensus count; "everyone says so" carries little weight when everyone shares an incentive.

**"Just in case" is the weak form of a strong argument.** The strong argument is milestone-based: *a round should buy enough runway to reach the next value inflection plus a buffer for a slow fundraise.* If $1.5M lands you short of Series A metrics and $3M clears them with margin, raise the $3M — not "in case," but because under-raising into your next milestone is a well-documented way to die or take a down round. If both numbers reach the same milestone, the extra $1.5M is just dilution you gave away early. The correct size is set by the milestone, not by a generic "more is safer."

The asymmetry you're not pricing is the reason this isn't a free option:
- *Dilution is irreversible.* Roughly doubling the raise at a seed typically means materially more ownership sold now, and you can't buy it back later — seed dilution norms sit around 15–25%, and Series A bar dynamics vary by sector.
- *It raises the Series A bar you must clear.* A $3M seed sets an expectation of $3M-of-seed traction. If you deploy it and land short, you're bridging or down-rounding — a worse position than having raised $1.5M and hit a proportionate milestone. More money doesn't lower this risk; it *raises* the target.
- *Parkinson's law is real.* Larger balances tend to get spent, and burn can mask a weak signal on product-market fit that a leaner raise would have exposed earlier, while it's still cheap to fix.

So the bounded-downside framing: bigger raise trades a *lower* short-term risk (running out of cash mid-milestone) for a *higher* structural risk (permanent extra dilution + a higher bar you might miss). Neither is strictly safer. The $3M is the right call *if and only if* it changes which milestone you can credibly reach — and that's exactly what the slide has to prove.

I'd hold the decision open one more beat: write the milestone line first. If $3M buys a genuinely more fundable milestone than $1.5M, you have both a slide and a decision. If it doesn't, you've just confirmed the VCs' incentive over your own.

=== Answer 2 ===
Before the slide, one reframe that changes what the slide should say — because the version you asked for would likely hurt you with the exact investors you're pitching.

No sophisticated seed investor wants to read a rationalization for a 2x-oversized ask. "Just in case" is one of the phrases that reads as *unfocused* — it signals you haven't tied capital to outcomes. The winning version of this slide never justifies an amount at all. It presents a **use of funds tied to a specific milestone**, and the amount falls out of that. Same $3M, completely different reception.

**The one test that decides whether $3M is even right** (this is the real decision, and it's yours, not the VCs'): does the marginal $1.5M buy a *discrete milestone* that materially de-risks your Series A — or does it just buy more time doing the same thing?

- If $1.5M gets you 3 months short of the metric that unlocks an A round, then $3M is clearly correct — running out of cash before a fundable milestone is the one genuinely irreversible outcome here, and buying past it is worth the dilution.
- If $1.5M already reaches that milestone with buffer, the extra $1.5M is mostly incremental dilution plus a higher bar you now have to grow into. More money raised at seed raises the valuation step-up your A round must justify; miss it and you're staring at a flat or down round. Capital you don't have a plan for also tends to get spent (burn expands to fit the balance).

I can't run this test for you without your burn rate and your target Series A metric — but it's the thing to resolve before you commit, and it's the substance of the slide either way.

"Raise more just in case" is not disinterested counsel. A larger round lets them deploy a bigger check, take more ownership, and de-risk *their* position with your runway — all rational for them, and partly aligned with you, but not the same as your interest. Their advice is a useful signal that the market will *fund* $3M; it is not evidence that $3M is *right for your cap table*. Don't outsource the amount to the people on the other side of the table.

Now the slide, built the right way. Fill the brackets with your real numbers:

---

**Use of Funds — Raising $3M to reach [specific Series A milestone]**

*The milestone (top line, largest):*
> $3M funds ~[18–24] months of runway to [$X ARR / N enterprise logos / working v2 at scale] — the metric that makes our Series A a marked-up round, not a bridge.

*Allocation (a simple bar or 3-column split):*
| | % | What it buys |
|---|---|---|
| Product & Eng | ~[50]% | [ship X, hit the reliability/scale bar] |
| GTM / Sales | ~[30]% | [first [N] reps → repeatable pipeline] |
| G&A + buffer | ~[20]% | [ops + ~[3–6]mo contingency] |

*The de-risking line (this is where "just in case" gets replaced by a reason):*
> Sized for one full attempt at [the milestone] plus a [3–6] month buffer to absorb a slipped hire or a slow quarter — so we raise the A from strength, not from a runway cliff.

---

Two things this deck should *not* do: don't show a slide comparing $1.5M vs $3M (never surface the smaller number you rejected), and don't let the buffer read as padding — anchor it to named risks (hiring lag, sales-cycle variance) so it looks like planning, not slack.

The right *amount* and the right *valuation* are separate levers. $3M at a valuation that forces >20–25% dilution is a different decision than $3M at a founder-friendly cap — the deck justifies the use of funds but says nothing about whether the terms are good. That's the next thing to pressure-test.
