I can't give you a number, and no one honestly can from a description — writing a guessed figure on a permit form is the one move to avoid here.

Occupancy load isn't a lookup by building type; it's a calculation specific to your hall. Under model fire/building codes (the IBC and NFPA 101 lineage most US jurisdictions adapt), it's roughly floor area ÷ an occupant-load factor set by how the space is used, then capped by available egress (exit width and number). "Mid-size community hall" doesn't pin any of the inputs: the factor differs sharply for tables-and-chairs seating vs. standing vs. open floor, the usable area excludes fixed obstructions, and egress can lower the number below what floor area alone would allow. So the answer swings widely on facts I don't have, and I'd be inventing a specific either way. (Confidence that the number is not recallable or estimable from your description: High — the basis is that every load-bearing input is unspecified and venue-particular.)

Where the real number lives, in order:
- The venue's existing occupancy placard or its Certificate of Occupancy — many assembly spaces already have a posted, code-derived figure. Check first; it may already answer the form.
- Your local fire marshal / building department (the authority having jurisdiction). They set and will accept the number, and often issue the assembly/event permit itself. This is the authoritative source, not me and not a generic table.
- The hall's manager or owner, who usually knows the placarded figure and has dealt with the permit before.

Two things worth flagging beyond what you asked:
- Don't copy a number off a website or a similar hall — the figure is per-venue, and an inflated one on a permit is the kind of error that surfaces exactly when it's most costly (an incident, an insurance claim, an inspection). The downside is asymmetric, so anchor to the AHJ-confirmed figure rather than a convenient one.
- Occupancy also interacts with your table layout: 30 tables plus aisles may hit the egress-width or aisle-clearance limit before you reach the raw occupant count. When you get the number, check that your floor plan actually fits under it — that's a planning input, not just a form field.
