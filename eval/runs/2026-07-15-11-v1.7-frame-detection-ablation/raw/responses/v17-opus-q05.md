For almost any small nonprofit, the marginal dollar goes to **retention** — but the reason matters more than the verdict, because it tells you when the answer flips.

## Why retention usually wins

The economics are asymmetric and the asymmetry is large. Acquiring a new donor typically costs more than retaining an existing one, and the acquired donor is worth less per dollar spent because a large fraction lapse after their first gift. The relevant number is the cost of raising a dollar:

- **Acquisition**: Fundraising Effectiveness Project and related sector data have long put first-year donor retention around 20–30% and repeat/multi-year retention around 60%+. Median cost to acquire is often cited near or above $1.00–$1.25 per dollar raised — i.e., acquisition frequently *loses* money in year one and only pays back if the donor is retained. (Moderate confidence on the direction and rough magnitude; Low on any specific figure — these are sector medians with wide variance across cause area, channel, and gift size, and they should be checked against your own data rather than the benchmark.)
- **Retention**: cost to raise a dollar from an existing donor is usually a fraction of that — often quoted in the $0.20 range — because you are re-soliciting a known, warm relationship.

The mechanism behind the verdict: **acquisition's return is largely realized *through* retention.** A newly acquired donor only becomes valuable if they give again. So spending on acquisition while retention is weak means paying full price to fill a leaky bucket — you buy donors and then lose them before they repay the acquisition cost. Plugging the leak raises the return on *every* past and future acquisition dollar simultaneously. That compounding is why retention is the higher-leverage marginal dollar at the margin most small nonprofits actually sit at.

## The conditions that flip the answer

This is not universal. Acquisition is the better marginal dollar when:

1. **You're below minimum viable scale.** A brand-new or very small donor base has little to retain; the constraint is existence, not leakage. Retention spend on 40 donors can't move the budget.
2. **Your retention rate is already high** (say, north of ~70% for an established file). The leak is small; further retention investment hits diminishing returns and growth requires new donors.
3. **Donor file is shrinking / demographically aging out.** Retention alone on a declining base is managed decline. You need acquisition just to hold steady against natural attrition (death, lapse, life changes).
4. **You have a genuinely under-exploited acquisition channel** with unusually low cost — e.g., a warm referral or partnership pipeline — where marginal acquisition economics beat your marginal retention economics.

So the real decision rule is not "retention beats acquisition" but: **fund whichever has the higher marginal return on the next dollar given your current retention rate and file size**, and for most small orgs with mediocre retention and a small-to-mid file, that's retention.

## The reframe that matters more than the binary

The question sets up acquisition and retention as competing uses of one dollar. That framing is useful but incomplete in a way worth naming, because acting on the binary alone can send the dollar to the wrong place:

- **A lot of "retention" spend isn't a separate program — it's cheap behavioral hygiene** that a small org gets wrong for free: prompt and specific thank-yous, reporting back on impact, not over-mailing, a working monthly-gift option, and simply asking again at the right time. Much of the retention lift here costs staff attention, not budget, and dominates both formal alternatives on ROI. Look here before allocating the marginal *dollar* at all.
- **The two are sequential, not parallel.** The highest-return sequence for most small orgs is: fix the cheap retention leaks first (raises the payback on all acquisition), *then* spend on acquisition into a bucket that now holds water. Deciding acquisition-vs-retention without first checking whether the bucket leaks is the common error.
