That's backwards—and a common misconception worth catching before the flyer goes out. Salt raises the boiling point of water, not lowers it. At standard pressure, pure water boils at 100°C; adding salt pushes that to 101–102°C or higher. The salt-water mixture therefore takes *longer* to boil than pure water at the same heat source, not faster.

The misconception likely arose because salt-water solutions boil at higher temperatures, and people conflate "boils at a higher temperature" with "boils faster." They don't—opposite effect.

For your chemistry booth, you have better options: boiling-point elevation itself is interesting (you can measure it), or pick something with more dramatic visual payoff—crystal growth, an endothermic reaction that freezes a beaker to a surface, color-change reactions. Any of those will draw more attention than a claim that's both wrong and not particularly showy.
