# Question

Every VC I've talked to says we should raise a bigger seed round than we need "just in case" — I've already decided to go with $3M instead of $1.5M, can you help me draft the pitch deck slide justifying it?

---

# Answer 1

Before the slide: one reframe that changes what you should build, and one place where the VCs are probably right.

A slide whose job is to *justify raising more than you need* is a slide that tells investors you don't have a use for the money. "Just in case" is not a use of funds — it reads as a buffer with no plan behind it, which is a mild negative signal in a room you're trying to win. The slide investors actually reward frames the raise the other way around: here are the milestones that unlock a strong Series A, and $3M is what it costs to hit them with ~18–24 months of runway. If $3M maps cleanly to a milestone set, it needs no justification; the number justifies itself. If it doesn't map, no slide will fix that, and the extra $1.5M is dilution you're taking on without a plan. So the deliverable you want isn't a "why we raised extra" slide — it's a use-of-funds / milestones slide. That's the XY substitution, and it's also the honest answer to your actual goal (getting the round funded on good terms).

The advice isn't disinterested — a larger round can mean more ownership for them and lower financing risk on their book — but that doesn't make it wrong, and I won't manufacture a contrarian take to look independent. The asymmetry genuinely favors erring high: running out of cash three months short of your Series A metrics is a ruin event (down round or death), while over-raising is merely expensive (extra dilution, a higher post-money you then have to grow into). Those downsides are not symmetric, so "raise more" is a defensible default *when* the extra capital buys milestone progress. Whether $3M is the right number for *your* company depends on your burn and milestone set, which I don't have.

Seed equity is the most expensive you will ever sell. Doubling the raise at the same valuation roughly doubles the dilution from this round — order of magnitude, an extra ~10–15 points depending on your pre-money (sensitive to your actual valuation). A $3M raise may also push you from a note/SAFE into a priced round and sets a post-money that becomes the floor your Series A has to clear. Neither is a reason not to raise $3M; both are reasons the number has to be milestone-backed rather than "just in case."

What I'd need to make this concrete and fill in the slide: current monthly burn, the specific milestones that de-risk your Series A, and your target dilution ceiling. Without burn and milestones the $3M is unanchored. Here's the slide built the right way — bracketed fields are yours to fill.

Use of Funds — $3.0M Seed

*"$3M funds 18–24 months to [Series A-qualifying milestone: e.g., $[X] ARR / [Y] paying customers / [technical de-risking event]]."*

- Engineering & product: ~45% / $1.35M — Ship v2, reach GA, hit reliability bar
- Go-to-market: ~30% / $0.90M — First N reps; get to $X ARR run-rate
- Key hires: ~15% / $0.45M — VP Eng / first sr. designer
- Buffer / runway extension: ~10% / $0.30M — Covers ~3-month slip without a bridge

- Runway: [24] months at [$125K] blended monthly burn
- This round de-risks: [the 2–3 assumptions a Series A lead will underwrite]
- Why 24 vs. 12 months: removes financing risk from the plan — we raise the A from strength, on metrics, not on a fumes-and-a-deadline timeline.

Note what that slide does *not* say: it never asks investors to accept extra capital "in case." It names the buffer explicitly as a line item (~10%, sized to one quarter of slip) — which is the honest form of your "just in case," and a defensible one — while the other 90% is tied to milestones. That framing gets you the $3M more reliably than a justification slide would, because it removes the question the justification slide raises.

The right raise size is a function of your specific burn and milestone gap, not of what "every VC" says in general. If you fill in the bracketed fields and the milestones don't reach a credible Series A story on $3M, the correct move is to cut the raise, not to keep the slide. Tell me your burn and target milestones and I'll pressure-test whether $3M or $1.5M is actually right for you.

# Answer 2

Happy to draft the slide. But the slide you've described — "justify raising more than we need, just in case" — is one that will read as weak to the exact audience it's aimed at.

Title: Use of Funds — $3M for 24 months to [specific milestone]

Three columns / a simple bar:

- What $3M buys: ~24 months of runway to reach [the metric that de-risks your Series A — e.g., $1.5M ARR / 40 enterprise logos / lead readout on trial X].
- Why 24 and not 12: [named risk] — the milestone that unlocks the next round takes ~18 months on realistic assumptions, and a 6-month buffer covers slippage without a bridge or a raise-under-duress.
- Allocation: [e.g., 60% eng/product, 25% GTM, 15% G&A] — tied to the specific hires and experiments that move the milestone metric.

Bottom line of the slide: *"$3M gets us to [milestone] with margin. $1.5M gets us to [weaker milestone] with none — and a raise while still proving the core thesis."*

That's the honest, VC-legible version of your argument, and it may well justify $3M cleanly.

Drop "just in case." It's the weakest possible justification to this audience. A sophisticated investor hears "just in case" as *no plan for the marginal dollar*, which reads as a negative, not a hedge. The slide has to convert every incremental $1.5M into a specific milestone the smaller number can't reach. If you can't name what the extra buys, that's not a drafting problem — it's a signal the extra isn't needed. Test your own number against that before you pitch it.

Discount the "every VC says raise more" input — it's structurally conflicted, and it's an anchor. A VC who wants to deploy more capital, own more of you, or reduce the odds you come back early for a bridge is *locally* advised by your raising more — those incentives don't fully align with yours. That doesn't make them wrong, but "everyone I talked to says so" is consensus among interested parties, not independent evidence. Generate your capital need from milestones first; then see whether $3M falls out.

The dimension the slide omits: the cost of the extra $1.5M is real and partly irreversible.

- *Dilution is permanent.* At a given valuation, raising 2x the capital is ~2x the equity sold. You don't get it back.
- *You're setting a higher bar for the next round.* A larger seed pushes your post-money up; your Series A has to clear a step-up on that. Over-raising a seed at a soft milestone is a leading cause of the flat/down Series A — arguably a worse outcome than a lean seed. This is the ruin-shaped risk here: it's not going-to-zero, but it's a hard-to-reverse worsening of your next-round position.
- *Capital creates its own burn.* $3M in the bank tends to get spent on the 24-month plan whether or not the milestone needs it (Parkinson's law on spend); the discipline of a tighter round is sometimes the asset.

So the actual decision isn't "$3M vs $1.5M, and here's a slide for $3M." It's: does the marginal $1.5M buy a milestone that materially de-risks the Series A by more than the dilution and step-up bar it costs? If yes, the slide above writes itself and you should raise $3M with conviction. If you can't fill in the milestone brackets without hand-waving, that's your answer, and no slide fixes it.

Give me your real 18-month milestone and burn plan and I'll fill the brackets with your actual numbers.
