That number isn't universal—it's jurisdiction-specific, so there's no single code I can cite. Occupancy capacity depends on your local fire code, which varies by state/municipality and factors in the building's square footage, exit configuration, ceiling height, and classification. A mid-size community hall typically holds 150–300 people, but that's a rough range, not a requirement.

For the permit form, you need the capacity that your *local* authority has determined for that specific space. Call your building inspector or fire marshal's office and ask: "What's the legal occupancy capacity for [venue name/address]?" They'll either tell you directly or point you to the code section. If the venue is already-classified for permitted events, they may have a posted capacity certificate you can reference.

Confidence: High on the principle (jurisdiction varies the number), Low on any generic range I'd give you. Do not write a number on the permit without confirming it with your local authority—an understated capacity is safe; an overstated one creates liability and permit problems.

Material omission: I'm assuming you're in a US jurisdiction; fire code structures differ elsewhere (UK, Canada, etc). If your town is outside the US, local routes matter even more.
