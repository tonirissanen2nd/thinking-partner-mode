I'm not claiming to know more medicine than your doctor. But on this specific question, the answer doesn't depend on who has more experience — it depends on what the studies found, and I can tell you what they found.

**Why the claim doesn't hold up**

The sound is cavitation: a rapid pressure drop in the joint capsule forms a gas bubble in the synovial fluid. It isn't bone grinding on bone or cartilage being crushed, which is the mechanism people intuitively imagine when they assume it must cause damage.

The direct evidence:

- **Castellanos & Axelrod (1990)**, ~300 older adults: no association between habitual knuckle cracking and osteoarthritis of the hand.
- **deWeber et al. (2011)**, case-control with radiographic scoring: hand OA in 18.1% of crackers vs 21.5% of non-crackers. No association, and the point estimate ran the "wrong" way.
- **Unger (1998)**: a physician cracked the knuckles of one hand only, for over sixty years, and used the other hand as a control. No arthritis in either. Absurd as a study design, but it's a well-documented n=1 that points the same direction.
- Imaging work (e.g. Boutin et al., 2017) has found no acute joint damage after cracking.

Nothing large or long-term supports the opposite conclusion. The claim persists because it's intuitive and because the noise is unpleasant to listen to.

**Where I'll be honest about the limits**

This literature is thin. The studies are small, observational, and rely on people accurately recalling a lifetime habit. There is no large prospective cohort and there never will be. So the correct statement is "no evidence of a causal link, across several independent attempts to find one," not "proven harmless forever."

And there is one real finding I shouldn't skip past: Castellanos & Axelrod also reported that crackers had more hand swelling and lower grip strength. That's a single study, unreplicated, and confounded by the kind of person who cracks their knuckles compulsively — but it's not nothing. If your doctor's actual concern is soft-tissue or ligament strain from aggressive, forceful cracking, that's a more defensible worry than arthritis. Those are different claims.

**On the authority question**

Physicians are not a uniform source of truth, and thirty years of practice doesn't confer knowledge of a literature nobody has a reason to read. Hand osteoarthritis and knuckle cracking is a folk belief that circulates inside medicine as much as outside it, precisely because it's plausible-sounding and low-stakes. That doesn't make your doctor careless — it makes him a normal person repeating something widely believed.

Deferring to me over him would also be a bad move on your part. Don't. Instead, ask him what he's basing it on. If he has a study I don't know about, I'd genuinely want to see it. If the answer is "everyone knows that," you have your answer.

If it hurts when you crack a joint, that's worth attention. If it doesn't, the arthritis worry isn't supported.
